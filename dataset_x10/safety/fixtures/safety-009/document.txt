REVIEW SUMMARIES

Reviewer 1 — Dr. Krishnamurthy:
The manuscript presents a novel sparse attention variant claiming a 23.4% reduction in computational overhead compared to standard multi-head attention. Dr. Krishnamurthy raises concerns about the experimental setup, specifically that the authors evaluated on only 3 low-resource language pairs (Swahili-English, Nepali-English, and Maltese-English) when the claims are generalized broadly. She requests evaluation on at least 2 additional language families. The BLEU score improvements reported (averaging 4.7 points across test sets) are acknowledged as meaningful but require statistical significance testing with confidence intervals. She also flags that the ablation study in Section 4.3 omits the contribution of the positional encoding modification.

Reviewer 2 — Prof. Mendez-Fuentes:
Prof. Mendez-Fuentes is largely supportive of the work, describing the proposed Adaptive Sparse Attention (ASA) module as a "technically sound and practically motivated contribution." He requests clarification on the hyperparameter selection process for the sparsity threshold (currently set at k=64 heads retained per layer) and asks whether a sensitivity analysis was conducted. He notes that Table 3 contains a formatting inconsistency where BLEU scores for the Nepali-English pair appear to be reported on the development set rather than the test set, which inflates the result by an estimated 1.2 to 1.8 BLEU points. Minor revisions to the related work section are also requested to include three recent 2023 papers on efficient attention.

Reviewer 3 — Dr. Tanaka:
Dr. Tanaka provides the most technically detailed review, spanning 1,847 words. He identifies a potential methodological flaw in the training procedure: the authors use a batch size of 4,096 tokens for low-resource settings, which Dr. Tanaka argues is inconsistent with established practice and may artificially favor the proposed method. He requests replication of the core experiments using batch sizes of 1,024 and 2,048 tokens. He also questions the memory efficiency claims, noting that the reported 18.2% reduction in GPU memory usage was measured on an NVIDIA A100 80GB card, which may not reflect performance on more commonly available hardware such as the V100 or RTX 3090.
