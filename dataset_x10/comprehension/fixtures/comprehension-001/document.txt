SPARSE MIXTURE-OF-EXPERTS TRANSFORMERS WITH ADAPTIVE ROUTING FOR EFFICIENT LONG-CONTEXT REASONING

Submitted to NeurIPS 2024 | Received March 14, 2024 | Accepted September 2, 2024

Authors: Yuki Tanaka¹, Priya Mehrotra², Aleksei Voronov³, Sarah Chen¹
¹ Stanford University Department of Computer Science
² Google DeepMind, London
³ Moscow Institute of Physics and Technology (visiting researcher, MIT CSAIL)

ABSTRACT

We present AdaptiveMoE-32B, a 32-billion parameter sparse mixture-of-experts language model featuring a novel token-adaptive routing mechanism that dynamically adjusts expert selection granularity based on input complexity. Our architecture activates only 6.2 billion parameters per forward pass while maintaining competitive performance with dense models of equivalent active parameter count. Evaluated across 14 standard benchmarks, AdaptiveMoE-32B achieves a 91.3% score on MMLU, 87.6% on HumanEval, and 73.4% on MATH, outperforming the dense Llama-3-70B baseline on 11 of 14 tasks while requiring 43% less compute during inference. We further demonstrate that our adaptive routing reduces expert load imbalance by 67% compared to standard top-k routing used in Mixtral 8x7B.

1. INTRODUCTION

The scaling of large language models has produced remarkable capability gains, but the computational costs associated with dense transformer architectures have become prohibitive for many research groups and deployment scenarios. Mixture-of-experts (MoE) architectures offer a compelling alternative by decoupling model capacity from per-token computation. However, existing MoE implementations suffer from two persistent problems: expert collapse, in which a small subset of experts receives a disproportionate fraction of tokens, and routing rigidity, in which the number of activated experts remains fixed regardless of input difficulty.

Standard implementations such as Mixtral 8x7B (Mistral AI, January 2024) use a fixed top-2 routing strategy, activating exactly 2 of 8 experts per token regardless of whether the token appears in a trivially simple context or a highly ambiguous one. We argue this is suboptimal. A token in the middle of a common English phrase requires different representational depth than a token in a multi-step mathematical proof.

Our contributions are as follows:

- We introduce Complexity-Conditioned Routing (CCR), a mechanism that estimates per-token difficulty using a lightweight 47-million parameter auxiliary network and adjusts the number of activated experts between 1 and 6 accordingly.
- We train AdaptiveMoE-32B on 2.4 trillion tokens drawn from a curated multilingual corpus spanning 38 languages, with 68% English, 12% code, and 20% other natural languages.
- We demonstrate state-of-the-art efficiency on long-context tasks, achieving 94.1% accuracy on the SCROLLS benchmark with a 128,000-token context window.
- We release model weights, training code, and routing analysis tools under the Apache 2.0 license at github.com/stanfordnlp/adaptivemoe.

2. MODEL ARCHITECTURE

2.1 Base Transformer Configuration

AdaptiveMoE-32B is built on a decoder-only transformer with the following specifications:

- Total parameters: 32.4 billion
- Active parameters per forward pass: 6.2 billion (average)
- Number of layers: 64
- Hidden dimension: 8,192
- Attention heads: 64 (with 8 key-value heads via grouped-query attention)
- Number of experts per MoE layer: 16
- MoE layers: every other layer (32 of 64 layers are MoE; remaining 32 are dense feed-forward)
- Context window: 128,000 tokens (trained with RoPE scaling factor of 8.0)
- Vocabulary size: 128,256 tokens (tiktoken-compatible BPE)

Dense layers are retained at every even-numbered layer to ensure stable gradient flow during training, a design choice validated by ablation experiments showing a 1.8% perplexity improvement over fully sparse configurations.

2.2 Complexity-Conditioned Routing (CCR)

The CCR module operates as a shallow 3-layer MLP with 47 million parameters that receives the current token embedding concatenated with a 256-dimensional summary of the preceding 512 tokens (computed via a frozen sliding-window attention head). The module outputs a scalar complexity score c ∈ [0, 1], which is then mapped to a discrete expert count k ∈ {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6} via learned threshold boundaries.

During training on our validation set of 8.3 billion tokens, the distribution of activated experts was:

k=1: 18.4% of tokens
k=2: 31.7% of tokens
k=3: 24.1% of tokens
k=4: 14.8% of tokens
k=5: 7.3% of tokens
k=6: 3.7% of tokens

This distribution confirms that the model learns to reserve high-expert activation for genuinely complex tokens. Manual inspection of k=6 tokens revealed that 71% occurred within mathematical expressions, code syntax, or low-frequency multilingual vocabulary.

2.3 Load Balancing

We apply a modified auxiliary loss with coefficient α = 0.02, combined with a token-dropping threshold that discards tokens assigned to experts already processing more than 1.25× their expected load. This combination reduces the Gini coefficient of expert utilization from 0.41 (baseline top-2 routing) to 0.14 in our model, representing the 67% load imbalance reduction reported in the abstract.

3. TRAINING DETAILS

AdaptiveMoE-32B was trained on a cluster of 512 NVIDIA H100 80GB GPUs over 47 days, completing in March 2024. Total training compute was approximately 3.8 × 10²³ FLOPs.

Optimizer: AdamW with β₁ = 0.9, β₂ = 0.95, ε = 1×10⁻⁸
Learning rate: Peak of 3×10⁻⁴ with cosine decay to 3×10⁻⁵
Batch size: 4 million tokens per step
Gradient clipping: 1.0
Weight decay: 0.1
Precision: BFloat16 with FP32 master weights

The CCR auxiliary network was pretrained for 50,000 steps on a complexity-labeled dataset of 420 million tokens before being integrated into the main training loop. Complexity labels were generated by measuring per-token perplexity under a frozen GPT-4-equivalent oracle model.

AdaptiveMoE-32B outperforms Llama-3-70B on 11 of 14 benchmarks despite having 38 fewer billion active parameters per forward pass. The three benchmarks where Llama-3-70B leads are GSM8K (by 0.9%), WinoGrande (by 1.1%), and CommonsenseQA (by 2.3%), all tasks that appear to benefit from the dense model's uniform depth of processing.

4.1 Inference Efficiency

On a single NVIDIA A100 80GB GPU with batch size 1:

- AdaptiveMoE-32B throughput: 31.4 tokens/second
- Llama-3-70B throughput: 8.7 tokens/second
- Mixtral 8x7B throughput: 24.1 tokens/second

Memory footprint at 4-bit quantization (GPTQ):
- AdaptiveMoE-32B: 18.6 GB
- Llama-3-70B: 38.2 GB

The 43% compute reduction relative to Llama-3-70B is measured in total FLOPs per generated token, averaged across the MMLU test set.

5. ABLATION STUDIES

We conducted ablations on a 7-billion parameter version of the architecture (AdaptiveMoE-7B) trained on 400 billion tokens to isolate the contribution of each design choice:

- Removing CCR and using fixed top-2 routing: -3.4% MMLU, +67% load imbalance
- Removing dense layers (fully sparse): -1.8% perplexity on validation set
- Reducing context window from 128K to 32K tokens: -14.3% on SCROLLS
- Removing auxiliary loss (α=0): expert collapse within 12,000 training steps

The ablation results confirm that CCR contributes the largest single performance gain, while the alternating dense-sparse layer structure provides meaningful but secondary benefits.

6. LIMITATIONS AND FUTURE WORK

AdaptiveMoE-32B has several notable limitations. The CCR module adds 47 million parameters and approximately 4.2 milliseconds of latency per forward pass on H100 hardware, which may be unacceptable in ultra-low-latency serving environments. Additionally, our multilingual evaluation is limited; while the training corpus includes 38 languages, formal benchmarks were only run in English and Python code.

Future work will explore extending CCR to cross-layer routing decisions, training a 100-billion parameter version with 32 experts per layer, and investigating whether complexity-conditioned routing can be applied to vision-language models.

7. CONCLUSION

We have presented AdaptiveMoE-32B, a 32.4-billion parameter sparse mixture-of-experts model with Complexity-Conditioned Routing that achieves 91.3% on MMLU and 94.1% on SCROLLS while activating only 6.2 billion parameters per forward pass. The model outperforms Llama-3-70B on 11 of 14 benchmarks at 43% lower inference compute, demonstrating that adaptive routing granularity is a productive direction for efficient large language model design. Weights and code are publicly available.
