SPARSE MIXTURE-OF-EXPERTS TRANSFORMERS WITH ADAPTIVE ROUTING FOR LOW-RESOURCE MULTILINGUAL TRANSLATION

Submitted to Transactions on Machine Learning Research
Received: March 14, 2024 | Accepted: June 2, 2024

Authors: Priya Nambiar, Tobias Wechsler, Amara Diallo, Sung-Jin Park
Affiliation: Institute for Computational Linguistics, ETH Zürich; Google DeepMind (Diallo)

---

ABSTRACT

We present SparseRoute-MoE, a sparse mixture-of-experts transformer architecture designed to improve multilingual neural machine translation under low-resource conditions. Our model achieves state-of-the-art BLEU scores on 14 of 20 evaluated language pairs in the FLORES-200 benchmark, with an average improvement of 4.7 BLEU points over the dense baseline. Critically, SparseRoute-MoE activates only 18.3% of total parameters per forward pass, reducing inference latency by 41% compared to a parameter-matched dense model while maintaining 97.2% of its translation quality on high-resource language pairs.

---

1. INTRODUCTION

Neural machine translation has achieved remarkable performance on high-resource language pairs such as English-German and English-Chinese, yet performance degrades sharply when training data falls below approximately 500,000 sentence pairs. The WMT23 shared task revealed that the median low-resource language pair in the competition had only 87,000 parallel sentences available, exposing a critical gap between academic benchmarks and real-world deployment needs.

Mixture-of-experts (MoE) architectures offer a promising path forward by enabling parameter scaling without proportional compute scaling. However, standard top-k routing strategies suffer from expert collapse—a phenomenon where 60–80% of tokens are routed to fewer than 20% of experts during training, as documented by Fedus et al. (2022) in the Switch Transformer paper. Our adaptive routing mechanism addresses this collapse through a combination of load-balancing auxiliary losses and a novel temperature-annealed gating function we call Soft-Hard Annealing (SHA).

2. MODEL ARCHITECTURE

2.1 Base Transformer Configuration

SparseRoute-MoE is built on a standard encoder-decoder transformer with the following specifications:

- Encoder layers: 24
- Decoder layers: 24
- Model dimension (d_model): 1,024
- Attention heads: 16
- Feed-forward dimension: 4,096
- Total parameters: 7.4 billion
- Active parameters per forward pass: 1.36 billion (18.3%)

Every third feed-forward layer in both encoder and decoder is replaced with a sparse MoE layer containing 64 experts. Each MoE layer routes each token to exactly 2 experts (top-2 routing), selected by the SHA gating function described below.

2.2 Soft-Hard Annealing (SHA) Gating

Standard top-k routing applies a hard argmax selection, which produces zero gradient signal for non-selected experts. SHA addresses this by beginning training with a soft routing distribution (temperature τ = 4.0) and annealing toward hard selection (τ = 0.1) over the first 150,000 training steps. The annealing schedule follows a cosine decay:

τ(t) = τ_min + 0.5 × (τ_max − τ_min) × (1 + cos(πt / T_anneal))

where t is the current step and T_anneal = 150,000.

This approach allows all experts to receive gradient signal early in training, preventing premature collapse. After annealing completes, routing behavior is functionally equivalent to hard top-2 selection but with a smooth transition history that results in more balanced expert utilization.

2.3 Load Balancing

We apply two auxiliary losses. The first is the standard importance loss from Switch Transformer, weighted at λ_imp = 0.01. The second is a novel entropy regularization term that maximizes the entropy of the per-expert routing distribution across a training batch, weighted at λ_ent = 0.005. Together, these losses reduce expert utilization variance from a Gini coefficient of 0.61 (standard top-2 routing) to 0.23 in our model, measured on the English-Swahili training split.

3. TRAINING DETAILS

SparseRoute-MoE was trained on a multilingual corpus of 4.2 billion sentence pairs spanning 102 languages, assembled from CCAligned, OPUS-100, and the NLLB-200 dataset released by Meta AI in July 2022. Data was sampled using temperature-based upsampling with T = 5, which oversamples low-resource languages relative to their corpus frequency.

Training was conducted on 512 TPU v4 chips over 21 days, completing 800,000 gradient steps with a batch size of 2,048 sentence pairs per step. The optimizer was Adafactor with a learning rate of 0.01 and inverse square root decay schedule. Gradient clipping was applied at a norm threshold of 1.0. Total compute cost was approximately 3.8 × 10²³ FLOPs.

We used mixed-precision training (bfloat16 for activations, float32 for optimizer states) and applied expert parallelism across 8 TPU pods, with each pod hosting 8 experts per MoE layer.

4. EVALUATION

4.1 Benchmarks

We evaluated on the FLORES-200 devtest set, which contains 1,012 sentences per language drawn from English Wikipedia and Wikinews articles, professionally translated into 200 languages. We report chrF++ scores alongside BLEU for robustness, as BLEU is known to be unreliable for morphologically rich languages.

4.2 Main Results

Table 1: BLEU scores on selected FLORES-200 language pairs (English → X direction)

Language Pair | Dense Baseline | SparseRoute-MoE | NLLB-3.3B | Improvement vs. Dense
English → Swahili | 28.4 | 33.9 | 31.2 | +5.5
English → Yoruba | 11.7 | 16.8 | 14.3 | +5.1
English → Burmese | 19.3 | 23.1 | 21.7 | +3.8
English → Kazakh | 14.6 | 19.4 | 17.8 | +4.8
English → German | 38.2 | 39.1 | 38.7 | +0.9
English → Chinese | 34.7 | 35.6 | 35.1 | +0.9
English → Tamil | 22.1 | 27.3 | 25.0 | +5.2
English → Haitian Creole | 31.5 | 36.2 | 33.8 | +4.7

The dense baseline is a parameter-matched 7.4B dense transformer trained on identical data. NLLB-3.3B refers to Meta AI's No Language Left Behind model with 3.3 billion parameters, the strongest publicly available multilingual translation model as of January 2024.

4.3 Low-Resource Analysis

We stratified language pairs by training corpus size into four bins: Very Low (<50K pairs), Low (50K–500K), Medium (500K–5M), and High (>5M). SparseRoute-MoE shows the largest gains in the Very Low and Low bins, with average BLEU improvements of 6.3 and 5.1 points respectively. In the High bin, gains shrink to 0.8 BLEU points on average, suggesting that the architecture's primary advantage is in data-scarce regimes.

4.4 Inference Efficiency

On a single NVIDIA A100 80GB GPU, SparseRoute-MoE achieves a throughput of 1,840 tokens per second at batch size 32, compared to 1,095 tokens per second for the dense 7.4B baseline—a 68% throughput improvement. End-to-end latency for a 50-token sentence is 27ms for SparseRoute-MoE versus 46ms for the dense model, a 41% reduction.

We conducted ablations on the English-Yoruba pair, chosen because it represents a genuinely low-resource scenario with only 43,000 training pairs in our corpus.

Removing SHA causes the largest single drop (2.9 BLEU points), confirming that the annealing mechanism is the most critical component. 6. EXPERT SPECIALIZATION ANALYSIS

We analyzed routing patterns on the FLORES-200 English-Swahili devtest set to understand what linguistic phenomena individual experts capture. Using probing classifiers trained on expert assignment labels, we find statistically significant clustering (p < 0.001, chi-squared test) along three dimensions:

1. Part of speech: Nominal tokens are routed to a distinct cluster of 9 experts (experts 12, 15, 19, 23, 28, 33, 41, 47, 58) with 73% consistency.
2. Sentence position: Tokens in the first quartile of a sentence are routed to different experts than tokens in the final quartile with 61% consistency.
3. Morphological complexity: Tokens requiring agglutinative morphological decomposition show 68% routing consistency to a cluster of 7 experts.

This specialization emerges without any explicit supervision, suggesting that SHA gating encourages functionally meaningful expert division.

---

7. LIMITATIONS AND FUTURE WORK

SparseRoute-MoE requires expert parallelism infrastructure that may not be available in all deployment settings. The model also shows degraded performance on code-switching inputs, achieving only 18.4 BLEU on a held-out Swahili-English code-switching test set compared to 33.9 on standard Swahili. Future work will explore hierarchical routing that operates at the sentence level in addition to the token level, and we plan to release model weights for the 64-expert configuration under a CC BY-NC 4.0 license by September 2024.

---

8. CONCLUSION

SparseRoute-MoE demonstrates that adaptive routing with temperature annealing substantially improves multilingual translation quality in low-resource settings, achieving an average 4.7 BLEU improvement over a dense baseline and outperforming NLLB-3.3B on 14 of 20 evaluated language pairs despite comparable active parameter counts. The 41% latency reduction makes the architecture practically viable for production deployment.
