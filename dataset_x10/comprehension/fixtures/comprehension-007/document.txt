SPARSE MIXTURE-OF-EXPERTS WITH DYNAMIC ROUTING FOR EFFICIENT LARGE-SCALE LANGUAGE MODELING

Published in Proceedings of the 41st International Conference on Machine Learning (ICML 2024)
Submission received: January 14, 2024 | Accepted: April 3, 2024

Authors: Priya Nambiar, Tobias Wehrle, Sungjin Ahn, Mei-Ling Zhao, Konstantinos Papadopoulos

ABSTRACT

We introduce AdaptiveMoE-7B, a sparse mixture-of-experts language model containing 7.2 billion total parameters with only 1.4 billion active parameters per forward pass. Our dynamic routing mechanism, which we term Confidence-Weighted Expert Selection (CWES), outperforms standard top-k routing by 3.7 points on the MMLU benchmark while reducing inference-time FLOPs by 61% compared to a dense baseline of equivalent total parameter count. AdaptiveMoE-7B achieves 73.4% accuracy on MMLU, 81.2% on HellaSwag, and 68.9% on ARC-Challenge, establishing new state-of-the-art results among models with fewer than 2 billion active parameters as of March 2024.

---

1. INTRODUCTION

The scaling laws established by Hoffmann et al. (2022) demonstrated that optimal model performance requires balancing parameter count against training compute. Mixture-of-experts (MoE) architectures offer a compelling alternative to dense scaling by increasing model capacity without proportional increases in per-token computation. However, standard MoE implementations suffer from load imbalance, where a small subset of experts receives disproportionate routing traffic, and from routing instability during training.

Previous work including Switch Transformer (Fedus et al., 2022) and Mixtral 8x7B (Mistral AI, 2024) demonstrated the viability of sparse expert models at scale. Our contribution addresses two persistent limitations: (1) the tendency of top-k routing to collapse toward a small number of dominant experts, and (2) the inability of existing routing mechanisms to adapt to input complexity at inference time.

We trained AdaptiveMoE-7B on 1.8 trillion tokens drawn from a curated multilingual corpus spanning 47 languages, using 512 H100 GPUs over 23 days of wall-clock training time.

2. MODEL ARCHITECTURE

2.1 Overall Design

AdaptiveMoE-7B follows a transformer decoder architecture with 32 layers. Every other layer (16 total) replaces the standard feed-forward network with a sparse MoE block containing 64 experts. The remaining 16 layers retain dense feed-forward networks with hidden dimension 4,096.

Key architectural parameters:

- Total parameters: 7.2 billion
- Active parameters per token: 1.4 billion
- Number of MoE layers: 16
- Experts per MoE layer: 64
- Top-k experts selected per token: 2 (dynamic range: 1-4 under CWES)
- Attention heads: 32
- Head dimension: 128
- Context window: 8,192 tokens
- Vocabulary size: 131,072 tokens (SentencePiece BPE)

2.2 Confidence-Weighted Expert Selection (CWES)

Standard top-k routing computes a softmax over expert logits and selects the k experts with highest probability, where k is fixed. CWES instead computes a confidence score C from the entropy of the routing distribution:

C = 1 - H(p) / log(N)

where H(p) is the Shannon entropy of the routing probability vector and N is the number of experts. When C falls below threshold τ_low = 0.31, the model activates an additional expert (up to a maximum of 4). When C exceeds τ_high = 0.78, the model activates only a single expert.

This adaptive behavior means that tokens representing ambiguous or complex semantic content recruit more expert capacity, while straightforward tokens (common function words, punctuation) are processed by a single expert. Empirically, 67% of tokens in our validation set were processed by exactly 2 experts, 21% by 1 expert, and 12% by 3 or 4 experts.

2.3 Load Balancing

We apply a modified auxiliary loss with coefficient α = 0.02 that penalizes the squared coefficient of variation of expert utilization across a batch, rather than the standard linear penalty used in Switch Transformer. This modification reduced expert collapse (defined as any single expert receiving more than 15% of routing traffic) from occurring in 34% of training runs with standard balancing to 4% of runs with our approach, measured across 50 independent training seeds on a smaller 1.3B parameter proxy model.

3. TRAINING DETAILS

3.1 Data

The training corpus of 1.8 trillion tokens was assembled from the following sources:

Source                          Tokens (billions)    Percentage
Common Crawl (filtered)         900                  50.0%
GitHub code                     270                  15.0%
ArXiv papers                    126                  7.0%
Wikipedia (47 languages)        108                  6.0%
Books3 corpus                   90                   5.0%
StackExchange                   72                   4.0%
PubMed abstracts                54                   3.0%
Multilingual news               54                   3.0%
Mathematical text (Lean/Coq)    36                   2.0%
Other curated sources           90                   5.0%

Data was filtered using a perplexity-based quality filter trained on a 10 million token held-out set, removing approximately 23% of raw Common Crawl content.

3.2 Optimization

- Optimizer: AdamW with β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.95, ε = 1e-8
- Peak learning rate: 3e-4 with cosine decay to 3e-5
- Warmup steps: 2,000
- Batch size: 4 million tokens (global)
- Gradient clipping: 1.0
- Weight decay: 0.1
- Training precision: BFloat16 with FP32 master weights
- Hardware: 512 NVIDIA H100 80GB SXM5 GPUs
- Training duration: 23 days (approximately 552 hours)
- Total training FLOPs: 2.4 × 10^23

A training instability occurred at step 47,300, manifesting as a loss spike from 2.31 to 4.87 over 200 steps. This was resolved by reverting to the checkpoint at step 46,800 and reducing the learning rate by 15% for 500 steps before resuming the original schedule.

4. EVALUATION RESULTS

4.1 Benchmark Performance

We evaluated AdaptiveMoE-7B against comparable models on standard benchmarks. All evaluations used 5-shot prompting unless otherwise noted.

Model                    Active Params    MMLU     HellaSwag    ARC-C    GSM8K    HumanEval
AdaptiveMoE-7B (ours)   1.4B             73.4%    81.2%        68.9%    52.3%    34.1%
Mistral 7B               7.0B             64.2%    81.3%        59.9%    35.4%    26.2%
Mixtral 8x7B             12.9B            70.6%    86.7%        66.0%    58.4%    40.2%
Llama 2 13B              13.0B            54.8%    80.7%        59.4%    28.7%    18.3%
Gemma 7B                 7.0B             64.3%    80.9%        61.1%    46.4%    32.3%
Phi-2 (2.7B)             2.7B             57.0%    75.1%        61.1%    57.2%    47.8%

AdaptiveMoE-7B achieves the highest MMLU score among models with fewer than 2 billion active parameters, outperforming Mistral 7B by 9.2 percentage points despite using 80% fewer active parameters per token.

4.2 Inference Efficiency

On a single H100 GPU with batch size 1:

- Tokens per second (generation): 847 tok/s
- Tokens per second (dense 7B baseline): 312 tok/s
- Memory footprint (weights only): 14.4 GB in BFloat16
- Time-to-first-token (512 token prompt): 38ms

The 2.71x throughput improvement over the dense baseline reflects the reduced active parameter count during autoregressive generation.

4.3 Ablation Studies

We conducted ablations on a 1.3B parameter proxy model trained for 100 billion tokens:

Configuration                           MMLU (5-shot)    Expert Utilization CV
CWES (full method)                      61.3%            0.18
Fixed top-2 routing                     58.9%            0.31
Fixed top-1 routing                     55.7%            0.44
CWES without load balancing             60.1%            0.67
Dense FFN baseline (1.3B active)        56.2%            N/A

The CWES mechanism provides a 2.4 point improvement over fixed top-2 routing on MMLU at the proxy scale, with the load balancing modification contributing an additional 1.2 points.

5. ANALYSIS

5.1 Expert Specialization

We analyzed expert activation patterns across 10,000 randomly sampled documents from the validation set. Using a mutual information metric between expert selection and token part-of-speech category, we find that experts in layers 8-16 show statistically significant specialization (p < 0.001, χ² test) for syntactic categories. Specifically, 11 of 64 experts in layer 12 activate preferentially for noun phrases (MI > 0.34), while 8 experts show strong preference for verb phrases (MI > 0.29).

5.2 Scaling Behavior

Training proxy models at 350M, 700M, and 1.3B active parameter scales suggests that CWES provides consistent gains of 2.1-2.6 MMLU points over fixed top-k routing across all scales tested, indicating the benefit does not diminish at larger active parameter counts.

---

6. LIMITATIONS AND FUTURE WORK

AdaptiveMoE-7B underperforms on code generation tasks relative to models like Phi-2 that emphasize code in training. The 34.1% HumanEval score, while competitive for the active parameter class, suggests that expert routing may not optimally handle the structured, long-range dependencies characteristic of code. Future work will investigate task-conditioned routing priors and extend the context window beyond 8,192 tokens using ring attention.
