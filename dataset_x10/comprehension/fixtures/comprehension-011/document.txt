SPARSE MIXTURE-OF-EXPERTS TRANSFORMERS WITH ADAPTIVE ROUTING FOR EFFICIENT LONG-CONTEXT REASONING

Published in Proceedings of the 41st International Conference on Machine Learning (ICML 2024), Vienna, Austria. Submitted February 14, 2024. Accepted May 3, 2024.

Authors: Priya Nambiar, Tobias Wechsler, Sunita Rao, Marcus Chen, Fatima Al-Rashid
Affiliation: DeepMind Research, London, UK; Stanford University Department of Computer Science, Stanford, CA

---

ABSTRACT

We introduce AdaptiveMoE-7B, a sparse mixture-of-experts language model with 7.2 billion total parameters and 1.4 billion active parameters per forward pass. Our architecture incorporates a novel adaptive routing mechanism, TopK-Softmax Gating with Load Balancing (TSGLB), which reduces expert collapse by 67% compared to standard top-k routing while maintaining inference throughput of 2,340 tokens per second on a single NVIDIA H100 GPU. Evaluated across 14 benchmarks including MMLU, HumanEval, GSM8K, and MATH, AdaptiveMoE-7B achieves an average score of 71.4%, outperforming dense models of equivalent active parameter count by 8.3 percentage points and matching the performance of dense 6.7B parameter models at 43% lower computational cost. We release model weights, training code, and evaluation scripts at github.com/deepmind/adaptivemoe.

---

1. INTRODUCTION

The scaling laws established by Kaplan et al. (2020) demonstrated that model performance improves predictably with parameter count, compute, and data. However, the computational cost of dense transformer models scales linearly with parameter count during inference, creating a fundamental tension between capability and deployment efficiency. Sparse mixture-of-experts (MoE) architectures address this by activating only a subset of parameters for each input token, enabling models with large total parameter counts to maintain manageable inference costs.

Despite their promise, MoE models suffer from two well-documented failure modes: expert collapse, wherein a small subset of experts receives the majority of routing decisions, and load imbalance, which creates computational bottlenecks during distributed inference. Prior work including Switch Transformer (Fedus et al., 2022) and Mixtral 8x7B (Mistral AI, January 2024) introduced auxiliary loss terms to encourage balanced routing, but these approaches introduce a tension between routing quality and load balance that degrades downstream task performance by an estimated 2-4 percentage points.

Our contribution is TSGLB, a routing mechanism that decouples load balancing from the primary routing objective through a two-stage gating process, eliminating the need for auxiliary loss terms while achieving superior expert utilization.

2. MODEL ARCHITECTURE

2.1 Overview

AdaptiveMoE-7B follows a decoder-only transformer architecture with 32 layers. Every other layer (16 total) replaces the standard feed-forward network with a sparse MoE block containing 16 experts, each with hidden dimension 4,096. The remaining 16 layers retain dense feed-forward networks with hidden dimension 14,336. The model uses grouped-query attention (GQA) with 32 query heads and 8 key-value heads, a context window of 32,768 tokens, and rotary positional embeddings (RoPE) with base frequency 500,000.

2.2 TSGLB Routing Mechanism

Standard top-k routing computes a softmax over expert logits and selects the k experts with highest probability. TSGLB modifies this in two stages:

Stage 1 (Candidate Selection): For each token, compute raw expert logits h = W_g * x, where W_g is a learned gating matrix. Select the top-8 candidate experts by logit value.

Stage 2 (Adaptive Reranking): Apply a lightweight 2-layer MLP (hidden dimension 128) that takes as input the token representation concatenated with current expert load statistics (updated with exponential moving average, decay rate 0.99) to rerank candidates and select the final top-2 experts.

This two-stage process allows the model to learn routing quality in Stage 1 without load constraints, then apply load-aware adjustments in Stage 2 without corrupting the primary routing signal. The Stage 2 MLP adds only 0.003% to total parameter count.

3. TRAINING

3.1 Data and Compute

AdaptiveMoE-7B was trained on 2.4 trillion tokens drawn from a mixture of web text (60%), code (20%), scientific papers (12%), and books (8%). Web data was filtered using a classifier trained on 50,000 human-labeled examples to remove low-quality content, reducing the raw Common Crawl corpus by 73%. Deduplication using MinHash LSH with 128 hash functions removed an additional 18% of remaining documents.

Training used 512 NVIDIA H100 80GB GPUs across 64 nodes connected via 3.2 Tbps InfiniBand fabric. Total training compute was 3.8 × 10^23 FLOPs, completed over 47 days beginning September 11, 2023 and concluding October 28, 2023. Peak GPU utilization was 84.3% with model flops utilization (MFU) of 51.7%.

3.2 Hyperparameters

- Optimizer: AdamW with β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.95, ε = 1e-8
- Learning rate: Peak 3e-4 with cosine decay to 3e-5
- Warmup: 2,000 steps
- Batch size: 4,194,304 tokens (4M tokens)
- Gradient clipping: 1.0
- Weight decay: 0.1
- Precision: BFloat16 with FP32 master weights

4. EVALUATION RESULTS

4.1 Benchmark Performance

We evaluated AdaptiveMoE-7B against four baseline models: Mistral-7B (dense, 7.3B parameters), Llama-2-13B (dense, 13.0B parameters), Mixtral 8x7B (MoE, 46.7B total / 12.9B active), and Phi-2 (dense, 2.7B parameters).

| Benchmark | AdaptiveMoE-7B | Mistral-7B | Llama-2-13B | Mixtral 8x7B | Phi-2 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| MMLU (5-shot) | 68.4% | 64.2% | 55.8% | 70.6% | 61.1% |
| HumanEval (pass@1) | 42.7% | 37.4% | 18.3% | 40.2% | 47.6% |
| GSM8K (8-shot) | 74.3% | 52.2% | 29.0% | 74.4% | 57.2% |
| MATH (4-shot) | 22.1% | 13.1% | 6.7% | 22.9% | 11.0% |
| HellaSwag | 83.2% | 81.3% | 80.7% | 86.5% | 73.1% |
| ARC-Challenge | 62.8% | 60.0% | 59.4% | 66.0% | 61.1% |
| TruthfulQA | 51.3% | 42.2% | 41.9% | 51.1% | 44.2% |
| Average (14 benchmarks) | 71.4% | 63.1% | 52.8% | 73.2% | 58.7% |

4.2 Efficiency Metrics

Inference throughput was measured on a single NVIDIA H100 80GB GPU with batch size 1, sequence length 2,048, using vLLM 0.3.1 with PagedAttention:

- AdaptiveMoE-7B: 2,340 tokens/second
- Mistral-7B: 2,180 tokens/second
- Llama-2-13B: 1,240 tokens/second
- Mixtral 8x7B: 890 tokens/second (requires 2× H100 for full precision)

Memory footprint at BFloat16 precision: AdaptiveMoE-7B requires 14.4 GB VRAM for model weights, fitting within a single 24GB consumer GPU when using 4-bit GPTQ quantization (7.8 GB).

4.3 Expert Utilization Analysis

We measured expert collapse using the coefficient of variation (CV) of expert selection frequency across 10 million tokens from the validation set. Lower CV indicates more balanced routing.

- Standard top-2 routing (no auxiliary loss): CV = 0.847
- Top-2 routing with auxiliary loss (λ = 0.01): CV = 0.412
- TSGLB (ours): CV = 0.279

TSGLB achieves 67% reduction in expert collapse relative to unregularized routing and 32% reduction relative to auxiliary-loss-based approaches, while avoiding the 2.1 percentage point performance degradation we observed when tuning auxiliary loss weight λ.

5. ABLATION STUDIES

We conducted ablations on three architectural choices using a smaller 1.3B parameter version of the model trained on 200 billion tokens:

| Configuration | MMLU | GSM8K | Throughput |
|---|---|---|---|
| Full AdaptiveMoE (TSGLB, top-2) | 58.3% | 61.2% | 2,340 tok/s |
| Standard top-2 routing | 55.1% | 57.8% | 2,380 tok/s |
| Top-2 + auxiliary loss (λ=0.01) | 56.8% | 59.4% | 2,375 tok/s |
| Top-1 routing (TSGLB) | 54.9% | 55.3% | 2,510 tok/s |
| 8 experts per MoE layer | 57.1% | 59.8% | 2,290 tok/s |

The two-stage TSGLB routing adds approximately 1.7% overhead to routing computation but yields consistent gains across tasks.

---

6. LIMITATIONS AND FUTURE WORK

AdaptiveMoE-7B underperforms on HumanEval relative to Phi-2 despite having substantially more parameters, likely reflecting differences in code data composition. Our training mixture contains 20% code compared to Phi-2's estimated 35-40%. Additionally, the TSGLB Stage 2 MLP introduces a sequential dependency that limits parallelism during expert routing; we estimate this could be resolved through asynchronous routing with 5-8% additional throughput gains.

Future work will explore scaling TSGLB to models with 64 or more experts, extending context length to 128,000 tokens using ring attention, and applying the adaptive routing framework to vision-language architectures.
