SPARSE MIXTURE-OF-EXPERTS TRANSFORMERS WITH ADAPTIVE ROUTING FOR EFFICIENT LARGE-SCALE LANGUAGE MODELING

Proceedings of the 41st International Conference on Machine Learning (ICML 2024)
Submitted: February 14, 2024 | Accepted: May 3, 2024

Authors: Priya Nambiar, Tobias Wehrle, Sung-Jin Cho, Amara Diallo, Rafael Monteiro
Affiliation: Distributed Intelligence Laboratory, Carnegie Mellon University; Google DeepMind (Wehrle, Diallo)

---

ABSTRACT

We present AdaptiveMoE-7B, a sparse mixture-of-experts language model featuring 7.2 billion total parameters with only 1.4 billion active parameters per forward pass. Our architecture introduces a learned routing mechanism we call Confidence-Weighted Dispatch (CWD), which dynamically adjusts expert selection based on token-level uncertainty estimates. Evaluated across 14 standard benchmarks, AdaptiveMoE-7B achieves performance competitive with dense 13B-parameter models while reducing inference FLOPs by 61.3% and training compute by 43.7% compared to a dense baseline of equivalent total parameter count. On MMLU, the model achieves 71.4% accuracy, surpassing Mistral-7B (64.2%) and matching LLaMA-2-13B (68.9%) on most sub-tasks. We release model weights, training code, and evaluation scripts at github.com/cmu-dil/adaptive-moe.

1. INTRODUCTION

The scaling laws established by Kaplan et al. (2020) demonstrated that language model performance improves predictably with compute, data, and parameter count. However, the computational cost of dense transformer inference scales linearly with parameter count, creating practical deployment barriers. Sparse mixture-of-experts (MoE) architectures offer a compelling alternative: by activating only a subset of parameters per token, they decouple total model capacity from per-token compute cost.

Prior work including Switch Transformer (Fedus et al., 2022) and GLaM (Du et al., 2022) demonstrated that MoE models can match or exceed dense model performance at reduced training cost. However, these systems rely on top-k routing with fixed k, which we argue is suboptimal because different tokens require different levels of specialization. A syntactic function word like "the" may require minimal expert consultation, while a domain-specific technical term may benefit from routing to multiple specialized experts simultaneously.

Our contribution is threefold: (1) we introduce Confidence-Weighted Dispatch, a routing mechanism that varies the number of active experts per token between 1 and 4 based on a learned confidence score; (2) we demonstrate that auxiliary load-balancing losses can be reduced by 34% without sacrificing expert utilization through our proposed Soft Capacity Constraint; and (3) we provide a comprehensive ablation study across 6 architectural variants trained on identical data.

2. MODEL ARCHITECTURE

2.1 Base Transformer Configuration

AdaptiveMoE-7B is built on a standard transformer decoder architecture with the following specifications:

- Number of layers: 32
- Hidden dimension: 4096
- Attention heads: 32
- Key/value heads: 8 (grouped-query attention)
- Vocabulary size: 32,000 tokens (BPE tokenization)
- Maximum sequence length: 8,192 tokens
- Positional encoding: RoPE with base frequency 10,000

Every other feed-forward layer (16 of 32 total) is replaced with a mixture-of-experts block. The remaining 16 layers retain standard dense feed-forward networks with intermediate dimension 11,008.

2.2 Confidence-Weighted Dispatch (CWD)

Each MoE layer contains 64 experts, each with hidden dimension 2,048. The router is a two-layer MLP with 512 hidden units that produces both a distribution over experts and a scalar confidence score c ∈ [0, 1]. The number of active experts k is determined by:

k = 1 + floor(3 × (1 - c))

This yields k = 4 for low-confidence tokens (c < 0.33), k = 2 for moderate-confidence tokens (0.33 ≤ c < 0.67), and k = 1 for high-confidence tokens (c ≥ 0.67). In practice, across our training corpus, 52.3% of tokens use k=1, 31.4% use k=2, and 16.3% use k=4.

2.3 Soft Capacity Constraint

Traditional MoE routing enforces hard capacity limits per expert, causing token dropping when an expert receives more tokens than its buffer allows. We replace this with a soft penalty that adds a quadratic cost to the routing loss when expert load exceeds 1.25× the mean load. This eliminates token dropping entirely while maintaining load balance within 8.2% standard deviation across experts, compared to 6.1% for hard-capacity routing but with zero dropped tokens versus 2.3% token drop rate in the baseline.

3. TRAINING DETAILS

3.1 Dataset and Preprocessing

AdaptiveMoE-7B was trained on a 1.8 trillion token corpus assembled from the following sources:

| Source | Tokens (billions) | Percentage |
|---|---|---|
| Common Crawl (filtered) | 900 | 50.0% |
| GitHub code | 270 | 15.0% |
| ArXiv papers | 180 | 10.0% |
| Books3 | 162 | 9.0% |
| Wikipedia (multilingual) | 126 | 7.0% |
| StackExchange | 90 | 5.0% |
| PubMed abstracts | 72 | 4.0% |

Data deduplication used MinHash LSH with 128 hash functions and a Jaccard similarity threshold of 0.8, removing 23.4% of the raw crawl data.

3.2 Training Configuration

Training ran for 90 days on 512 NVIDIA H100 80GB GPUs across 64 nodes at the Pittsburgh Supercomputing Center, beginning September 11, 2023 and completing December 9, 2023. Key hyperparameters:

- Optimizer: AdamW with β1=0.9, β2=0.95, ε=1e-8
- Peak learning rate: 3e-4 with cosine decay to 3e-5
- Warmup steps: 2,000
- Batch size: 4 million tokens per step
- Total training steps: 450,000
- Gradient clipping: 1.0
- Weight decay: 0.1
- Precision: BFloat16 with FP32 master weights

The auxiliary load-balancing loss coefficient was set to 0.01, and the router confidence loss coefficient was 0.005. Total training cost was approximately 2.1 × 10²³ FLOPs.

4. EXPERIMENTAL RESULTS

4.1 Main Benchmark Results

We evaluated AdaptiveMoE-7B against five baseline models across 14 benchmarks. Results below report accuracy (%) for classification tasks and exact match for generation tasks:

| Benchmark | AdaptiveMoE-7B | Mistral-7B | LLaMA-2-7B | LLaMA-2-13B | Mixtral-8x7B |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| MMLU (5-shot) | 71.4 | 64.2 | 45.3 | 68.9 | 70.6 |
| HellaSwag | 82.1 | 81.3 | 77.2 | 80.7 | 86.5 |
| ARC-Challenge | 63.7 | 60.0 | 53.7 | 59.4 | 66.0 |
| WinoGrande | 78.4 | 78.4 | 74.2 | 77.0 | 81.7 |
| GSM8K (8-shot) | 52.3 | 52.1 | 14.6 | 28.7 | 58.4 |
| HumanEval | 34.1 | 30.5 | 12.8 | 18.3 | 40.2 |
| TruthfulQA | 46.8 | 42.2 | 38.8 | 41.9 | 51.3 |
| BoolQ | 87.2 | 86.5 | 77.7 | 82.9 | 88.7 |

4.2 Efficiency Analysis

Inference throughput was measured on a single NVIDIA A100 80GB GPU with batch size 1 and sequence length 512:

- AdaptiveMoE-7B: 47.3 tokens/second
- Dense 7B baseline: 89.1 tokens/second
- Dense 13B baseline: 41.6 tokens/second

Despite having 7.2B total parameters, AdaptiveMoE-7B achieves 13.7% higher throughput than a dense 13B model while outperforming it on 9 of 14 benchmarks. Memory footprint at BFloat16 precision is 14.4 GB, compared to 26.0 GB for a dense 13B model.

4.3 Ablation Study

We trained six 1B-parameter variants for 100B tokens each to isolate architectural contributions:

| Variant | MMLU | GSM8K | Params Active |
|---|---|---|---|
| Dense baseline | 51.2 | 21.3 | 1.0B |
| Fixed top-1 MoE | 53.4 | 23.1 | 0.4B |
| Fixed top-2 MoE | 55.1 | 25.7 | 0.6B |
| Fixed top-4 MoE | 56.3 | 27.2 | 1.0B |
| CWD (ours, no soft cap) | 57.8 | 28.9 | 0.58B avg |
| CWD + Soft Capacity (ours) | 58.9 | 30.4 | 0.58B avg |

CWD with Soft Capacity outperforms fixed top-2 routing by 3.8 MMLU points while using equivalent average active parameters.

5. ANALYSIS AND DISCUSSION

5.1 Expert Specialization

We analyzed expert activation patterns across 10 million tokens from the Pile validation set. Experts in layers 8-16 show strong domain specialization: the top 8 experts by activation frequency for code tokens overlap with the top 8 experts for mathematical text by only 1.2 experts on average, suggesting genuine functional specialization rather than load-balanced generalism.

5.2 Confidence Score Calibration

The router's confidence scores are well-calibrated: tokens assigned k=1 have a mean perplexity of 4.3 on held-out data, while tokens assigned k=4 have mean perplexity of 31.7, confirming that the router correctly identifies difficult tokens requiring more expert consultation.

5.3 Limitations

AdaptiveMoE-7B underperforms Mixtral-8x7B on code generation (HumanEval: 34.1 vs. 40.2) and mathematical reasoning (GSM8K: 52.3 vs. 58.4). We attribute this to Mixtral's substantially larger total parameter count (46.7B vs. 7.2B). Additionally, variable k routing introduces non-deterministic latency that may complicate real-time deployment.

---

6. CONCLUSION

We introduced AdaptiveMoE-7B and the Confidence-Weighted Dispatch routing mechanism, demonstrating that adaptive expert selection improves over fixed-k routing by 3.8 MMLU points at equivalent compute cost. The model achieves 71.4% on MMLU, surpassing all 7B-class dense models evaluated and matching LLaMA-2-13B performance at 61.3% lower inference FLOPs. Future work will explore extending CWD to vision-language models and investigating whether confidence scores can serve as uncertainty estimates for downstream calibration tasks.
