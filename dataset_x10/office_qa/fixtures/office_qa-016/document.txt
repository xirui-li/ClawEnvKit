EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents a comprehensive compound annual growth rate (CAGR) analysis of the Federal Highway Trust Fund (HTF) for the ten-year period spanning fiscal year 2014 through fiscal year 2024. The analysis encompasses both the Highway Account and the Mass Transit Account, examining revenue receipts, expenditure patterns, investment income, and fund balance trajectories. The CAGR computations contained herein are intended to support congressional budget justifications, inform the Federal Highway Administration's long-range planning obligations under 23 U.S.C. § 104, and provide transparency to the public regarding the fiscal health of the nation's primary surface transportation financing mechanism.

The Highway Trust Fund received its primary revenue from federal motor fuel excise taxes, which have remained fixed at 18.4 cents per gallon for gasoline and 24.4 cents per gallon for diesel fuel since October 1, 1993. This structural rigidity in the revenue base, combined with increasing fuel efficiency standards and the gradual electrification of the vehicle fleet, has created persistent funding pressures that are quantified and contextualized throughout this document.

---

SECTION 1: FUND STRUCTURE AND STATUTORY AUTHORITY

The Federal Highway Trust Fund was established under the Highway Revenue Act of 1956 and operates under the authority of 26 U.S.C. §§ 9503 and 9504. The fund is divided into two discrete accounts:

1. The Highway Account, which finances federal-aid highway programs administered primarily through the Federal Highway Administration (FHWA).
2. The Mass Transit Account, which finances transit capital and operating assistance programs administered through the Federal Transit Administration (FTA).

Revenue credited to the fund derives from the following excise taxes:
- Gasoline tax: 18.4 cents per gallon (15.44 cents to Highway Account; 2.86 cents to Mass Transit Account)
- Diesel fuel tax: 24.4 cents per gallon
- Special fuels tax: 18.4 cents per gallon
- Truck and trailer sales tax: 12 percent of retail price
- Heavy vehicle use tax: up to $550 per vehicle annually
- Tire excise tax: applicable to tires weighing over 40 pounds

Uninvested balances in the fund are invested in special-issue U.S. Treasury securities pursuant to 31 U.S.C. § 9602, with interest credited monthly at rates determined by the Secretary of the Treasury.

SECTION 3: CAGR COMPUTATION METHODOLOGY

The compound annual growth rate is computed using the standard financial formula:

CAGR = [(Ending Value / Beginning Value)^(1/n)] - 1

Where:
- Ending Value = the fund metric value at the conclusion of the measurement period
- Beginning Value = the fund metric value at the commencement of the measurement period
- n = the number of years in the measurement period

For the ten-year period FY 2014 through FY 2024, n = 10.

All CAGR calculations in this report use FY 2014 as the base year and FY 2024 as the terminal year. Intermediate-year values are presented for informational purposes and to illustrate volatility, but do not alter the endpoint-to-endpoint CAGR computation. Calculations were performed by the Division of Trust Fund Accounting and Actuarial Services using Treasury's proprietary Financial Analysis Modeling System (FAMS), version 4.2, updated September 2024.

---

SECTION 4: CAGR RESULTS — REVENUE RECEIPTS

Applying the CAGR formula to total HTF receipts:

CAGR (Total HTF Receipts, FY 2014–FY 2024):
= ($49,483 / $39,959)^(1/10) - 1
= (1.23836)^(0.10) - 1
= 1.02155 - 1
= 2.155 percent per annum

Highway Account CAGR (FY 2014–FY 2024):
= ($43,102 / $34,812)^(1/10) - 1
= (1.23814)^(0.10) - 1
= 2.153 percent per annum

Mass Transit Account CAGR (FY 2014–FY 2024):
= ($6,381 / $5,147)^(1/10) - 1
= (1.23983)^(0.10) - 1
= 2.168 percent per annum

The Mass Transit Account demonstrated marginally stronger compound growth than the Highway Account over the ten-year period, reflecting proportionally higher ridership recovery following the pandemic and increased urbanization trends driving transit demand in metropolitan statistical areas.

Critically, the 2.155 percent CAGR for total receipts significantly underperforms the average Consumer Price Index (CPI) growth rate of 3.47 percent per annum over the same period, confirming that the real purchasing power of Highway Trust Fund revenues has declined materially. In inflation-adjusted terms, the fund's FY 2024 receipts of $49,483 million are equivalent to approximately $35,614 million in FY 2014 dollars, representing a real decline of approximately 10.9 percent from the FY 2014 baseline.

SECTION 5: FUND BALANCE AND INVESTMENT INCOME ANALYSIS

The following summarizes beginning-of-year fund balances and investment income for selected fiscal years:

Fiscal Year | Beginning Balance ($ millions) | Investment Income ($ millions) | Ending Balance ($ millions)
FY 2014     | $1,614                         | $42                            | $2,108
FY 2017     | $18,741                        | $287                           | $19,344
FY 2020     | $16,892                        | $412                           | $11,203
FY 2022     | $22,417                        | $318                           | $28,614
FY 2024     | $51,203                        | $2,847                         | $54,611

The dramatic increase in investment income from $42 million in FY 2014 to $2,847 million in FY 2024 reflects both the substantially higher fund balance maintained following general fund transfers authorized under the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law (Public Law 117-58, enacted November 15, 2021) and the elevated interest rate environment of FY 2023–2024. The CAGR of investment income over the ten-year period is computed as follows:

CAGR (Investment Income, FY 2014–FY 2024):
= ($2,847 / $42)^(1/10) - 1
= (67.786)^(0.10) - 1
= 52.94 percent per annum

This extraordinary CAGR of 52.94 percent reflects the compounding effect of both the $118 billion in general fund transfers authorized under the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law and the Federal Reserve's interest rate increases, which pushed the Treasury special-issue rate applicable to the HTF from 0.125 percent in FY 2021 to 5.25 percent in FY 2024.

---

SECTION 6: EXPENDITURE CAGR ANALYSIS

Total obligations against the Highway Trust Fund grew from $50,114 million in FY 2014 to $72,388 million in FY 2024, yielding an expenditure CAGR of:

= ($72,388 / $50,114)^(1/10) - 1
= (1.44444)^(0.10) - 1
= 3.742 percent per annum

The expenditure CAGR of 3.742 percent substantially exceeded the revenue CAGR of 2.155 percent, confirming the structural imbalance that has required repeated congressional intervention in the form of general fund transfers totaling $275 billion since FY 2008. The largest single-year transfer occurred in FY 2022, when $90 billion was credited to the fund pursuant to the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law.

---

SECTION 7: PROJECTED CAGR UNDER ALTERNATIVE SCENARIOS

The Division of Trust Fund Accounting and Actuarial Services has modeled three forward-looking scenarios for the period FY 2024 through FY 2034:

Scenario A — Current Law (No New Revenue Measures):
Projected CAGR of total receipts: 1.2 percent per annum
Projected fund exhaustion: FY 2031

Scenario B — Indexed Fuel Tax (CPI Adjustment Beginning FY 2026):
Projected CAGR of total receipts: 3.8 percent per annum
Projected fund solvency maintained through FY 2034 with a terminal balance of $14,200 million

Scenario C — Vehicle Miles Traveled Fee Phased In FY 2027–FY 2030:
Projected CAGR of total receipts: 5.1 percent per annum
Projected terminal balance FY 2034: $38,700 million

---

SECTION 8: CONCLUSIONS AND FINDINGS

Based on the foregoing analysis, the Division of Trust Fund Accounting and Actuarial Services presents the following findings:

1. Total Highway Trust Fund receipts grew at a CAGR of 2.155 percent from FY 2014 to FY 2024, from $39,959 million to $49,483 million.

2. This growth rate lagged CPI inflation by 1.315 percentage points per annum, resulting in a real purchasing power decline of approximately 10.9 percent over the decade.

3. The Mass Transit Account outperformed the Highway Account on a CAGR basis (2.168 percent versus 2.153 percent).

4. Investment income CAGR of 52.94 percent reflects the combined impact of the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law transfers and rising interest rates, not organic revenue growth.

5. Expenditure obligations grew at a CAGR of 3.742 percent, creating a structural deficit requiring ongoing general fund support.

6. Under current law with no new revenue measures, the fund is projected to be exhausted by FY 2031.

7. The federal motor fuel excise tax has not been increased since October 1, 1993, now representing 31 years of nominal rate stagnation.
