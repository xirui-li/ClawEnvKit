EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents a comprehensive Compound Annual Growth Rate (CAGR) analysis of the Federal Highway Trust Fund (HTF) for the ten-year period spanning fiscal year 2014 through fiscal year 2024. The analysis encompasses both the Highway Account and the Mass Transit Account, examining revenue inflows, expenditure patterns, and net fund balances. The findings are intended to support congressional budget deliberations, inform the Federal Highway Administration's (FHWA) long-term capital planning, and satisfy reporting requirements under 23 U.S.C. § 9503.

The Highway Trust Fund recorded a total balance of $47.3 billion as of September 30, 2024, compared to $15.8 billion on September 30, 2014. This represents a ten-year CAGR of 11.57% on the combined fund balance, driven primarily by a series of general fund transfers authorized by Congress between 2014 and 2021 and modest but consistent growth in motor fuel tax receipts.


SECTION 1: BACKGROUND AND FUND STRUCTURE

The Federal Highway Trust Fund was established under the Highway Revenue Act of 1956 and is funded primarily through excise taxes on motor fuels, truck and trailer sales, heavy vehicle use taxes, and tire taxes. The fund is divided into two accounts:

1. Highway Account — finances federal-aid highway programs
2. Mass Transit Account — finances federal public transportation programs

The federal gasoline tax rate has remained at 18.4 cents per gallon since 1993, and the diesel tax rate has remained at 24.4 cents per gallon since the same year. These rates are not indexed to inflation, which has been a persistent structural challenge for the fund's long-term solvency.

The Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (IIJA), signed into law on November 15, 2021, authorized $118 billion in new spending from the Highway Trust Fund over five years (fiscal years 2022–2026), representing the largest single surface transportation authorization in the fund's history.


SECTION 2: CAGR METHODOLOGY

The Compound Annual Growth Rate is calculated using the following standard formula:

CAGR = (Ending Value / Beginning Value) ^ (1 / Number of Years) − 1

For this analysis:
- Beginning Value (FY2014 closing balance): $15.8 billion
- Ending Value (FY2024 closing balance): $47.3 billion
- Number of Years: 10

CAGR = ($47.3B / $15.8B) ^ (1/10) − 1
CAGR = (2.9937) ^ (0.10) − 1
CAGR = 1.1157 − 1
CAGR = 11.57%

Separate CAGR calculations were performed for the Highway Account and the Mass Transit Account, as well as for individual revenue streams including motor fuel taxes, truck and trailer excise taxes, and general fund transfers.

SECTION 4: REVENUE STREAM ANALYSIS AND INDIVIDUAL CAGR COMPUTATIONS

4.1 Motor Fuel Tax Receipts

Motor fuel tax receipts represent the largest organic revenue source for the Highway Trust Fund. In FY2014, total motor fuel tax receipts deposited to the HTF were $34.1 billion. By FY2024, this figure had grown to $41.7 billion, reflecting a ten-year CAGR of 2.03%.

This modest growth rate reflects the competing effects of increased vehicle miles traveled (VMT), which reached 3.26 trillion miles in calendar year 2023 according to FHWA data, offset by improving fuel economy standards under EPA Corporate Average Fuel Economy (CAFE) regulations, which raised the fleet average to 49.0 miles per gallon for model year 2026 vehicles.

4.2 Truck and Trailer Excise Tax Receipts

Excise taxes on the sale of heavy trucks, trailers, and tractors generated $4.8 billion in FY2014 and $6.3 billion in FY2024, representing a ten-year CAGR of 2.75%. Growth in this category has been supported by sustained demand in freight transportation and e-commerce logistics expansion.

4.3 Heavy Vehicle Use Tax

The Heavy Vehicle Use Tax (HVUT), collected annually on vehicles with a gross weight of 55,000 pounds or more, generated $1.4 billion in FY2014 and $1.7 billion in FY2024, a ten-year CAGR of 1.96%.

4.4 General Fund Transfers

Between FY2014 and FY2024, Congress authorized a cumulative total of $70.4 billion in general fund transfers to the Highway Trust Fund to prevent insolvency and maintain program continuity. These transfers occurred in FY2014 ($10.8 billion), FY2015 ($6.1 billion), FY2016 ($70 million as a technical correction), FY2018 ($2.5 billion), and FY2021 ($13.6 billion), with the remainder attributable to IIJA advance appropriations.


SECTION 5: HIGHWAY ACCOUNT CAGR — DETAILED COMPUTATION

Highway Account Beginning Balance (FY2014): $12.6 billion
Highway Account Ending Balance (FY2024): $38.9 billion
Number of Years: 10

CAGR = ($38.9B / $12.6B) ^ (1/10) − 1
CAGR = (3.0873) ^ (0.10) − 1
CAGR = 1.1193 − 1
CAGR = 11.93%

The Highway Account's CAGR of 11.93% slightly exceeds the combined fund CAGR of 11.57%, reflecting proportionally larger general fund transfers directed to highway programs relative to transit programs.


SECTION 6: MASS TRANSIT ACCOUNT CAGR — DETAILED COMPUTATION

Mass Transit Account Beginning Balance (FY2014): $3.2 billion
Mass Transit Account Ending Balance (FY2024): $8.4 billion
Number of Years: 10

CAGR = ($8.4B / $3.2B) ^ (1/10) − 1
CAGR = (2.625) ^ (0.10) − 1
CAGR = 1.1013 − 1
CAGR = 10.13%

The Mass Transit Account's lower CAGR of 10.13% compared to the Highway Account reflects the statutory allocation formula under 23 U.S.C. § 9503, which directs 80.32% of fuel tax receipts to the Highway Account and 15.44% to the Mass Transit Account, with the remainder allocated to the Leaking Underground Storage Tank Trust Fund.

SECTION 8: FORWARD PROJECTIONS AND RISK FACTORS

Based on current IIJA authorization levels and projected motor fuel tax receipts, the Office of Financial Analysis projects the combined HTF balance will decline to approximately $31.4 billion by September 30, 2026, as IIJA disbursements peak. Without additional legislative action, the Congressional Budget Office (CBO) has projected HTF insolvency by fiscal year 2028, at which point annual revenues of approximately $43.2 billion would be insufficient to cover projected annual outlays of approximately $68.7 billion.

Key risk factors include:
- Accelerating electric vehicle adoption, projected to reduce gasoline tax receipts by $2.1 billion annually by FY2030 under FHWA baseline scenario
- Inflation-driven construction cost increases averaging 6.8% annually since FY2021
- Potential expiration of IIJA authorization after FY2026 without reauthorization


SECTION 9: CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS

The Federal Highway Trust Fund has achieved a ten-year CAGR of 11.57% on its combined balance from FY2014 to FY2024, with the Highway Account growing at 11.93% and the Mass Transit Account at 10.13%. However, this growth is structurally dependent on discretionary congressional transfers rather than self-sustaining revenue mechanisms.

The Office of Financial Analysis recommends that Congress consider indexing the federal motor fuel tax to the Consumer Price Index, a measure that would have generated an estimated additional $14.3 billion in cumulative revenue between FY2014 and FY2024 had it been implemented in FY2014. Additionally, a vehicle miles traveled (VMT) fee pilot program, currently authorized under Section 13001 of the IIJA with $125 million in funding, should be evaluated for potential full-scale implementation no later than FY2027.
