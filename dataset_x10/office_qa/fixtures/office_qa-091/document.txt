EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents a comprehensive analysis of United States defense spending for Fiscal Year 2024 (October 1, 2023 through September 30, 2024), including year-over-year comparisons, programmatic breakdowns, and forward-looking projections through FY2027. Total obligated defense spending reached $886.4 billion in FY2024, representing a 3.2 percent increase over the FY2023 enacted level of $858.5 billion. This figure encompasses the base defense budget, Overseas Contingency Operations (OCO) funding, and emergency supplemental appropriations passed by Congress.

─────────────────────────────────────────────

SECTION 1: OVERVIEW OF FY2024 DEFENSE APPROPRIATIONS

The National Defense Authorization Act for Fiscal Year 2024, signed into law by President Biden on December 22, 2023, authorized $886.3 billion in total defense-related spending. This authorization was accompanied by the Consolidated Appropriations Act, 2024, which provided the actual appropriated funding. The enacted base budget for the Department of Defense stood at $849.8 billion, with an additional $36.6 billion allocated through supplemental appropriations, primarily directed toward security assistance to Ukraine and Israel under the Emergency National Security Supplemental Appropriations Act passed in April 2024.

Defense spending as a share of Gross Domestic Product (GDP) remained at approximately 3.1 percent in FY2024, consistent with the FY2023 level and reflecting a gradual decline from the post-9/11 peak of 4.7 percent recorded in FY2010.

The most significant percentage increase occurred in Research, Development, Test and Evaluation (RDT&E), which grew by 10.1 percent to $143.2 billion. This increase reflects sustained investment in next-generation capabilities including hypersonic weapons systems, artificial intelligence integration, and advanced space-based assets. The Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency (DARPA) received $4.1 billion within this category, a 7.8 percent increase from its FY2023 allocation of $3.8 billion.

Operations and Maintenance (O&M) remained the single largest appropriation category at $321.7 billion, accounting for 36.3 percent of total defense spending. Elevated O&M costs are attributable to increased operational tempo in the Indo-Pacific region, higher fuel costs averaging $3.47 per gallon for military-grade JP-8 jet fuel (up from $3.12 per gallon in FY2023), and expanded training exercises under the Pacific Deterrence Initiative.

The Department of the Air Force recorded the highest percentage growth at 6.1 percent, driven largely by the continued procurement of F-35A aircraft and accelerated investment in the B-21 Raider bomber program. The Air Force took delivery of 48 F-35A aircraft in FY2024 at a unit cost of approximately $82.5 million per aircraft, compared to 43 aircraft delivered in FY2023.

The Department of the Navy's $242.1 billion allocation supported the construction of two Virginia-class submarines at a combined cost of $7.4 billion, one Gerald R. Ford-class aircraft carrier (CVN-81, USS Enterprise) at a current program cost of $12.8 billion, and the continued development of the DDG(X) next-generation destroyer program, which received $847 million in FY2024 for design and early development work.

SECTION 4: MAJOR WEAPONS PROGRAMS AND PROCUREMENT

The following programs represented the largest individual procurement expenditures in FY2024:

1. F-35 Joint Strike Fighter Program (all variants): $15.6 billion total across all services, including 48 F-35A (Air Force), 14 F-35B (Marine Corps), and 16 F-35C (Navy) aircraft.

2. Columbia-class Ballistic Missile Submarine: $6.2 billion in FY2024, with lead boat USS Columbia (SSBN-826) approximately 42 percent complete as of September 30, 2024.

3. B-21 Raider Long-Range Strike Bomber: $2.9 billion in FY2024; Northrop Grumman Corporation holds the prime contract valued at $21.4 billion for the initial 21 aircraft.

4. Sentinel Intercontinental Ballistic Missile (formerly GBSD): $3.7 billion in FY2024; Northrop Grumman's contract for this program has grown to an estimated $131 billion over its lifecycle, prompting a Nunn-McCurdy critical breach notification in January 2024.

5. CH-47F Chinook Block II Helicopter: $1.1 billion for 24 aircraft procured from Boeing Defense, Space & Security.

6. Guided Multiple Launch Rocket System (GMLRS) munitions: $1.8 billion for approximately 14,000 rockets, reflecting sustained demand driven by security assistance transfers.

─────────────────────────────────────────────

SECTION 5: PERSONNEL COSTS AND END STRENGTH

Military personnel costs totaled $181.9 billion in FY2024, representing 20.5 percent of total defense spending. Active duty end strength across all services stood at 1,328,400 personnel as of September 30, 2024, a decrease of 11,600 from the FY2023 level of 1,340,000, reflecting ongoing recruiting challenges across all service branches.

The Army fell 7,500 personnel short of its authorized end strength of 452,000, ending FY2024 with 444,500 active duty soldiers. In response, the Army implemented a $25,000 enlistment bonus for high-demand military occupational specialties including 68W Combat Medic and 25U Signal Support Systems Specialist.

The average annual compensation for an active duty service member, including base pay, housing allowance, subsistence allowance, and benefits, reached $119,400 in FY2024, up from $113,200 in FY2023, an increase of 5.5 percent. The FY2024 National Defense Authorization Act authorized a 5.2 percent pay raise for all uniformed personnel, the largest such increase since FY2002.

─────────────────────────────────────────────

SECTION 6: INTERNATIONAL SECURITY ASSISTANCE

The Emergency National Security Supplemental Appropriations Act, signed April 24, 2024, provided $60.8 billion in total national security funding, of which $26.4 billion was directed to Ukraine security assistance, $14.1 billion to Israel security assistance, and $8.1 billion to Indo-Pacific security initiatives. Of the Ukraine-related funding, $13.8 billion was designated for Presidential Drawdown Authority replenishment, allowing the Department of Defense to replace equipment transferred from existing U.S. stocks.

Since February 2022, cumulative U.S. security assistance to Ukraine has totaled $61.4 billion as of September 30, 2024, making Ukraine the largest recipient of U.S. military assistance in the post-Cold War era.

─────────────────────────────────────────────

SECTION 7: COMPARATIVE CONTEXT AND PROJECTIONS

The United States accounted for approximately 37 percent of total global defense spending in 2024, according to preliminary estimates from the Stockholm International Peace Research Institute (SIPRI). China's defense budget, as officially reported, reached $225 billion in 2024, though independent analysts estimate actual spending at between $310 billion and $340 billion when accounting for off-budget expenditures.

NATO allies collectively spent an estimated $1.34 trillion on defense in 2024. Twenty-three of thirty-two NATO member nations met or exceeded the alliance's 2 percent of GDP defense spending benchmark in 2024, compared to only seven nations meeting the benchmark in 2014.

Forward projections from the Congressional Budget Office (CBO) estimate that defense spending will reach $1.0 trillion annually by FY2030 under current policy assumptions, representing an average annual growth rate of approximately 2.1 percent in real terms. The primary cost drivers identified by CBO include nuclear modernization programs ($756 billion projected over the next decade), shipbuilding requirements to maintain a 355-ship Navy, and rising personnel compensation costs.

─────────────────────────────────────────────

SECTION 8: AUDIT AND FINANCIAL MANAGEMENT

The Department of Defense completed its seventh consecutive department-wide financial statement audit in November 2024. For the seventh consecutive year, the Department received a disclaimer of opinion from independent auditors, indicating that auditors were unable to express an opinion on the financial statements due to material weaknesses. However, auditors noted measurable progress, with the number of material weaknesses declining from 28 in FY2022 to 21 in FY2024. The Department has invested $1.2 billion in financial management improvement initiatives since FY2018.

─────────────────────────────────────────────

CONCLUSION

FY2024 defense spending of $886.4 billion reflects the United States' sustained commitment to maintaining military readiness, modernizing the force, and supporting allied security. Key trends include accelerating investment in advanced technology research, rising personnel costs driven by pay increases and benefit enhancements, and significant supplemental expenditures tied to overseas security commitments. The trajectory toward $1 trillion in annual defense spending by FY2030 will require continued congressional attention to programmatic cost growth, recruiting challenges, and the long-term sustainability of nuclear modernization investments.
