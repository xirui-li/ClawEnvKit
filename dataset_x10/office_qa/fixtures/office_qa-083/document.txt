EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents a comprehensive analysis of yield spreads across U.S. Treasury securities for fiscal year 2023 (October 1, 2022 through September 30, 2023). The analysis examines the relationship between short-term and long-term borrowing costs, compares Treasury yields against benchmark corporate and municipal rates, and evaluates the implications of current spread dynamics for federal financing strategy. During the reporting period, the Federal Reserve raised the federal funds rate seven times, bringing the target range from 3.00–3.25 percent at the start of the fiscal year to 5.25–5.50 percent by July 26, 2023. These actions produced significant distortions in the yield curve and created the most pronounced yield curve inversion since 2000.

─────────────────────────────────────────

SECTION 1: OVERVIEW OF TREASURY YIELD CURVE CONDITIONS

1.1 Yield Curve Inversion and Duration

The U.S. Treasury yield curve remained inverted throughout the entirety of fiscal year 2023. The 2-year/10-year spread, the most widely cited measure of curve inversion, reached its most negative point on March 8, 2023, when the 2-year Treasury note yielded 5.08 percent and the 10-year Treasury note yielded 3.96 percent, producing a spread of negative 112 basis points. This represented the deepest inversion recorded since September 22, 2000, when the same spread reached negative 114 basis points.

As of September 29, 2023 (the final trading day of the fiscal year), the 2-year Treasury note yielded 5.03 percent and the 10-year Treasury note yielded 4.57 percent, resulting in a spread of negative 46 basis points. This narrowing from the March low reflected market expectations that the Federal Reserve was approaching the end of its tightening cycle.

SECTION 2: SPREAD ANALYSIS AGAINST CORPORATE AND AGENCY BENCHMARKS

2.1 Option-Adjusted Spreads: Investment-Grade Corporate Debt

The ICE BofA U.S. Corporate Index Option-Adjusted Spread (OAS) began fiscal year 2023 at 163 basis points over comparable Treasury securities. By the end of the fiscal year on September 29, 2023, the OAS had compressed to 124 basis points, reflecting improved investor risk appetite and reduced recession fears relative to the prior year. The tightest reading during the fiscal year occurred on February 2, 2023, when the OAS reached 115 basis points, the narrowest level since January 2022.

2.2 High-Yield Corporate Spread Dynamics

The ICE BofA U.S. High Yield Index OAS opened fiscal year 2023 at 537 basis points. The spread widened to a fiscal-year peak of 551 basis points on October 13, 2022, before compressing steadily through the first half of calendar year 2023. By September 29, 2023, the high-yield OAS stood at 389 basis points, a compression of 148 basis points from the fiscal year opening level. This compression occurred despite the Federal Reserve's continued rate increases, suggesting that credit markets were pricing in a soft-landing scenario rather than a severe recession.

2.3 Agency Mortgage-Backed Securities

The current-coupon 30-year Fannie Mae mortgage-backed securities (MBS) spread over the 10-year Treasury note averaged 168 basis points during fiscal year 2023, compared to an average of 134 basis points during fiscal year 2022. The elevated spread reflected ongoing Federal Reserve balance sheet runoff, which removed a significant buyer from the MBS market. The Fed's System Open Market Account (SOMA) holdings of agency MBS declined from approximately $2.72 trillion in October 2022 to $2.46 trillion by September 2023, a reduction of roughly $260 billion.

2.4 Municipal Bond Spreads

The Bloomberg Municipal Bond Index yield-to-worst averaged 3.41 percent during fiscal year 2023. The ratio of 10-year AAA-rated municipal yields to 10-year Treasury yields — a standard measure of relative value — averaged 67 percent during the fiscal year, compared to a historical average of approximately 85 percent. This below-average ratio indicated that municipal bonds were expensive relative to Treasuries on a pre-tax basis, though they remained attractive to investors in the highest marginal tax brackets. The ratio reached its lowest point of 59 percent on April 18, 2023, before recovering to 72 percent by fiscal year end.

SECTION 3: IMPLICATIONS FOR FEDERAL BORROWING COSTS

3.1 Weighted Average Maturity of the Debt

The weighted average maturity (WAM) of Treasury debt held by the public stood at 75.7 months (approximately 6.3 years) as of September 30, 2023, compared to 76.2 months at the start of the fiscal year. The Office of Debt Management has maintained a policy of extending the WAM since 2009, when it stood at approximately 49 months. The modest decline during fiscal year 2023 reflected the Treasury's decision to increase issuance of shorter-dated bills to meet elevated financing needs while managing rollover risk.

3.2 Net Interest Cost

The federal government paid $879.3 billion in gross interest on the public debt during fiscal year 2023, compared to $717.8 billion in fiscal year 2022, an increase of $161.5 billion or 22.5 percent. Net interest costs — which subtract interest income received by the government — totaled $659.8 billion in fiscal year 2023, representing 2.5 percent of gross domestic product. This was the highest net interest cost as a share of GDP since fiscal year 1998, when net interest reached 2.8 percent of GDP.

3.3 Average Interest Rate on Outstanding Debt

The average interest rate on total outstanding Treasury debt rose to 2.97 percent as of September 30, 2023, up from 2.07 percent on September 30, 2022. This 90-basis-point increase over a single fiscal year was the largest annual increase in the average rate since fiscal year 1981. The Congressional Budget Office projects that the average rate on outstanding debt will continue rising, reaching approximately 3.5 percent by fiscal year 2025 as lower-coupon securities issued during the 2020–2021 period mature and are refinanced at current market rates.

3.4 Rollover Risk Assessment

During fiscal year 2023, the Treasury refinanced approximately $14.6 trillion in maturing securities, including both principal repayment and new borrowing. Of this total, $7.2 trillion represented bills with maturities of one year or less. The concentration of short-term financing creates meaningful rollover risk in an environment of elevated rates. The Office of Debt Management estimates that each 100-basis-point increase in short-term rates adds approximately $72 billion to annual interest costs within 12 months, given the current maturity profile.

SECTION 5: FORWARD OUTLOOK AND POLICY CONSIDERATIONS

5.1 Market-Implied Rate Path

As of September 29, 2023, federal funds futures contracts implied a peak policy rate of 5.50 percent, consistent with the existing target range, with the first 25-basis-point rate cut priced for the June 2024 Federal Open Market Committee meeting. The implied 10-year Treasury yield one year forward, derived from the 9-year/10-year forward rate, stood at 4.71 percent, suggesting markets expected only modest long-end relief even as short rates declined.

5.2 Debt Management Recommendations

Based on the yield spread analysis contained in this report, the Office of Debt Management recommends the following for fiscal year 2024:

- Maintain the current quarterly refunding schedule, with 3-year note auction size held at $48 billion, 10-year note auction size at $38 billion, and 30-year bond auction size at $24 billion.
- Increase Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS) issuance by $2 billion per auction across the 5-year and 10-year tenors, reflecting real yields at their highest levels since 2009 (10-year TIPS real yield: 2.20 percent as of September 29, 2023).
- Monitor the 2-year/10-year spread closely; a return to positive territory would signal an opportunity to extend the weighted average maturity of new issuance at relatively lower long-term cost.
- Evaluate the reintroduction of the 20-year bond buyback program, suspended in March 2020, to improve liquidity in off-the-run securities and support price discovery.
