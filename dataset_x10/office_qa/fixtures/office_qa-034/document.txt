EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents findings from a comprehensive statistical distribution fitting exercise conducted on Pennsylvania's major revenue and expenditure categories spanning fiscal years 2018 through 2023. The analysis was commissioned following the General Assembly's directive under House Resolution 412, passed June 7, 2023, which required the Department of the Treasury to improve probabilistic forecasting methods for the General Fund. Using maximum likelihood estimation (MLE), the Anderson-Darling goodness-of-fit test, and the Kolmogorov-Smirnov test, analysts evaluated 14 candidate probability distributions across six revenue categories and four expenditure categories. The results indicate that Pennsylvania's personal income tax receipts are best modeled by a log-normal distribution, while Medicaid expenditures exhibit characteristics consistent with a negative binomial distribution when analyzed at the monthly disbursement level. These findings will directly inform the FY2025 budget stress-testing framework and the Commonwealth's reserve fund adequacy assessment.

SECTION 1: BACKGROUND AND MOTIVATION

Pennsylvania's General Fund collected $42.8 billion in total revenues during FY2023, representing a 3.1% increase over FY2022's $41.5 billion. Despite this growth, year-over-year variance in quarterly receipts has widened considerably since FY2018, when total revenues stood at $34.7 billion. The coefficient of variation for quarterly personal income tax receipts increased from 0.087 in FY2018 to 0.134 in FY2023, signaling heightened unpredictability in the Commonwealth's single largest revenue source.

Traditional budget forecasting employed by the Independent Fiscal Office (IFO) and the Governor's Budget Office has relied primarily on point estimates derived from econometric regression models. While these models perform adequately in stable economic environments, the COVID-19 pandemic, subsequent federal stimulus disbursements, and labor market disruptions between 2020 and 2022 exposed significant limitations in point-estimate approaches. During FY2021, actual personal income tax receipts exceeded the IFO's April 2020 forecast by $2.3 billion, or 18.4%, largely due to unanticipated capital gains realizations and enhanced unemployment compensation flows.

This analysis addresses that gap by characterizing the full probability distributions of key fiscal variables, enabling risk managers to generate confidence intervals, tail-risk estimates, and reserve adequacy thresholds.

SECTION 2: DATA AND METHODOLOGY

2.1 Data Sources

Monthly revenue and expenditure data were extracted from the Commonwealth's Integrated Enterprise System (IES) for the period July 1, 2017 through June 30, 2023, yielding 72 monthly observations per variable. Data were validated against published Comptroller General monthly statements and reconciled to within $1.2 million aggregate discrepancy, which falls below the 0.003% materiality threshold established by the Office of the Budget.

Revenue categories analyzed:
- Personal Income Tax (PIT)
- Sales and Use Tax (SUT)
- Corporate Net Income Tax (CNIT)
- Inheritance and Estate Tax
- Realty Transfer Tax
- Lottery Fund Transfers

Expenditure categories analyzed:
- Medicaid (Medical Assistance)
- K-12 Education Subsidy Payments
- Corrections Operating Expenditures
- Debt Service Payments

2.2 Candidate Distributions

Fourteen distributions were evaluated for each variable: Normal, Log-Normal, Gamma, Weibull, Beta, Exponential, Pareto, Generalized Extreme Value (GEV), Gumbel, Logistic, Log-Logistic, Inverse Gaussian, Negative Binomial, and Poisson. Parameters were estimated using maximum likelihood estimation implemented in R version 4.3.1 using the fitdistrplus package (version 1.1-11) and the MASS library.

2.3 Goodness-of-Fit Testing

Three criteria were applied to rank distributions:
1. Anderson-Darling (AD) statistic — weighted to emphasize tail fit
2. Kolmogorov-Smirnov (KS) statistic — overall distributional distance
3. Akaike Information Criterion (AIC) — penalizes parameter complexity

A distribution was accepted as the best fit when it ranked first or second on at least two of the three criteria at the 5% significance level.

SECTION 3: RESULTS BY REVENUE CATEGORY

3.1 Personal Income Tax

PIT is Pennsylvania's largest revenue source, averaging $1.247 billion per month over the study period, with a standard deviation of $167 million. The log-normal distribution achieved the best fit with parameters μ = 20.944 and σ = 0.134 (on the log scale). The Anderson-Darling statistic was 0.412, well below the critical value of 2.502 at α = 0.05. The normal distribution was rejected with an AD statistic of 3.871, driven by positive skewness in April receipts attributable to final tax settlements.

The fitted log-normal distribution implies a 5th percentile monthly receipt of $987 million and a 95th percentile of $1.531 billion, yielding a 90% confidence interval width of $544 million. This interval is 23% wider than the IFO's current scenario range, suggesting that existing budget scenarios understate downside risk.

3.2 Sales and Use Tax

SUT receipts averaged $1.089 billion per month, with a standard deviation of $98 million. The Gamma distribution provided the best fit (shape α = 123.4, rate β = 0.1134), with an AIC of 1,847.3 compared to 1,851.9 for the log-normal alternative. Seasonal decomposition revealed that December SUT receipts consistently exceeded the annual monthly mean by 31.7%, while February receipts fell 14.2% below the mean.

3.3 Corporate Net Income Tax

CNIT exhibited the highest volatility of all revenue categories, with a coefficient of variation of 0.412. The Generalized Extreme Value distribution provided the best fit (ξ = 0.287, μ = 312.4 million, σ = 89.6 million), consistent with the heavy-tailed nature of corporate profit distributions. The 99th percentile monthly CNIT receipt under this fitted distribution is $741 million, compared to an observed maximum of $698 million in April 2022.

SECTION 4: RESULTS BY EXPENDITURE CATEGORY

4.1 Medicaid Expenditures

Monthly Medicaid disbursements averaged $2.314 billion over the study period, making it the Commonwealth's single largest expenditure category. At the monthly disbursement level, the negative binomial distribution provided the best fit when expenditures were measured in units of $100,000, with parameters r = 18.4 and p = 0.443. The AD statistic was 0.388. Notably, the normal distribution was strongly rejected (AD = 5.214), confirming that Medicaid spending exhibits overdispersion relative to a Poisson process, likely reflecting episodic high-cost utilization events.

4.2 K-12 Education Subsidy Payments

Education subsidy payments are largely formula-driven and exhibited the lowest volatility of all expenditure categories, with a coefficient of variation of 0.061. The normal distribution provided an adequate fit (AD = 0.334), with mean monthly disbursements of $1.087 billion and standard deviation of $66 million. The primary source of variance is timing differences in quarterly payment schedules rather than underlying demand uncertainty.

4.3 Reserve Fund Adequacy Implications

Using the fitted distributions and assuming zero correlation between revenue and expenditure shocks (a conservative assumption tested via Spearman rank correlation, which yielded ρ = -0.14 between PIT and Medicaid, not statistically significant at α = 0.10), the analysis estimates that a reserve fund of $1.84 billion would be sufficient to cover a simultaneous 5th-percentile revenue outcome and 95th-percentile expenditure outcome in any given fiscal year with 90% probability. Pennsylvania's actual Budget Stabilization Reserve Fund balance as of June 30, 2023 was $1.27 billion, representing a shortfall of approximately $570 million relative to this threshold.

SECTION 5: LIMITATIONS AND FUTURE WORK

Several limitations constrain the generalizability of these findings. First, 72 monthly observations represent a relatively small sample for fitting heavy-tailed distributions such as the GEV; additional years of data will improve parameter stability. Second, the analysis treats each variable independently; a multivariate copula framework is recommended for the FY2025 update to capture joint tail behavior. Third, structural breaks associated with the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017 and the American Rescue Plan Act of 2021 were addressed through indicator variables but not through formal change-point detection algorithms.

The Division of Quantitative Methods plans to extend this framework in Q3 FY2024 to incorporate Bayesian updating procedures, allowing parameter estimates to be revised monthly as new revenue data arrive. A pilot implementation using the Stan probabilistic programming language is scheduled for completion by September 30, 2024.

SECTION 6: RECOMMENDATIONS

1. The Governor's Budget Office should adopt log-normal distributional assumptions for PIT forecasting in place of symmetric scenario ranges, effective for the FY2026 budget submission.
2. The Budget Stabilization Reserve Fund target should be increased from its current statutory floor of $750 million to $1.84 billion, consistent with the 90% coverage threshold identified in Section 4.3.
3. The IFO should publish 80% and 95% confidence intervals alongside point estimates in its quarterly revenue updates beginning with the May 2024 release.
4. A formal model validation review should be conducted by an external panel no later than December 31, 2025.
