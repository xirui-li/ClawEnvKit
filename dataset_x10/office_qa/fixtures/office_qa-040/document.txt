EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents a comprehensive analysis of federal government expenditure patterns across the five-year period from fiscal year 2019 through fiscal year 2024. Total federal outlays grew from $4.45 trillion in FY2019 to $6.13 trillion in FY2024, representing a cumulative increase of 37.8 percent over the review period. The analysis identifies key drivers of expenditure growth, examines departmental spending trajectories, and highlights areas of fiscal concern requiring legislative attention in the upcoming budget cycle. Mandatory spending programs continue to represent the dominant share of total outlays, accounting for 63.4 percent of all federal expenditures in FY2024, up from 60.1 percent in FY2019.

SECTION 1: TOTAL EXPENDITURE OVERVIEW

Federal outlays have followed an upward trajectory throughout the review period, with notable acceleration during FY2020 and FY2021 attributable to emergency pandemic-related spending. The following table summarizes total annual expenditures and year-over-year growth rates:

Fiscal Year | Total Outlays | Year-over-Year Change | % Change
FY2019      | $4.45 trillion | —                    | —
FY2020      | $6.55 trillion | +$2.10 trillion       | +47.2%
FY2021      | $6.82 trillion | +$0.27 trillion       | +4.1%
FY2022      | $6.27 trillion | -$0.55 trillion       | -8.1%
FY2023      | $6.13 trillion | -$0.14 trillion       | -2.3%
FY2024      | $6.13 trillion | $0.00 trillion        | 0.0%

The extraordinary spike in FY2020 reflects $2.59 trillion in emergency supplemental appropriations, including $1.76 trillion disbursed through the Coronavirus Aid, Relief, and Economic Security (CARES) Act signed on March 27, 2020. The subsequent normalization in FY2022 and FY2023 reflects the expiration of most temporary pandemic programs, though baseline spending has not returned to pre-pandemic levels due to permanent expansions in several entitlement categories.

SECTION 2: MANDATORY SPENDING ANALYSIS

Mandatory spending—comprising Social Security, Medicare, Medicaid, and other entitlement programs—represents the largest and fastest-growing component of the federal budget.

2.1 Social Security Administration

Social Security outlays totaled $1.35 trillion in FY2024, compared to $1.05 trillion in FY2019, reflecting a 28.6 percent increase over five years. The primary drivers include:

- A 12.4 percent cost-of-living adjustment (COLA) applied cumulatively across FY2022 and FY2023, the largest two-year adjustment since 1981
- Growth in the beneficiary population from 64.2 million recipients in FY2019 to 67.8 million in FY2024
- Average monthly benefit increasing from $1,461 in FY2019 to $1,907 in FY2024

The Social Security Trust Fund's combined reserves stood at $2.79 trillion as of December 31, 2023, with the Office of the Chief Actuary projecting depletion of the Old-Age and Survivors Insurance (OASI) Trust Fund by 2033 under current law.

2.2 Medicare and Medicaid

Combined Medicare and Medicaid outlays reached $1.89 trillion in FY2024, up from $1.41 trillion in FY2019, a 34.0 percent increase. Medicare alone accounted for $1.02 trillion in FY2024, while Medicaid expenditures totaled $871 billion. The Medicaid expansion under the Affordable Care Act continues to drive enrollment growth, with 94.5 million individuals enrolled as of January 2024, compared to 75.8 million in January 2019.

SECTION 3: DISCRETIONARY SPENDING ANALYSIS

Discretionary spending, subject to annual appropriations by Congress, totaled $1.71 trillion in FY2024, representing 27.9 percent of total outlays.

3.1 Defense Discretionary Spending

The Department of Defense received $858 billion in total obligational authority in FY2024, compared to $676 billion in FY2019, a 26.9 percent increase. Key expenditure categories within defense include:

- Operations and Maintenance: $312 billion (FY2024), up from $261 billion (FY2019)
- Military Personnel: $176 billion (FY2024), up from $153 billion (FY2019)
- Procurement: $163 billion (FY2024), up from $134 billion (FY2019)
- Research, Development, Test and Evaluation: $145 billion (FY2024), up from $104 billion (FY2019)

The 39.4 percent increase in RDT&E funding reflects accelerated investment in hypersonic weapons systems, artificial intelligence integration, and space-based capabilities under the National Defense Strategy released in October 2022.

3.2 Non-Defense Discretionary Spending

Non-defense discretionary outlays totaled $854 billion in FY2024. The Department of Education received $238 billion, the largest single non-defense discretionary allocation, largely driven by $79 billion in student loan program modifications following administrative actions in FY2023. The Department of Transportation received $105 billion, reflecting Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (IIJA) appropriations flowing through the system following the law's enactment on November 15, 2021.

SECTION 4: NET INTEREST EXPENDITURES

Net interest payments on the federal debt represent the fastest-growing expenditure category in the review period, increasing from $375 billion in FY2019 to $870 billion in FY2024—a 132.0 percent increase in five years. This category now constitutes 14.2 percent of total federal outlays, up from 8.4 percent in FY2019.

The acceleration in interest costs reflects two compounding factors:

1. Debt accumulation: Gross federal debt grew from $22.7 trillion at the end of FY2019 to $34.0 trillion as of January 2024, an increase of $11.3 trillion or 49.8 percent.

2. Interest rate normalization: The Federal Reserve raised the federal funds rate from near-zero levels to a target range of 5.25–5.50 percent between March 2022 and July 2023, substantially increasing the government's borrowing costs. The weighted average interest rate on Treasury securities held by the public rose from 1.6 percent in FY2021 to 3.1 percent in FY2024.

Under current Congressional Budget Office projections released in February 2024, net interest payments are forecast to reach $1.06 trillion by FY2026, which would make interest the second-largest single expenditure category behind Social Security.

SECTION 6: FISCAL DEFICIT IMPLICATIONS

Total federal revenues in FY2024 reached $4.44 trillion, producing a fiscal year deficit of $1.69 trillion. This compares to a pre-pandemic deficit of $984 billion in FY2019. The deficit-to-GDP ratio stood at 6.2 percent in FY2024, compared to 4.6 percent in FY2019. The Congressional Budget Office's baseline projection, published February 7, 2024, estimates cumulative deficits of $20.0 trillion over the FY2025–FY2034 window under current law.

SECTION 7: CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS

This analysis identifies three primary areas requiring policy attention:

1. Mandatory spending growth: Without legislative reform, Social Security and Medicare expenditures will continue to grow at rates exceeding nominal GDP growth, which averaged 5.3 percent annually from FY2019 through FY2024. The ratio of mandatory spending to GDP is projected to reach 15.8 percent by FY2030, compared to 12.9 percent in FY2019.

2. Interest burden escalation: The 132 percent increase in net interest costs over five years represents a structural fiscal constraint that limits flexibility in both discretionary and emergency spending. Debt stabilization measures should be incorporated into the FY2026 budget resolution.

3. Departmental efficiency review: The Office of Management and Budget has been directed to conduct a comprehensive program effectiveness review across all agencies with FY2024 outlays exceeding $50 billion, with findings due to the Director by September 30, 2024.
