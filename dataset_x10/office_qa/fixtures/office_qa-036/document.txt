EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents a comprehensive compound annual growth rate (CAGR) analysis of the Federal Highway Trust Fund (HTF) for the ten-year period spanning fiscal year 2014 through fiscal year 2024. The analysis encompasses total fund receipts, expenditure obligations, and net balance positions across both the Highway Account and the Mass Transit Account. The findings are intended to support congressional budget deliberations, inform the Federal Highway Administration's long-range planning activities, and satisfy reporting requirements under 23 U.S.C. § 9503.

The Highway Trust Fund recorded a beginning balance of $14.7 billion in fiscal year 2014 and reached a total balance of $56.3 billion by the close of fiscal year 2024, reflecting a CAGR of 14.4 percent over the ten-year measurement period. This growth, however, is substantially attributable to a series of general fund transfers authorized by Congress rather than organic revenue growth from dedicated excise tax receipts.

---

SECTION 1: BACKGROUND AND FUND STRUCTURE

The Federal Highway Trust Fund was established by the Highway Revenue Act of 1956 and is funded primarily through federal motor fuel excise taxes, truck and trailer sales taxes, heavy vehicle use taxes, and tire excise taxes. The Fund is divided into two accounts:

1. The Highway Account, which finances the Federal-Aid Highway Program
2. The Mass Transit Account, which finances transit capital and operating assistance programs

As of September 30, 2024, the Highway Account held $46.1 billion and the Mass Transit Account held $10.2 billion, for a combined total of $56.3 billion.

The federal gasoline excise tax rate has remained fixed at 18.4 cents per gallon since October 1, 1993, and the diesel excise tax rate has remained at 24.4 cents per gallon since the same date. These rates are not indexed to inflation, which has created a structural revenue gap that has widened progressively over the measurement period.

---

SECTION 2: METHODOLOGY

The CAGR computation follows the standard financial formula:

CAGR = [(Ending Value / Beginning Value) ^ (1 / Number of Years)] - 1

For this analysis:
- Beginning Value (FY 2014 total fund balance): $14.7 billion
- Ending Value (FY 2024 total fund balance): $56.3 billion
- Number of Years: 10

CAGR = [($56.3B / $14.7B) ^ (1/10)] - 1
CAGR = [3.8299] ^ (0.10) - 1
CAGR = 1.1440 - 1
CAGR = 0.1440, or 14.4 percent

Separate CAGR computations were performed for the Highway Account and Mass Transit Account individually, as well as for gross receipts and net expenditures. All dollar figures are expressed in nominal terms unless otherwise noted. Inflation-adjusted figures using the Bureau of Economic Analysis GDP deflator are presented in Appendix B of this report.

Fiscal Year | Gasoline Tax Receipts | Diesel Tax Receipts | Other Excise Taxes | Total Receipts
FY 2014     | $23.8                 | $10.4               | $2.9               | $37.1
FY 2024     | $24.6                 | $11.3               | $3.1               | $39.0

CAGR of Total Gross Receipts (FY 2014–FY 2024): 0.5 percent

This figure underscores the structural challenge facing the Highway Trust Fund. While the total fund balance has grown at 14.4 percent annually due to congressional transfers, organic revenue from dedicated excise taxes has grown at only 0.5 percent per year over the same period. Adjusted for inflation using the Consumer Price Index (CPI-U, base year 2014), real excise tax receipts have declined at a rate of approximately 2.4 percent per year, representing a cumulative real-dollar loss of purchasing power of approximately 21.3 percent over the decade.

Total general fund transfers to the Highway Trust Fund between FY 2008 and FY 2024 have amounted to approximately $275 billion, according to Congressional Budget Office records published in the CBO's Budget and Economic Outlook: 2024 to 2034, released February 7, 2024.

---

SECTION 5: EXPENDITURE OBLIGATIONS CAGR

Total obligations charged against the Highway Trust Fund in FY 2014 were $40.2 billion. By FY 2024, total obligations had risen to $67.8 billion, reflecting a CAGR of 5.4 percent over the ten-year period.

The Mass Transit Account obligations grew from $8.6 billion in FY 2014 to $14.9 billion in FY 2024, a CAGR of 5.6 percent.

The Highway Account obligations grew from $31.6 billion in FY 2014 to $52.9 billion in FY 2024, a CAGR of 5.3 percent.

The gap between the expenditure obligation CAGR (5.4 percent) and the organic receipts CAGR (0.5 percent) represents the core fiscal sustainability challenge of the Highway Trust Fund and is the primary driver of the fund's dependence on general fund transfers.

---

SECTION 6: COMPARATIVE ANALYSIS WITH SOCIAL SECURITY TRUST FUNDS

For contextual reference, the Old-Age and Survivors Insurance (OASI) Trust Fund reported a beginning balance of $2.764 trillion in FY 2014 and a balance of $2.788 trillion in FY 2024, reflecting a CAGR of approximately 0.09 percent. The Disability Insurance (DI) Trust Fund recovered from near-depletion, rising from $32.3 billion in FY 2014 to $186.4 billion in FY 2024, a CAGR of 19.2 percent, largely attributable to legislative changes enacted in the Bipartisan Budget Act of 2015.

The Highway Trust Fund's 14.4 percent CAGR over this period exceeds the OASI Trust Fund growth rate by approximately 14.3 percentage points, though this comparison must be interpreted with caution given the fundamentally different revenue structures and policy interventions involved.

---

SECTION 7: PROJECTIONS AND FORWARD OUTLOOK

Based on current excise tax rates, projected vehicle miles traveled growth of 1.1 percent annually, and the continued penetration of electric vehicles (which are not subject to federal fuel excise taxes), the Congressional Budget Office projects that organic Highway Trust Fund receipts will reach approximately $41.2 billion by FY 2030 absent legislative changes.

Projected obligations under the IIJA through FY 2026 total approximately $147 billion from the Highway Trust Fund. Without additional general fund transfers or new dedicated revenue sources, the Office of Management and Budget projects the Highway Account could face a shortfall of approximately $22 billion by the end of FY 2028.

The Federal Highway Administration, in its 2024 Status of the Nation's Highways, Bridges, and Transit report, estimated that annual investment needs for highway and bridge improvements alone total $125 billion, compared to current annual Highway Account outlays of approximately $52.9 billion.

---

SECTION 8: KEY FINDINGS SUMMARY

- The Federal Highway Trust Fund total balance grew from $14.7 billion (FY 2014) to $56.3 billion (FY 2024), a CAGR of 14.4 percent.
- The Highway Account balance reached $46.1 billion and the Mass Transit Account reached $10.2 billion as of September 30, 2024.
- Organic excise tax receipts grew at a CAGR of only 0.5 percent, from $37.1 billion to $39.0 billion over the same period.
- The federal gasoline excise tax of 18.4 cents per gallon has not changed since October 1, 1993.
- Total general fund transfers to the HTF since FY 2008 have totaled approximately $275 billion.
- The FAST Act, signed December 4, 2015, authorized a $70 billion general fund transfer, causing the 62.2 percent balance increase in FY 2016.
- The IIJA, enacted November 15, 2021, authorized $118 billion in HTF spending authority over five years.
- Total fund obligations grew from $40.2 billion (FY 2014) to $67.8 billion (FY 2024), a CAGR of 5.4 percent.
- Real excise tax receipts have declined at approximately 2.4 percent per year in inflation-adjusted terms.
- The OMB projects a potential Highway Account shortfall of approximately $22 billion by end of FY 2028 without new revenue or transfers.
