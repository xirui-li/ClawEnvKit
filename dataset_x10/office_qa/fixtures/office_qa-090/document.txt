EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents a comprehensive analysis of Commonwealth expenditure patterns across all major appropriation categories for fiscal years 2019 through 2024. Total General Fund expenditures grew from $32.7 billion in FY2019 to $45.1 billion in FY2024, representing a cumulative increase of 37.9 percent over the five-year period. The average annual growth rate of 6.6 percent significantly exceeded the Pennsylvania Consumer Price Index average of 3.8 percent during the same period, indicating real expenditure growth beyond inflationary pressures. This analysis was prepared by the Bureau of Financial Management under the direction of Budget Secretary Uri Monson and is intended to inform legislative appropriations deliberations for FY2025.

SECTION 1: METHODOLOGY

Data for this analysis was drawn from the Commonwealth's Integrated Enterprise System (IES), the official financial management platform maintained by the Office of Comptroller Operations. All figures represent actual expenditures as recorded through June 30, 2024, the close of FY2024. Expenditures are reported on a modified accrual basis consistent with Generally Accepted Accounting Principles for governmental entities as established by the Governmental Accounting Standards Board (GASB).

Expenditure categories follow the functional classification system established under the Commonwealth's Chart of Accounts, last revised in January 2021. Year-over-year comparisons account for one-time supplemental appropriations, including $3.2 billion in federal American Rescue Plan Act (ARPA) pass-through funds distributed in FY2022 and FY2023, which are identified separately to avoid distorting trend analysis. Inflation adjustments use the Bureau of Labor Statistics Philadelphia-Camden-Wilmington Metropolitan Area CPI series.

SECTION 3: EXPENDITURE BY MAJOR CATEGORY

3.1 Health and Human Services

Health and Human Services (HHS) remained the single largest expenditure category throughout the analysis period, accounting for 42.3 percent of total General Fund spending in FY2024, or approximately $19.1 billion. The Department of Human Services (DHS) administered $16.4 billion of this total, with Medicaid (Medical Assistance) representing $11.7 billion — a 44.4 percent increase from the FY2019 Medicaid expenditure of $8.1 billion.

Key drivers of Medicaid growth include:
- Enrollment expansion: Pennsylvania Medicaid enrollment reached 3.6 million individuals as of June 2024, up from 2.9 million in June 2019, a 24.1 percent increase
- Long-term care costs: Nursing facility and home- and community-based services expenditures rose from $3.2 billion in FY2019 to $4.9 billion in FY2024
- Behavioral health services: Spending increased 61.2 percent from $1.3 billion to $2.1 billion over the period, reflecting expanded mental health and substance use disorder treatment programs

3.2 Education

Education expenditures across all levels totaled $14.2 billion in FY2024, representing 31.5 percent of General Fund spending. This compares to $11.8 billion in FY2019, a 20.3 percent increase over five years.

The Basic Education Funding formula, revised by the General Assembly in Act 55 of 2023, directed an additional $567 million in FY2024 toward the 100 lowest-wealth school districts as identified by the Pennsylvania School Boards Association's adequacy study. The Pre-K Counts program saw the largest percentage growth, expanding from 19,400 funded slots in FY2019 to 28,700 slots in FY2024.

3.3 Transportation

The Department of Transportation (PennDOT) administered $5.8 billion in FY2024, representing 12.9 percent of total expenditures. This figure includes $1.4 billion in federal highway formula funds passed through the state budget. Capital expenditures for highway and bridge construction totaled $3.1 billion, while maintenance operations accounted for $1.6 billion. The remaining $1.1 billion supported mass transit subsidies, including $487 million to the Southeastern Pennsylvania Transportation Authority (SEPTA) and $312 million to the Port Authority of Allegheny County (PAAC).

3.4 Public Safety and Corrections

The Department of Corrections operated with a FY2024 budget of $2.97 billion, up from $2.41 billion in FY2019, a 23.2 percent increase. The average daily inmate population declined from 47,600 in FY2019 to 38,900 in FY2024, a 18.3 percent reduction, meaning per-inmate costs rose from approximately $50,600 to $76,300 annually — a 50.8 percent increase driven primarily by healthcare costs, staffing wages, and facility maintenance. The Pennsylvania State Police received $1.44 billion in FY2024, compared to $1.19 billion in FY2019.

SECTION 4: PERSONNEL COSTS

Personnel costs — including salaries, wages, and benefits — represented 28.4 percent of total General Fund expenditures in FY2024, totaling $12.8 billion. This compares to $10.3 billion in FY2019, a 24.3 percent increase. The Commonwealth employed 76,840 full-time equivalent positions as of June 30, 2024, down from 79,210 in June 2019, reflecting workforce reductions through attrition.

Average compensation per FTE increased from $130,000 in FY2019 to $166,600 in FY2024, a 28.2 percent increase. This growth reflects:

- Collective bargaining agreements negotiated with AFSCME Council 13, which represents approximately 42,000 Commonwealth employees, providing cumulative wage increases of 14.5 percent between 2020 and 2024
- Healthcare premium costs per employee rising from $18,400 to $24,700 annually, a 34.2 percent increase
- Pension contribution rates increasing from 34.1 percent of payroll in FY2019 to 36.8 percent in FY2024 under the State Employees' Retirement System (SERS)

SECTION 5: DEBT SERVICE

General obligation debt service totaled $1.87 billion in FY2024, representing 4.1 percent of General Fund expenditures. Pennsylvania's outstanding general obligation debt stood at $11.3 billion as of June 30, 2024, down from $12.1 billion in FY2019, reflecting the Commonwealth's policy of not issuing new general obligation bonds since 2016. Moody's Investors Service affirmed Pennsylvania's Aa3 bond rating in March 2024, citing improved reserve fund balances and declining debt burden as positive factors.

SECTION 6: RESERVE FUND STATUS

The Commonwealth's Budget Stabilization Reserve Fund (Rainy Day Fund) held a balance of $6.1 billion as of June 30, 2024, equivalent to 13.5 percent of annual General Fund expenditures. This represents a dramatic improvement from the $340 million balance recorded at the close of FY2019. The fund reached its current level through deliberate transfers authorized by the General Assembly in FY2021 ($1.2 billion), FY2022 ($2.9 billion), and FY2023 ($1.6 billion), funded in part by surplus revenues and federal pandemic relief receipts.

SECTION 7: OUTLOOK AND CONCLUSIONS

Projections prepared by the Independent Fiscal Office (IFO) in its June 2024 revenue and expenditure outlook anticipate General Fund expenditures of $47.6 billion in FY2025, a 5.5 percent increase from FY2024. The primary pressure areas identified are Medicaid managed care capitation rate adjustments estimated at $680 million in additional cost, pension contributions under SERS and PSERS (Public School Employees' Retirement System), and the full-year impact of the Basic Education Funding formula changes enacted in FY2024.

The Bureau of Financial Management recommends that the General Assembly maintain the current Rainy Day Fund balance above 10 percent of annual expenditures, consistent with the Government Finance Officers Association (GFOA) best practice standard, and that any new programmatic expenditures in FY2025 be evaluated against a five-year cost projection to ensure structural budget balance.
