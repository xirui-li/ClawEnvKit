EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents a comprehensive yield spread analysis of the federal government's debt portfolio for fiscal year 2023, covering the period from October 1, 2022, through September 30, 2023. The analysis examines the relationship between Treasury securities across the yield curve, evaluates borrowing cost implications for the federal government, and assesses market conditions that influenced debt issuance strategy throughout the fiscal year. Total federal debt subject to the statutory limit reached $33.17 trillion as of September 30, 2023, with net interest costs to the federal government totaling $879 billion for the fiscal year, representing a 39.2 percent increase over the $632 billion recorded in fiscal year 2022.

The 2-year/10-year spread, one of the most closely monitored indicators of yield curve shape, stood at negative 46 basis points on September 30, 2023. This inversion reached its most extreme level of negative 108 basis points on March 8, 2023, the widest inversion recorded since 1981. The 3-month/10-year spread, which the Federal Reserve Bank of New York uses in its recession probability model, registered negative 96 basis points at fiscal year-end.

SECTION 2: FEDERAL RESERVE POLICY AND SPREAD DYNAMICS

The Federal Open Market Committee raised the federal funds target rate seven times between October 2022 and September 2023, bringing the target range from 3.00–3.25 percent at the start of the fiscal year to 5.25–5.50 percent by July 26, 2023, where it remained through fiscal year-end. This represented a cumulative increase of 225 basis points during the fiscal year alone, following 300 basis points of increases in calendar year 2022.

The aggressive tightening cycle materially affected the government's short-term borrowing costs. Treasury bill issuance, which accounted for 22.4 percent of total marketable debt outstanding as of September 30, 2023, carried a weighted average interest rate of 5.31 percent, compared to 3.42 percent one year prior. The weighted average interest rate across all marketable Treasury securities rose to 2.92 percent as of September 30, 2023, up from 1.97 percent on September 30, 2022.

SECTION 3: SPREAD ANALYSIS BY SECURITY TYPE

3.1 Treasury Bills vs. Overnight Reverse Repurchase Rate

The spread between the 3-month Treasury bill yield and the Federal Reserve's overnight reverse repurchase agreement (ON RRP) facility rate averaged negative 12 basis points during fiscal year 2023, reflecting strong demand for short-dated government securities from money market funds. The ON RRP facility rate stood at 5.30 percent as of September 30, 2023, with outstanding balances of $1.37 trillion, down from a peak of $2.55 trillion recorded on December 30, 2022.

3.2 On-the-Run vs. Off-the-Run Spreads

Liquidity premiums in the Treasury market widened modestly during periods of stress. The on-the-run/off-the-run spread for 10-year notes averaged 8.3 basis points during fiscal year 2023, compared to 6.1 basis points in fiscal year 2022. The widest on/off-the-run spread of 14.7 basis points was observed on March 13, 2023, coinciding with the failure of Silicon Valley Bank and the subsequent market volatility. The Office of Debt Management coordinated with the Federal Reserve Bank of New York's Open Market Desk during this period to monitor secondary market functioning.

3.3 TIPS vs. Nominal Treasury Spreads (Breakeven Inflation)

The 10-year breakeven inflation rate, derived from the spread between nominal 10-year Treasury notes and 10-year Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS), averaged 2.31 percent during fiscal year 2023. At fiscal year-end, the 10-year breakeven stood at 2.27 percent, down from 2.47 percent on October 1, 2022. The 5-year breakeven inflation rate averaged 2.44 percent during the fiscal year, ending at 2.33 percent on September 30, 2023.

TIPS outstanding totaled $1.97 trillion as of September 30, 2023, representing 8.6 percent of total marketable debt. The real yield on 10-year TIPS reached 2.30 percent at fiscal year-end, the highest level since January 2009, reflecting the significant tightening of financial conditions.

SECTION 4: DEBT ISSUANCE STRATEGY AND COST IMPLICATIONS

4.1 Weighted Average Maturity

The weighted average maturity (WAM) of the Treasury's marketable debt portfolio was 73.4 months (6.1 years) as of September 30, 2023, compared to 74.2 months on September 30, 2022. The Office of Debt Management maintained its strategy of extending the WAM where cost-effective, though the inverted yield curve created disincentives for long-term issuance relative to short-term financing.

4.3 Floating Rate Note Analysis

Treasury Floating Rate Notes (FRNs), indexed to the 13-week Treasury bill rate plus a spread, saw their effective coupon rates rise substantially during the fiscal year. The weighted average spread on outstanding FRNs was negative 4.2 basis points as of September 30, 2023, reflecting the competitive auction environment at issuance. Outstanding FRN balances totaled $624 billion, representing 2.7 percent of marketable debt.

Foreign official holdings of U.S. Treasury securities totaled $3.47 trillion as of September 30, 2023, representing 15.1 percent of total publicly held debt. Japan remained the largest foreign holder with $1.10 trillion, followed by China with $805 billion, down from $933 billion one year prior.

SECTION 6: RISK ASSESSMENT AND ROLLOVER EXPOSURE

The Office of Debt Management estimates that approximately $8.9 trillion in Treasury securities will mature within the next 12 months, representing 38.7 percent of total marketable debt outstanding. This elevated rollover concentration reflects the significant short-term issuance undertaken during the COVID-19 pandemic period and subsequent fiscal years.

Sensitivity analysis conducted by the Office of Debt Management indicates that a sustained 100-basis-point increase in interest rates across all maturities would increase projected net interest costs by approximately $112 billion annually within a 12-month horizon, rising to $214 billion on a fully rolled-over basis over 10 years. These estimates were prepared using the Office of Management and Budget's economic assumptions published in the Mid-Session Review of the Budget, released July 14, 2023.

SECTION 7: CONCLUSIONS AND FORWARD GUIDANCE

The yield spread environment in fiscal year 2023 presented the federal government with its most challenging borrowing conditions since the early 1980s. The combination of an inverted yield curve, elevated short-term rates, and record gross borrowing needs resulted in net interest costs of $879 billion, equivalent to 3.3 percent of GDP, the highest ratio since 1998.

The Office of Debt Management will continue to evaluate the maturity composition of new issuance on a quarterly basis through the Treasury Borrowing Advisory Committee (TBAC) process. The next quarterly refunding announcement is scheduled for October 30, 2023. The Department remains committed to financing the government's borrowing needs at the lowest cost over time while managing rollover risk, consistent with the mandate established under 31 U.S.C. § 3121.
