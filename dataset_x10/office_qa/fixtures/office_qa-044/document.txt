EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This technical report presents the results of a comprehensive statistical distribution fitting exercise applied to five major Pennsylvania state revenue categories over the five-year period spanning fiscal years 2019 through 2023. The analysis was commissioned in response to a recommendation from the Governor's Office of the Budget dated September 8, 2023, which identified inadequate probabilistic modeling as a contributing factor to a $312 million revenue shortfall experienced in FY2021. The primary objective is to identify optimal probability distributions for each revenue stream to improve the accuracy of 12-month forward revenue projections and to quantify uncertainty bounds for budget planning purposes.

The analysis examined 1,247 monthly revenue observations across five categories. Key findings indicate that Personal Income Tax receipts are best modeled by a log-normal distribution with parameters μ = 10.847 and σ = 0.312, while Corporate Net Income Tax receipts exhibit characteristics consistent with a generalized extreme value (GEV) distribution. Adoption of the recommended distributional models is projected to reduce forecast error by approximately 18.4 percent compared to the current linear extrapolation methodology.

SECTION 1: BACKGROUND AND DATA DESCRIPTION

1.1 Revenue Categories Analyzed

The five revenue streams selected for this analysis collectively accounted for $38.7 billion of the Commonwealth's $45.2 billion in total General Fund revenues during FY2023. The categories and their FY2023 totals are as follows:

Revenue Category | FY2023 Total | Share of General Fund
Personal Income Tax (PIT) | $16.8 billion | 37.2%
Sales and Use Tax (SUT) | $12.1 billion | 26.8%
Corporate Net Income Tax (CNIT) | $5.4 billion | 11.9%
Inheritance and Estate Tax (IET) | $1.3 billion | 2.9%
Realty Transfer Tax (RTT) | $0.6 billion | 1.3%

1.2 Data Sources and Preparation

Monthly revenue data were extracted from the Commonwealth's Integrated Enterprise System (IES) maintained by the Office of the Comptroller. The dataset spans July 1, 2018 through June 30, 2023, yielding 60 monthly observations per revenue category. All figures are reported in nominal dollars. Seasonal adjustment was performed using the U.S. Census Bureau's X-13ARIMA-SEATS procedure, version 1.1.57, prior to distribution fitting. Three data points were flagged as anomalous: April 2020 PIT receipts ($892 million below trend due to the COVID-19 filing deadline extension to July 15, 2020), April 2021 PIT receipts ($1.14 billion above trend due to the same extension's reversal effect, and March 2020 SUT receipts ($234 million below trend due to pandemic-related business closures). These observations were retained in the dataset but flagged, and sensitivity analyses were conducted both with and without these points.

SECTION 2: METHODOLOGY

2.1 Candidate Distributions

For each revenue category, the following eight candidate probability distributions were evaluated:

1. Normal (Gaussian)
2. Log-normal
3. Gamma
4. Weibull
5. Beta (applied to normalized revenue ratios)
6. Generalized Extreme Value (GEV)
7. Negative Binomial (applied to count-based transaction data)
8. Exponential

2.2 Fitting Procedure

Maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) was used as the primary parameter estimation method for all distributions. For the GEV distribution, L-moments estimation was applied as a robustness check, given the known sensitivity of MLE to outliers in heavy-tailed distributions. Goodness-of-fit was assessed using three complementary tests:

- Kolmogorov-Smirnov (KS) test at the 5 percent significance level
- Anderson-Darling (AD) test, which places greater weight on distributional tails
- Akaike Information Criterion (AIC) for model comparison across distributions with differing parameter counts

All computations were performed in R version 4.3.1 using the fitdistrplus package (version 1.1-11) and the ismev package (version 1.42) for GEV fitting.

2.3 Forecast Validation

To assess predictive performance, a rolling 12-month out-of-sample validation was conducted. Models were estimated on data through June 2022 and used to generate probabilistic forecasts for July 2022 through June 2023. Forecast accuracy was measured using Mean Absolute Percentage Error (MAPE) and the Continuous Ranked Probability Score (CRPS).

SECTION 3: RESULTS BY REVENUE CATEGORY

3.1 Personal Income Tax

The PIT revenue series, after seasonal adjustment, exhibited moderate right skewness (skewness coefficient = 0.74) and excess kurtosis of 1.23. The log-normal distribution provided the best fit, with MLE-estimated parameters μ = 10.847 and σ = 0.312. The KS test statistic was 0.071 (p-value = 0.934), and the AD statistic was 0.318, both indicating failure to reject the null hypothesis of log-normal fit at the 5 percent level. The AIC for the log-normal model was 847.3, compared to 861.9 for the normal distribution and 854.6 for the gamma distribution. The 90 percent prediction interval for annual PIT receipts under this model spans $15.1 billion to $18.9 billion.

3.2 Sales and Use Tax

SUT receipts demonstrated the most stable distributional behavior of all five categories, with a coefficient of variation of only 8.3 percent across the study period. The normal distribution provided an adequate fit (KS p-value = 0.812), with estimated mean $1.008 billion per month and standard deviation $83.6 million. However, the gamma distribution achieved a marginally lower AIC of 712.4 versus 714.1 for the normal, and is recommended as the preferred model given the theoretical constraint that revenue cannot be negative.

3.3 Corporate Net Income Tax

CNIT receipts exhibited the most complex distributional behavior, characterized by pronounced right skewness (skewness = 1.87) and heavy tails (excess kurtosis = 4.61). This pattern reflects the concentration of corporate tax liability among a small number of large filers: in FY2023, the top 50 corporate taxpayers accounted for 61.4 percent of total CNIT receipts. The GEV distribution provided the best fit, with estimated location parameter μ = 387.2 million, scale parameter σ = 142.6 million, and shape parameter ξ = 0.241. The positive shape parameter confirms the heavy-tailed (Fréchet-type) behavior. The AD statistic of 0.447 was the lowest among all candidate distributions. The 95th percentile of the fitted GEV distribution implies a one-in-twenty chance of monthly CNIT receipts exceeding $891 million, a figure relevant to stress-testing scenarios.

3.4 Inheritance and Estate Tax

IET receipts are driven by a relatively small number of large estate settlements, producing a highly irregular monthly series. The log-normal distribution was selected as the best fit (KS p-value = 0.701, AIC = 389.2), with parameters μ = 18.63 and σ = 0.508 (in thousands of dollars). The coefficient of variation for this series was 42.7 percent, the highest among the five categories, reflecting the inherent unpredictability of mortality timing and estate composition.

3.5 Realty Transfer Tax

RTT receipts are closely linked to residential real estate transaction volumes and exhibited a structural break beginning in October 2022, coinciding with the Federal Reserve's interest rate increases. A two-component mixture model was fitted to account for the pre- and post-break regimes, with the pre-break component modeled as normal (mean = $54.3 million/month, SD = $8.1 million) and the post-break component as normal (mean = $41.7 million/month, SD = $6.4 million). The mixture weight for the post-break component was estimated at 0.31, reflecting the proportion of the sample period falling after October 2022.

SECTION 4: FORECAST PERFORMANCE COMPARISON

The table below summarizes out-of-sample forecast performance for the recommended distributional models versus the current linear extrapolation methodology used by the Office of the Budget.

Revenue Category | Current MAPE | Model MAPE | Improvement
PIT | 6.8% | 5.1% | 25.0%
SUT | 4.2% | 3.7% | 11.9%
CNIT | 14.3% | 10.9% | 23.8%
IET | 19.6% | 17.1% | 12.8%
RTT | 11.4% | 9.8% | 14.0%
Weighted Average | 8.1% | 6.6% | 18.4%

The weighted average improvement of 18.4 percent in MAPE corresponds to an estimated reduction in annual forecast error of approximately $57 million in absolute dollar terms, based on FY2023 revenue levels.

SECTION 5: RECOMMENDATIONS AND IMPLEMENTATION

5.1 Immediate Actions

The Division of Quantitative Methods recommends the following actions for implementation prior to the FY2025 budget cycle, with a target completion date of June 30, 2024:

- Adopt the log-normal model for PIT and IET revenue projections
- Adopt the gamma model for SUT revenue projections
- Adopt the GEV model for CNIT revenue projections, with quarterly re-estimation as new data become available
- Integrate the RTT mixture model into the budget system, with the regime-switching threshold tied to the 30-year fixed mortgage rate published by Freddie Mac

5.2 Uncertainty Quantification

All future revenue projections submitted to the General Assembly should include explicit 70 percent and 90 percent prediction intervals derived from the fitted distributions. This practice aligns with standards adopted by the Congressional Budget Office in its January 2020 Budget and Economic Outlook and by the State of California Department of Finance in its 2022 Revenue Volatility Report.

5.3 Limitations

This analysis is subject to several limitations. The 60-month sample period is relatively short for fitting heavy-tailed distributions such as the GEV. The analysis does not account for potential structural changes in the tax code, including the scheduled expiration of the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act provisions in December 2025, which may materially alter the distributional properties of PIT and CNIT receipts. Annual re-estimation of all models is recommended.
