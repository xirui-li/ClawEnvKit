EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents a comprehensive analysis of yield changes across U.S. Treasury securities during the third quarter of fiscal year 2024, covering the period from April 1 through June 30, 2024. During this period, the Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC) maintained the federal funds rate target range at 5.25 to 5.50 percent, a level unchanged since July 26, 2023. Despite the absence of a policy rate adjustment, Treasury yields across the curve experienced notable movements driven by evolving inflation expectations, labor market data, and shifting investor sentiment regarding the timing of potential rate cuts.

The 10-year Treasury note yield rose from 4.20 percent on April 1, 2024, to 4.36 percent on June 28, 2024, representing an increase of 16 basis points over the quarter. The 2-year Treasury note, more sensitive to near-term monetary policy expectations, declined from 4.62 percent to 4.71 percent before retreating to close the quarter at 4.75 percent, a net increase of 13 basis points. The 30-year Treasury bond yield moved from 4.35 percent to 4.51 percent, an increase of 16 basis points.

SECTION 1: METHODOLOGY AND DATA SOURCES

1.1 Yield Calculation Framework

All yield figures in this report are derived from the Treasury's Daily Yield Curve Rates, published by the Bureau of the Fiscal Service. Yields are calculated on a bond-equivalent basis using the following standard formula for yield to maturity (YTM):

Price = Σ [Coupon / (1 + YTM/2)^t] + [Face Value / (1 + YTM/2)^n]

Where:
- Coupon = semiannual coupon payment in dollars
- YTM = annual yield to maturity expressed as a decimal
- t = period number (1 through n)
- n = total number of semiannual periods to maturity

Basis point changes are calculated as the arithmetic difference between closing yield observations on the first and last business day of the reporting quarter. One basis point equals 0.01 percentage points.

1.2 Benchmark Securities

The following on-the-run Treasury securities served as benchmarks for this analysis:

- 2-Year Note: CUSIP 91282CKL4, issued March 26, 2024, coupon rate 4.625%, maturity March 31, 2026
- 5-Year Note: CUSIP 91282CKM2, issued March 28, 2024, coupon rate 4.375%, maturity March 31, 2029
- 10-Year Note: CUSIP 91282CKN0, issued February 15, 2024, coupon rate 4.250%, maturity February 15, 2034
- 30-Year Bond: CUSIP 912810TZ6, issued February 15, 2024, coupon rate 4.500%, maturity February 15, 2054

1.3 Data Sources

Primary data was sourced from the Federal Reserve H.15 Statistical Release, the Treasury's TreasuryDirect yield curve database, and Bloomberg LP terminal data cross-referenced for accuracy. All figures represent end-of-day secondary market yields.

SECTION 3: MONTHLY YIELD MOVEMENT ANALYSIS

3.1 April 2024

April proved to be the most volatile month of the quarter. The release of the March Consumer Price Index (CPI) on April 10, 2024, showing headline inflation of 3.5 percent year-over-year, above the consensus estimate of 3.4 percent, triggered a sharp selloff in Treasuries. The 10-year yield rose 22 basis points in a single week, moving from 4.20 percent on April 1 to 4.42 percent by April 16. The 2-year yield peaked at 4.99 percent on April 30, its highest level since November 2023.

Total Treasury market trading volume in April averaged $672 billion per day, according to data from the Securities Industry and Financial Markets Association (SIFMA), compared to a daily average of $598 billion in January 2024.

3.2 May 2024

Yields partially reversed in May following softer-than-expected labor market data. The April employment situation report, released May 3, 2024, showed nonfarm payrolls increased by 175,000, below the consensus forecast of 238,000. The unemployment rate edged up to 3.9 percent from 3.8 percent. In response, the 10-year yield fell from 4.68 percent on May 1 to 4.34 percent by May 15, a decline of 34 basis points in two weeks.

The Federal Reserve's May 1, 2024, FOMC statement, authored under the direction of Chair Jerome Powell, reiterated that the Committee does not expect it will be appropriate to reduce the target range until it has gained greater confidence that inflation is moving sustainably toward 2 percent.

3.3 June 2024

June saw a modest yield increase as the May CPI report, released June 12, 2024, showed core inflation (excluding food and energy) at 3.4 percent year-over-year, unchanged from April. The FOMC meeting on June 11-12, 2024, resulted in no policy change, and the updated Summary of Economic Projections (the "dot plot") showed the median projection for rate cuts in 2024 was revised from three cuts to one cut, a significant hawkish shift from the March projection. The 10-year yield closed June at 4.36 percent.

SECTION 4: YIELD CURVE SHAPE ANALYSIS

4.1 Inversion Metrics

The Treasury yield curve remained inverted throughout the quarter, as it has been since July 5, 2022. The 2-year/10-year spread, a widely monitored recession indicator, began the quarter at -42 basis points (4.62% minus 4.20%) and ended at -39 basis points (4.75% minus 4.36%), a narrowing of 3 basis points. This represents the least inverted reading since October 2023.

The 3-month/10-year spread, preferred by the Federal Reserve Bank of New York for recession probability modeling, stood at -105 basis points on June 28, 2024, compared to -118 basis points on April 1, 2024.

4.2 Term Premium Estimation

Using the Adrian, Crump, and Moench (ACM) model maintained by the Federal Reserve Bank of New York, the 10-year term premium was estimated at -0.08 percent as of June 28, 2024, compared to -0.31 percent on April 1, 2024. This increase of 23 basis points in term premium suggests that a portion of the yield increase during the quarter reflected rising compensation demanded by investors for holding longer-duration securities, rather than solely revised rate expectations.

5.2 Incremental Borrowing Cost Estimate

The 16 basis point increase in the 10-year yield over the quarter has material implications for federal borrowing costs. The Treasury issued approximately $214 billion in 10-year notes during Q3 FY2024. Applying the 16 basis point yield increase to this issuance volume, the incremental annual interest cost attributable to the yield rise is estimated at approximately $342 million per year, or $3.42 billion over the 10-year life of these securities, assuming the securities are held to maturity.

SECTION 6: COMPARATIVE ANALYSIS

6.1 Year-Over-Year Comparison

The 10-year Treasury yield on June 28, 2024, of 4.36 percent compares to 3.84 percent on June 30, 2023, representing a year-over-year increase of 52 basis points. The 2-year yield of 4.75 percent on June 28, 2024, compares to 4.87 percent on June 30, 2023, a year-over-year decrease of 12 basis points, reflecting the market's expectation that the peak in short-term rates has passed.

6.2 International Comparison

As of June 28, 2024, the U.S. 10-year yield of 4.36 percent compared to the German 10-year Bund yield of 2.50 percent, a spread of 186 basis points. The U.K. 10-year Gilt yielded 4.18 percent, 18 basis points below the U.S. benchmark. The Japanese 10-year Government Bond yielded 1.07 percent, reflecting the Bank of Japan's continued accommodative policy stance.

SECTION 7: OUTLOOK AND RISK FACTORS

The Office of Debt Management identifies the following key risk factors for Treasury yields in Q4 FY2024 (July 1 – September 30, 2024):

- Federal Reserve policy path: Any acceleration in disinflation progress could prompt earlier or more aggressive rate cuts, compressing yields across the curve. Conversely, a reacceleration of inflation could push yields higher.
- Federal fiscal position: The Congressional Budget Office's June 2024 baseline projects a fiscal year 2024 deficit of $1.915 trillion, requiring continued elevated Treasury issuance that may exert upward pressure on term premiums.
- Foreign demand: Indirect bidder participation, a proxy for foreign official demand, averaged 68.4 percent across 10-year note auctions in Q3, above the trailing 12-month average of 65.1 percent, suggesting continued robust international appetite for U.S. government securities.
- Election uncertainty: The November 5, 2024, presidential election introduces potential fiscal policy uncertainty that may widen term premiums in the latter portion of the quarter.
