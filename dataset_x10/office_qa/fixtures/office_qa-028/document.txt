EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Total federal tax revenues for Fiscal Year 2023 reached $4.44 trillion, representing a decline of 9.3 percent from the record $4.90 trillion collected in Fiscal Year 2022. This contraction, the largest single-year percentage decline since FY 2009, reflects the expiration of pandemic-era tax provisions, a significant reduction in capital gains realizations, and lower corporate profit margins across several key sectors. Despite the year-over-year decline, FY 2023 revenues remained 17.2 percent above the pre-pandemic FY 2019 baseline of $3.46 trillion, indicating that the federal revenue base has structurally expanded over the four-year period.

This report was prepared under the direction of Deputy Assistant Secretary for Tax Analysis Margaret L. Forsythe and draws on data compiled by the Internal Revenue Service, the Congressional Budget Office, and the Bureau of Economic Analysis.

SECTION 1: TOTAL REVENUE OVERVIEW

Federal revenues in FY 2023 totaled $4.439 trillion against total outlays of $6.134 trillion, producing a unified budget deficit of $1.695 trillion. This deficit represents 6.3 percent of gross domestic product, up from 5.4 percent in FY 2022.

SECTION 2: INDIVIDUAL INCOME TAX ANALYSIS

Individual income taxes remained the single largest source of federal revenue at $2.176 trillion, though this figure declined by $63.1 billion compared to FY 2022's $2.239 trillion. The primary driver of this decline was a sharp reduction in capital gains realizations. According to IRS Statistics of Income data, net capital gains reported on individual returns fell from approximately $1.8 trillion in tax year 2021 to an estimated $863 billion in tax year 2022, a decline of more than 52 percent, reflecting the broad equity market correction that began in January 2022.

Key Individual Income Tax Metrics:

- Number of individual returns filed in calendar year 2022: 153.8 million
- Median adjusted gross income for all filers: $46,240
- Top 1 percent of earners (AGI above $548,336) paid 45.8 percent of all individual income taxes
- Top 10 percent of earners paid 76.0 percent of all individual income taxes
- Effective tax rate for middle quintile (AGI $50,000–$75,000): 9.1 percent
- Withholding collections for FY 2023: $1.97 trillion, up 3.2 percent from FY 2022
- Estimated tax payments: $421.3 billion, down 18.7 percent from FY 2022

The decline in estimated tax payments is directly attributable to reduced capital gains activity and lower pass-through business income reported by S-corporation and partnership owners. The IRS issued 96.3 million refunds totaling $334.8 billion during FY 2023, with an average refund of $3,476, down from $3,651 in FY 2022.

SECTION 3: PAYROLL TAX ANALYSIS

Payroll taxes were the single strongest-performing revenue category in FY 2023, growing by $120.8 billion or 8.1 percent to reach $1.614 trillion. This growth reflects continued labor market strength throughout the fiscal year. The unemployment rate averaged 3.6 percent across FY 2023, and total wages and salaries subject to payroll taxation grew by an estimated 6.8 percent, driven by both employment gains and wage inflation.

Payroll Tax Breakdown:

- Social Security (OASDI) taxes: $1,100.4 billion (6.2% employee + 6.2% employer on wages up to $160,200 in 2023)
- Medicare (HI) taxes: $393.2 billion (1.45% employee + 1.45% employer, no wage cap)
- Additional Medicare Tax (0.9% on wages above $200,000 for single filers): $20.2 billion
- Railroad Retirement taxes: $5.8 billion
- Federal Unemployment Tax (FUTA): $7.2 billion

The Social Security wage base increased from $147,000 in calendar year 2022 to $160,200 in calendar year 2023, a 9.0 percent increase that contributed approximately $18.4 billion in additional OASDI revenue.

SECTION 4: CORPORATE INCOME TAX ANALYSIS

Corporate income tax receipts fell to $419.7 billion in FY 2023, a decline of $121.5 billion or 22.4 percent from the $541.2 billion collected in FY 2022. This represents the steepest single-year decline in corporate tax receipts since FY 2009, when revenues fell 54.9 percent during the financial crisis.

Contributing factors to the FY 2023 decline include:

1. Compression of corporate profit margins across manufacturing, technology, and retail sectors as input costs remained elevated while consumer pricing power weakened.

2. The mandatory amortization of research and development expenditures under Section 174, which took effect January 1, 2022, generated large deductions that reduced taxable income in FY 2023 filings.

3. Bonus depreciation began phasing down from 100 percent to 80 percent for property placed in service after December 31, 2022, though the remaining 80 percent allowance still provided substantial deductions.

4. Net operating loss carryforwards from FY 2020 and FY 2021 continued to offset taxable income for firms in the airline, hospitality, and energy sectors.

The 15 percent corporate alternative minimum tax established by the Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 applied to corporations with average annual adjusted financial statement income exceeding $1 billion. Treasury estimates this provision generated approximately $8.8 billion in additional corporate tax revenue in FY 2023, its first full year of implementation.

SECTION 5: EXCISE TAXES AND OTHER REVENUES

Excise tax collections totaled $96.3 billion in FY 2023, a modest increase of $3.4 billion or 3.7 percent from FY 2022. Highway-related excise taxes, including the 18.4 cents-per-gallon federal gasoline tax and the 24.4 cents-per-gallon diesel tax, generated $38.7 billion. Tobacco excise taxes contributed $12.1 billion, continuing a long-term declining trend as smoking rates fall. Alcohol excise taxes generated $10.4 billion. Aviation-related excise taxes, including the 7.5 percent ticket tax and the $4.80 per-segment fee, produced $17.2 billion, reflecting the continued recovery of air travel to pre-pandemic levels.

Estate and gift tax revenues declined to $34.1 billion from $39.9 billion in FY 2022, a drop of 14.6 percent. The basic exclusion amount for estate tax purposes rose to $12.92 million per individual in 2023, up from $12.06 million in 2022, reducing the number of taxable estates. The IRS processed approximately 4,100 taxable estate tax returns in calendar year 2022, the most recent year for which complete data is available.

Customs duties fell to $80.0 billion from $85.3 billion in FY 2022, a decline of 6.2 percent, reflecting lower import volumes as consumer goods demand moderated and supply chain normalization reduced emergency import activity.

SECTION 7: OUTLOOK FOR FISCAL YEAR 2024

The Congressional Budget Office projects FY 2024 federal revenues of $4.664 trillion, representing a recovery of 5.1 percent from FY 2023 levels. This projection assumes continued payroll tax growth consistent with a labor market that gradually softens toward a 4.1 percent unemployment rate by September 2024, a partial recovery in capital gains realizations as equity markets stabilize, and the first full-year effect of the stock buyback excise tax of 1 percent established by the Inflation Reduction Act, projected to generate approximately $6.3 billion.

Key risks to the FY 2024 revenue outlook include potential recessionary conditions that could suppress wage growth and corporate profitability, further deterioration in capital gains activity if equity markets decline, and legislative changes to tax provisions currently under Congressional consideration.
