EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents a comparative analysis of appropriated and expended budgets across major Commonwealth departments for fiscal year 2023–2024 (July 1, 2023 through June 30, 2024) against the prior fiscal year 2022–2023. The total General Fund appropriation for FY 2023–2024 was $47.6 billion, representing an increase of $2.1 billion, or 4.6 percent, over the FY 2022–2023 appropriation of $45.5 billion. Actual expenditures for FY 2023–2024 totaled $46.8 billion, leaving an unspent balance of $812 million across all departments.

Governor Josh Shapiro's administration submitted the FY 2023–2024 budget proposal on February 6, 2024, and the General Assembly passed the final appropriations bill on July 11, 2024, following a brief impasse. This report was compiled under the direction of Budget Secretary Uri Monson and reflects data certified by the Office of the Comptroller as of August 31, 2024.

SECTION 1: METHODOLOGY AND DATA SOURCES

All figures in this report are drawn from the Commonwealth's Integrated Enterprise System (IES), the official financial management platform maintained by the Office of Administration. Appropriation data reflects enacted legislation as signed into law. Expenditure data reflects all obligations and disbursements recorded through the close of the fiscal year on June 30, 2024, with a 60-day lapse period ending August 31, 2024.

Percentage changes are calculated using the formula: ((FY24 figure – FY23 figure) / FY23 figure) × 100. Figures are rounded to the nearest thousand dollars in departmental tables and to the nearest million in summary tables. Federal fund pass-throughs are excluded from General Fund comparisons unless otherwise noted.

SECTION 3: NOTABLE FINDINGS AND ANALYSIS

3.1 Department of Education — Largest Proportional Increase

The Department of Education recorded the largest proportional increase among major departments, with appropriations rising 8.09 percent from $8.211 billion in FY 2022–2023 to $8.876 billion in FY 2023–2024. This increase was driven primarily by the Basic Education Funding line item, which grew from $6.744 billion to $7.328 billion, an increase of $584 million. This expansion reflects the administration's response to the February 2023 Commonwealth Court ruling in William Penn School District v. Pennsylvania Department of Education, in which Judge Renée Cohn Jubelirer found the existing school funding system unconstitutional. Special Education funding also increased by $100.4 million, reaching $1.228 billion for FY 2023–2024.

3.2 Department of Human Services — Largest Absolute Dollar Increase

The Department of Human Services (DHS) received the largest absolute dollar increase of any single department, with appropriations growing by $772.4 million. The Medical Assistance — Capitation line item, which funds managed care for Medicaid recipients, accounted for $441.6 million of that increase, rising to $9.87 billion. The Long-Term Care line item grew by $118.3 million to $1.94 billion, reflecting increased enrollment among elderly Pennsylvanians. DHS Secretary Dr. Val Arkoosh noted in her August 2024 testimony before the Appropriations Committee that Medicaid enrollment in Pennsylvania stood at approximately 3.6 million individuals as of June 30, 2024.

3.3 Department of Corrections — Stabilizing Costs

The Department of Corrections saw a more modest increase of 3.66 percent, the second-lowest rate among major departments. The department's inmate population declined from an average daily count of 37,842 in FY 2022–2023 to 36,914 in FY 2023–2024, a reduction of 928 individuals, or 2.45 percent. Personnel costs, which represent 68.4 percent of the department's total budget, increased by $74.2 million due to a negotiated collective bargaining agreement with AFSCME Council 13 that took effect January 1, 2024, providing a 3.5 percent wage increase for corrections officers.

3.4 Pennsylvania State Police

The Pennsylvania State Police appropriation increased by $67.3 million, or 4.52 percent, to $1.556 billion. Trooper recruitment and training costs accounted for $22.1 million of the increase, as the department graduated three cadet classes totaling 412 new troopers during the fiscal year. Fleet modernization, including the purchase of 847 new patrol vehicles at an average unit cost of $38,400, added $32.5 million to capital expenditures.

3.5 Department of Agriculture — Second-Highest Proportional Increase

The Department of Agriculture recorded an 8.18 percent increase in appropriations, the second-highest rate among major departments. The Agricultural Research line item grew by $9.4 million to $47.6 million, and the Farmland Preservation Program received an additional $11.2 million, bringing its total to $62.8 million. Secretary Russell Redding attributed the increases to rising input costs for farmers and expanded conservation programming under the Pennsylvania Agricultural Surplus System.

SECTION 5: REVENUE CONTEXT

Total General Fund revenues for FY 2023–2024 reached $47.1 billion, falling $500 million short of the $47.6 billion appropriated. The shortfall was covered through a $500 million transfer from the Rainy Day Fund, which held a balance of $5.1 billion as of June 30, 2024. Personal income tax collections totaled $17.4 billion, representing 36.9 percent of total General Fund revenue. Sales and use tax collections reached $12.8 billion, up 2.3 percent from $12.5 billion in FY 2022–2023. Corporate net income tax collections declined by $340 million to $4.6 billion, reflecting the scheduled rate reduction from 8.99 percent to 8.49 percent that took effect January 1, 2024, pursuant to Act 53 of 2022.

SECTION 6: OUTLOOK FOR FISCAL YEAR 2024–2025

The Governor's proposed FY 2024–2025 budget, submitted to the General Assembly on February 4, 2025, requests total General Fund appropriations of $48.9 billion, an increase of $1.3 billion, or 2.73 percent, over FY 2023–2024 enacted levels. The Department of Education is proposed to receive $9.4 billion, an additional $523.6 million, as the administration continues to address the school funding equity mandate. The Independent Fiscal Office projects General Fund revenues of $48.4 billion for FY 2024–2025, suggesting a potential structural gap of approximately $500 million that will require resolution through either additional revenue measures or expenditure adjustments before final enactment.
