EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents a comparative analysis of departmental budget allocations and expenditures for the Commonwealth of Pennsylvania across fiscal years 2022–2023 and 2023–2024. The Commonwealth's total enacted general fund budget for FY 2023–2024 was $47.6 billion, representing an increase of $2.1 billion, or 4.6 percent, over the FY 2022–2023 enacted budget of $45.5 billion. This document is intended for use by the General Assembly, the Governor's Office, department secretaries, and the public in evaluating fiscal performance and resource allocation priorities.

The analysis covers fourteen major executive departments and identifies areas of significant growth, reduction, and reallocation. Particular attention is given to the Departments of Education, Human Services, Transportation, and Corrections, which together account for approximately 68.4 percent of total general fund expenditures.

SECTION 1: METHODOLOGY AND SCOPE

Data for this report was compiled from the Commonwealth's Integrated Enterprise System (IES), the official financial management platform maintained by the Office of Comptroller Operations. Figures reflect enacted appropriations as signed by Governor Josh Shapiro on July 11, 2023, for FY 2023–2024, and enacted appropriations signed by Governor Tom Wolf on June 30, 2022, for FY 2022–2023.

All dollar figures are presented in millions unless otherwise noted. Percentage changes are calculated using the enacted appropriation as the baseline. Actual expenditure figures for FY 2022–2023 are final and audited. Actual expenditure figures for FY 2023–2024 reflect spending through June 30, 2024, and are considered preliminary pending final audit by the Auditor General's Office, expected to be completed by December 31, 2024.

SECTION 3: ANALYSIS OF MAJOR DEPARTMENTS

3.1 Department of Education

The Department of Education received the largest single-year dollar increase of any department, with its allocation rising from $14,217.4 million in FY 2022–2023 to $15,308.6 million in FY 2023–2024, an increase of $1,091.2 million or 7.7 percent. This increase was driven primarily by the Basic Education Funding formula, which received an additional $567.3 million, bringing total Basic Education Funding to $8,142.7 million. Special Education funding increased by $100.0 million to $1,421.4 million. The increase reflects the recommendations of the Basic Education Funding Commission, which issued its final report on January 11, 2023, finding that Pennsylvania's school funding system was unconstitutional in its distribution methodology.

Secretary of Education Khalid Mumin identified rural and low-wealth school districts as the primary beneficiaries of the new allocation formula. Approximately 371 of Pennsylvania's 500 school districts received increased per-pupil allocations under the revised formula.

3.2 Department of Human Services

Human Services remained the second-largest department by appropriation, with funding increasing from $13,982.1 million to $14,401.3 million, a gain of $419.2 million or 3.0 percent. The Medical Assistance program, which provides Medicaid coverage to approximately 3.4 million Pennsylvanians, accounted for $9,887.6 million of the department's total budget. The Children's Health Insurance Program (CHIP) received $318.4 million, unchanged from the prior year. Behavioral health services funding increased by $84.7 million, reaching $1,203.6 million, reflecting continued investment in opioid treatment and mental health crisis response infrastructure.

The department's Long-Term Care line item increased by $112.3 million to $2,144.8 million, driven by increased enrollment in home and community-based services programs and rising provider reimbursement rates, which were adjusted upward by 3.5 percent effective January 1, 2024.

3.3 Department of Transportation

PennDOT's general fund appropriation increased from $4,118.6 million to $4,276.9 million, a rise of $158.3 million or 3.8 percent. This figure does not include federal highway funds administered through PennDOT, which totaled an additional $2.1 billion in FY 2023–2024 under the federal Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act. The department's capital budget included $612.4 million for bridge rehabilitation projects, an increase of $88.6 million over the prior year. Secretary Mike Carroll noted that 3,300 of Pennsylvania's approximately 25,000 state-owned bridges were rated structurally deficient as of January 2024.

3.4 Department of Corrections

The Department of Corrections budget increased by $106.5 million, from $3,204.7 million to $3,311.2 million, a 3.3 percent increase. The department operates 23 state correctional institutions housing approximately 37,400 incarcerated individuals as of June 30, 2024. Personnel costs account for 74.2 percent of the department's total budget, or approximately $2,456.9 million. The average annual cost to incarcerate one individual in a Pennsylvania state facility was $56,112 in FY 2023–2024, up from $53,847 in FY 2022–2023, an increase of 4.2 percent.

3.5 Department of Health

The Department of Health saw the second-highest percentage increase among major departments, with its budget rising 8.4 percent from $912.4 million to $988.7 million. The increase of $76.3 million was directed primarily toward infectious disease surveillance ($18.4 million increase), maternal and child health programs ($14.7 million increase), and the expansion of the WIC program to serve an estimated 192,000 participants statewide.

SECTION 4: DEPARTMENTS WITH REDUCED APPROPRIATIONS

Three departments experienced year-over-year budget reductions:

Department of Labor and Industry: Reduced by $13.6 million (-1.8%), from $744.8 million to $731.2 million. The reduction reflects the expiration of federal pandemic-era unemployment compensation administrative grants that had supplemented the department's operating budget through FY 2022–2023.

Department of General Services: Reduced by $5.3 million (-3.2%), from $163.7 million to $158.4 million. The reduction was attributed to the completion of the Capitol Complex East Wing renovation project, which had required $9.2 million in one-time capital expenditures in the prior fiscal year.

Department of Community and Economic Development: Experienced the largest percentage reduction, falling from $138.6 million to $111.7 million, a decrease of $26.9 million or 19.4 percent. This reduction reflects the conclusion of the Pennsylvania Broadband Development Authority's initial capitalization phase, which had received $31.0 million in one-time appropriations in FY 2022–2023 using American Rescue Plan Act funds.

SECTION 6: CONCLUSIONS AND FORWARD OUTLOOK

The FY 2023–2024 budget reflects the Commonwealth's continued prioritization of education funding reform, healthcare access, and infrastructure investment. The 4.6 percent overall increase in general fund appropriations was supported by stronger-than-projected revenue collections in FY 2022–2023, which produced a year-end surplus of approximately $1.4 billion.

The Office of the Budget projects that general fund revenues for FY 2024–2025 will grow by approximately 2.8 percent, presenting a more constrained fiscal environment than the prior two years. Departments are advised to submit FY 2024–2025 budget requests by October 15, 2024, in accordance with the Governor's budget preparation instructions issued August 28, 2024.
