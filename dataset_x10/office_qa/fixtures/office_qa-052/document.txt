EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents a comparative analysis of appropriated and expended budgets across major Commonwealth departments for fiscal years ending June 30, 2023 and June 30, 2024. The analysis was prepared under the direction of Budget Secretary Uri Monson and reflects data compiled from the Commonwealth's SAP financial management system as of September 30, 2024. Total general fund expenditures for FY 2023–2024 reached $42.8 billion, representing an increase of $2.1 billion, or 5.2 percent, over the prior fiscal year's total of $40.7 billion. This report is intended to assist the General Assembly, the Governor's Office, and the public in understanding year-over-year spending trends and departmental budget performance.

SECTION 1: METHODOLOGY AND DATA SOURCES

All figures presented in this report are drawn from the Commonwealth's Integrated Enterprise System (IES), which serves as the official accounting system of record. Appropriation data reflects enacted budgets as signed by Governor Josh Shapiro on July 11, 2023 (FY 2022–2023 supplemental) and July 9, 2024 (FY 2023–2024 general appropriations act). Expenditure figures include all obligations posted through June 30 of each respective fiscal year, with lapse calculations finalized by September 30, 2024.

Departments are categorized by their primary funding source. This report covers only general fund appropriations and does not include federal funds, special funds, or augmentations unless specifically noted. Percentage changes are calculated on the basis of total appropriated amounts rather than actual expenditures to provide a consistent baseline for comparison.

Total Expended:               $40.7 billion       $42.8 billion       +$2.1 billion     +5.2%

The statewide lapse rate increased from 1.9 percent in FY 2022–2023 to 2.5 percent in FY 2023–2024, indicating that departments collectively left a larger share of appropriated funds unspent. The Bureau of Financial Management and Analysis notes that this increase is partially attributable to delayed hiring in the Department of Human Services and deferred capital expenditures in the Department of Transportation.

SECTION 3: DEPARTMENT-BY-DEPARTMENT ANALYSIS

3.1 Department of Education

The Department of Education received the largest single appropriation in FY 2023–2024, totaling $14.2 billion, compared to $13.5 billion in FY 2022–2023, an increase of $700 million or 5.2 percent. This increase reflects the continued implementation of the Basic Education Funding formula following the Commonwealth Court's February 7, 2023 ruling in William Penn School District v. Pennsylvania Department of Education, which found the existing school funding system unconstitutional.

Key expenditure categories within Education:
- Basic Education Funding: $7.89 billion (up from $7.32 billion, +7.8%)
- Special Education: $1.27 billion (up from $1.21 billion, +5.0%)
- Pre-K Counts and Head Start Supplemental: $402 million (up from $375 million, +7.2%)
- Higher Education (State System): $618 million (unchanged from prior year)
- School Safety and Security Grants: $100 million (new line item, no prior year comparison)

Actual expenditures reached $13.97 billion, leaving a lapse of approximately $230 million, or 1.6 percent of the total appropriation.

3.2 Department of Human Services

The Department of Human Services (DHS), led by Secretary Dr. Val Arkoosh, received an appropriation of $15.6 billion in FY 2023–2024, compared to $14.9 billion in FY 2022–2023, an increase of $700 million or 4.7 percent. DHS remains the second-largest department by general fund appropriation.

Key expenditure categories within DHS:
- Medical Assistance (Medicaid): $8.4 billion (up from $7.9 billion, +6.3%)
- Long-Term Care Services: $2.1 billion (up from $2.0 billion, +5.0%)
- Child Welfare Services: $1.3 billion (up from $1.2 billion, +8.3%)
- Mental Health and Substance Abuse: $890 million (up from $845 million, +5.3%)
- Cash Assistance Programs: $312 million (down from $328 million, -4.9%)

DHS reported a lapse of $480 million, or 3.1 percent of its appropriation, the highest lapse rate among major departments. The Bureau attributes this primarily to 1,247 unfilled positions as of June 30, 2024, resulting in approximately $89 million in unspent personnel costs, and to delays in the procurement of a new Medicaid Management Information System replacement contract, which pushed $310 million in planned technology expenditures into FY 2024–2025.

3.3 Department of Transportation

PennDOT received a general fund appropriation of $2.3 billion in FY 2023–2024, compared to $2.1 billion in FY 2022–2023, an increase of $200 million or 9.5 percent. This figure does not include federal highway funds administered through PennDOT, which totaled an additional $4.7 billion in FY 2023–2024.

Key expenditure categories within Transportation:
- Highway Maintenance and Operations: $1.04 billion (up from $940 million, +10.6%)
- Bridge Program: $410 million (up from $385 million, +6.5%)
- Public Transit Assistance: $525 million (up from $490 million, +7.1%)
- Aviation Programs: $48 million (down from $52 million, -7.7%)
- Driver and Vehicle Services: $112 million (up from $104 million, +7.7%)

PennDOT's lapse rate was 1.1 percent, or approximately $25 million, below the statewide average.

3.4 Department of Corrections

The Department of Corrections received $3.1 billion in FY 2023–2024, compared to $2.95 billion in FY 2022–2023, an increase of $150 million or 5.1 percent. The department operates 23 state correctional institutions housing approximately 37,400 incarcerated individuals as of June 30, 2024.

Key expenditure categories within Corrections:
- Institutional Operations (Personnel): $1.89 billion (up from $1.78 billion, +6.2%)
- Healthcare Services: $478 million (up from $441 million, +8.4%)
- Inmate Programs and Education: $87 million (up from $79 million, +10.1%)
- Capital Maintenance: $215 million (up from $198 million, +8.6%)

The per-inmate daily cost rose from $119.42 in FY 2022–2023 to $127.18 in FY 2023–2024, an increase of 6.5 percent, driven primarily by healthcare cost inflation and a 4.2 percent increase in correctional officer base wages effective January 1, 2024 under the AFSCME Council 13 collective bargaining agreement.

3.5 Department of Military and Veterans Affairs

DMVA received $215 million in FY 2023–2024, compared to $198 million in FY 2022–2023, an increase of $17 million or 8.6 percent. The department operates six veterans homes across the Commonwealth with a combined licensed capacity of 1,050 residents.

SECTION 4: NOTABLE BUDGET VARIANCES AND FINDINGS

The following departments exceeded their prior-year appropriations by the largest margins in percentage terms:

1. Department of Agriculture: +12.4% ($98 million to $110 million), driven by expanded farmland preservation funding and new avian influenza response reserves.
2. Department of Drug and Alcohol Programs: +11.8% ($42 million to $47 million), reflecting increased opioid settlement fund matching requirements.
3. Department of Corrections: +5.1%, as noted above.

The following departments saw reductions in appropriation:

1. Office of Administration: -3.2% ($312 million to $302 million), reflecting consolidation of IT infrastructure contracts.
2. Department of Revenue: -1.8% ($189 million to $185.6 million), reflecting efficiency gains from expanded online filing systems.

SECTION 5: CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS

The Bureau of Financial Management and Analysis offers the following observations based on FY 2023–2024 data:

First, the overall 5.2 percent increase in general fund expenditures is consistent with the Commonwealth's five-year average growth rate of 4.9 percent and reflects both inflationary pressures and deliberate policy expansions in education and human services.

Second, the elevated lapse rate in the Department of Human Services warrants continued monitoring. The Bureau recommends that DHS submit a workforce recruitment and retention plan to the Office of the Budget by December 31, 2024, addressing the 1,247 vacant positions identified as of fiscal year-end.

Third, the per-inmate cost increase of 6.5 percent in the Department of Corrections outpaces general inflation and should be examined in the context of the department's forthcoming population management and healthcare contracting review scheduled for the first quarter of FY 2024–2025.

Fourth, the new $100 million School Safety and Security Grant line item within the Department of Education should be subject to a performance audit by the Office of the Auditor General no later than June 30, 2025, to ensure funds are being deployed in accordance with Act 44 of 2023.
