EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents a comprehensive compound annual growth rate (CAGR) analysis of the Federal Highway Trust Fund (HTF) for the ten-year period spanning fiscal year 2014 through fiscal year 2023. The analysis was conducted in accordance with the requirements set forth under 31 U.S.C. § 9101 and the Fixing America's Surface Transportation (FAST) Act of 2015. The Bureau of Fiscal Service, in coordination with the Federal Highway Administration (FHWA) and the Federal Transit Administration (FTA), prepared this document to provide Congress, the Office of Management and Budget, and the public with a transparent accounting of trust fund growth, revenue trends, and expenditure patterns over the referenced period.

The Highway Trust Fund recorded a beginning balance of $14.7 billion at the close of fiscal year 2014 and a balance of $21.3 billion at the close of fiscal year 2023. The computed ten-year CAGR for total fund balance stands at 4.18 percent. This figure reflects the combined effects of dedicated fuel tax revenues, general fund transfers authorized by Congress, and disbursements for highway and transit programs.

---

SECTION 1: BACKGROUND AND STATUTORY AUTHORITY

The Federal Highway Trust Fund was established under the Highway Revenue Act of 1956 and is funded primarily through federal excise taxes on motor fuels. The current federal gasoline tax rate of 18.4 cents per gallon has remained unchanged since 1993, and the diesel fuel tax rate of 24.4 cents per gallon has similarly remained static. These rates are not indexed to inflation, which has created a structural revenue gap that Congress has addressed through a series of general fund transfers totaling $275 billion between fiscal year 2008 and fiscal year 2023.

The Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (IIJA), signed into law on November 15, 2021, authorized $110 billion in new spending for roads, bridges, and major projects over five years, with $47.3 billion directed specifically to the Highway Account and $12.2 billion to the Transit Account of the HTF.

---

SECTION 2: METHODOLOGY FOR CAGR COMPUTATION

The CAGR was calculated using the standard formula:

CAGR = (Ending Value / Beginning Value) ^ (1 / Number of Years) - 1

For this analysis:
- Beginning Value (FY 2014 closing balance): $14,700,000,000
- Ending Value (FY 2023 closing balance): $21,300,000,000
- Number of Years: 9 (from end of FY 2014 to end of FY 2023)

CAGR = ($21.3B / $14.7B) ^ (1/9) - 1
CAGR = (1.4490) ^ (0.1111) - 1
CAGR = 1.0418 - 1
CAGR = 0.0418, or 4.18 percent

Separate CAGR computations were performed for the Highway Account and the Transit Account, as well as for dedicated tax revenues and total receipts including general fund transfers. All figures are expressed in nominal dollars unless otherwise noted. Inflation-adjusted figures using the Bureau of Labor Statistics Consumer Price Index (CPI-U) are provided in Appendix B of this report.

Notable observations from Table 1:

- The sharpest single-year decline occurred in FY 2020, when total fund balance fell by 5.91 percent, from $18.6 billion to $17.5 billion. This contraction is directly attributable to reduced vehicle miles traveled during the COVID-19 pandemic, which caused fuel tax receipts to fall by approximately $3.1 billion below projections for that fiscal year.

- The strongest single-year growth occurred in FY 2021, when the balance increased by 9.14 percent, reflecting both a recovery in fuel consumption and a $6.0 billion general fund transfer authorized under the American Rescue Plan Act of 2021.

- The Highway Account CAGR over the nine-year period is 4.12 percent, while the Transit Account CAGR is 4.38 percent, indicating marginally faster proportional growth in transit funding.

Key findings from revenue analysis:

- Dedicated fuel tax revenues grew at a combined CAGR of only 1.21 percent between FY 2014 and FY 2023, far below the general inflation rate of 3.4 percent over the same period as measured by the CPI-U.

- General fund transfers grew at a CAGR of 4.10 percent, reflecting Congress's increasing reliance on non-dedicated revenue to sustain trust fund solvency.

- As of FY 2023, general fund transfers represent approximately 17.1 percent of total HTF receipts, compared to 13.9 percent in FY 2014.

- The Congressional Budget Office (CBO) projects that without legislative action, the Highway Account will face insolvency by fiscal year 2028, based on current expenditure trajectories under the IIJA.

SECTION 5: EXPENDITURE TRENDS AND DISBURSEMENT CAGR

Total disbursements from the Highway Trust Fund increased from $43.2 billion in FY 2014 to $61.8 billion in FY 2023, representing a nine-year CAGR of 4.07 percent. The Highway Account disbursements grew from $34.1 billion to $48.9 billion (CAGR: 4.09 percent), while Transit Account disbursements grew from $9.1 billion to $12.9 billion (CAGR: 3.96 percent).

The five largest program categories by FY 2023 disbursement were:

1. National Highway Performance Program (NHPP): $17.4 billion
2. Surface Transportation Block Grant Program (STBGP): $12.6 billion
3. Federal Transit Formula Grants (Section 5307): $7.2 billion
4. Highway Safety Improvement Program (HSIP): $3.1 billion
5. Congestion Mitigation and Air Quality Improvement Program (CMAQ): $2.4 billion

---

SECTION 6: INFLATION-ADJUSTED CAGR ANALYSIS

When adjusted for inflation using the CPI-U base year of 2014, the real (inflation-adjusted) CAGR of the total HTF balance is negative 0.22 percent over the nine-year period. This finding underscores the erosion of purchasing power within the fund and the declining real value of the 18.4-cent-per-gallon gasoline tax, which would need to be approximately 30.1 cents per gallon in 2023 dollars to maintain its 1993 purchasing power.

The real CAGR of total disbursements is 0.63 percent, indicating that actual program spending has grown modestly in real terms, largely due to the supplemental general fund transfers and IIJA authorizations.

---

SECTION 7: CONCLUSIONS AND POLICY IMPLICATIONS

The ten-year nominal CAGR of 4.18 percent for the Federal Highway Trust Fund balance presents a superficially positive picture of fund growth. However, the underlying revenue analysis reveals structural vulnerabilities. Dedicated fuel tax revenues, which form the constitutional and statutory foundation of the trust fund concept, are growing at a CAGR of only 1.21 percent in nominal terms and are declining in real terms. The increasing penetration of electric vehicles — which reached 7.6 percent of new vehicle sales in calendar year 2023 according to the Energy Information Administration — is expected to further suppress fuel tax receipts in coming years.

The Office of Financial Analysis and Reporting recommends that Congress consider the following actions prior to the expiration of IIJA surface transportation authorizations in September 2026:

- Evaluate indexing the federal fuel tax to inflation or to a construction cost index such as the FHWA's National Highway Construction Cost Index (NHCCI).
- Expand the existing vehicle miles traveled (VMT) fee pilot program authorized under IIJA Section 13001, which received $125 million in initial funding.
- Assess the long-term revenue implications of the federal electric vehicle tax credit program and its interaction with trust fund receipts.
