EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents a comprehensive analysis of Commonwealth expenditure trends across all major appropriation categories for the five-year period spanning Fiscal Year 2018–19 through Fiscal Year 2023–24. Total general fund expenditures grew from $32.7 billion in FY 2018–19 to $42.8 billion in FY 2023–24, representing a cumulative increase of 30.9 percent over the review period. This analysis was prepared by the Bureau of Financial Management under the direction of Budget Secretary Uri Monson and is intended to inform the Governor's Office, the General Assembly, and the public regarding the trajectory of Commonwealth spending obligations.

SECTION 1: TOTAL GENERAL FUND EXPENDITURES

Pennsylvania's general fund serves as the primary operating account for state government. The following table summarizes total expenditures by fiscal year:

Fiscal Year | Total Expenditures | Year-over-Year Change | % Change
FY 2018–19  | $32.7 billion      | —                     | —
FY 2019–20  | $34.1 billion      | +$1.4 billion         | +4.3%
FY 2020–21  | $36.9 billion      | +$2.8 billion         | +8.2%
FY 2021–22  | $39.2 billion      | +$2.3 billion         | +6.2%
FY 2022–23  | $41.5 billion      | +$2.3 billion         | +5.9%
FY 2023–24  | $42.8 billion      | +$1.3 billion         | +3.1%

The largest single-year increase occurred in FY 2020–21, driven primarily by federal American Rescue Plan Act (ARPA) pass-through expenditures totaling $7.3 billion and elevated Medicaid enrollment stemming from the COVID-19 public health emergency. The more modest growth of 3.1 percent in FY 2023–24 reflects a deliberate effort by the Shapiro Administration to moderate discretionary spending growth while maintaining core service commitments.

SECTION 2: EXPENDITURE BY MAJOR CATEGORY

2.1 Health and Human Services

The Department of Human Services (DHS) consistently represents the largest single expenditure category in the Commonwealth's general fund. DHS expenditures increased from $13.4 billion in FY 2018–19 to $18.6 billion in FY 2023–24, a 38.8 percent increase over five years. Medicaid (Medical Assistance) accounts for the dominant share of DHS spending, reaching $14.2 billion in FY 2023–24 alone.

Key drivers of DHS growth include:
- Medicaid enrollment expansion: enrollment grew from 2.87 million recipients in FY 2018–19 to 3.41 million in FY 2022–23, before declining to 3.19 million in FY 2023–24 following the end of continuous enrollment protections in April 2023
- Long-term care costs: nursing facility and home- and community-based services expenditures reached $4.1 billion in FY 2023–24
- Behavioral health services: spending increased 47.2 percent over the review period, reaching $1.8 billion in FY 2023–24

2.2 Education

Combined education expenditures (PreK–12 and higher education) grew from $7.9 billion in FY 2018–19 to $11.3 billion in FY 2023–24, an increase of 43.0 percent. This category experienced the highest proportional growth of any major spending area.

The Basic Education Funding appropriation increased from $6.1 billion in FY 2018–19 to $8.2 billion in FY 2023–24. Notably, the FY 2023–24 enacted budget included a $567 million increase in basic education funding, the largest single-year increase in the Commonwealth's history, responding in part to the February 2023 Commonwealth Court ruling in William Penn School District v. Pennsylvania Department of Education, which found the existing school funding system unconstitutional.

Higher education appropriations to the State System of Higher Education (PASSHE) declined from $477 million in FY 2018–19 to $452 million in FY 2023–24, a reduction of 5.2 percent, reflecting ongoing enrollment pressures at PASSHE's 10 universities.

2.3 Transportation

The Pennsylvania Department of Transportation (PennDOT) general fund appropriations remained relatively stable, ranging between $1.1 billion and $1.3 billion annually. However, total transportation spending including federal funds and Motor License Fund revenues reached $9.7 billion in FY 2023–24. The Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (IIJA), enacted November 2021, provided Pennsylvania with an estimated $11.4 billion in federal transportation funding over five years, substantially supplementing state appropriations beginning in FY 2022–23.

2.4 Public Safety and Corrections

The Department of Corrections (DOC) expenditures declined from $2.4 billion in FY 2018–19 to $2.2 billion in FY 2023–24, a reduction of 8.3 percent, attributable to a declining incarcerated population. The state correctional population fell from 47,579 in January 2019 to 37,842 in June 2024, a reduction of 20.5 percent, driven by criminal justice reform initiatives and sentencing guideline revisions implemented under Act 115 of 2019.

Pennsylvania State Police appropriations increased from $1.3 billion to $1.6 billion over the same period, a 23.1 percent increase, reflecting expanded trooper hiring and increased personnel costs.

SECTION 3: PERSONNEL AND COMPENSATION TRENDS

Commonwealth personnel costs represent approximately 28.4 percent of total general fund expenditures. Total compensation costs, including salaries, wages, and benefits, reached $12.2 billion in FY 2023–24, compared to $9.8 billion in FY 2018–19, an increase of 24.5 percent.

The Commonwealth employed 76,843 full-time equivalent (FTE) positions as of June 30, 2024, compared to 79,211 FTEs in June 2019, a net reduction of 2,368 positions (3.0 percent). Despite the workforce reduction, compensation costs increased due to:

- Collective bargaining agreements negotiated with AFSCME Council 13 and SEIU Healthcare Pennsylvania, which provided cumulative wage increases of 14.5 percent between 2019 and 2024
- Healthcare benefit costs per employee increasing from $18,240 annually in FY 2018–19 to $23,710 in FY 2023–24, a 30.0 percent increase
- Pension contribution rates for the State Employees' Retirement System (SERS) averaging 34.6 percent of payroll in FY 2023–24, compared to 30.1 percent in FY 2018–19

SECTION 4: DEBT SERVICE AND CAPITAL EXPENDITURES

General obligation debt service expenditures totaled $1.04 billion in FY 2023–24, representing 2.4 percent of total general fund expenditures. This reflects a modest decline from $1.12 billion in FY 2018–19 as older bond issuances matured. The Commonwealth's outstanding general obligation debt stood at $10.8 billion as of June 30, 2024.

Capital budget expenditures funded through the Capital Facilities Fund totaled $487 million in FY 2023–24. The Commonwealth's capital program has been constrained by the constitutional debt limit, which restricts net non-electoral debt to 1.75 times the average annual tax revenues of the preceding five fiscal years.

SECTION 5: FEDERAL FUNDS AND INTERGOVERNMENTAL REVENUES

Federal funds constitute a critical component of total Commonwealth spending. In FY 2023–24, federal reimbursements and grants totaled $28.3 billion across all funds, compared to $22.1 billion in FY 2018–19. The federal medical assistance percentage (FMAP) for Pennsylvania was 54.01 percent in FY 2023–24, meaning the federal government reimbursed approximately $0.54 for every dollar of Medicaid expenditure.

The enhanced FMAP of 6.2 percentage points provided under the Families First Coronavirus Response Act (FFCRA) expired effective December 31, 2023, resulting in an estimated $800 million reduction in federal Medicaid reimbursements in the second half of FY 2023–24.

SECTION 6: FIVE-YEAR OUTLOOK AND STRUCTURAL CONSIDERATIONS

The Independent Fiscal Office (IFO) projects general fund expenditure growth of approximately 4.2 percent annually through FY 2028–29, driven primarily by Medicaid cost growth, pension obligations, and education funding commitments arising from the William Penn litigation. Under this baseline projection, total general fund expenditures would reach approximately $52.6 billion by FY 2028–29.

Structural budget balance remains a concern. The IFO's October 2024 Revenue and Economic Outlook estimates a structural deficit of $3.2 billion emerging by FY 2026–27 if current spending trajectories continue without corresponding revenue growth. The Commonwealth's Budget Stabilization Reserve Fund (Rainy Day Fund) held a balance of $6.1 billion as of June 30, 2024, providing approximately 14.3 percent of annual general fund expenditures as a reserve cushion.

CONCLUSION

The five-year expenditure trend analysis demonstrates sustained growth in Commonwealth spending, with health and human services and education representing the primary cost drivers. The 30.9 percent cumulative increase in general fund expenditures from FY 2018–19 to FY 2023–24 reflects both deliberate policy investments and structural cost pressures. The Bureau of Financial Management will continue to monitor these trends and publish quarterly updates through the Commonwealth's Financial Reporting System (CFRS) portal at www.budget.pa.gov.
