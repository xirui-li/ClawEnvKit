Statistical Distribution Fitting Analysis of State Revenue Streams
Fiscal Years 2019–2023

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This technical report presents the results of a comprehensive statistical distribution fitting analysis applied to five major Pennsylvania state revenue categories over the fiscal years 2019 through 2023. The analysis was undertaken to improve the accuracy of short-term revenue forecasting models used by the Office of the Budget and to quantify uncertainty bounds around quarterly revenue projections. Using maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) and the Kolmogorov-Smirnov goodness-of-fit test, analysts evaluated twelve candidate probability distributions against observed monthly revenue data. The findings indicate that Personal Income Tax (PIT) receipts are best modeled by a log-normal distribution, while Corporate Net Income Tax (CNIT) receipts exhibit characteristics consistent with a generalized Pareto distribution. These results have direct implications for the $45.3 billion general fund revenue forecast for fiscal year 2024–2025.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

1. INTRODUCTION AND MOTIVATION

Pennsylvania's General Fund collected $44.1 billion in total revenue during fiscal year 2022–2023, representing a 3.2 percent decline from the record $45.6 billion collected in fiscal year 2021–2022. The volatility observed across revenue categories—particularly the 11.4 percent year-over-year drop in CNIT receipts from $6.1 billion to $5.4 billion—underscored the need for more rigorous probabilistic modeling of revenue streams.

Traditional point-estimate forecasting methods employed by the Commonwealth prior to fiscal year 2020 did not adequately capture tail risks or asymmetric distributions in revenue data. The COVID-19 fiscal shock of fiscal year 2019–2020, during which total General Fund revenues fell to $35.7 billion (a 14.8 percent contraction from the prior year's $41.9 billion), demonstrated the operational and budgetary consequences of underestimating downside risk.

This report responds to a directive issued by Secretary of the Budget Uri Monson on September 6, 2023, requesting that the Division of Quantitative Methods develop distribution-based confidence intervals for all major revenue categories beginning with the fiscal year 2024–2025 budget cycle.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

2. DATA AND METHODOLOGY

2.1 Data Sources

Monthly revenue collection data were obtained from the Pennsylvania Treasury Department's Revenue Accounting System (RAS) for the period July 2018 through June 2023, yielding 60 monthly observations per revenue category. Data were adjusted for one-time transfers and extraordinary items, including the $1.87 billion federal CARES Act transfer recorded in May 2020 and a $312 million tax amnesty program receipt recorded in November 2021.

The five revenue categories analyzed are:

1. Personal Income Tax (PIT)
2. Corporate Net Income Tax (CNIT)
3. Sales and Use Tax (SUT)
4. Realty Transfer Tax (RTT)
5. Inheritance and Estate Tax (IET)

2.2 Candidate Distributions

Twelve probability distributions were evaluated for each revenue category:

Normal, Log-Normal, Gamma, Weibull, Beta, Exponential, Generalized Pareto, Gumbel, Frechet, Logistic, Log-Logistic, and Burr Type XII.

2.3 Estimation and Testing

Parameters were estimated using maximum likelihood estimation implemented in R version 4.3.1 using the fitdistrplus package (version 1.1-11). Goodness-of-fit was assessed using three criteria:

- Kolmogorov-Smirnov (KS) test statistic and associated p-value
- Akaike Information Criterion (AIC)
- Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC)

A distribution was accepted as the best fit if it produced the lowest AIC score and a KS test p-value greater than 0.05.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

3. RESULTS BY REVENUE CATEGORY

3.1 Personal Income Tax

PIT is the Commonwealth's largest revenue source, averaging $14.8 billion annually over the study period. Monthly PIT receipts ranged from a low of $742 million (April 2020) to a high of $2.31 billion (April 2022), reflecting strong seasonality tied to the April filing deadline.

The log-normal distribution provided the best fit with estimated parameters mu = 13.97 and sigma = 0.41. The KS test statistic was 0.089 with a p-value of 0.712, indicating no statistically significant departure from the fitted distribution. The AIC score of 1,204.3 was 38.7 points lower than the next best candidate (Gamma, AIC = 1,243.0).

Implication: The 5th percentile of the fitted distribution corresponds to a monthly PIT receipt of approximately $891 million, while the 95th percentile corresponds to $2.18 billion. These bounds will be incorporated into the fiscal year 2024–2025 revenue risk report.

3.2 Corporate Net Income Tax

CNIT exhibited the highest coefficient of variation (CV = 0.68) among all five categories, reflecting its sensitivity to corporate profitability cycles and federal tax policy changes. Monthly receipts ranged from $98 million (August 2020) to $891 million (March 2022).

The generalized Pareto distribution provided the best fit with shape parameter xi = 0.23, scale parameter sigma = 187.4, and location parameter mu = 102.6. The KS test statistic was 0.104 with a p-value of 0.531. The AIC score was 876.2, compared to 901.5 for the log-normal distribution.

The positive shape parameter (xi > 0) confirms heavy-tailed behavior, meaning extreme monthly CNIT receipts—both high and low—occur more frequently than a normal distribution would predict. This finding supports maintaining a CNIT revenue reserve of at least $340 million, equivalent to approximately 6.3 percent of the annual CNIT forecast.

3.3 Sales and Use Tax

SUT is the second-largest revenue category, averaging $11.2 billion annually. Monthly receipts showed less volatility (CV = 0.19) than PIT or CNIT, reflecting the relative stability of consumer spending.

The Gamma distribution provided the best fit with shape parameter alpha = 27.4 and rate parameter beta = 0.029. The KS test statistic was 0.071 with a p-value of 0.894. The AIC score of 1,187.6 was the lowest among all candidate distributions for this category.

3.4 Realty Transfer Tax

RTT demonstrated pronounced sensitivity to housing market conditions. Monthly receipts peaked at $143 million in June 2021 during the pandemic-era housing boom and fell to $52 million in December 2022 as mortgage rates rose above 7 percent.

The log-logistic distribution provided the best fit with scale parameter alpha = 89.3 and shape parameter beta = 4.17. The KS test statistic was 0.118 with a p-value of 0.362. Annual RTT collections averaged $1.04 billion over the study period.

3.5 Inheritance and Estate Tax

IET is the smallest of the five categories analyzed, averaging $1.1 billion annually. Monthly receipts ranged from $61 million to $134 million. The Weibull distribution provided the best fit with shape parameter k = 4.82 and scale parameter lambda = 98.7. The KS test statistic was 0.096 with a p-value of 0.648.

5. FORECAST IMPLICATIONS FOR FISCAL YEAR 2024–2025

Based on the fitted distributions and current economic projections from the Pennsylvania Independent Fiscal Office (IFO), the Division of Quantitative Methods has constructed the following probabilistic revenue forecast for fiscal year 2024–2025:

- Central estimate (50th percentile): $45.3 billion
- Optimistic scenario (75th percentile): $47.1 billion
- Pessimistic scenario (25th percentile): $43.6 billion
- Severe downside scenario (5th percentile): $40.8 billion

The $4.5 billion spread between the 5th and 95th percentile outcomes ($40.8 billion to $50.1 billion) represents the full uncertainty range that budget planners should consider when setting reserve fund targets. The IFO's February 2024 baseline forecast of $45.6 billion falls within the 55th percentile of our modeled distribution, suggesting a modest upside bias in the official forecast.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

6. LIMITATIONS AND FUTURE WORK

This analysis is subject to several limitations. First, 60 monthly observations represent a relatively small sample for fitting heavy-tailed distributions such as the generalized Pareto. Future iterations of this analysis should incorporate data back to fiscal year 2009–2010, which would provide 168 monthly observations and improve parameter stability.

Second, the analysis treats each revenue category as independent. In practice, PIT and SUT receipts exhibit a Pearson correlation coefficient of 0.74 over the study period, and a multivariate copula framework would better capture joint tail behavior. The Division plans to implement a Gaussian copula model for the fiscal year 2025–2026 budget cycle.

Third, structural breaks associated with the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017 and the Pennsylvania corporate net income tax rate reduction from 9.99 percent to 8.99 percent (effective January 1, 2023) may affect the stationarity assumptions underlying the fitted distributions.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

7. CONCLUSIONS

This report demonstrates that Pennsylvania's major revenue streams are best described by asymmetric, non-normal probability distributions. The log-normal distribution for PIT, generalized Pareto for CNIT, Gamma for SUT, log-logistic for RTT, and Weibull for IET collectively provide a more accurate characterization of revenue risk than conventional normal-distribution assumptions. Implementation of these models in the fiscal year 2024–2025 budget process will enable the Office of the Budget to communicate revenue uncertainty more transparently to the Governor's Office and the General Assembly.
