EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents the methodology, calculations, and findings related to changes in U.S. Treasury bond yields during the third quarter of fiscal year 2024. The analysis covers yield movements across the primary maturity spectrum, examines the drivers of those movements, and quantifies the estimated impact on federal borrowing costs. The report is prepared in accordance with the Federal Credit Reform Act of 1990 and Treasury Directive 31 CFR Part 306, and is intended for use by the Office of Management and Budget, the Congressional Budget Office, and relevant oversight committees.

During the quarter ending June 30, 2024, the benchmark 10-year Treasury note yield rose from 4.20 percent on April 1 to 4.36 percent on June 28, representing an increase of 16 basis points. The 2-year Treasury note yield declined from 4.62 percent to 4.71 percent before retreating to 4.47 percent by quarter close, a net decrease of 15 basis points from the opening rate. These movements reflect ongoing Federal Reserve policy uncertainty, persistent core inflation readings, and elevated net Treasury issuance totaling $847 billion during the quarter.

SECTION 1: METHODOLOGY FOR BOND YIELD CHANGE CALCULATION

1.1 Data Sources and Reference Rates

All yield data referenced in this report are drawn from the H.15 Selected Interest Rates statistical release published daily by the Federal Reserve Board of Governors. Constant maturity yields are used as the standard reference, calculated by the Treasury using the daily yield curve derived from actively traded securities with remaining maturities of 1 month, 3 months, 6 months, 1 year, 2 years, 3 years, 5 years, 7 years, 10 years, 20 years, and 30 years.

The primary analyst of record for this report is Dr. Melissa Hartwell, Senior Economist, Office of Debt Management, who may be contacted at (202) 622-2960.

1.2 Basis Point Calculation

Yield changes are expressed in basis points (bps), where 1 basis point equals 0.01 percentage points. The standard formula applied is:

Yield Change (bps) = (Yield_End – Yield_Start) × 100

For example, the 10-year yield change from April 1 (4.20%) to June 28 (4.36%) is calculated as:

(4.36 – 4.20) × 100 = +16 basis points

1.3 Duration-Weighted Impact on Borrowing Costs

To translate yield changes into estimated dollar cost impacts, the Office of Debt Management applies a modified duration model. For each maturity bucket, the formula is:

Dollar Impact = Face Value × Modified Duration × Yield Change (in decimal form)

For the 10-year note with $312 billion in outstanding issuance during the quarter and a modified duration of approximately 8.7 years:

Dollar Impact = $312,000,000,000 × 8.7 × 0.0016 = approximately $4.34 billion in additional present-value cost

This calculation is performed across all maturity buckets and aggregated to produce the total estimated borrowing cost impact.

Key Observation: The yield curve exhibited a notable steepening dynamic during Q3 FY2024. Short-term yields declined across all maturities of 5 years and under, while yields on maturities of 7 years and above increased. This bear steepening pattern — where long-term yields rise faster than short-term yields fall — is consistent with market expectations of delayed Federal Reserve rate cuts combined with concerns about long-term fiscal sustainability.

SECTION 3: DRIVERS OF YIELD CHANGES

3.1 Federal Reserve Policy Signals

The Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC) maintained the federal funds target rate at 5.25 to 5.50 percent throughout the entire quarter, a level unchanged since July 26, 2023. At the June 12, 2024 FOMC meeting, the Committee revised its median projection for year-end 2024 rate cuts from three reductions (as projected in March 2024) to one reduction, citing core Personal Consumption Expenditures (PCE) inflation of 2.8 percent recorded in April 2024. This revision contributed significantly to the upward pressure on longer-dated yields.

3.2 Treasury Issuance Volume

Net marketable borrowing during Q3 FY2024 totaled $847 billion, compared to $657 billion in Q3 FY2023, representing a year-over-year increase of 29.1 percent. The Treasury Borrowing Advisory Committee (TBAC), chaired by Meghan Graper of Barclays Capital, noted in its May 1, 2024 report that elevated issuance at longer maturities was placing upward pressure on term premiums. The 10-year term premium, as estimated by the Adrian-Crump-Moench (ACM) model maintained by the Federal Reserve Bank of New York, rose from -0.04 percent on April 1 to +0.22 percent on June 28, an increase of 26 basis points.

3.3 Inflation Data

The Bureau of Labor Statistics reported that the Consumer Price Index (CPI) for All Urban Consumers rose 3.4 percent year-over-year in April 2024 and 3.3 percent in May 2024. Core CPI, excluding food and energy, registered 3.6 percent in April and 3.4 percent in May. These readings, while declining from the 2022 peak of 9.1 percent, remained above the Federal Reserve's 2.0 percent target and reinforced market expectations that rate cuts would be delayed, supporting higher long-term yields.

3.4 International Demand Factors

Foreign and international accounts held $8.1 trillion in U.S. Treasury securities as of May 31, 2024, according to the Treasury International Capital (TIC) system. Japan remained the largest foreign holder at $1.128 trillion, followed by China at $768.3 billion. Net foreign purchases of long-term Treasuries totaled $62.4 billion in April 2024, providing partial offset to domestic supply pressures.

SECTION 5: COMPARISON TO PRIOR QUARTERS

The net additional borrowing cost of $9.03 billion in Q3 FY2024 compares to a net cost savings of $3.17 billion in Q2 FY2024 (January 1 – March 31, 2024), when yields across most maturities declined amid expectations of imminent Federal Reserve rate reductions. The reversal of $12.2 billion between quarters represents one of the largest quarter-over-quarter swings in estimated yield-driven borrowing costs since Q4 FY2022, when the Federal Reserve's aggressive rate hiking cycle produced an estimated $18.6 billion increase in quarterly borrowing costs.

On a fiscal year-to-date basis (October 1, 2023 through June 30, 2024), the cumulative yield-driven impact on borrowing costs is estimated at +$6.84 billion, reflecting the net effect of declining short-term yields and rising long-term yields across the portfolio.

SECTION 6: OUTLOOK AND RISK FACTORS

The Office of Debt Management projects that if the FOMC executes one 25-basis-point rate reduction in September 2024 as reflected in the June 2024 Summary of Economic Projections, the 2-year yield could decline to approximately 4.20 percent by September 30, 2024. The 10-year yield trajectory is less certain, with the primary risk being continued upward pressure from elevated issuance and persistent term premium normalization.

The Congressional Budget Office's June 2024 Budget and Economic Outlook projects net interest costs of $892 billion for fiscal year 2024, representing 3.4 percent of GDP and exceeding defense discretionary spending of $874 billion for the first time in modern budget history. A sustained 10-basis-point increase in long-term yields above the CBO baseline would add approximately $19 billion to net interest costs over the subsequent 10-year budget window.
