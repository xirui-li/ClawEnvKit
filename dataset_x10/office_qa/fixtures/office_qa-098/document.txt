EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Total federal tax revenues for Fiscal Year 2023 reached $4.44 trillion, representing a decline of 9.3 percent compared to the $4.90 trillion collected in Fiscal Year 2022. This contraction marks the first year-over-year revenue decrease since Fiscal Year 2020 and reflects the expiration of pandemic-era tax provisions, a cooling labor market in the second half of the fiscal year, and reduced capital gains realizations following the equity market correction of calendar year 2022. Despite the overall decline, individual income tax receipts remained the largest single revenue source, accounting for 49.4 percent of total collections. This report, prepared under the direction of Deputy Assistant Secretary for Tax Analysis Margaret L. Forsythe, provides a comprehensive breakdown of revenue by category, comparative trend analysis, and projections for Fiscal Year 2024.

SECTION 1: TOTAL REVENUE OVERVIEW

Federal tax receipts for FY2023 totaled $4.439 trillion, down from $4.896 trillion in FY2022. The $457 billion shortfall was the largest single-year nominal decline in federal revenue since records were standardized under the Budget Enforcement Act of 1990. The decline was anticipated in the Mid-Session Review published July 14, 2023, which projected revenues of $4.47 trillion, placing actual collections approximately $31 billion below that revised estimate.

Revenue as a share of Gross Domestic Product fell to 16.5 percent in FY2023, compared to 19.6 percent in FY2022 and a 50-year historical average of approximately 17.4 percent. The GDP figure used for this calculation is the Bureau of Economic Analysis advance estimate of $26.9 trillion for calendar year 2023.

2.1 Individual Income Taxes

The $456 billion decline in individual income tax receipts is the primary driver of the overall revenue shortfall. Three factors account for the majority of this decrease:

- Capital gains realizations fell sharply. The S&P 500 declined 19.4 percent in calendar year 2022, reducing taxable gains reported on FY2023 returns. The IRS Statistics of Income Division estimates capital gains-related tax liability fell by approximately $180 billion compared to the prior year.

- Bonus and stock compensation declined. Technology sector layoffs beginning in November 2022 and continuing through March 2023 reduced withholding from supplemental wage payments. Companies including Meta, Amazon, and Alphabet collectively eliminated approximately 50,000 positions during this period, with associated compensation reductions affecting withholding collections from January through April 2023.

- Refund volumes increased. Total individual refunds issued through September 30, 2023 reached $334.2 billion, up 14.7 percent from $291.4 billion in FY2022, partly reflecting delayed processing of FY2021 amended returns related to unemployment compensation exclusions under the American Rescue Plan Act.

2.2 Payroll Taxes

Payroll tax collections of $1.6138 trillion represent the strongest performance among major revenue categories, growing 8.8 percent year-over-year. This growth reflects continued wage inflation, with average hourly earnings rising 4.3 percent over the fiscal year according to Bureau of Labor Statistics data. The Social Security wage base increased from $147,000 in calendar year 2022 to $160,200 in calendar year 2023, expanding the taxable earnings pool for higher-wage workers. Medicare's Hospital Insurance tax, which has no wage cap, collected an estimated $374 billion, up from $341 billion in FY2022.

2.3 Corporate Income Taxes

Corporate receipts declined modestly by $5.2 billion, or 1.2 percent, to $419.7 billion. The relative stability reflects the lagged nature of corporate estimated payments, which are based on prior-year liability. The new 15 percent Corporate Alternative Minimum Tax enacted under the Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 contributed an estimated $4.8 billion in its first full fiscal year of collections, partially offsetting declines in regular corporate tax payments from sectors experiencing profit compression, particularly retail and residential real estate.

2.4 Excise Taxes

Excise tax collections grew modestly to $92.0 billion. Highway-related excise taxes, including the 18.4 cents-per-gallon federal gasoline tax, generated $36.1 billion. Tobacco excise taxes continued their long-term structural decline, falling to $11.8 billion from $12.4 billion in FY2022, consistent with a 3.2 percent reduction in taxable cigarette sales volume.

2.5 Estate and Gift Taxes

Estate and gift tax receipts fell 25.6 percent to $34.0 billion, the lowest level since FY2019. The decline reflects both the elevated unified credit amount ($12.92 million per individual in calendar year 2023) and a reduction in large taxable estates attributable to asset value declines in the 2022 market correction. The number of taxable estate returns filed during FY2023 is estimated at 6,400, compared to approximately 8,200 in FY2022.

SECTION 3: MONTHLY REVENUE TRENDS

Monthly collections during FY2023 showed significant variation. April 2023, the primary individual income tax filing month, generated $638.5 billion in gross receipts, the highest single-month total of the fiscal year, though net collections after refunds were substantially lower. The weakest month was August 2023, with net receipts of $211.3 billion, reflecting the seasonal trough in estimated tax payments and reduced withholding during summer months.

The first quarter of FY2023 (October–December 2022) produced $1.12 trillion, representing 25.2 percent of annual collections. The second quarter (January–March 2023) generated $1.09 trillion, or 24.6 percent of the annual total, with March 2023 showing particular weakness as corporate estimated payments declined 11.4 percent compared to March 2022.

SECTION 4: FIVE-YEAR TREND ANALYSIS

Federal tax revenues have grown substantially over the past five fiscal years, though FY2023 represents a correction from the exceptional FY2022 peak:

FY2019: $3.462 trillion (16.3% of GDP)
FY2020: $3.420 trillion (16.3% of GDP)
FY2021: $4.047 trillion (17.8% of GDP)
FY2022: $4.896 trillion (19.6% of GDP)
FY2023: $4.439 trillion (16.5% of GDP)

The compound annual growth rate from FY2019 to FY2023 is 6.4 percent, exceeding nominal GDP growth of approximately 5.1 percent over the same period. The FY2021 surge reflects the economic recovery from the COVID-19 recession and enhanced business activity. The FY2022 peak was driven by extraordinary capital gains realizations and elevated corporate profits. The FY2023 normalization brings revenue closer to the long-term trend line.

SECTION 5: FISCAL YEAR 2024 PROJECTIONS

Based on current economic conditions and existing tax law, the Office of Fiscal Analysis projects FY2024 federal tax revenues of $4.71 trillion, representing a recovery of 6.1 percent from FY2023. Key assumptions underlying this projection include:

- Real GDP growth of 1.5 percent for calendar year 2024
- Average unemployment rate of 4.2 percent
- Wage growth moderating to 3.1 percent
- S&P 500 index appreciation of approximately 8 percent in calendar year 2023 generating modestly higher capital gains realizations in FY2024 filings
- No significant changes to the Internal Revenue Code

The Social Security wage base for calendar year 2024 has been set at $168,600, an increase of $8,400 or 5.2 percent from 2023, which will contribute approximately $12 billion in additional payroll tax revenue.

The projected revenue figure does not account for potential legislative changes related to expiring provisions of the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017, several of which are scheduled to sunset after December 31, 2025. The Congressional Budget Office has estimated that full extension of those provisions would reduce revenues by approximately $3.5 trillion over the FY2026–FY2035 window.
