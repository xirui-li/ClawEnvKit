EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents a comprehensive analysis of federal government expenditure trends across the five-year period from fiscal year 2019 through fiscal year 2024. Total federal outlays grew from $4.45 trillion in FY2019 to $6.13 trillion in FY2024, representing a cumulative increase of 37.8 percent over the review period. The COVID-19 pandemic produced a pronounced spike in FY2020 and FY2021, with emergency relief spending accounting for the largest single-year deviation from the baseline trend. Mandatory spending programs continued to dominate the federal budget, comprising 63.4 percent of total outlays in FY2024. This report identifies key drivers of expenditure growth, examines departmental performance against appropriated budgets, and provides projections through FY2026.

SECTION 1: TOTAL FEDERAL OUTLAYS, FY2019–FY2024

The following table summarizes total federal outlays for each fiscal year under review, alongside the year-over-year percentage change and the deficit or surplus recorded.

Fiscal Year | Total Outlays | YoY Change | Deficit/(Surplus)
FY2019      | $4.45 trillion | +8.2%     | ($984 billion)
FY2020      | $6.55 trillion | +47.2%    | ($3.13 trillion)
FY2021      | $6.82 trillion | +4.1%     | ($2.77 trillion)
FY2022      | $6.27 trillion | -8.1%     | ($1.38 trillion)
FY2023      | $6.13 trillion | -2.2%     | ($1.70 trillion)
FY2024      | $6.13 trillion | +0.0%     | ($1.83 trillion)

Note: FY2024 figures are based on preliminary actuals as of January 31, 2024, with final reconciliation expected by May 2024.

The extraordinary jump of 47.2 percent in FY2020 reflects the enactment of the Coronavirus Aid, Relief, and Economic Security (CARES) Act on March 27, 2020, which authorized $2.2 trillion in emergency spending. Subsequent legislation, including the American Rescue Plan Act signed on March 11, 2021, contributed to the elevated FY2021 baseline.

SECTION 2: EXPENDITURE BY MAJOR CATEGORY

2.1 Mandatory Spending

Mandatory spending—which includes Social Security, Medicare, Medicaid, and other entitlement programs—reached $3.89 trillion in FY2024, up from $2.73 trillion in FY2019. This represents a 42.5 percent increase over five years.

Social Security outlays alone totaled $1.35 trillion in FY2024, driven by a 3.2 percent cost-of-living adjustment (COLA) applied in January 2024 and a beneficiary population that grew to 71.6 million recipients as of December 2023. Medicare expenditures reached $1.02 trillion in FY2024, reflecting both enrollment growth and a 4.1 percent increase in per-beneficiary costs attributed to pharmaceutical pricing and post-pandemic utilization recovery.

Medicaid spending stood at $616 billion in FY2024. The unwinding of continuous enrollment provisions, which began April 1, 2023 under the Consolidated Appropriations Act of 2023, resulted in approximately 14.2 million individuals being disenrolled by December 2023, moderating what had been projected as a $648 billion expenditure.

2.2 Discretionary Spending

Total discretionary spending in FY2024 was $1.70 trillion, divided between defense and non-defense categories.

Defense discretionary spending: $886 billion (52.1% of discretionary total)
Non-defense discretionary spending: $814 billion (47.9% of discretionary total)

The Department of Defense received $858 billion in base appropriations for FY2024 under the National Defense Authorization Act signed December 22, 2023, with an additional $28 billion in supplemental appropriations primarily directed toward Ukraine Security Assistance and Indo-Pacific deterrence initiatives.

The Department of Education received $79.6 billion in discretionary appropriations, while the Department of Transportation received $27.3 billion, supplemented by $89 billion in mandatory infrastructure funding flowing from the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act of 2021.

2.3 Net Interest

Net interest payments on the federal debt reached $659 billion in FY2024, the fastest-growing expenditure category over the review period. In FY2019, net interest stood at $375 billion. The 75.7 percent increase over five years reflects both the accumulation of pandemic-era debt and the Federal Reserve's rate-hiking cycle, which began in March 2022 and brought the federal funds rate to a target range of 5.25–5.50 percent by July 2023. The average interest rate on outstanding federal debt rose from 2.4 percent in FY2022 to 3.1 percent in FY2024.

SECTION 3: DEPARTMENTAL EXPENDITURE PERFORMANCE

The following departments recorded the most significant variances between enacted appropriations and actual expenditures in FY2023 (the most recent year with fully audited figures):

Department of Health and Human Services
Enacted appropriation: $1.72 trillion (mandatory + discretionary)
Actual expenditure: $1.69 trillion
Variance: -$30 billion (-1.7%)
Primary driver of underspend: Lower-than-projected Medicaid enrollment following continuous enrollment unwind.

Department of Veterans Affairs
Enacted appropriation: $301.4 billion
Actual expenditure: $308.9 billion
Variance: +$7.5 billion (+2.5%)
Primary driver of overspend: PACT Act implementation costs, including expanded toxic exposure screenings for 3.5 million veterans, exceeded initial estimates by $6.2 billion.

Department of Transportation
Enacted appropriation: $105.7 billion
Actual expenditure: $98.3 billion
Variance: -$7.4 billion (-7.0%)
Primary driver of underspend: Delays in obligating Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act formula grants due to state-level planning and environmental review timelines.

SECTION 4: EXPENDITURE AS A PERCENTAGE OF GDP

Federal expenditures as a share of gross domestic product provide a normalized measure of fiscal scale. The following figures use Bureau of Economic Analysis GDP estimates.

FY2019: $4.45T outlays / $21.43T GDP = 20.8% of GDP
FY2020: $6.55T outlays / $21.06T GDP = 31.1% of GDP
FY2021: $6.82T outlays / $23.32T GDP = 29.3% of GDP
FY2022: $6.27T outlays / $25.72T GDP = 24.4% of GDP
FY2023: $6.13T outlays / $27.36T GDP = 22.4% of GDP
FY2024: $6.13T outlays / $28.18T GDP (est.) = 21.7% of GDP

The FY2020 peak of 31.1 percent of GDP represents the highest federal expenditure ratio recorded since World War II. The post-pandemic normalization trend is evident, though the FY2024 ratio of 21.7 percent remains above the 40-year historical average of 20.4 percent.

SECTION 5: PROJECTIONS FOR FY2025–FY2026

Based on current law, enacted appropriations, and Congressional Budget Office baseline projections released February 7, 2024, the Department of Finance projects the following:

FY2025 projected outlays: $6.49 trillion
Projected deficit: $1.97 trillion
Key drivers: Social Security COLA of 3.2% effective January 2025; Medicare Part D redesign under the Inflation Reduction Act capping out-of-pocket costs at $2,000 annually beginning January 2025; continued elevated net interest costs.

FY2026 projected outlays: $6.72 trillion
Projected deficit: $2.01 trillion
Key drivers: Full-year impact of Medicare drug negotiation provisions; expiration of certain Tax Cuts and Jobs Act provisions scheduled for December 31, 2025, which will affect revenue but not direct expenditures.

SECTION 6: KEY FINDINGS AND OBSERVATIONS

1. Net interest is now the third-largest single expenditure item in the federal budget, surpassing Medicaid for the first time in FY2024.

2. Defense spending as a share of total outlays declined from 15.4 percent in FY2019 to 14.4 percent in FY2024, despite nominal dollar increases, because mandatory and interest spending grew faster.

3. The five-year compound annual growth rate (CAGR) for total federal outlays from FY2019 to FY2024 is 6.6 percent, compared to a nominal GDP CAGR of 5.6 percent over the same period.

4. Emergency supplemental appropriations totaling $4.6 trillion were enacted between March 2020 and March 2021, representing the largest peacetime emergency spending mobilization in U.S. history.

5. Administrative overhead costs across civilian agencies averaged 3.8 percent of departmental budgets in FY2023, down from 4.2 percent in FY2019, reflecting workforce reductions and shared services consolidation.

SECTION 7: METHODOLOGY AND DATA SOURCES

Expenditure data in this report are drawn from the Monthly Treasury Statement published by the Bureau of the Fiscal Service, the President's Budget for Fiscal Year 2025 submitted to Congress on March 11, 2024, and audited financial statements from individual agency Inspectors General. GDP figures are sourced from the Bureau of Economic Analysis National Income and Product Accounts, Third Estimate, released February 28, 2024. All figures are presented in nominal dollars unless otherwise noted. Interagency transfers have been netted to avoid double-counting. Rounding may cause minor discrepancies in totals.
