EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents the Office of Debt Management's analysis of yield spreads across U.S. Treasury securities for the third quarter of fiscal year 2024 (Q3 FY2024), covering the period July 1 through September 30, 2024. The analysis examines the spread relationships between short-term and long-term Treasury instruments, compares current conditions against historical benchmarks, and evaluates the implications for federal borrowing costs and debt issuance strategy.

During Q3 FY2024, the 2-year/10-year Treasury yield spread narrowed from negative 36 basis points at the start of the quarter to negative 14 basis points by September 30, 2024, reflecting market anticipation of Federal Reserve rate reductions. The Federal Open Market Committee's 50-basis-point rate cut on September 18, 2024 — the first reduction since March 2020 — materially influenced spread dynamics throughout the quarter. Total marketable debt outstanding reached $27.4 trillion as of September 30, 2024, with weighted average interest costs rising to 3.32 percent, compared to 2.97 percent in Q3 FY2023.

The most pronounced yield declines occurred in the 1-year to 2-year segment, consistent with historical patterns following the initiation of Federal Reserve easing cycles. The 30-year yield declined by only 41 basis points, reflecting persistent term premium concerns related to long-run fiscal trajectory and elevated debt issuance volumes.

1.2 Key Spread Metrics

The following spread relationships are monitored as primary indicators of market conditions and borrowing cost dynamics:

- 2-Year / 10-Year Spread: Closed the quarter at -14 basis points, compared to -36 basis points on June 28, 2024. The spread has been continuously negative since July 5, 2022, representing 27 consecutive months of inversion as of September 30, 2024.

- 3-Month / 10-Year Spread: Closed at -118 basis points on September 30, 2024, compared to -108 basis points on June 28, 2024. This spread, which the Federal Reserve Bank of New York uses as a recession probability indicator, has remained negative since October 25, 2022.

- 5-Year / 30-Year Spread: Widened to 55 basis points from 17 basis points at quarter open, reflecting a steepening of the long end of the curve as short-term rates fell more rapidly than long-term rates.

- 10-Year / 30-Year Spread: Widened modestly to 34 basis points from 14 basis points, consistent with the broader long-end steepening trend.

SECTION 2: HISTORICAL CONTEXT AND COMPARATIVE ANALYSIS

2.1 Inversion Duration and Depth

The current inversion of the 2-year/10-year spread is among the most prolonged in modern U.S. history. The previous record inversion lasted approximately 22 months, from August 1978 through June 1980. The current episode, which began July 5, 2022, has now exceeded that duration.

At its deepest point, the 2-year/10-year spread reached negative 108 basis points on March 8, 2023, the most extreme inversion since September 1981, when the spread touched negative 121 basis points during the Volcker-era tightening cycle. The average inversion depth during Q3 FY2024 was negative 26 basis points, compared to negative 47 basis points in Q3 FY2023.

SECTION 3: IMPLICATIONS FOR FEDERAL BORROWING COSTS

3.1 Weighted Average Interest Rate on Marketable Debt

As of September 30, 2024, the weighted average interest rate on total outstanding marketable debt was 3.32 percent, representing an increase of 35 basis points from 2.97 percent recorded on September 30, 2023. This figure reflects the ongoing rollover of lower-rate debt issued during the 2020–2021 period into current higher-rate instruments.

The Congressional Budget Office projects that net interest costs will reach $892 billion in fiscal year 2024, representing 3.1 percent of gross domestic product. This compares to $659 billion in net interest costs recorded in fiscal year 2023, an increase of $233 billion or approximately 35.4 percent year-over-year.

3.2 Issuance Strategy and Maturity Profile

The Treasury's issuance strategy during Q3 FY2024 continued to emphasize bills and shorter-dated notes, consistent with guidance from the Quarterly Refunding Announcement issued May 1, 2024. Key issuance statistics for the quarter include:

- Total gross issuance of marketable securities: $7.2 trillion
- Treasury bills (maturities up to 52 weeks): $5.1 trillion, representing 70.8 percent of gross issuance
- Treasury notes (2-year through 10-year): $1.7 trillion
- Treasury bonds (20-year and 30-year): $156 billion
- Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS): $89 billion

The weighted average maturity of newly issued debt during Q3 FY2024 was 2.4 years, compared to a weighted average maturity of 6.1 years for the total outstanding portfolio. This gap reflects the continued concentration of issuance at the short end of the curve, a strategy that reduces near-term coupon costs but increases rollover risk.

3.3 Sensitivity Analysis: Impact of Spread Normalization

The Office of Debt Management conducted sensitivity analysis examining the fiscal impact of a full yield curve normalization scenario, defined as the 2-year/10-year spread returning to its 20-year historical average of positive 93 basis points by December 31, 2025.

Under this scenario, assuming the 10-year yield stabilizes near 4.00 percent and the 2-year yield declines to approximately 3.07 percent:

- Annual interest savings on newly issued 2-year notes would be approximately $18.4 billion relative to Q3 FY2024 issuance rates
- The weighted average interest rate on total marketable debt would decline to an estimated 3.18 percent by September 30, 2025
- Net interest costs as a share of GDP would remain above 3.0 percent through fiscal year 2026 under all modeled scenarios, given the volume of debt requiring rollover

SECTION 4: CREDIT SPREAD MONITORING — AGENCY AND MUNICIPAL COMPARISONS

4.1 Agency Spreads to Treasuries

The Office of Debt Management monitors spreads between Treasury securities and obligations of government-sponsored enterprises as an indicator of broader credit market conditions. As of September 30, 2024:

- Fannie Mae 10-year benchmark notes traded at a spread of 62 basis points over the 10-year Treasury, compared to 71 basis points on June 28, 2024
- Freddie Mac 5-year reference notes traded at 54 basis points over the 5-year Treasury, compared to 61 basis points at quarter open
- Federal Home Loan Bank 2-year bonds traded at 28 basis points over the 2-year Treasury

The tightening of agency spreads during the quarter reflects improved risk appetite following the Federal Reserve's September 18 rate cut and reduced uncertainty regarding the near-term policy path.

4.2 Municipal Bond Spread Observations

The ratio of 10-year AAA-rated municipal bond yields to 10-year Treasury yields, a standard measure of relative value in the municipal market, stood at 67 percent as of September 30, 2024. This compares to 72 percent on June 28, 2024, and the 10-year historical average ratio of 84 percent. The below-average ratio indicates that municipal bonds are relatively expensive compared to Treasuries on an after-tax-equivalent basis for investors in the 37 percent federal income tax bracket.

SECTION 5: OUTLOOK AND RISK FACTORS

The Office of Debt Management's baseline projection for Q4 FY2024 (October 1 – December 31, 2024) anticipates continued gradual normalization of the yield curve, with the 2-year/10-year spread expected to reach positive territory by late November 2024 if the Federal Reserve proceeds with an additional 25-basis-point rate reduction at its November 6–7, 2024 meeting, as implied by federal funds futures pricing as of the report date.

Key risk factors that could alter this trajectory include:

- Upside inflation surprises that delay further Federal Reserve easing
- Elevated Treasury supply requirements driven by fiscal deficits projected at $1.833 trillion for fiscal year 2024
- Geopolitical developments affecting global demand for U.S. Treasury securities as a safe-haven asset
- Potential disruptions to foreign official sector demand, which represented 23.4 percent of total Treasury holdings as of August 31, 2024
