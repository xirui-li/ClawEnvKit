EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Internal Revenue Service, in coordination with the Office of Management and Budget and the Bureau of Fiscal Service, presents this comprehensive analysis of federal tax revenue collected during Fiscal Year 2023 (October 1, 2022 through September 30, 2023). Total federal tax revenue for FY2023 reached $4.44 trillion, representing a decline of 9.3 percent from the FY2022 record of $4.90 trillion. This report examines revenue composition, year-over-year trends, compliance metrics, and distributional data to inform legislative and administrative decision-making.

This analysis was prepared under the direction of IRS Commissioner Daniel Werfel, who assumed leadership of the agency on April 4, 2023, following Senate confirmation. The findings herein draw upon data from the Statistics of Income Division, the Office of Research, Applied Analytics and Statistics, and the Financial Management Information System maintained by the Bureau of Fiscal Service.

SECTION 1: TOTAL REVENUE OVERVIEW

Federal tax receipts for FY2023 totaled $4.44 trillion, down from $4.90 trillion in FY2022. This $460 billion reduction represents the largest single-year nominal decline in federal tax revenue since FY2009, when receipts fell by approximately $420 billion during the financial crisis. The decline is attributable primarily to reduced individual income tax collections following the expiration of certain pandemic-era capital gains realizations and a normalization of financial market conditions after the exceptional equity performance of 2021.

As a share of Gross Domestic Product, federal tax revenue stood at 16.5 percent in FY2023, compared to 19.6 percent in FY2022 and a 50-year historical average of approximately 17.4 percent.

SECTION 2: INDIVIDUAL INCOME TAX ANALYSIS

Individual income taxes remain the single largest source of federal revenue, accounting for 49.1 percent of total receipts in FY2023. The $450 billion year-over-year decline in this category warrants detailed examination.

Key Contributing Factors:

- Capital gains realizations declined sharply. In calendar year 2021, taxpayers reported an estimated $1.87 trillion in net capital gains, driven by equity market appreciation and elevated asset sales. In calendar year 2022, net capital gains fell to approximately $1.10 trillion, a reduction of roughly 41 percent, as the S&P 500 declined 19.4 percent during that year. Tax payments on 2022 gains flowed primarily into FY2023 collections.

- Withholding receipts, by contrast, grew modestly. Wage and salary withholding collections increased 3.2 percent to approximately $1.47 trillion, reflecting continued labor market strength. The Bureau of Labor Statistics reported average unemployment of 3.6 percent for calendar year 2023, and total private-sector wages and salaries grew 5.1 percent year-over-year.

- Estimated tax payments from self-employed individuals and investors declined 22.4 percent, consistent with reduced investment income and business income normalization following the post-pandemic surge.

- The average effective individual income tax rate for all filers was 13.7 percent in tax year 2022 (the most recent year for which complete data is available), compared to 14.9 percent in tax year 2021.

Returns Filed and Processing Statistics (FY2023):

- Total individual returns filed: 162.0 million
- Returns filed electronically: 152.6 million (94.2 percent)
- Returns claiming refunds: 107.3 million
- Average refund amount: $2,753
- Total refunds issued: $295.3 billion
- Returns filed using paid preparers: approximately 79.3 million (48.9 percent)

SECTION 3: PAYROLL TAX REVENUE

Payroll taxes, comprising Federal Insurance Contributions Act (FICA) taxes for Social Security and Medicare as well as Self-Employment Contributions Act (SECA) taxes, generated $1.61 trillion in FY2023, an increase of $130 billion or 8.8 percent over FY2022. This growth rate outpaced nominal GDP growth of approximately 6.3 percent for the same period.

The Social Security taxable wage base increased from $147,000 in calendar year 2022 to $160,200 in calendar year 2023, a 9.0 percent increase reflecting the Social Security Administration's cost-of-living adjustment methodology. This expansion of the taxable base contributed an estimated $18 billion in additional collections.

Medicare payroll taxes, which carry no wage cap, grew in line with overall wage growth. The additional 0.9 percent Medicare surtax on wages above $200,000 (individual) and $250,000 (married filing jointly), enacted under the Affordable Care Act in 2013, generated approximately $14.2 billion in FY2023.

SECTION 4: CORPORATE INCOME TAX ANALYSIS

Corporate income tax receipts held steady at $0.42 trillion in FY2023, unchanged from FY2022 in nominal terms. This stability is notable given the broader revenue decline and reflects several offsetting dynamics.

The corporate tax rate of 21 percent, established by the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of December 2017, remained in effect throughout FY2023. The Inflation Reduction Act of 2022, signed by President Biden on August 16, 2022, introduced a 15 percent Corporate Alternative Minimum Tax (CAMT) applicable to corporations with average adjusted financial statement income exceeding $1 billion. The CAMT generated an estimated $4.7 billion in its first full fiscal year of application.

Additionally, the Inflation Reduction Act imposed a 1 percent excise tax on corporate stock buybacks effective January 1, 2023. This provision generated approximately $8.6 billion in FY2023, slightly below the Joint Committee on Taxation's initial estimate of $9.0 billion for the first year.

S-corporation and partnership income, which flows through to individual returns rather than corporate returns, is estimated to have contributed approximately $380 billion to individual income tax collections in tax year 2022, based on preliminary Statistics of Income data.

SECTION 5: TAX COMPLIANCE AND ENFORCEMENT

The IRS estimates the gross tax gap — the difference between taxes legally owed and taxes voluntarily and timely paid — at approximately $688 billion per year based on the most recent Tax Gap Study covering tax years 2014 through 2016. Updated estimates incorporating tax years 2017 through 2019 are expected to be released in mid-2024 and are projected to show a gross tax gap exceeding $700 billion annually.

Enforcement revenue in FY2023 totaled $63.6 billion, comprising:

- Examination (audit) assessments and collections: $31.9 billion
- Collection activity on delinquent accounts: $22.4 billion
- Document matching and automated underreporter program: $9.3 billion

The IRS audit rate for individual returns in FY2023 was 0.44 percent, compared to 0.38 percent in FY2022. The increase reflects initial deployment of resources funded under the Inflation Reduction Act's $79.6 billion IRS funding provision, of which approximately $45.6 billion was designated for enforcement activities over a ten-year period.

High-income audit rates (returns reporting income above $1 million) increased from 1.1 percent in FY2022 to 1.4 percent in FY2023. The IRS has publicly committed to reaching an audit rate of 4.7 percent for this income category by FY2026.

SECTION 6: FIVE-YEAR REVENUE TREND

Fiscal Year     Total Revenue     % of GDP     Year-over-Year Change
FY2019          $3.46 trillion    16.3%        +4.0%
FY2020          $3.42 trillion    16.3%        -1.2%
FY2021          $4.05 trillion    17.8%        +18.4%
FY2022          $4.90 trillion    19.6%        +21.0%

The FY2021 and FY2022 surges reflect the extraordinary economic and fiscal conditions of the pandemic recovery period, including elevated asset prices, enhanced business activity, and the recognition of deferred income. The FY2023 normalization was anticipated in the FY2023 Mid-Session Review published by the Office of Management and Budget on July 28, 2023, which projected total receipts of $4.43 trillion — a forecast that proved accurate to within 0.2 percent.

SECTION 7: OUTLOOK FOR FISCAL YEAR 2024

The Congressional Budget Office's February 2024 Budget and Economic Outlook projects FY2024 federal revenues of $4.93 trillion, representing an 11.0 percent increase over FY2023. This projection assumes continued labor market strength, partial recovery in capital gains realizations as equity markets rebounded approximately 24 percent during calendar year 2023, and full-year collection of the corporate stock buyback excise tax and CAMT.

Key risks to the revenue outlook include potential economic slowdown, continued uncertainty in commercial real estate valuations affecting pass-through income, and the scheduled expiration at the end of calendar year 2025 of numerous individual income tax provisions from the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017, which would affect taxpayer behavior and planning in the interim period.
