EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This technical report presents the results of a comprehensive statistical distribution fitting analysis applied to five major Commonwealth revenue streams over the fiscal years 2019 through 2023. The analysis was commissioned following the Governor's Budget Office directive of September 8, 2023, which mandated improved probabilistic forecasting methods for the FY2025 budget cycle. Using maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) and goodness-of-fit testing across seven candidate distributions, the Division of Quantitative Methods identified optimal distributional models for Personal Income Tax, Corporate Net Income Tax, Sales and Use Tax, Inheritance and Estate Tax, and Lottery Fund revenues. These models will directly inform the $45.3 billion revenue forecast submitted to the General Assembly in February 2024.

---

1. INTRODUCTION AND POLICY CONTEXT

Pennsylvania's General Fund receives revenues from 23 distinct tax and fee categories. Historically, the Office of Fiscal Analysis has relied on point estimates and simple linear trend extrapolation for budget projections. This approach produced a mean absolute percentage error (MAPE) of 4.7% across the 2015–2022 period, resulting in cumulative forecast errors totaling approximately $2.1 billion over seven fiscal years.

In response to persistent forecast inaccuracies—most notably the $687 million revenue shortfall recorded in FY2020 and the $1.14 billion surplus in FY2022—the Secretary of Revenue, James Whitfield, authorized the adoption of distributional modeling techniques beginning with the FY2025 planning cycle. The methodology described herein draws on established actuarial and econometric literature, including the approach outlined by Klugman, Panjer, and Willmot (2012) in "Loss Models: From Data to Decisions."

---

2. DATA AND METHODOLOGY

2.1 Data Sources

Monthly revenue collection figures were extracted from the Commonwealth's Integrated Enterprise System (IES) for the period July 1, 2018 through June 30, 2023, yielding 60 monthly observations per revenue category. All figures are expressed in nominal dollars. Seasonal adjustment was applied using the U.S. Census Bureau's X-13ARIMA-SEATS procedure, version 1.1.57.

2.2 Candidate Distributions

Seven probability distributions were evaluated for each revenue stream:

1. Normal (Gaussian)
2. Log-Normal
3. Gamma
4. Weibull
5. Beta (applied to revenue-to-GDP ratio)
6. Generalized Extreme Value (GEV)
7. Negative Binomial (applied to transaction count data)

2.3 Fitting Procedure

Parameters were estimated using maximum likelihood estimation implemented in R version 4.3.1, utilizing the fitdistrplus package (version 1.1-11). Goodness-of-fit was assessed using three complementary tests:

- Kolmogorov-Smirnov (KS) test at the 5% significance level
- Anderson-Darling (AD) test, which weights tail behavior more heavily
- Akaike Information Criterion (AIC) for model comparison

Visual diagnostics including Q-Q plots and probability-probability plots were reviewed for all candidate distributions.

---

3. RESULTS BY REVENUE CATEGORY

3.1 Personal Income Tax (PIT)

PIT is the Commonwealth's largest revenue source, generating $16.8 billion in FY2023, representing 37.1% of total General Fund revenues. Over the five-year study period, annual PIT collections ranged from $13.2 billion (FY2020) to $17.4 billion (FY2022).

The Log-Normal distribution provided the best fit for monthly PIT collections, with estimated parameters μ = 21.14 and σ = 0.31. The KS test statistic was 0.071 (p-value = 0.934), and the AD statistic was 0.412, both indicating failure to reject the null hypothesis of distributional adequacy. The Normal distribution was rejected at the 5% level (KS p-value = 0.031), primarily due to positive skewness in April collections driven by final tax payments.

The fitted Log-Normal model implies a 90th percentile monthly collection of $1.87 billion and a 10th percentile of $1.02 billion, providing budget planners with a formal uncertainty range for the first time.

3.2 Corporate Net Income Tax (CNIT)

CNIT generated $6.1 billion in FY2023, up from $3.8 billion in FY2019, representing a 60.5% increase over the study period. This growth reflects both economic expansion and the statutory rate reduction from 9.99% to 8.99% enacted under Act 53 of 2022, which took effect January 1, 2023.

Monthly CNIT collections exhibit high variance and pronounced right skewness, with a coefficient of variation of 0.89. The Gamma distribution provided the best fit, with shape parameter α = 1.27 and rate parameter β = 0.0026. The AIC for the Gamma model was 1,847.3, compared to 1,863.1 for the Log-Normal and 1,891.4 for the Normal distribution.

The 95th percentile of the fitted Gamma distribution corresponds to monthly collections of $892 million, while the 5th percentile is $41 million, reflecting the extreme variability associated with estimated payment timing and large corporate settlements.

3.3 Sales and Use Tax (SUT)

SUT collections totaled $12.4 billion in FY2023, making it the second-largest General Fund revenue source at 27.4% of total receipts. Monthly collections are the most stable of the five categories analyzed, with a coefficient of variation of 0.14.

The Normal distribution provided an adequate fit for seasonally adjusted monthly SUT collections, with estimated mean μ = $1,033 million and standard deviation σ = $144 million. The KS test statistic was 0.063 (p-value = 0.971). However, the Generalized Extreme Value distribution achieved a marginally lower AIC (1,612.4 versus 1,614.7 for Normal), and its use is recommended for stress-testing scenarios involving tail events such as the consumption collapse observed in April and May 2020, when monthly collections fell to $621 million and $698 million respectively.

3.4 Inheritance and Estate Tax

Inheritance and Estate Tax revenues totaled $1.34 billion in FY2023. This revenue stream is characterized by high month-to-month volatility driven by the irregular timing of large estate settlements. The five-year average monthly collection was $108.7 million, but individual months ranged from $34 million (August 2019) to $287 million (March 2022).

The Weibull distribution provided the best fit, with shape parameter k = 1.18 and scale parameter λ = 116.4 million. The AD test statistic of 0.638 was below the critical value of 2.502 at the 5% significance level. The Log-Normal distribution was a close second (AD = 0.701), and either may be used for simulation purposes. The heavy right tail of the Weibull fit appropriately captures the influence of large estate settlements exceeding $50 million, of which 14 were recorded during the study period.

3.5 Lottery Fund Revenues

The Pennsylvania Lottery transferred $1.18 billion to programs for older Pennsylvanians in FY2023, consistent with its statutory mandate under the State Lottery Law (72 P.S. § 3761-101). Monthly net revenue transfers ranged from $71 million to $134 million over the study period.

The Beta distribution, fitted to the ratio of monthly lottery revenue to the trailing 12-month average, provided the best characterization of relative variability, with parameters α = 4.83 and β = 3.21. For absolute dollar modeling, the Normal distribution with μ = $98.3 million and σ = $14.7 million was deemed adequate (KS p-value = 0.812) and is recommended for operational forecasting given its interpretive simplicity.

5. IMPLICATIONS FOR REVENUE FORECASTING

The distributional models identified in this analysis have been incorporated into the Commonwealth's Monte Carlo simulation framework, which runs 100,000 iterations to generate probabilistic revenue forecasts. For FY2025, the simulation yields a central estimate of $46.8 billion in total General Fund revenues, with a 90% confidence interval of $43.1 billion to $50.6 billion.

The width of this interval—$7.5 billion—quantifies forecast uncertainty in a manner that was previously unavailable to budget planners. The Governor's Office of the Budget has adopted the 25th percentile estimate of $44.9 billion as the conservative planning baseline for FY2025 appropriations, reducing the probability of a mid-year revenue shortfall requiring emergency spending reductions.

---

6. LIMITATIONS AND FUTURE WORK

Several limitations warrant acknowledgment. First, the 60-month sample period includes the COVID-19 pandemic, which introduced structural breaks that may reduce the stationarity of fitted distributions. Second, the analysis treats revenue categories as independent, whereas correlations between PIT and CNIT (Pearson r = 0.74 over the study period) suggest that joint distributional modeling using copula methods would improve aggregate forecast accuracy. Third, the statutory CNIT rate reduction effective January 2023 may render pre-2023 data less predictive of future collections.

The Division of Quantitative Methods will extend this analysis in Technical Report TR-2025-02, scheduled for release in October 2024, to incorporate copula-based joint modeling and Bayesian updating procedures that allow parameter estimates to evolve as new monthly data become available.

---

7. CONCLUSION

This report demonstrates that Pennsylvania's major revenue streams are well-characterized by standard parametric distributions, with Log-Normal, Gamma, Normal, Weibull, and Beta distributions providing statistically adequate fits across the five categories analyzed. Implementation of these models in the Commonwealth's forecasting infrastructure represents a material improvement over historical point-estimate methods and is expected to reduce the MAPE from 4.7% to an estimated 2.9% based on backtesting over the FY2019–FY2023 period.
