EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Total state tax revenue for fiscal year 2023 reached $29.4 billion, representing a decline of 3.8 percent from the fiscal year 2022 total of $30.6 billion. This marks the first year-over-year decrease in state tax collections since fiscal year 2010, when revenues fell 6.2 percent during the aftermath of the Great Recession. Despite the overall decline, collections exceeded the February 2023 forecast by $312 million, or 1.1 percent, indicating that economic conditions, while softening, remained more resilient than projected earlier in the year.

The decline was driven primarily by a significant contraction in individual income tax receipts and capital gains realizations, partially offset by continued strength in sales tax collections and modest growth in corporate franchise tax revenues.

SECTION 1: INDIVIDUAL INCOME TAX

Individual income tax remains the largest single source of state revenue, accounting for $14.8 billion in fiscal year 2023, or approximately 50.3 percent of total tax collections.

Key findings:

- FY2023 individual income tax collections: $14.8 billion
- FY2022 individual income tax collections: $16.1 billion
- Year-over-year change: -$1.3 billion (-8.1 percent)
- Withholding payments: $11.2 billion (up 4.3 percent from FY2022)
- Estimated payments: $2.1 billion (down 31.4 percent from FY2022)
- Final payments and refunds (net): $1.5 billion (down 22.6 percent from FY2022)

The sharp decline in estimated and final payments reflects the dramatic reduction in capital gains realizations. In calendar year 2021, Minnesota taxpayers reported $18.7 billion in capital gains income, generating substantial tax payments that flowed into FY2022 collections. By calendar year 2022, capital gains realizations fell to an estimated $9.3 billion statewide, a decline of 50.3 percent, driven by the broad equity market correction that saw the S&P 500 decline 19.4 percent over the calendar year.

Withholding collections, by contrast, grew 4.3 percent, consistent with continued wage growth. Average weekly wages in Minnesota rose 4.1 percent in calendar year 2022 according to data from the Minnesota Department of Employment and Economic Development, and the state unemployment rate averaged 3.1 percent during fiscal year 2023.

The top marginal income tax rate in Minnesota remained at 9.85 percent for tax year 2022, applicable to taxable income above $171,220 for married joint filers. Approximately 68,400 Minnesota returns were subject to this top rate, representing 2.6 percent of all filers but contributing an estimated 26.8 percent of total income tax liability.

SECTION 2: SALES AND USE TAX

Sales and use tax collections totaled $8.9 billion in fiscal year 2023, an increase of $341 million or 4.0 percent over the FY2022 total of $8.56 billion. This represents the fourth consecutive year of growth in this revenue category.

The most notable trend within sales tax collections is the continued rapid growth of digital products and services, which increased 18.9 percent to $541 million. This category, which includes streaming services, downloaded software, and digital entertainment, has grown at an average annual rate of 14.7 percent since Minnesota expanded its sales tax to cover these products in fiscal year 2019. The Department of Revenue estimates that approximately 2.3 million Minnesota households subscribed to at least one taxable streaming service during fiscal year 2023.

Food service and restaurant collections grew 8.7 percent, reflecting both price inflation and continued recovery in dining activity following pandemic-era disruptions. The Minnesota Restaurant Association reported that total restaurant industry sales in the state reached $11.4 billion in calendar year 2022, recovering to 97 percent of pre-pandemic 2019 levels.

Building materials and construction declined 2.3 percent, consistent with a slowdown in residential construction activity. Single-family housing permits in Minnesota fell 18.6 percent in calendar year 2022 compared to 2021, according to the U.S. Census Bureau.

SECTION 3: CORPORATE FRANCHISE TAX

Corporate franchise tax collections totaled $3.2 billion in fiscal year 2023, an increase of $87 million or 2.8 percent from the FY2022 total of $3.11 billion.

Minnesota's corporate franchise tax rate is 9.8 percent, the third-highest statutory corporate tax rate among U.S. states. Despite this, collections growth remained positive due to continued strong corporate profitability in several key sectors.

Notable sector-level observations:

- Financial services sector contributed an estimated $680 million, representing 21.3 percent of total corporate franchise tax receipts
- Manufacturing sector contributed approximately $510 million, or 15.9 percent of total
- Retail and wholesale trade contributed $390 million, or 12.2 percent of total
- Healthcare and social assistance contributed $340 million, or 10.6 percent of total

The number of corporate returns filed for tax year 2021 (the most recent year with complete audit data) was 94,712, of which 61,340 showed positive net income. The average tax liability among profitable corporations was $52,100.

The most dramatic decline among secondary revenue sources was the deed and mortgage registry tax, which fell 41.4 percent from $290 million to $170 million. This reflects the sharp contraction in real estate transaction volume as mortgage interest rates rose from approximately 3.1 percent in January 2022 to 6.9 percent by December 2022. The Minnesota Association of Realtors reported that existing home sales in the state declined 24.7 percent in calendar year 2022.

Tobacco and cigarette tax collections declined 6.1 percent, continuing a long-term trend. The number of taxable cigarette packs sold in Minnesota fell to an estimated 47.3 million in fiscal year 2023, down from 50.4 million in FY2022 and representing a 44 percent decline from the FY2005 peak of 84.6 million packs. The state excise tax rate on cigarettes is $3.04 per pack, unchanged since 2013.

SECTION 5: FORECAST COMPARISON AND OUTLOOK

The February 2023 forecast, prepared by Minnesota Management and Budget under the direction of Commissioner Erin Campbell, projected total FY2023 tax revenues of $29.09 billion. Actual collections of $29.4 billion exceeded this forecast by $312 million.

The primary sources of forecast variance were:

1. Individual income tax: +$89 million above forecast (withholding stronger than projected)
2. Sales tax: +$156 million above forecast (consumer spending more resilient than expected)
3. Corporate franchise tax: +$74 million above forecast
4. All other sources: -$7 million below forecast (combined)

Looking ahead to fiscal year 2024, the November 2023 forecast will incorporate updated economic projections. The consensus among the Department of Revenue's economic advisory panel, which includes economists from the University of Minnesota, Wells Fargo Economics, and the Federal Reserve Bank of Minneapolis, is that Minnesota GDP growth will moderate to approximately 1.4 percent in calendar year 2023 before recovering to 2.1 percent in 2024.

SECTION 6: COMPLIANCE AND ENFORCEMENT

The Department of Revenue's audit and compliance activities generated $487 million in additional assessments during fiscal year 2023, an increase of 6.3 percent from the $458 million collected in FY2022. The department employed 312 revenue tax specialists as of June 30, 2023, and completed 14,847 individual audits and 2,103 corporate audits during the fiscal year.

The voluntary compliance rate for individual income tax filers, measured as the percentage of estimated tax liability paid without enforcement action, was 94.2 percent in tax year 2021, consistent with the prior three-year average of 94.0 percent.

CONCLUSION

Fiscal year 2023 presented a transitional revenue environment for Minnesota, with the unwinding of pandemic-era capital gains windfalls creating a headline decline that obscures continued underlying strength in wage-based income and consumer spending. The $29.4 billion in total collections, while 3.8 percent below FY2022, represents the second-highest total in state history and positions Minnesota's general fund on stable footing entering fiscal year 2024.
