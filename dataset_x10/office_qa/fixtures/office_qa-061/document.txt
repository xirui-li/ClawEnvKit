EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents a comprehensive analysis of United States defense expenditures for Fiscal Year 2024, covering appropriations enacted under the National Defense Authorization Act (NDAA) signed by President Biden on December 22, 2023. Total defense-related spending authorized for FY2024 stands at $886.3 billion, representing a 3.2 percent increase over the FY2023 enacted level of $858.5 billion. This document examines spending by category, compares current allocations against historical benchmarks, and evaluates cost efficiency across major program areas.

SECTION 1: TOTAL APPROPRIATIONS OVERVIEW

The FY2024 National Defense Authorization Act allocates $886.3 billion in total defense spending. This figure encompasses both discretionary and mandatory defense-related expenditures across the Department of Defense (DoD) and related agencies.

Breakdown by Major Category:

Category | FY2024 Allocation | FY2023 Allocation | % Change
Military Personnel | $176.2 billion | $170.8 billion | +3.2%
Operations & Maintenance | $295.4 billion | $283.1 billion | +4.3%
Procurement | $170.6 billion | $163.9 billion | +4.1%
Research, Development, Test & Evaluation (RDT&E) | $145.9 billion | $139.8 billion | +4.4%
Military Construction | $16.8 billion | $15.2 billion | +10.5%
Family Housing | $1.9 billion | $1.8 billion | +5.6%
Revolving & Management Funds | $4.1 billion | $3.9 billion | +5.1%
Other Defense Programs | $75.4 billion | $80.0 billion | -5.8%
TOTAL | $886.3 billion | $858.5 billion | +3.2%

Defense spending as a share of Gross Domestic Product (GDP) stands at approximately 3.1 percent for FY2024, down marginally from 3.2 percent in FY2023, reflecting continued economic growth outpacing defense budget increases. The United States remains the world's largest defense spender, accounting for approximately 37 percent of total global military expenditure as measured by the Stockholm International Peace Research Institute (SIPRI) in its 2023 annual report.

SECTION 2: SERVICE-BRANCH ALLOCATIONS

Funding is distributed across the four primary military branches and the Space Force as follows:

- Department of the Army: $185.7 billion (20.9% of total)
- Department of the Navy (including Marine Corps): $230.8 billion (26.0% of total)
- Department of the Air Force: $215.1 billion (24.3% of total)
- United States Space Force: $29.4 billion (3.3% of total)
- Defense-Wide Agencies and Activities: $225.3 billion (25.4% of total)

The Space Force allocation of $29.4 billion represents a 14.8 percent increase over its FY2023 budget of $25.6 billion, the largest proportional increase of any service branch. This growth reflects continued investment in satellite communications, space domain awareness, and next-generation launch capabilities under the direction of Chief of Space Operations General B. Chance Saltzman.

SECTION 3: MAJOR PROCUREMENT PROGRAMS

The $170.6 billion procurement budget funds acquisition of weapons systems, aircraft, naval vessels, and ground equipment. The following programs represent the largest individual line items:

F-35 Lightning II Joint Strike Fighter Program
- FY2024 Procurement Funding: $9.1 billion
- Units Requested: 83 aircraft across all variants
- Cumulative Program Cost (as of January 2024): $412.7 billion
- Program Executive Officer: Rear Admiral Michael Muhlenfeld
- The F-35 program remains the most expensive weapons acquisition program in U.S. history.

Columbia-Class Ballistic Missile Submarine
- FY2024 Procurement Funding: $6.2 billion
- Lead vessel (USS Columbia, SSBN-826) scheduled for delivery in 2027
- Total program cost estimate: $132.0 billion for 12 submarines
- Average cost per vessel: $11.0 billion

Virginia-Class Attack Submarine
- FY2024 Procurement Funding: $7.8 billion
- Two submarines funded in FY2024
- Cumulative fleet: 22 boats in commission as of October 2023

B-21 Raider Long-Range Strike Bomber
- FY2024 Procurement Funding: $5.7 billion
- Manufactured by Northrop Grumman at facilities in Palmdale, California
- Initial operational capability projected for 2026
- Unit cost estimate: $692 million per aircraft (FY2023 dollars)

Guided Multiple Launch Rocket System (GMLRS) Resupply
- FY2024 Funding: $1.4 billion
- Reflects accelerated production to replenish stocks transferred to Ukraine
- Production rate increased from 10,000 to 14,000 rockets per year beginning March 2024

SECTION 4: RESEARCH, DEVELOPMENT, TEST AND EVALUATION

The $145.9 billion RDT&E budget represents the highest single-year investment in defense research in American history when adjusted for inflation. Priority investment areas include:

Artificial Intelligence and Autonomous Systems: $3.7 billion
- Managed through the Chief Digital and Artificial Intelligence Office (CDAO) under Director Radha Plumb
- Includes $890 million for Project Maven continuation
- Autonomous logistics systems: $420 million

Hypersonic Weapons Development: $4.7 billion
- Army Long-Range Hypersonic Weapon (LRHW): $1.1 billion
- Navy Conventional Prompt Strike (CPS): $1.3 billion
- Air-Launched Rapid Response Weapon (ARRW): $258 million (program wind-down phase)

Directed Energy Weapons: $1.2 billion
- High Energy Laser programs across all services
- Solid State Laser - Technology Maturation (SSL-TM): $147 million

Microelectronics and Semiconductor Research: $3.1 billion
- Coordinated with the CHIPS and Science Act implementation
- Domestic fabrication facility investments at facilities in Arizona and Ohio

SECTION 5: OPERATIONS AND MAINTENANCE — READINESS ASSESSMENT

The Operations and Maintenance (O&M) account at $295.4 billion funds day-to-day military readiness, training, base operations, and sustainment. Key findings from the DoD Comptroller's readiness review, completed January 31, 2024:

- Overall force readiness rating: 72 percent of combat units assessed as "fully mission capable," up from 68 percent in FY2022
- Fuel costs consumed $18.3 billion of the O&M budget, reflecting a 6.1 percent decrease from FY2023 due to declining jet fuel prices
- Depot maintenance backlog: $26.4 billion as of September 30, 2023, a reduction of $2.1 billion from the prior year
- Cyber operations and defense: $13.2 billion, a 9.8 percent increase over FY2023
- Base operations support: $47.6 billion across 750 domestic installations and 514 overseas locations

SECTION 6: UKRAINE SECURITY ASSISTANCE — SUPPLEMENTAL ANALYSIS

Through the end of FY2023 (September 30, 2023), the United States had committed $44.2 billion in security assistance to Ukraine since the Russian invasion of February 24, 2022. This figure does not appear in the FY2024 base budget but is subject to separate supplemental appropriations requests.

The Presidential Drawdown Authority (PDA), used to transfer equipment directly from DoD stocks, accounted for $18.6 billion of total assistance. The Ukraine Security Assistance Initiative (USAI), which funds new procurement for Ukraine, accounted for $17.9 billion. Remaining funds covered intelligence sharing, training, and logistics support.

As of February 2024, the Biden administration's supplemental request of $61.4 billion for Ukraine, Israel, and Indo-Pacific security remained pending before Congress.

SECTION 7: HISTORICAL COMPARISON AND TREND ANALYSIS

Defense spending in constant FY2024 dollars over the past decade:

FY2015: $618.7 billion
FY2016: $622.4 billion
FY2017: $634.1 billion
FY2018: $700.5 billion (reflects Budget Control Act relief)
FY2019: $738.0 billion
FY2020: $753.5 billion
FY2021: $768.2 billion
FY2022: $801.9 billion
FY2023: $858.5 billion
FY2024: $886.3 billion

The compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of defense spending from FY2015 to FY2024 is 4.1 percent in nominal terms and approximately 1.6 percent in inflation-adjusted terms. The largest single-year nominal increase occurred in FY2018, when Congress passed the Bipartisan Budget Act of 2018, lifting sequestration caps and adding $80.4 billion over the prior year.

SECTION 8: COST EFFICIENCY AND AUDIT FINDINGS

The Department of Defense failed its sixth consecutive full financial audit in November 2023, though auditors noted measurable improvement in asset accountability. Key findings from the FY2023 audit, conducted by independent firms including Ernst & Young and Deloitte under contract to the DoD Inspector General:

- $3.8 billion in assets could not be properly accounted for or located
- Army property accountability improved by 14 percentage points compared to FY2022
- The Defense Logistics Agency achieved a clean audit opinion for the third consecutive year
- Estimated cost of the audit process: $218 million in FY2023

Deputy Secretary of Defense Kathleen Hicks stated on November 15, 2023, that the Department expects to achieve a clean audit opinion by FY2028.

SECTION 9: CONCLUSIONS AND FORWARD PROJECTIONS

The Congressional Budget Office projects that under current policy, defense spending will reach $1.0 trillion annually by FY2028, driven by personnel cost growth averaging 4.5 percent per year, continued hypersonic and space investment, and nuclear modernization costs associated with the Columbia-class submarine and B-21 programs entering full-rate production simultaneously.

The FY2025 President's Budget Request, expected to be submitted to Congress on March 11, 2024, is anticipated to propose $895.2 billion in total defense spending, representing a 1.0 percent nominal increase constrained by broader federal deficit reduction negotiations.
