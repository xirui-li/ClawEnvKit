EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents the methodology and findings of the Treasury Department's quarterly bond yield change calculation for the period ending June 30, 2024. The analysis covers yield movements across the full maturity spectrum of U.S. Treasury securities, from 3-month bills through 30-year bonds. During the reference quarter, the 10-year Treasury note yield rose from 4.20 percent on April 1, 2024, to 4.36 percent on June 28, 2024, representing a net increase of 16 basis points. The 2-year Treasury note, which is more sensitive to Federal Reserve policy expectations, declined from 4.62 percent to 4.71 percent before closing the quarter at 4.75 percent, a net increase of 13 basis points. The 30-year Treasury bond yield moved from 4.35 percent to 4.51 percent over the same period, widening the 10-year/30-year spread by 1 basis point to 15 basis points.

These yield changes carry direct implications for federal borrowing costs. The Treasury Department estimates that the 16-basis-point increase in the 10-year yield, applied across $1.84 trillion in notes maturing or being refinanced during calendar year 2024, will add approximately $2.94 billion in annualized interest expense to the federal budget.

SECTION 1: METHODOLOGY FOR YIELD CHANGE CALCULATION

1.1 Data Sources and Reference Rates

All yield figures in this report are drawn from the H.15 Selected Interest Rates statistical release published daily by the Federal Reserve Board. Yields represent the constant maturity rates as of 3:30 p.m. Eastern Time on each trading day. The baseline date for quarterly comparison is the last business day of the preceding quarter (March 28, 2024), and the terminal date is the last business day of the current quarter (June 28, 2024).

1.2 Basis Point Calculation Formula

Yield changes are expressed in basis points (bps), where 1 basis point equals 0.01 percentage points. The formula applied is:

Change (bps) = [Yield(terminal date) – Yield(baseline date)] × 100

For example, the 10-year note:
Change = (4.36% – 4.20%) × 100 = 16 basis points

1.3 Duration-Weighted Cost Impact

To translate yield changes into dollar cost impacts, the Office of Debt Management applies a duration-weighted methodology. The modified duration of each security class is multiplied by the outstanding principal subject to refinancing, then multiplied by the yield change. For 10-year notes with an average modified duration of 8.7 years and $212 billion in scheduled issuance during Q3 2024, a 16-basis-point increase translates to a price decline of approximately 1.39 percent per $100 face value, or $2.95 billion in additional present-value cost across the quarter's issuance.

SECTION 3: INTRA-QUARTER YIELD VOLATILITY

3.1 Peak and Trough Analysis

The 10-year Treasury yield reached its quarterly high of 4.74 percent on April 25, 2024, following the release of the Bureau of Economic Analysis's advance estimate of Q1 2024 GDP, which showed real growth of 1.6 percent annualized, below the consensus forecast of 2.4 percent but accompanied by a Personal Consumption Expenditures (PCE) price index reading of 3.4 percent, higher than expected. This combination of slower growth and persistent inflation temporarily pushed yields sharply higher.

The quarterly low for the 10-year yield was 4.20 percent, recorded on April 1, 2024, the first trading day of the quarter.

The 30-year bond yield reached an intra-quarter high of 4.86 percent on April 25, 2024, before retreating as subsequent data releases showed moderation in inflation pressures.

3.2 Key Market Events Affecting Yields

April 10, 2024: The Bureau of Labor Statistics released the Consumer Price Index for March 2024, showing a year-over-year increase of 3.5 percent, above the consensus estimate of 3.4 percent. The 10-year yield rose 18 basis points on that single day, from 4.37 percent to 4.55 percent, the largest single-day move of the quarter.

May 15, 2024: The April 2024 CPI report showed a year-over-year increase of 3.4 percent, in line with expectations. The 10-year yield fell 9 basis points on the day, from 4.49 percent to 4.40 percent.

June 12, 2024: The Federal Open Market Committee held the federal funds rate steady at 5.25–5.50 percent. The updated Summary of Economic Projections (the "dot plot") indicated that FOMC members projected a median of one rate cut in 2024, down from three cuts projected in the March 2024 dot plot. The 2-year yield rose 12 basis points on June 12 in response.

SECTION 4: IMPACT ON FEDERAL BORROWING COSTS

4.1 Gross Issuance During the Quarter

During Q2 FY2024 (April–June 2024), the Treasury Department issued the following securities:

- Treasury Bills (4-week through 52-week): $2.84 trillion gross issuance
- Treasury Notes (2-year through 10-year): $892 billion gross issuance
- Treasury Bonds (20-year and 30-year): $108 billion gross issuance
- Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS): $62 billion gross issuance
- Floating Rate Notes (FRNs): $94 billion gross issuance

Total gross issuance for the quarter: $4.00 trillion

4.2 Weighted Average Yield on New Issuance

The weighted average yield on all coupon-bearing securities issued during Q2 FY2024 was 4.58 percent, compared to 4.41 percent for the same securities issued in Q2 FY2023. This 17-basis-point year-over-year increase in the weighted average new-issue yield adds approximately $3.1 billion in annual interest expense on the $1.84 trillion in coupon securities issued during the quarter.

4.3 Comparison to Congressional Budget Office Projections

The Congressional Budget Office's February 2024 Budget and Economic Outlook projected a 10-year Treasury yield of 4.1 percent for calendar year 2024. The actual average 10-year yield during Q2 2024 was 4.48 percent, exceeding the CBO projection by 38 basis points. If this differential persists through the remainder of fiscal year 2024, the Treasury estimates total net interest costs for FY2024 will reach approximately $892 billion, compared to the CBO's February projection of $870 billion.

SECTION 6: FORWARD OUTLOOK AND RISK FACTORS

The Office of Debt Management identifies the following factors as most likely to influence Treasury yield levels during Q3 FY2024 (July–September 2024):

- Federal Reserve policy decisions: The July 30–31, 2024, FOMC meeting is expected to hold rates steady. Futures markets as of June 28, 2024, assigned a 64 percent probability to at least one 25-basis-point rate cut by the September 18, 2024, FOMC meeting.
- Inflation trajectory: The PCE price index for May 2024, released June 28, 2024, showed a year-over-year increase of 2.6 percent, down from 2.7 percent in April 2024, suggesting gradual progress toward the Federal Reserve's 2.0 percent target.
- Treasury supply: The Office of Debt Management announced on May 1, 2024, that it expects to borrow $847 billion in the April–June 2024 quarter and $243 billion in the July–September 2024 quarter, a significant reduction in supply that may exert modest downward pressure on yields.
- Fiscal deficit: The Congressional Budget Office projects the FY2024 federal deficit at $1.915 trillion, which requires continued large-scale Treasury issuance throughout the fiscal year.
