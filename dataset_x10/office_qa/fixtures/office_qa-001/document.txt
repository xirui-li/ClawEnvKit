EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Department of Defense received a total appropriation of $858.5 billion for Fiscal Year 2024, representing a 3.2 percent increase over the FY2023 enacted level of $831.7 billion. This report provides a comprehensive breakdown of defense expenditures across all major categories, examines year-over-year trends, and assesses the alignment of spending with stated national security objectives as outlined in the 2022 National Defense Strategy. The analysis was prepared under the direction of CBO Director Phillip Swagel and incorporates data submitted by the Office of the Secretary of Defense through January 31, 2024.

SECTION 1: TOTAL DEFENSE BUDGET OVERVIEW

The FY2024 National Defense Authorization Act, signed into law by President Biden on December 22, 2023, authorized $886.3 billion in total national defense spending. This figure encompasses both the base defense budget and supplemental appropriations. The breakdown is as follows:

Base Department of Defense Budget: $858.5 billion
Department of Energy (National Nuclear Security Administration): $24.3 billion
Other Defense-Related Activities: $3.5 billion
TOTAL NATIONAL DEFENSE: $886.3 billion

The $858.5 billion base DoD budget represents 12.9 percent of total projected federal outlays for FY2024, which are estimated at $6.65 trillion. As a share of Gross Domestic Product, defense spending stands at approximately 3.1 percent, consistent with the 3.0–3.2 percent range maintained since FY2020.

Operations and Maintenance (O&M) remains the single largest category at $295.8 billion, or 34.5 percent of the total budget. This represents an increase of $9.4 billion over FY2023's O&M appropriation of $286.4 billion. The growth in O&M is attributable in part to elevated fuel costs, increased operational tempo in the Indo-Pacific region, and expanded cybersecurity maintenance contracts.

Research, Development, Test and Evaluation (RDT&E) funding of $145.9 billion reflects a continued prioritization of next-generation capabilities. This figure is the highest RDT&E appropriation in DoD history in nominal terms, surpassing the previous record of $139.8 billion set in FY2023.

SECTION 4: MAJOR PROCUREMENT PROGRAMS

The following programs represent the ten largest individual procurement line items in the FY2024 budget:

1. F-35 Joint Strike Fighter Program (Lockheed Martin): $15.6 billion
   — Funds procurement of 83 aircraft across all variants (A, B, C)
   — Cumulative program cost to date: $412 billion

2. Columbia-Class Submarine (General Dynamics Electric Boat): $6.2 billion
   — Advance procurement for Boat 3 and continued construction of Boats 1–2

3. DDG-51 Arleigh Burke Destroyer (Huntington Ingalls / Bath Iron Works): $5.8 billion
   — Funds two destroyers in FY2024

4. B-21 Raider Bomber (Northrop Grumman): $5.1 billion
   — Low-rate initial production; first operational aircraft expected FY2026

5. CH-53K King Stallion Helicopter (Sikorsky): $2.4 billion
   — 35 aircraft funded in FY2024

6. Hypersonic Weapons Development (Multiple Contractors): $4.7 billion
   — Includes Long-Range Hypersonic Weapon (LRHW) and Air-Launched Rapid Response Weapon (ARRW)

7. Next Generation Interceptor (Northrop Grumman / Raytheon): $1.8 billion

8. MQ-9 Reaper Unmanned Aerial Vehicle (General Atomics): $1.1 billion

9. Paladin Integrated Management (BAE Systems): $0.9 billion

10. Guided Multiple Launch Rocket System (GMLRS) Resupply (Lockheed Martin): $1.7 billion
    — Partially reflects replenishment of stocks transferred to Ukraine

SECTION 5: UKRAINE SECURITY ASSISTANCE AND SUPPLEMENTAL SPENDING

Through January 2024, the United States has committed approximately $75.4 billion in total assistance to Ukraine since the Russian invasion began on February 24, 2022. Of this total:

Security Assistance (weapons, equipment, training): $46.6 billion
Economic and Humanitarian Assistance: $28.8 billion

The Presidential Drawdown Authority has been invoked 47 times since February 2022, transferring equipment valued at $24.9 billion directly from DoD inventories. The Ukraine Security Supplemental Appropriations Act of FY2023 provided $61.4 billion, of which $25.9 billion was designated for DoD replenishment and new procurement.

The GMLRS resupply line item noted above ($1.7 billion) directly reflects the drawdown of approximately 8,000 GMLRS rockets transferred to Ukrainian forces. The Army projects full inventory restoration by Q3 FY2026.

SECTION 6: PERSONNEL AND COMPENSATION

Military personnel costs of $176.2 billion support an active-duty end strength of 1,328,500 personnel as authorized in the FY2024 NDAA. This represents a reduction of 3,000 from the FY2023 authorized end strength of 1,331,500, reflecting recruiting shortfalls that have persisted since FY2022.

The Army fell 10,000 soldiers short of its FY2023 recruiting goal of 65,000, achieving only 55,000 accessions — the service's worst recruiting year since 1979. In response, the Army increased enlistment bonuses to a maximum of $50,000 for high-demand specialties and launched the "Future Soldier Preparatory Course" in August 2022.

The FY2024 budget includes a 5.2 percent pay raise for all uniformed military personnel, effective January 1, 2024. This is the largest military pay increase since FY2002 and is intended to address retention concerns and maintain competitiveness with private-sector compensation. The pay raise adds an estimated $8.7 billion to the military personnel account compared to FY2023 baseline projections.

The compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of the DoD base budget from FY2015 to FY2024 is 5.4 percent in nominal terms. Adjusted for inflation using the DoD deflator, real growth over the same period is approximately 2.1 percent annually. The most significant single-year increase occurred between FY2017 and FY2018 (+$54.5 billion, +9.5%), following the enactment of the Bipartisan Budget Act of 2018, which suspended statutory spending caps.

SECTION 8: FINDINGS AND OBSERVATIONS

The Congressional Budget Office offers the following observations based on this analysis:

1. Structural Cost Growth: Personnel and O&M costs together consume 55 percent of the total DoD budget, limiting flexibility for investment accounts. Without structural reforms to compensation and healthcare (TRICARE costs reached $57.8 billion in FY2024), investment accounts will face increasing pressure.

2. Program Affordability: The F-35 program's cumulative cost of $412 billion and ongoing per-unit cost of approximately $110 million for the F-35A variant raise long-term affordability concerns, particularly as the Air Force simultaneously funds B-21 development.

3. Industrial Base Capacity: The Columbia-class submarine program and DDG-51 procurement are constrained by shipyard capacity. Huntington Ingalls Industries' Newport News facility is currently operating at 97 percent capacity, and the Navy has acknowledged delivery delays of 12–18 months on multiple vessels.

4. Recruiting and Retention: Persistent recruiting shortfalls across the Army, Navy, and Air Force represent a structural risk to readiness. The DoD's FY2024 budget allocates $1.2 billion specifically for recruiting initiatives, a 34 percent increase over FY2022 levels.
