EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents a comprehensive Compound Annual Growth Rate (CAGR) analysis of the Federal Highway Trust Fund (HTF) for the ten-year period spanning fiscal year 2014 through fiscal year 2024. The analysis encompasses both the Highway Account and the Mass Transit Account, examining revenue inflows, expenditure patterns, and net fund balances. The findings are intended to support congressional budget deliberations, inform the Federal Highway Administration's (FHWA) long-range planning activities, and satisfy reporting requirements under 31 U.S.C. § 9503.

The Highway Trust Fund recorded a combined balance of $52.3 billion as of September 30, 2024, compared to $41.7 billion on September 30, 2014. Over this ten-year period, the fund's total balance grew at a CAGR of 2.29 percent. This modest growth rate reflects the well-documented structural imbalance between fuel tax revenues—which have remained nominally flat due to the 18.4 cents-per-gallon federal gasoline excise tax rate unchanged since 1993—and rising infrastructure expenditure demands.


SECTION 1: BACKGROUND AND STATUTORY FRAMEWORK

The Federal Highway Trust Fund was established by the Highway Revenue Act of 1956 and is funded primarily through federal motor fuel excise taxes, truck and trailer sales taxes, heavy vehicle use taxes, and tire taxes. The fund is divided into two accounts:

1. Highway Account — finances federal-aid highway programs
2. Mass Transit Account — finances federal transit assistance programs

The current federal excise tax rates applicable to the HTF are as follows:

Gasoline: 18.4 cents per gallon (of which 15.44 cents flows to the Highway Account and 2.86 cents to the Mass Transit Account)
Diesel fuel: 24.4 cents per gallon
Compressed natural gas (CNG): 18.3 cents per gallon
Liquefied natural gas (LNG): 24.3 cents per gallon

These rates have not been adjusted for inflation since October 1, 1997, when the deficit reduction portion of the tax was redirected to the HTF. The purchasing power of the 18.4-cent gasoline tax has eroded by approximately 47 percent in real terms since 1993, based on Bureau of Labor Statistics CPI data through September 2024.


SECTION 2: CAGR METHODOLOGY

The Compound Annual Growth Rate is calculated using the following standard formula:

CAGR = (Ending Value / Beginning Value) ^ (1 / Number of Years) — 1

For this analysis:
— Beginning Value (FY2014 balance): $41.7 billion
— Ending Value (FY2024 balance): $52.3 billion
— Number of Years: 10

CAGR = ($52.3B / $41.7B) ^ (1/10) — 1
CAGR = (1.2542) ^ (0.10) — 1
CAGR = 1.02293 — 1
CAGR = 2.29 percent

Separate CAGR computations were performed for the Highway Account and the Mass Transit Account, as well as for revenue inflows and expenditure outflows independently.

= ($42.1B / $34.8B) ^ (1/10) — 1

Mass Transit Account CAGR (FY2014–FY2024):
= ($10.2B / $6.9B) ^ (1/10) — 1
= (1.4783) ^ (0.10) — 1
= 1.03986 — 1
= 3.99 percent

The Mass Transit Account's higher CAGR of 3.99 percent compared to the Highway Account's 1.92 percent reflects sustained congressional appropriations supplements and the prioritization of transit funding under successive surface transportation authorization acts, including the Fixing America's Surface Transportation (FAST) Act of 2015 and the IIJA.

Key observations from Table 2:

— Total HTF revenues grew at a CAGR of only 0.63 percent over the ten-year period, significantly below the rate of inflation.

— The most dramatic growth occurred in interest on investments, which grew from $0.3 billion in FY2014 to $1.9 billion in FY2024, representing a CAGR of 20.27 percent. This reflects the Federal Reserve's interest rate increases beginning in March 2022, which raised the yield on Treasury securities held by the fund.

— General fund transfers declined at a CAGR of negative 6.88 percent, falling from $6.2 billion in FY2014 to $3.0 billion in FY2024, as Congress reduced supplemental transfers following the stabilization of fund balances.

— Gasoline excise tax revenues grew at a negligible CAGR of 0.51 percent, reflecting the combination of a fixed nominal tax rate and declining per-capita vehicle miles traveled (VMT) growth, partially offset by population increases.


SECTION 5: EXPENDITURE ANALYSIS

Total HTF expenditures in FY2024 reached $51.0 billion, compared to $46.3 billion in FY2014, representing a CAGR of 0.97 percent. The expenditure CAGR of 0.97 percent slightly exceeded the revenue CAGR of 0.63 percent, confirming the structural imbalance that has required periodic general fund transfers since FY2008.

SECTION 6: IMPACT OF THE INFRASTRUCTURE INVESTMENT AND JOBS ACT

The IIJA authorized $110 billion in new spending for roads, bridges, and major projects over five fiscal years (FY2022–FY2026), with $39.2 billion specifically designated for bridge repair and replacement. Annual HTF-supported IIJA outlays are projected to average $22.0 billion per year through FY2026.

Projected CAGR under IIJA spending trajectory (FY2024–FY2029):
— Projected FY2029 combined balance: $48.7 billion (assuming no new general fund transfers)
— Implied CAGR: ($48.7B / $52.3B) ^ (1/5) — 1 = negative 1.42 percent

This negative projected CAGR underscores the Treasury's assessment that without either an increase in the federal motor fuel excise tax rate or a new dedicated revenue mechanism—such as a vehicle miles traveled (VMT) fee—the HTF will face renewed solvency pressure by FY2028.


SECTION 7: CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS

The ten-year CAGR analysis of the Federal Highway Trust Fund reveals the following principal findings:

1. The combined HTF balance grew at a CAGR of 2.29 percent from FY2014 ($41.7 billion) to FY2024 ($52.3 billion).
2. The Highway Account CAGR of 1.92 percent trailed the Mass Transit Account CAGR of 3.99 percent over the same period.
3. Total revenue inflows grew at a CAGR of only 0.63 percent, well below the 10-year average CPI inflation rate of approximately 3.1 percent.
4. Interest income was the fastest-growing revenue component, with a CAGR of 20.27 percent driven by rising interest rates.
5. General fund transfers declined at a CAGR of negative 6.88 percent as supplemental appropriations were reduced.
6. Expenditures grew at a CAGR of 0.97 percent, modestly exceeding revenue growth.
7. Forward projections under current IIJA spending levels suggest a negative CAGR of 1.42 percent through FY2029.

The Office of Trust Fund Management recommends that the Office of Management and Budget and the Congressional Budget Office jointly model the revenue implications of a 10-cent-per-gallon increase in the federal gasoline excise tax, which the Congressional Budget Office estimated in its February 2023 Options for Reducing the Deficit report would generate approximately $26 billion in additional HTF revenues over ten years.
