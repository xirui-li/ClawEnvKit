EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents a detailed analysis of yield changes across the U.S. Treasury bond portfolio during the third quarter of fiscal year 2024. The Federal Open Market Committee maintained the federal funds target rate at 5.25–5.50 percent throughout the quarter, a level unchanged since July 26, 2023. Despite the stable policy rate, secondary market yields exhibited meaningful movement across maturities, driven by evolving inflation expectations, labor market data releases, and shifting demand from foreign official institutions. This document quantifies those yield changes, explains the calculation methodology employed by the Office of Debt Management, and estimates the resulting impact on federal borrowing costs and outstanding debt valuation.

─────────────────────────────────────────────

SECTION 1: METHODOLOGY FOR YIELD CHANGE CALCULATION

1.1 Data Sources

All yield figures cited in this report are drawn from the H.15 Selected Interest Rates statistical release published daily by the Federal Reserve Board of Governors. Constant maturity Treasury (CMT) yields are used as the benchmark series. These yields represent the yield on a hypothetical Treasury security with a fixed maturity, interpolated from the daily yield curve. The Office of Debt Management supplements CMT data with auction results published on TreasuryDirect.gov and with secondary market closing prices provided by ICE Data Services under contract number TREAS-2022-0047.

1.2 Calculation Framework

Yield change (ΔY) for any given maturity is calculated as:

ΔY = Y(end) – Y(begin)

Where Y(end) is the CMT yield on June 28, 2024 (the last business day of the quarter) and Y(begin) is the CMT yield on March 28, 2024 (the last business day of the prior quarter).

Basis point (bp) conversion: 1 basis point = 0.01 percentage point. All changes are expressed in basis points unless otherwise noted.

Duration-adjusted price impact is calculated using the modified duration formula:

ΔP ≈ –D(mod) × ΔY × P

Where D(mod) is the modified duration of the security, ΔY is the yield change expressed as a decimal, and P is the face-adjusted market price.

SECTION 3: IMPACT ON SPECIFIC BENCHMARK SECURITIES

3.1 The 10-Year Treasury Note (CUSIP 91282CJL6)

The on-the-run 10-year Treasury note auctioned on May 8, 2024, carried a coupon rate of 4.500 percent and a maturity date of May 15, 2034. The auction cleared at a high yield of 4.483 percent, with a bid-to-cover ratio of 2.49. Total issuance at that auction was $42 billion.

By June 28, 2024, the secondary market yield on this note had risen to approximately 4.40 percent (per CMT interpolation), representing a decline of 8 basis points from the auction clearing yield. The modified duration of this note at issuance was approximately 8.11 years.

Price impact calculation:
ΔP ≈ –8.11 × (–0.0008) × $100 = +$0.065 per $100 face value

This implies that holders of the May 2034 note experienced a modest mark-to-market gain of approximately 6.5 cents per $100 face value between the auction date and June 28, 2024.

3.2 The 30-Year Treasury Bond (CUSIP 91282CJJ1)

The on-the-run 30-year bond auctioned on February 8, 2024, carried a coupon rate of 4.750 percent and a maturity date of February 15, 2054. The auction cleared at a high yield of 4.360 percent, with a bid-to-cover ratio of 2.42. Total issuance was $25 billion.

Between March 28 and June 28, 2024, the 30-year CMT yield rose 22 basis points. The modified duration of this bond was approximately 17.4 years at the start of the quarter.

Price impact calculation:
ΔP ≈ –17.4 × 0.0022 × $100 = –$3.83 per $100 face value

Holders of the February 2054 bond experienced a mark-to-market loss of approximately $3.83 per $100 face value during Q3 FY2024, equivalent to a 3.83 percent price decline on a par-valued position.

SECTION 4: AGGREGATE BORROWING COST IMPLICATIONS

4.1 New Issuance During Q3 FY2024

The Treasury issued a total of $2.847 trillion in marketable securities during Q3 FY2024, comprising:

- Treasury bills (maturities of 52 weeks or less): $2.214 trillion
- Treasury notes (maturities of 2 to 10 years): $521 billion
- Treasury bonds (maturities of 20 and 30 years): $75 billion
- Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS): $37 billion

4.2 Weighted Average Interest Rate on New Issuance

The weighted average interest rate on all new marketable debt issued during Q3 FY2024 was 5.31 percent, compared to 5.18 percent during Q2 FY2024 (January 1 – March 31, 2024). This 13-basis-point increase in the average borrowing rate on new issuance translates to an estimated additional annualized interest cost of approximately $3.7 billion on the $2.847 trillion in securities issued during the quarter, assuming the securities roll over at the same rate.

4.3 Total Marketable Debt Outstanding

As of June 30, 2024, total marketable Treasury debt outstanding stood at $27.4 trillion. The weighted average interest rate on the entire outstanding portfolio was 3.32 percent, up from 3.09 percent as of June 30, 2023. This 23-basis-point increase over twelve months reflects the ongoing rollover of older, lower-coupon debt into new securities issued at current market rates.

─────────────────────────────────────────────

SECTION 5: FOREIGN DEMAND AND YIELD IMPLICATIONS

Foreign official institutions held $3.87 trillion in U.S. Treasury securities as of May 31, 2024, according to the Treasury International Capital (TIC) data released July 16, 2024. Japan remained the largest foreign holder with $1.128 trillion, followed by China at $768.3 billion. China's holdings declined by $22.7 billion from February 2024 to May 2024, a trend that contributed to upward pressure on long-end yields during the quarter.

Primary dealer data from the Federal Reserve Bank of New York indicated that net foreign purchases of coupon-bearing Treasury securities totaled $89.4 billion during Q3 FY2024, down from $114.2 billion in Q2 FY2024. The Office of Debt Management estimates that this $24.8 billion reduction in foreign demand contributed approximately 4 to 6 basis points of the 20-basis-point rise in the 10-year yield observed during the quarter.

─────────────────────────────────────────────

SECTION 6: INFLATION BREAKEVEN ANALYSIS

The 10-year breakeven inflation rate, calculated as the difference between the 10-year nominal CMT yield and the 10-year TIPS yield, moved from 2.33 percent on March 28, 2024, to 2.30 percent on June 28, 2024, a decline of 3 basis points. This marginal compression in breakeven inflation suggests that the 20-basis-point rise in nominal 10-year yields was driven primarily by an increase in real yields (approximately 23 basis points) rather than by rising inflation expectations.

The 10-year TIPS yield rose from 1.87 percent to 2.10 percent over the same period, confirming that tighter real financial conditions were the dominant driver of long-end yield increases during Q3 FY2024.

─────────────────────────────────────────────

SECTION 7: OUTLOOK AND RISK FACTORS

The Office of Debt Management identifies the following key risk factors for Q4 FY2024 (July 1 – September 30, 2024):

1. Federal Reserve policy path: Futures markets as of June 28, 2024, priced in approximately 46 basis points of rate cuts by December 2024, implying one to two 25-basis-point reductions. Any upside surprise in core PCE inflation, which stood at 2.6 percent year-over-year as of May 2024, could delay cuts and push short-end yields higher.

2. Fiscal trajectory: The Congressional Budget Office projected a federal deficit of $1.915 trillion for fiscal year 2024 in its May 2024 Budget and Economic Outlook, requiring continued heavy Treasury issuance.

3. Debt ceiling: The current suspension of the debt ceiling expires January 1, 2025. Extraordinary measures and associated uncertainty may affect bill issuance patterns beginning in Q1 FY2025.
