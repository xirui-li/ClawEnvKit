EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Commonwealth of Pennsylvania's General Fund revenues for the third quarter of fiscal year 2023-2024 totaled $11.84 billion, representing an increase of $1.23 billion, or 11.6 percent, compared to the second quarter total of $10.61 billion. This quarter-over-quarter improvement reflects stronger-than-anticipated personal income tax collections driven by seasonal wage growth and elevated bonus distributions in the financial services sector concentrated in the Philadelphia and Pittsburgh metropolitan areas.

Total General Fund expenditures for Q3 FY2023-2024 reached $12.07 billion, up from $11.49 billion in Q2, an increase of $580 million or 5.0 percent. The resulting quarterly deficit of $233 million compares favorably to the Q2 deficit of $880 million, representing a 73.5 percent improvement in the quarterly shortfall. The cumulative fiscal year deficit through March 31, 2024 stands at $1.42 billion, which the Office of the Budget projects will be substantially offset by anticipated Q4 revenues, particularly from corporate net income tax settlements typically concentrated in April and May.

1.2 Notable Revenue Developments

Personal Income Tax (PIT): The $673.3 million quarter-over-quarter increase in PIT collections is the largest single-quarter gain recorded since Q3 FY2021-2022, when pandemic-era federal stimulus interactions produced an anomalous spike. The Q3 FY2024 increase is attributed primarily to elevated withholding from financial sector bonuses paid in January 2024, estimated by the Department of Revenue at approximately $390 million of the total gain. The remaining $283 million reflects improved employment figures, with Pennsylvania's seasonally adjusted unemployment rate declining from 3.8 percent in December 2023 to 3.5 percent in March 2024.

Sales and Use Tax: The marginal decline of $17.4 million in Sales and Use Tax reflects post-holiday retail contraction typical of the January-March period. Collections of $3.087 billion remain 2.1 percent above the official estimate published in the Governor's Executive Budget submitted February 6, 2024.

Other Revenue: The $730.8 million increase in Other Revenue is largely attributable to a $698 million settlement received February 14, 2024 from a multistate litigation agreement involving pharmaceutical distribution companies, including AmerisourceBergen Corporation, Cardinal Health, and McKesson Corporation, related to opioid remediation funding. This non-recurring item significantly inflated Q3 Other Revenue figures and should not be extrapolated into future quarters.

2.2 Notable Expenditure Developments

Medical Assistance: The $286.7 million increase in Medicaid expenditures represents the most significant quarter-over-quarter change in the expenditure portfolio. Secretary of Human Services Dr. Valerie Arkoosh attributed the increase to three primary factors: (1) the ongoing unwinding of continuous enrollment provisions that paradoxically increased costs as higher-acuity individuals were identified during redetermination processes; (2) a 3.2 percent rate increase for long-term care providers effective January 1, 2024; and (3) elevated pharmaceutical costs associated with GLP-1 receptor agonist medications, which the Department of Human Services estimates added approximately $41 million in Q3 expenditures compared to Q2.

All Other Expenditures: The $222.3 million increase in the All Other category reflects a $187 million disbursement made March 8, 2024 from the Commonwealth's Rainy Day Fund to the Pennsylvania Emergency Management Agency (PEMA) for costs associated with the February 2024 winter storm events affecting 42 of Pennsylvania's 67 counties. Governor Josh Shapiro declared a disaster emergency on February 13, 2024, triggering the expenditure authorization.

Higher Education: Quarterly appropriations to the four state-related universities — Penn State University, the University of Pittsburgh, Temple University, and Lincoln University — remained flat at $381.2 million, consistent with the annual appropriation structure that distributes funds in equal quarterly installments.

SECTION 3: FUND BALANCE AND CASH POSITION

As of March 31, 2024, the General Fund unappropriated balance stood at $2.87 billion, compared to $3.10 billion as of December 31, 2023, a decrease of $230 million. The Commonwealth's Rainy Day Fund (formally the Budget Stabilization Reserve Fund) held $1.83 billion as of March 31, 2024, reduced from $2.02 billion following the February emergency disbursement.

The State Treasury, under Treasurer Stacy Garrity, reported that the Commonwealth's short-term investment pool generated $87.4 million in interest income during Q3, compared to $91.2 million in Q2, reflecting a slight reduction in average daily cash balances available for investment. The weighted average yield on the investment pool was 5.31 percent as of March 31, 2024.

---

SECTION 4: YEAR-TO-DATE PERFORMANCE VERSUS ESTIMATE

Through nine months of fiscal year 2023-2024 (July 1, 2023 through March 31, 2024), total General Fund revenues of $31.96 billion are running $412 million, or 1.3 percent, above the official estimate. Total expenditures of $34.19 billion are $228 million, or 0.7 percent, above estimate. The cumulative year-to-date deficit of $2.23 billion compares to an estimated deficit of $2.05 billion for the same period, a variance of $180 million.

The Office of the Budget projects that full-year revenues will reach $43.8 billion against full-year appropriations of $45.0 billion, with the resulting gap of $1.2 billion to be addressed through a combination of Q4 corporate tax settlements, lapses in unspent agency appropriations estimated at $340 million, and a planned $860 million transfer from the Rainy Day Fund authorized under Act 7 of 2023.

---

SECTION 5: OUTLOOK AND RISK FACTORS

The Office of the Budget identifies the following material risks to Q4 FY2023-2024 projections:

- Federal Medicaid matching rate adjustments pending CMS rulemaking could reduce federal reimbursements by an estimated $95 million to $180 million in Q4.
- Corporate Net Income Tax collections are sensitive to federal bonus depreciation provisions currently under Congressional consideration; a failure to extend 100 percent bonus depreciation could reduce Q4 CNIT receipts by approximately $220 million.
- The Pennsylvania Supreme Court's pending decision in William Penn School District v. Pennsylvania Department of Education, expected no later than June 30, 2024, may require supplemental education appropriations estimated between $1.1 billion and $4.5 billion annually if the Court orders a comprehensive remedy.
