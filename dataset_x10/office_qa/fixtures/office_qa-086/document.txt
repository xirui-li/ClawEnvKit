EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents a comprehensive Compound Annual Growth Rate (CAGR) analysis of the Federal Highway Trust Fund (HTF) for the ten-year period spanning Fiscal Year 2014 through Fiscal Year 2024. The analysis was commissioned in response to Congressional Budget Office inquiry dated August 3, 2024, and incorporates data from the Federal Highway Administration (FHWA), the Internal Revenue Service Excise Tax Division, and the Bureau of the Fiscal Service. The Highway Trust Fund, established under the Federal Aid Highway Act of 1956, receives its primary revenues from federal motor fuel excise taxes and related transportation-related levies.

Over the ten-year measurement period, the Highway Account of the Trust Fund recorded a CAGR of 3.47 percent, while the Mass Transit Account recorded a CAGR of 4.12 percent. Total combined Trust Fund receipts grew from $37.8 billion in FY2014 to $53.6 billion in FY2024, representing cumulative growth of 41.8 percent over the decade.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SECTION 1: BACKGROUND AND FUND STRUCTURE

The Federal Highway Trust Fund is divided into two primary accounts:

1. The Highway Account, which funds federal-aid highway programs and receives approximately 84.4 percent of total motor fuel tax receipts.
2. The Mass Transit Account, which funds transit capital and operating assistance and receives approximately 15.6 percent of total motor fuel tax receipts.

Primary revenue sources include:
- Federal gasoline excise tax: 18.4 cents per gallon (unchanged since 1993)
- Federal diesel fuel excise tax: 24.4 cents per gallon
- Heavy vehicle use tax: $550 per year for vehicles weighing 55,000 pounds, with graduated rates up to $100 per year per 1,000 pounds over 75,000 pounds
- Truck and trailer sales taxes: 12 percent on retail price of heavy trucks and trailers
- Tire excise taxes: graduated rates based on tire weight

The Trust Fund does not receive general fund appropriations under normal operating conditions, though Congress authorized general fund transfers totaling $143.9 billion between FY2008 and FY2021 to maintain solvency during periods when excise tax receipts were insufficient to cover authorized expenditures.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SECTION 2: CAGR METHODOLOGY

The Compound Annual Growth Rate is calculated using the following standard formula:

CAGR = (Ending Value / Beginning Value)^(1/n) - 1

Where:
- Ending Value = Total Trust Fund receipts in FY2024
- Beginning Value = Total Trust Fund receipts in FY2014
- n = Number of years in the measurement period (10)

For this analysis, "receipts" are defined as net excise tax transfers to the Trust Fund after IRS administrative costs, inclusive of interest earnings credited to the fund but exclusive of general fund transfers and interfund borrowings. All figures are reported in nominal dollars unless otherwise noted. Inflation-adjusted (real) CAGR figures using the GDP Price Deflator are provided in Section 4.

CAGR Calculations:

Highway Account CAGR (FY2014–FY2024):
= ($45.21 / $31.86)^(1/10) - 1
= (1.4190)^(0.10) - 1
= 1.03566 - 1
= 3.57 percent

Mass Transit Account CAGR (FY2014–FY2024):
= ($8.39 / $5.94)^(1/10) - 1
= (1.4124)^(0.10) - 1
= 1.03497 - 1
= 3.50 percent

Combined Trust Fund CAGR (FY2014–FY2024):
= ($53.60 / $37.80)^(1/10) - 1
= (1.4180)^(0.10) - 1
= 1.03556 - 1
= 3.56 percent

Note: The combined CAGR of 3.56 percent reflects a slight revision from the preliminary estimate of 3.47 percent published in the August 2024 interim report, due to upward revision of FY2024 fourth-quarter diesel excise tax receipts by $0.34 billion following IRS reconciliation completed October 31, 2024.

SECTION 4: INFLATION-ADJUSTED ANALYSIS

When adjusted for inflation using the GDP Price Deflator (base year FY2012 = 100), the real CAGR of total Trust Fund receipts over the ten-year period is substantially lower, reflecting the erosion of purchasing power and the static nature of per-gallon excise tax rates.

TABLE 2: Real vs. Nominal CAGR Comparison

Account               | Nominal CAGR | Real CAGR (GDP Deflator) | Difference
Highway Account       | 3.57%        | 0.31%                    | -3.26 pp
Mass Transit Account  | 3.50%        | 0.24%                    | -3.26 pp
Combined Total        | 3.56%        | 0.30%                    | -3.26 pp

The near-zero real CAGR underscores a persistent structural challenge facing the Trust Fund: because the federal motor fuel excise tax rate has not been adjusted since October 1, 1993, the real value of per-gallon revenue has declined by approximately 47 percent since that date when measured against the Consumer Price Index for All Urban Consumers (CPI-U). The Congressional Budget Office estimated in its September 2024 Budget and Economic Outlook that maintaining current purchasing power of Trust Fund revenues would require a gasoline tax rate of approximately 34.2 cents per gallon in 2024 dollars.

SECTION 5: NOTABLE ANOMALIES AND EXPLANATORY FACTORS

FY2020 Revenue Decline
Total receipts fell by $3.00 billion (6.94 percent) in FY2020 compared to FY2019, representing the largest single-year decline in the measurement period. This decline is directly attributable to reduced vehicle miles traveled (VMT) during the COVID-19 pandemic. The Federal Highway Administration reported that total VMT fell by 430.2 billion miles in calendar year 2020, a 13.2 percent reduction from 2019 levels. Gasoline consumption declined by 10.8 percent year-over-year, reducing Highway Account receipts by $2.53 billion.

FY2022–FY2023 Accelerated Growth
The Trust Fund experienced above-trend growth of 10.43 percent in FY2022 and 11.54 percent in FY2023. Contributing factors include:
- Post-pandemic VMT recovery, with total VMT reaching 3.174 trillion miles in calendar year 2022, surpassing the pre-pandemic 2019 level of 3.261 trillion miles by approximately 2.7 percent below, then recovering fully by mid-2023
- Increased heavy truck activity driven by supply chain normalization and elevated freight demand, boosting diesel excise tax receipts
- Higher truck and trailer sales volumes in FY2022, with retail sales subject to the 12 percent excise tax increasing by 18.3 percent year-over-year

Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (IIJA) Impact
The Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act, signed into law on November 15, 2021, authorized $550 billion in new federal infrastructure spending over five years. While the IIJA does not directly alter excise tax rates, it authorized $118 billion in additional general fund transfers to the Highway Trust Fund through FY2026 to supplement excise tax receipts and fund expanded program authorizations.

SECTION 6: FIVE-YEAR FORWARD PROJECTION

Based on the ten-year historical CAGR of 3.56 percent and incorporating FHWA vehicle miles traveled projections, the Office of Fiscal Analysis projects the following Trust Fund receipt trajectory through FY2029:

These projections assume no legislative changes to excise tax rates, continued growth in electric vehicle adoption at the rate projected by the Energy Information Administration (EIA) Annual Energy Outlook 2024, and macroeconomic conditions consistent with the Office of Management and Budget's FY2025 Mid-Session Review baseline. The EIA projects that electric vehicles will account for 14.7 percent of new light-duty vehicle sales by FY2029, which the FHWA estimates will reduce gasoline excise tax receipts by approximately $1.2 billion annually by that year relative to a no-EV-adoption baseline.

SECTION 7: CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS

The Federal Highway Trust Fund demonstrated a nominal CAGR of 3.56 percent over the FY2014–FY2024 period, driven primarily by post-pandemic VMT recovery and elevated commercial vehicle activity. However, the real CAGR of 0.30 percent reveals that the Trust Fund's revenue base has remained essentially flat in inflation-adjusted terms, a consequence of the unchanged 18.4 cents per gallon gasoline tax rate in effect since 1993.

The Division of Transportation Finance recommends that Congress consider the following policy options, which are presented for informational purposes only and do not constitute official Administration policy:

1. Indexing the motor fuel excise tax to the CPI-U, which the Joint Committee on Taxation estimates would generate an additional $8.3 billion in annual receipts by FY2027 if implemented in FY2025.
2. Establishing a vehicle miles traveled fee pilot program as authorized under Section 13001 of the IIJA, with full program evaluation to be completed by December 31, 2026.
3. Expanding the heavy vehicle use tax to include electric commercial vehicles, which the Congressional Budget Office estimates could generate $340 million annually by FY2028.
