DEPARTMENT OF THE TREASURY
OFFICE OF FISCAL ANALYSIS

ANNUAL TAX REVENUE ANALYSIS
FISCAL YEAR 2023

Published: February 14, 2024
Report Number: OFA-2024-TR-007

---

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Total federal tax revenue for Fiscal Year 2023 (October 1, 2022 through September 30, 2023) reached $4.44 trillion, representing a decline of 9.3 percent from the record $4.90 trillion collected in Fiscal Year 2022. This contraction marks the first year-over-year decrease in federal tax receipts since Fiscal Year 2020, when revenues fell 1.2 percent during the initial phase of the COVID-19 pandemic. Despite the overall decline, individual income tax collections remained the dominant revenue source, accounting for 49.3 percent of all federal receipts. This report provides a comprehensive breakdown of revenue by category, examines contributing economic factors, and offers projections for Fiscal Year 2024.

---

SECTION 1: TOTAL REVENUE OVERVIEW

Federal receipts by major category for Fiscal Year 2023 are as follows:

Individual Income Taxes: $2.176 trillion
Payroll Taxes (Social Insurance): $1.614 trillion
Corporate Income Taxes: $420.0 billion
Excise Taxes: $78.9 billion
Estate and Gift Taxes: $34.0 billion
Customs Duties: $80.0 billion
Miscellaneous Receipts: $37.1 billion

TOTAL: $4.440 trillion

For comparative purposes, Fiscal Year 2022 collections were:

Individual Income Taxes: $2.632 trillion
Payroll Taxes: $1.483 trillion
Corporate Income Taxes: $425.0 billion
Excise Taxes: $75.3 billion
Estate and Gift Taxes: $37.2 billion
Customs Duties: $99.0 billion
Miscellaneous Receipts: $148.5 billion

TOTAL: $4.900 trillion

The $460 billion decline between fiscal years represents the largest nominal dollar decrease in federal revenue since records have been maintained in the modern budgetary framework. As a share of Gross Domestic Product, total federal revenues fell from 19.6 percent in FY2022 to 16.5 percent in FY2023, a reduction of 3.1 percentage points.

---

SECTION 2: INDIVIDUAL INCOME TAX ANALYSIS

Individual income taxes declined by $456 billion, or 17.3 percent, between FY2022 and FY2023. Treasury economists, led by Deputy Assistant Secretary for Tax Analysis Janet Holbrook, attribute this decline primarily to three factors:

1. Reduced capital gains realizations. In calendar year 2022, equity markets experienced significant losses, with the S&P 500 declining 19.4 percent over the course of the year. Taxpayers who realized gains in 2021 — when markets rose 26.9 percent — generated substantial tax liability paid in April 2022 (within FY2022). The reversal of this pattern reduced estimated tax payments and final settlements throughout FY2023.

2. Expiration of pandemic-era provisions. Several temporary tax credits established under the American Rescue Plan Act of 2021, including the expanded Child Tax Credit of up to $3,600 per child, were not renewed for tax year 2022. While this reduced refund outlays, the net effect on receipts was a modest negative, as lower advance credit payments had already been incorporated into withholding tables.

3. Increased standard deduction utilization. For tax year 2022, the standard deduction rose to $12,950 for single filers and $25,900 for married couples filing jointly, reflecting inflation adjustments under the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017. Approximately 87.3 percent of all returns filed in 2023 claimed the standard deduction, up from 85.1 percent in the prior year.

Monthly individual income tax collections showed notable variation. The highest single-month collection occurred in April 2023, when $638.4 billion was received, reflecting final payments for tax year 2022. This compared to April 2022's record collection of $864.2 billion. The lowest monthly collection in FY2023 occurred in November 2022, at $98.7 billion.

---

SECTION 3: PAYROLL TAX ANALYSIS

Payroll tax collections increased by $131 billion, or 8.8 percent, rising from $1.483 trillion in FY2022 to $1.614 trillion in FY2023. This growth reflects continued labor market strength throughout the fiscal year. The Bureau of Labor Statistics reported that total nonfarm payroll employment grew by approximately 3.0 million jobs between October 2022 and September 2023. Average hourly earnings for all private-sector employees rose 4.3 percent year-over-year as of September 2023, directly expanding the wage base subject to Social Security and Medicare taxes.

The Social Security taxable wage base increased from $147,000 in calendar year 2022 to $160,200 in calendar year 2023, a jump of $13,200 or 9.0 percent — the largest single-year increase in the program's history. This adjustment, made by the Social Security Administration in October 2022, contributed an estimated additional $22.4 billion in Old-Age, Survivors, and Disability Insurance receipts during the fiscal year.

Medicare payroll taxes, which carry no wage cap, generated $387.6 billion in FY2023, compared to $356.1 billion in FY2022, an increase of 8.8 percent consistent with overall wage growth.

---

SECTION 4: CORPORATE INCOME TAX ANALYSIS

Corporate income tax receipts totaled $420.0 billion in FY2023, a decrease of $5.0 billion or 1.2 percent from the $425.0 billion collected in FY2022. This relative stability masks significant underlying shifts. The Inflation Reduction Act of 2022, signed into law on August 16, 2022, introduced a 15 percent Corporate Alternative Minimum Tax (CAMT) applicable to corporations with average annual adjusted financial statement income exceeding $1 billion. Treasury estimates that the CAMT generated approximately $8.5 billion in additional revenue during FY2023, its first year of implementation.

Partially offsetting this new revenue source was a decline in corporate profitability across several sectors. S&P 500 aggregate earnings per share fell approximately 4.6 percent in calendar year 2022 compared to 2021, reducing estimated tax payments made in FY2023. The financial sector, which contributed $67.3 billion in corporate taxes in FY2022, saw its contribution fall to $54.8 billion in FY2023, a decline of 18.6 percent, driven by higher loan loss provisions and reduced investment banking activity.

The technology sector remained the largest single industry contributor to corporate tax receipts, generating an estimated $89.2 billion, or 21.2 percent of total corporate income tax revenue, despite a sector-wide contraction in profit margins.

---

SECTION 5: EXCISE TAXES, CUSTOMS, AND OTHER RECEIPTS

Excise tax collections rose modestly from $75.3 billion in FY2022 to $78.9 billion in FY2023, an increase of 4.8 percent. Highway-related excise taxes, including the federal gasoline tax of 18.4 cents per gallon and the diesel tax of 24.4 cents per gallon, contributed $39.7 billion. Aviation excise taxes generated $17.4 billion, reflecting a full recovery in air travel demand to pre-pandemic levels. Tobacco excise taxes continued their long-term decline, falling to $11.2 billion from $12.1 billion in FY2022.

Customs duties declined sharply from $99.0 billion in FY2022 to $80.0 billion in FY2023, a decrease of 19.2 percent. This decline reflects reduced import volumes as consumer spending shifted from goods toward services, as well as lower commodity prices reducing the dutiable value of certain imports. Imports from China, subject to Section 301 tariffs ranging from 7.5 to 25 percent on approximately $370 billion in goods, generated an estimated $42.3 billion in duties during FY2023.

Miscellaneous receipts fell dramatically from $148.5 billion in FY2022 to $37.1 billion in FY2023. The FY2022 figure was inflated by $111.0 billion in Federal Reserve remittances to the Treasury, which ceased entirely in FY2023 as the Federal Reserve began operating at a net loss due to interest paid on reserve balances exceeding earnings on its securities portfolio.

---

SECTION 6: STATE-LEVEL REVENUE CONTEXT

While federal revenue declined, state and local tax collections demonstrated greater resilience. According to data compiled by the Urban-Brookings Tax Policy Center, aggregate state tax revenues for calendar year 2022 totaled approximately $1.19 trillion, a 15.6 percent increase over 2021. California remained the largest state tax collector with $234.6 billion in total state tax revenue for FY2022-23, followed by New York at $109.4 billion and Texas at $87.2 billion. Texas, which levies no individual income tax, derived 57.4 percent of its state tax revenue from sales and use taxes.

---

SECTION 7: FISCAL YEAR 2024 PROJECTIONS

The Congressional Budget Office, in its February 2024 Budget and Economic Outlook, projects federal revenues of $4.73 trillion for Fiscal Year 2024, representing a recovery of 6.5 percent from FY2023 levels. Individual income taxes are projected to rebound to $2.39 trillion as capital gains realizations normalize and wage growth continues. Corporate income taxes are projected at $503.0 billion, reflecting the full-year impact of the Corporate Alternative Minimum Tax and anticipated improvement in corporate earnings.

The Office of Management and Budget, under Director Shalanda Young, projects a slightly more optimistic revenue figure of $4.81 trillion for FY2024, citing stronger-than-expected labor market performance in the first quarter of the fiscal year. October and November 2023 combined receipts of $527.4 billion came in 8.2 percent above the same period in FY2023, providing early support for the more optimistic projection.

---

CONCLUSION

Fiscal Year 2023 presented a complex revenue environment characterized by declining individual income tax receipts driven by capital markets normalization, offset partially by robust payroll tax growth reflecting continued labor market strength. The implementation of new corporate tax provisions under the Inflation Reduction Act provided a modest but meaningful new revenue stream. Looking ahead, the convergence of normalizing capital gains activity, sustained employment growth, and new statutory revenue measures positions federal receipts for a meaningful recovery in Fiscal Year 2024.
