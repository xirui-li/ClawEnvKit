DEPARTMENT OF THE TREASURY
INTERNAL REVENUE SERVICE
FISCAL YEAR 2023 TAX REVENUE ANALYSIS REPORT
Published: February 14, 2024

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The United States federal government collected $4.44 trillion in total tax revenue during Fiscal Year 2023 (October 1, 2022 through September 30, 2023), representing a decline of 9.3 percent from the record $4.90 trillion collected in Fiscal Year 2022. This report, prepared by the Office of Tax Analysis under the direction of Assistant Secretary for Tax Policy Lily Batchelder, examines the composition, trends, and contributing factors behind federal tax receipts across all major revenue categories. Despite the year-over-year decline, FY2023 revenues remained 17.4 percent above pre-pandemic FY2019 levels of $3.46 trillion, reflecting sustained economic growth and elevated nominal incomes.

SECTION 1: TOTAL REVENUE OVERVIEW

Federal tax receipts for FY2023 totaled $4.44 trillion, falling short of the Office of Management and Budget's February 2023 projection of $4.73 trillion by approximately $290 billion. The Congressional Budget Office had similarly projected $4.71 trillion in its May 2023 baseline estimate. The shortfall was driven primarily by reduced capital gains realizations, lower corporate profits, and a significant decline in nonwithheld individual income tax payments compared to the extraordinary asset appreciation environment of 2021 and early 2022.

Total federal outlays for FY2023 were $6.13 trillion, producing a unified budget deficit of $1.70 trillion — an increase of $320 billion from the FY2022 deficit of $1.38 trillion. The deficit as a percentage of GDP stood at 6.3 percent, up from 5.4 percent in FY2022.

SECTION 3: INDIVIDUAL INCOME TAX ANALYSIS

Individual income taxes remained the largest single source of federal revenue at $2.176 trillion, accounting for 49.0 percent of total receipts. However, this represented a decline of $456 billion, or 17.3 percent, from FY2022's record $2.632 trillion — the largest single-year dollar decline in individual income tax receipts in U.S. history.

Key drivers of the decline include:

- Capital Gains Realizations: Estimated net capital gains reported on 2022 tax returns fell to approximately $1.1 trillion from $1.9 trillion in 2021, a decline of roughly 42 percent. This directly reduced nonwithheld tax payments made in April, June, and September 2023.

- Nonwithheld Payments: Quarterly estimated tax payments and final balance-due payments declined by an estimated $380 billion year-over-year, reflecting both lower capital gains and reduced pass-through business income.

- Withheld Taxes: Despite the overall decline, taxes withheld from wages and salaries grew by approximately 3.2 percent, reaching an estimated $1.87 trillion, consistent with continued labor market strength. The unemployment rate averaged 3.6 percent during calendar year 2023, and average hourly earnings grew 4.3 percent year-over-year as of September 2023.

- Refunds: Individual income tax refunds totaled $334 billion in FY2023, compared to $321 billion in FY2022, a modest increase of 4.1 percent. The average refund amount for returns processed through September 30, 2023 was $2,812, down from $3,039 in the prior year.

SECTION 4: PAYROLL TAX ANALYSIS

Payroll taxes, which fund Social Security and Medicare programs, increased by $130 billion to $1.614 trillion in FY2023, representing 36.4 percent of total federal receipts. This was the strongest growth category among major revenue sources.

The Social Security taxable wage base increased from $147,000 in calendar year 2022 to $160,200 in calendar year 2023, an increase of 8.98 percent — the largest percentage increase since 1983. The combined employee-employer Social Security tax rate remained at 12.4 percent, and the Medicare tax rate remained at 2.9 percent, with the additional 0.9 percent Medicare surtax applying to wages above $200,000 for single filers and $250,000 for married filers.

Total employment covered by Social Security reached approximately 182.5 million workers in 2023, with total covered wages estimated at $10.8 trillion. Payroll tax receipts benefited from both the higher wage base and continued strong employment growth, with nonfarm payrolls expanding by 2.7 million jobs during calendar year 2023.

SECTION 5: CORPORATE INCOME TAX ANALYSIS

Corporate income tax receipts declined modestly by $5 billion, or 1.2 percent, to $420 billion in FY2023. Corporate receipts had surged to a 35-year high of $425 billion in FY2022 following the strong profit environment of 2021. The slight decline reflected moderating corporate profit growth amid higher interest costs and slowing economic activity in interest-sensitive sectors.

The statutory corporate income tax rate remained at 21 percent, as established by the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of December 2017. The new 15 percent Corporate Alternative Minimum Tax (CAMT), enacted as part of the Inflation Reduction Act signed by President Biden on August 16, 2022, applied for the first time to tax years beginning after December 31, 2022. Treasury estimates that the CAMT generated approximately $5 billion in additional corporate tax liability in its first year of applicability, partially offsetting declines from lower book income among affected corporations.

The 1 percent excise tax on corporate stock buybacks, also enacted under the Inflation Reduction Act and effective January 1, 2023, generated approximately $8.5 billion in FY2023, included within the excise tax category above.

SECTION 6: EXCISE TAXES AND OTHER RECEIPTS

Federal excise taxes totaled $92 billion in FY2023, up 5.7 percent from $87 billion in FY2022. Major components included:

- Highway Trust Fund excise taxes (motor fuels): $38.4 billion
- Airport and Airway Trust Fund taxes: $17.2 billion
- Tobacco taxes: $12.1 billion
- Alcohol taxes: $9.8 billion
- Stock buyback excise tax: $8.5 billion
- Other excise taxes: $6.0 billion

Customs duties declined sharply to $80 billion from $99 billion in FY2022, a drop of 19.2 percent, reflecting lower import volumes as consumer goods demand moderated following the post-pandemic surge and as some tariff exclusions were extended.

Estate and gift taxes rose to $35 billion, reflecting continued elevated asset values despite equity market volatility. The estate tax exemption for 2023 was $12.92 million per individual, up from $12.06 million in 2022.

SECTION 7: HISTORICAL CONTEXT AND TREND ANALYSIS

Federal tax revenues as a percentage of GDP have fluctuated considerably over the past decade:

Fiscal Year | Revenue ($ trillions) | % of GDP
2019 | 3.46 | 16.3%
2020 | 3.42 | 16.3%
2021 | 4.05 | 18.1%
2022 | 4.90 | 19.6%
2023 | 4.44 | 16.5%

The FY2022 revenue-to-GDP ratio of 19.6 percent was the highest since 2000, when revenues reached 20.6 percent of GDP during the dot-com boom. The FY2023 ratio of 16.5 percent represents a return toward the 40-year historical average of approximately 17.4 percent of GDP.

SECTION 8: OUTLOOK FOR FISCAL YEAR 2024

The Congressional Budget Office's February 2024 baseline projects FY2024 federal revenues of $4.94 trillion, representing a recovery of 11.3 percent from FY2023 levels. This projection assumes continued labor market strength, modest capital gains realizations, and the full-year effect of the Inflation Reduction Act revenue provisions. Individual income taxes are projected to recover to approximately $2.43 trillion, while payroll taxes are expected to grow to $1.73 trillion as the Social Security taxable wage base increases to $168,600 for calendar year 2024.
