OFFICE OF DEBT MANAGEMENT
U.S. DEPARTMENT OF THE TREASURY
FISCAL YEAR 2023 ANNUAL REPORT

YIELD SPREAD ANALYSIS: FEDERAL DEBT INSTRUMENTS AND MARKET CONDITIONS
Prepared by the Office of Debt Management, Bureau of the Fiscal Service
Publication Date: November 14, 2023

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents a comprehensive analysis of yield spreads across U.S. Treasury securities for fiscal year 2023 (October 1, 2022 through September 30, 2023). The analysis examines the relationship between short-term and long-term Treasury yields, compares domestic sovereign spreads against select foreign government benchmarks, and evaluates the cost implications for federal borrowing. During FY2023, the Federal Reserve raised the federal funds rate target seven times, bringing the upper bound from 4.50 percent to 5.50 percent, creating significant pressure on the yield curve and producing an inverted spread environment that persisted for the majority of the fiscal year.

Total federal debt subject to the statutory limit stood at $33.17 trillion as of September 30, 2023. Net interest costs to the federal government reached $659 billion in FY2023, representing a 39 percent increase over the $474 billion recorded in FY2022 and constituting 14.7 percent of total federal outlays.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SECTION 1: YIELD CURVE OVERVIEW AND INVERSION METRICS

The U.S. Treasury yield curve remained inverted throughout most of FY2023, a condition that began in July 2022 and extended well into the current reporting period. The 2-year/10-year spread, widely regarded as the primary inversion indicator, reached its most negative point on March 8, 2023, when the 2-year Treasury yield closed at 5.07 percent and the 10-year yield closed at 3.96 percent, producing a spread of negative 111 basis points.

Key yield benchmarks as of September 29, 2023 (fiscal year-end close):

  3-Month Treasury Bill:        5.45 percent
  6-Month Treasury Bill:        5.54 percent
  1-Year Treasury Note:         5.44 percent
  2-Year Treasury Note:         5.03 percent
  5-Year Treasury Note:         4.61 percent
  10-Year Treasury Note:        4.57 percent
  20-Year Treasury Bond:        4.96 percent
  30-Year Treasury Bond:        4.70 percent

The 2-year/10-year spread at fiscal year-end stood at negative 46 basis points, a narrowing from the March extreme but still deeply inverted by historical standards. The 3-month/10-year spread, which the Federal Reserve Bank of New York uses in its recession probability model, registered negative 88 basis points at year-end, the widest negative reading for that metric since 1981.

SECTION 2: HISTORICAL SPREAD COMPARISON

The following table presents the 2-year/10-year spread at fiscal year-end for the preceding five years, illustrating the dramatic shift in the yield curve environment.

FISCAL YEAR-END 2-YEAR/10-YEAR SPREAD (BASIS POINTS)

  FY2019 (Sept. 30, 2019):     +5 bps
  FY2020 (Sept. 30, 2020):     +55 bps
  FY2021 (Sept. 30, 2021):     +122 bps
  FY2022 (Sept. 30, 2022):     -42 bps
  FY2023 (Sept. 30, 2023):     -46 bps

The steepest positive slope in the five-year window occurred at the end of FY2021, when accommodative monetary policy and pandemic-era fiscal stimulus contributed to a 122 basis point premium for 10-year notes over 2-year notes. The transition from a 122 basis point positive spread in FY2021 to a 46 basis point negative spread in FY2023 represents a 168 basis point swing in 24 months, the largest two-year shift in the 2/10 spread since the tightening cycle of 1979-1981 under Federal Reserve Chairman Paul Volcker.

SECTION 3: COST IMPLICATIONS FOR FEDERAL BORROWING

The inverted yield curve has had material consequences for the composition and cost of federal debt issuance. The Office of Debt Management issued $22.7 trillion in gross Treasury securities during FY2023, of which $20.1 trillion represented rollovers of maturing debt and $2.6 trillion represented net new issuance to finance the $1.695 trillion federal deficit recorded for the fiscal year.

The weighted average interest rate on total outstanding marketable debt rose from 1.94 percent at the start of FY2023 to 2.92 percent by September 30, 2023. This 98 basis point increase in the weighted average rate over a single fiscal year is the largest annual increase since 1982 and directly reflects the rollover of low-rate pandemic-era debt into the current high-rate environment.

Bills (maturities of one year or less) accounted for 22.4 percent of total marketable debt outstanding as of September 30, 2023, compared to 15.8 percent at the end of FY2021. This shift toward shorter-duration instruments reflects both market demand and Treasury's tactical response to the inverted curve, where issuing at the short end—despite higher nominal rates—avoids locking in elevated long-term rates should the curve normalize.

Average auction results for selected securities in FY2023:

  4-Week Treasury Bill:         Average stop-out rate of 5.28 percent
                                Average bid-to-cover ratio of 2.87
  13-Week Treasury Bill:        Average stop-out rate of 5.19 percent
                                Average bid-to-cover ratio of 3.12
  2-Year Treasury Note:         Average stop-out rate of 4.71 percent
                                Average bid-to-cover ratio of 2.63
  10-Year Treasury Note:        Average stop-out rate of 3.96 percent
                                Average bid-to-cover ratio of 2.41

The declining bid-to-cover ratios at longer maturities reflect reduced institutional appetite for duration risk in an uncertain rate environment, a trend that contributed to the August 2023 Treasury market volatility following Fitch Ratings' downgrade of U.S. long-term debt from AAA to AA+ on August 1, 2023.

SECTION 4: INTERNATIONAL SOVEREIGN SPREAD COMPARISONS

To contextualize U.S. Treasury yields within the global sovereign debt market, the Office of Debt Management tracks yield spreads between 10-year U.S. Treasury notes and comparable benchmark instruments issued by select foreign governments. All figures represent September 29, 2023 closing yields.

10-YEAR SOVEREIGN YIELD SPREADS VERSUS U.S. TREASURY (BASIS POINTS)

  Germany (Bund):               4.57% - 2.84% = +173 bps (U.S. premium)
  United Kingdom (Gilt):        4.57% - 4.44% = +13 bps (U.S. premium)
  Japan (JGB):                  4.57% - 0.76% = +381 bps (U.S. premium)
  Canada:                       4.57% - 4.21% = +36 bps (U.S. premium)
  Australia:                    4.57% - 4.48% = +9 bps (U.S. premium)
  France (OAT):                 4.57% - 3.47% = +110 bps (U.S. premium)
  Italy (BTP):                  4.57% - 4.91% = -34 bps (U.S. discount)

The U.S. premium over German Bunds of 173 basis points is the widest since reunification-era divergence in the early 1990s and reflects the more aggressive tightening path pursued by the Federal Reserve relative to the European Central Bank. The negative spread versus Italian BTPs of 34 basis points—meaning Italian 10-year bonds yielded more than comparable U.S. Treasuries—reflects the ongoing sovereign risk premium embedded in Italian debt due to Italy's debt-to-GDP ratio of approximately 140 percent.

The U.S./Japan spread of 381 basis points warrants particular attention. The Bank of Japan maintained its yield curve control policy through most of FY2023, capping 10-year JGB yields near 0.50 percent before adjusting the ceiling to 1.00 percent in July 2023. This artificial suppression of Japanese yields has contributed to sustained yen weakness and significant capital flows into U.S. dollar-denominated assets, supporting demand at Treasury auctions from Japanese institutional investors.

SECTION 5: TIPS BREAKEVEN INFLATION SPREADS

Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS) breakeven rates—the spread between nominal Treasury yields and real TIPS yields of equivalent maturity—provide a market-based measure of inflation expectations embedded in federal borrowing costs.

TIPS BREAKEVEN RATES AS OF SEPTEMBER 29, 2023

  5-Year Breakeven:             2.44 percent
  10-Year Breakeven:            2.33 percent
  30-Year Breakeven:            2.38 percent

The 10-year breakeven rate of 2.33 percent compares to 2.16 percent at the start of FY2023 and represents a modest increase in long-run inflation expectations despite the Federal Reserve's tightening campaign. The 5-year/5-year forward breakeven—measuring expected inflation from 2028 to 2033—stood at 2.22 percent, suggesting market participants believe the Fed will ultimately succeed in returning inflation to near its 2 percent target over the medium term.

The real yield on 10-year TIPS reached 2.24 percent at fiscal year-end, the highest level since January 2009, implying that investors are demanding meaningful compensation above expected inflation for holding long-duration government debt.

SECTION 6: OUTLOOK AND RISK FACTORS

The Office of Debt Management identifies the following primary risks to the federal borrowing cost outlook for FY2024:

1. RATE NORMALIZATION TIMING: If the Federal Reserve begins cutting rates in the first half of calendar year 2024, as implied by federal funds futures pricing as of September 30, 2023 (which assigned a 68 percent probability to at least one cut by June 2024), the yield curve may begin to disinvert, potentially increasing long-term borrowing costs even as short-term rates decline.

2. DEBT CEILING UNCERTAINTY: The Fiscal Responsibility Act of 2023, signed by President Biden on June 3, 2023, suspended the debt ceiling through January 1, 2025. The subsequent resumption of the limit will require extraordinary measures and may create temporary distortions in bill issuance patterns.

3. FOREIGN DEMAND SENSITIVITY: Foreign and international investors held $7.62 trillion in U.S. Treasury securities as of July 2023, representing 23 percent of total outstanding debt. Any reduction in foreign central bank reserve accumulation, particularly from China (which held $821 billion) or Japan (which held $1.10 trillion), could pressure yields upward.

4. TERM PREMIUM COMPRESSION: The term premium on 10-year Treasuries, as estimated by the Adrian-Crump-Moench model maintained by the Federal Reserve Bank of New York, turned positive in September 2023 for the first time since 2021, reaching 0.19 percent. Further increases in the term premium would raise long-term borrowing costs independent of Federal Reserve policy actions.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

CONCLUSION

FY2023 represented one of the most challenging borrowing environments for the federal government in four decades. Net interest costs of $659 billion, a weighted average debt rate increase of 98 basis points, and a persistently inverted yield curve collectively underscore the sensitivity of federal finances to monetary policy conditions. The Office of Debt Management will continue to monitor yield spread dynamics and adjust issuance strategy accordingly, with quarterly updates to be published in the Quarterly Refunding Announcements scheduled for November 1, 2023, February 2024, May 2024, and August 2024.
