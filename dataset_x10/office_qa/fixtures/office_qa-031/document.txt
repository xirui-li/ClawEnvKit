EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Department of Defense submitted a Fiscal Year 2024 budget request totaling $842 billion, representing a 3.2 percent increase over the enacted FY2023 budget of $816.7 billion. This report provides a comprehensive analysis of defense appropriations, expenditure trends, program-level allocations, and comparative international benchmarks. The analysis draws on data from the Office of Management and Budget, the Congressional Budget Office, the Defense Finance and Accounting Service, and the Stockholm International Peace Research Institute (SIPRI).

Defense spending as a share of Gross Domestic Product stands at approximately 3.1 percent for FY2024, down from a post-9/11 peak of 4.7 percent recorded in FY2010. Total obligated authority across all defense-related accounts, including military construction and Department of Energy national security programs, reaches $886 billion when supplemental appropriations are incorporated.

SECTION 1: BUDGET OVERVIEW AND APPROPRIATIONS STRUCTURE

The FY2024 National Defense Authorization Act (NDAA), signed into law on December 22, 2023, authorized $886.3 billion in total national defense spending. The base Department of Defense budget accounts for $842 billion of this total. The remaining $44.3 billion covers Department of Energy national security programs ($32.1 billion) and other defense-related activities ($12.2 billion).

The base DoD budget is divided across four primary appropriations titles:

TITLE I — Military Personnel: $176.2 billion (20.9% of base budget)
TITLE II — Operation and Maintenance: $295.8 billion (35.1% of base budget)
TITLE III — Procurement: $170.4 billion (20.2% of base budget)
TITLE IV — Research, Development, Test and Evaluation (RDT&E): $145.9 billion (17.3% of base budget)
TITLE V — Military Construction and Family Housing: $17.1 billion (2.0% of base budget)
TITLE VI — Revolving and Management Funds: $36.6 billion (4.3% of base budget)

Year-over-year changes from FY2023 enacted levels are as follows:

Operations & Maintenance | $283.1B | $295.8B | +$12.7B | +4.5%

Defense funding is distributed across the four military departments and defense-wide accounts. The Army receives the largest share of military personnel funding, while the Navy and Marine Corps combined account for the largest share of procurement spending due to shipbuilding programs.

SECTION 3: MAJOR PROGRAM EXPENDITURES

3.1 Aviation Programs

The F-35 Joint Strike Fighter program remains the single largest acquisition program in the DoD portfolio. The FY2024 budget requests $11.3 billion for F-35 procurement and sustainment, funding the purchase of 83 aircraft across all three variants. The program's total acquisition cost is currently estimated at $412 billion, with a full lifecycle cost projection of $1.7 trillion through 2088, according to the Government Accountability Office's March 2023 assessment.

The B-21 Raider long-range strike bomber program received $5.8 billion in FY2024, reflecting accelerated production timelines following the aircraft's public rollout on December 2, 2022, at Northrop Grumman's Palmdale, California facility.

3.2 Naval Shipbuilding

The Shipbuilding and Conversion, Navy (SCN) account totals $32.8 billion in FY2024, the largest single-year shipbuilding investment since FY1988. Key procurements include:

- 2 Virginia-class submarines at a unit cost of $3.4 billion each
- 1 Gerald R. Ford-class aircraft carrier (CVN-82) at $12.9 billion
- 3 Arleigh Burke-class destroyers at $2.1 billion each
- 1 San Antonio-class amphibious transport dock at $1.8 billion

3.3 Missile Defense and Space

The Missile Defense Agency's $30.2 billion allocation represents a $2.7 billion increase over FY2023. Priorities include the Next Generation Interceptor program ($1.4 billion), the Hypersonic and Ballistic Tracking Space Sensor ($1.1 billion), and continued development of the Ground-based Midcourse Defense system based at Fort Greely, Alaska.

The Space Force, established December 20, 2019, receives $29.4 billion in FY2024, a 13.7 percent increase over its FY2023 allocation of $25.9 billion.

SECTION 4: PERSONNEL AND COMPENSATION

The FY2024 budget funds an active-duty end strength of 1,284,500 personnel, a reduction of 3,200 from the FY2023 authorized level of 1,287,700. Reserve component end strength is authorized at 773,900 personnel.

Military personnel received a 5.2 percent pay raise effective January 1, 2024, the largest increase since 2002. This raise was driven by the Employment Cost Index benchmark established under 37 U.S.C. § 1009 and reflects sustained pressure on military recruitment and retention. The Army missed its FY2023 recruiting goal by approximately 10,000 soldiers, achieving 76 percent of its target.

Average annual compensation per active-duty service member, including pay, allowances, and benefits, is estimated at $137,400 for FY2024. Healthcare costs under the TRICARE system account for $58.4 billion of the military personnel and operations budgets combined.

SECTION 6: INTERNATIONAL COMPARISONS

According to SIPRI data for calendar year 2022, the United States accounted for 39 percent of total global military expenditure, spending $877 billion compared to China's $292 billion (second globally), Russia's $86.4 billion (third), India's $81.4 billion (fourth), and Saudi Arabia's $75 billion (fifth). The combined defense spending of all 31 NATO member nations in 2023 reached approximately $1.26 trillion, with the United States contributing roughly 68 percent of that total.

Only 11 of 31 NATO members met the alliance's benchmark of spending 2 percent of GDP on defense in 2023, including Poland (which led at 3.9 percent), the United States (3.5 percent), Greece (2.5 percent), Estonia (2.4 percent), and the United Kingdom (2.1 percent). Germany spent 1.57 percent of GDP on defense in 2023, below the 2 percent threshold despite commitments made following Russia's February 24, 2022 invasion of Ukraine.

SECTION 7: OVERSIGHT AND AUDIT STATUS

The Department of Defense has now completed six consecutive department-wide financial statement audits, initiated under the mandate of the National Defense Authorization Act for FY2018. The FY2023 audit, completed November 15, 2023, resulted in a disclaimer of opinion for the seventh consecutive year, meaning auditors could not confirm the accuracy of DoD's financial statements. The audit identified 28 material weaknesses, a reduction from 37 in FY2019, indicating incremental progress.

The DoD Inspector General, Robert P. Storch, reported that the department has remediated 1,143 audit findings since FY2018 while 2,891 findings remain open. The cost of conducting the annual audit reached $218 million in FY2023.

SECTION 8: CONCLUSIONS AND FORWARD PROJECTIONS

The Congressional Budget Office projects that under current law, defense spending will reach approximately $1.0 trillion in nominal terms by FY2032, representing an average annual growth rate of 2.1 percent in real terms. Sustained investment in hypersonic weapons, artificial intelligence integration, and nuclear modernization — including the Columbia-class submarine program ($132 billion total estimated cost) and the Sentinel ICBM program ($150 billion total estimated cost) — will drive procurement accounts upward through the late 2030s.

The Under Secretary of Defense (Comptroller), Michael J. McCord, has emphasized that achieving audit readiness and improving financial management systems remains a departmental priority alongside meeting operational requirements in an increasingly contested strategic environment.
