EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents a comprehensive analysis of yield changes across the United States Treasury securities portfolio for the third quarter of fiscal year 2024. During the period from April 1 through June 30, 2024, the Treasury market experienced notable volatility driven by persistent inflation data, Federal Reserve communications regarding the timing of rate reductions, and shifting investor sentiment regarding domestic economic growth. The 10-year Treasury note yield rose by 48 basis points over the quarter, closing at 4.36 percent on June 28, 2024, compared to 3.88 percent on March 29, 2024. This report details the methodology used to calculate yield changes, presents maturity-specific data, and provides context for observed movements.

---

SECTION 1: METHODOLOGY FOR BOND YIELD CHANGE CALCULATION

1.1 Data Sources and Collection

All yield data referenced in this report is sourced from the Daily Treasury Par Yield Curve Rates published by the U.S. Department of the Treasury. Yields are recorded as of 3:00 PM Eastern Time on each business day. For quarter-end comparisons, the final business day of each respective quarter is used as the reference date. For Q3 FY2024, the opening reference date is March 29, 2024, and the closing reference date is June 28, 2024.

1.2 Yield Change Calculation Formula

Bond yield changes are expressed in two formats throughout this report:

Absolute Change (in basis points): Calculated as the closing yield minus the opening yield, multiplied by 100 to convert from percentage to basis points.

Formula: Absolute Change (bps) = (Yield_Close – Yield_Open) × 100

Percentage Change: Calculated as the absolute change in yield divided by the opening yield, expressed as a percentage.

Formula: Percentage Change = [(Yield_Close – Yield_Open) / Yield_Open] × 100

Example Calculation — 10-Year Treasury Note:
Opening Yield (March 29, 2024): 3.88%
Closing Yield (June 28, 2024): 4.36%
Absolute Change: (4.36 – 3.88) × 100 = +48 basis points
Percentage Change: (0.48 / 3.88) × 100 = +12.37%

1.3 Price Impact Estimation

To estimate the approximate price impact of yield changes on outstanding securities, the Office of Debt Management applies modified duration calculations. For a standard 10-year Treasury note with a modified duration of approximately 8.7 years, a 48 basis point increase in yield corresponds to an estimated price decline of approximately 4.18 percent per $100 of face value.

Formula: Estimated Price Change (%) ≈ –Modified Duration × Yield Change (in decimal)
= –8.7 × 0.0048 = –0.04176, or approximately –4.18%

Key Observations:
- The largest absolute yield increase occurred at the 10-year and 30-year maturities, both rising 48 basis points.
- Short-term yields (1-month through 6-month) remained relatively stable, reflecting the Federal Reserve's unchanged federal funds rate target range of 5.25 to 5.50 percent throughout the quarter.
- The yield curve remained inverted at quarter-end, with the 3-month yield (5.49%) exceeding the 10-year yield (4.36%) by 113 basis points.
- The 2-year to 10-year spread narrowed from –74 basis points on March 29 to –35 basis points on June 28, 2024.

SECTION 3: NOTABLE YIELD EVENTS DURING Q3 FY2024

3.1 April 2024 — Inflation Data Shock

The Consumer Price Index report released on April 10, 2024, showed headline inflation of 3.5 percent year-over-year for March 2024, exceeding the consensus forecast of 3.4 percent. On that single trading day, the 10-year Treasury yield jumped 18 basis points, from 4.35 percent to 4.53 percent, representing one of the largest single-day moves of the calendar year. The 2-year yield rose 22 basis points on the same date, closing at 4.97 percent.

3.2 May 2024 — Federal Reserve Meeting

The Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC) meeting concluded on May 1, 2024, with the Committee voting unanimously to maintain the federal funds rate at 5.25 to 5.50 percent. Federal Reserve Chair Jerome Powell's press conference signaled that rate cuts were unlikely in the near term, contributing to a 7 basis point rise in the 10-year yield on May 1, closing at 4.68 percent.

3.3 June 2024 — Labor Market Data and Partial Reversal

The Bureau of Labor Statistics reported on June 7, 2024, that nonfarm payrolls increased by 272,000 in May 2024, significantly above the consensus estimate of 185,000. This stronger-than-expected employment figure pushed the 10-year yield up 14 basis points on that date. However, subsequent softer inflation readings in mid-June allowed yields to partially retrace, with the 10-year yield declining from a quarterly high of 4.73 percent on June 12 to 4.36 percent at quarter-end.

SECTION 4: IMPACT ON OUTSTANDING TREASURY DEBT

4.1 Portfolio Composition

As of June 30, 2024, total marketable Treasury debt outstanding stood at $27.4 trillion. Of this amount:
- Treasury bills (maturities of one year or less): $6.1 trillion, representing 22.3% of total marketable debt
- Treasury notes (maturities of 2 to 10 years): $14.8 trillion, representing 54.0% of total marketable debt
- Treasury bonds (maturities greater than 10 years): $4.6 trillion, representing 16.8% of total marketable debt
- Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS): $1.9 trillion, representing 6.9% of total marketable debt

4.2 Mark-to-Market Valuation Impact

Based on the yield changes observed during Q3 FY2024 and applying weighted average modified duration estimates for each maturity segment, the Office of Debt Management estimates that the mark-to-market value of outstanding Treasury notes and bonds declined by approximately $612 billion during the quarter. This figure is a theoretical valuation metric and does not affect the government's actual debt service obligations, which are fixed at issuance.

SECTION 7: GLOSSARY OF KEY TERMS

Basis Point (bps): One one-hundredth of one percentage point (0.01%). Used as the standard unit of measurement for yield changes.

Modified Duration: A measure of a bond's price sensitivity to changes in yield, expressed in years. A modified duration of 8.7 means that a 1 percent increase in yield results in approximately an 8.7 percent decrease in price.

Par Yield Curve: A yield curve constructed from the yields of Treasury securities priced at par (face value), used as the standard benchmark for U.S. government borrowing costs.

Yield Inversion: A condition in which shorter-maturity yields exceed longer-maturity yields, often interpreted as a signal of economic uncertainty.
