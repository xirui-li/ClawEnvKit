EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents a comprehensive analysis of yield changes across U.S. Treasury securities during the second quarter of fiscal year 2024, covering the period from January 1, 2024 through March 31, 2024. The analysis documents significant upward pressure on yields across the maturity spectrum, driven primarily by persistent inflation readings above the Federal Reserve's 2.0 percent target and revised market expectations regarding the timing and magnitude of Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC) rate reductions. The 10-year Treasury note yield rose by 46 basis points during the quarter, closing at 4.20 percent on March 31, 2024, compared to 3.74 percent at the close of December 29, 2023. Total marketable debt outstanding as of March 31, 2024 stood at $27.4 trillion, with an average interest rate of 3.28 percent across all maturities.

SECTION 1: METHODOLOGY AND DATA SOURCES

1.1 Calculation Framework

Yield change calculations in this report follow the standard basis point methodology adopted by the Office of Debt Management in accordance with Treasury Circular 570 and the Government Securities Act of 1986. One basis point (bp) is defined as one one-hundredth of one percentage point (0.01%). Yield changes are calculated as the arithmetic difference between the closing constant maturity yield on the final trading day of the reference quarter and the closing constant maturity yield on the final trading day of the preceding quarter.

Formula applied:

Yield Change (bps) = [Yield(t) – Yield(t-1)] × 100

Where:
Yield(t) = Closing yield on March 31, 2024
Yield(t-1) = Closing yield on December 29, 2023

All yield data are sourced from the H.15 Selected Interest Rates statistical release published daily by the Federal Reserve Board of Governors. Constant maturity yields are derived from the Treasury's daily yield curve, which is constructed using the methodology developed by the Federal Reserve Bank of New York and updated by Treasury's Office of Debt Management in 2019.

1.2 Scope of Securities Analyzed

This report covers the following categories of marketable Treasury securities:
- Treasury Bills (4-week, 8-week, 13-week, 26-week, and 52-week)
- Treasury Notes (2-year, 3-year, 5-year, 7-year, and 10-year)
- Treasury Bonds (20-year and 30-year)
- Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (5-year, 10-year, and 30-year TIPS)

SECTION 3: ANALYSIS OF YIELD MOVEMENTS BY MATURITY SEGMENT

3.1 Short-Term Segment (Bills, 4-Week to 52-Week)

Short-term yields remained anchored near the upper bound of the federal funds target rate, which the FOMC held at 5.25–5.50 percent throughout the entire quarter. The 4-week bill yield increased by only 11 basis points, reflecting the near-term policy rate anchor. The 52-week bill, however, showed a more pronounced increase of 31 basis points, rising from 4.79 percent to 5.10 percent, as markets repriced the expected path of rate cuts further into the future. At the start of January 2024, federal funds futures markets were pricing approximately 6.5 rate cuts of 25 basis points each for calendar year 2024. By March 31, 2024, that expectation had been revised downward to approximately 3 cuts, representing a substantial repricing of the monetary policy outlook.

3.2 Intermediate-Term Segment (2-Year to 7-Year Notes)

The intermediate segment experienced the most significant repricing relative to starting levels. The 5-year note yield rose 37 basis points from 3.84 percent to 4.21 percent, while the 7-year note rose 41 basis points from 3.88 percent to 4.29 percent. The 2-year note, which is most sensitive to near-term policy expectations, increased 19 basis points to close at 4.62 percent. The spread between the 2-year and 10-year notes, a closely monitored indicator of yield curve inversion, narrowed from negative 69 basis points on December 29, 2023 to negative 42 basis points on March 31, 2024, suggesting a modest steepening of the inverted curve.

3.3 Long-Term Segment (10-Year Note, 20-Year and 30-Year Bonds)

The benchmark 10-year Treasury note yield rose 46 basis points during the quarter, the largest absolute increase in the report period. The 20-year bond matched this increase at 46 basis points, closing at 4.51 percent. The 30-year bond yield increased 37 basis points to 4.34 percent. The 10-year TIPS yield rose 42 basis points to 2.07 percent, implying a 10-year breakeven inflation rate of approximately 2.13 percent as of March 31, 2024, compared to 2.09 percent at the end of December 2023. This modest increase in breakeven inflation suggests that real yield increases, rather than inflation expectations, drove the majority of the nominal yield rise.

SECTION 4: CONTRIBUTING FACTORS

4.1 Inflation Data

The Consumer Price Index (CPI) for February 2024, released by the Bureau of Labor Statistics on March 12, 2024, showed a year-over-year increase of 3.2 percent, above the consensus forecast of 3.1 percent. Core CPI, excluding food and energy, rose 3.8 percent year-over-year, also above expectations. The Personal Consumption Expenditures (PCE) price index for January 2024, released February 29, 2024, showed a year-over-year increase of 2.4 percent, while core PCE rose 2.8 percent. Both readings reinforced the view that the Federal Reserve's path to its 2.0 percent inflation target remained protracted.

4.2 Federal Reserve Communications

Federal Reserve Chair Jerome Powell testified before the Senate Banking Committee on March 7, 2024, stating that the Committee was "not yet confident" that inflation was sustainably moving toward the 2 percent target and that rate cuts would require "greater confidence" in the disinflation process. This testimony contributed to a single-day increase of 8 basis points in the 10-year yield on March 7, 2024.

4.3 Treasury Issuance and Auction Results

During Q2 FY2024, the Treasury Department issued approximately $7.2 trillion in gross marketable debt, including rollovers of maturing securities. Net new issuance totaled approximately $386 billion. The February 2024 auction of 10-year notes on February 7, 2024 produced a high yield of 4.166 percent with a bid-to-cover ratio of 2.53, compared to the prior auction's bid-to-cover of 2.56, indicating slightly softer demand. The 30-year bond auction on February 8, 2024 produced a high yield of 4.360 percent with a bid-to-cover ratio of 2.42.

SECTION 5: INTEREST COST IMPLICATIONS

5.1 Impact on Debt Service Costs

The average interest rate on total outstanding marketable debt as of March 31, 2024 was 3.28 percent, up from 2.97 percent as of March 31, 2023. Applied to the $27.4 trillion outstanding balance, this implies annualized gross interest expense of approximately $899 billion, compared to $726 billion in the prior fiscal year second quarter. This represents an increase of approximately $173 billion, or 23.8 percent year-over-year.

The weighted average maturity of the debt portfolio as of March 31, 2024 was 5 years and 10 months, down from 6 years and 2 months as of March 31, 2023, reflecting the Treasury's increased reliance on shorter-duration bill issuance to meet financing needs.

5.2 Duration Sensitivity

A parallel shift of 100 basis points in the yield curve applied to the current portfolio would increase annualized interest costs by an estimated $274 billion over a 12-month horizon, based on the current maturity profile and rollover schedule maintained by the Office of Debt Management as of March 31, 2024.

SECTION 7: OUTLOOK AND RISK FACTORS

The Office of Debt Management identifies the following key risk factors for yield movements in the subsequent quarter (April 1 – June 30, 2024):

- Continued above-target inflation readings that delay FOMC rate reductions
- Federal deficit financing requirements estimated at $514 billion for Q3 FY2024
- Potential changes in foreign official holdings of Treasury securities, which totaled $3.3 trillion as of January 2024 per Treasury International Capital data
- Geopolitical risk premiums affecting safe-haven demand for U.S. Treasuries
