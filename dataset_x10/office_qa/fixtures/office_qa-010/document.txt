EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents a comprehensive analysis of Commonwealth expenditure patterns for Fiscal Year 2022-2023 (July 1, 2022 through June 30, 2023), with comparative data spanning the preceding four fiscal years. Total general fund expenditures reached $42.7 billion in FY 2022-2023, representing a 6.3 percent increase over the FY 2021-2022 total of $40.2 billion. This analysis was prepared by the Bureau of Financial Management under the direction of Budget Secretary Uri Monson and is intended to inform legislative appropriations deliberations for FY 2024-2025.

SECTION 2: EXPENDITURE BY MAJOR CATEGORY — FY 2022-2023

2.1 Health and Human Services

Health and Human Services (HHS) remained the largest expenditure category, accounting for $18.9 billion, or 44.3 percent of total general fund spending. The Department of Human Services (DHS) administered $16.2 billion of this total, with Medical Assistance (Medicaid) comprising $11.4 billion — a 9.1 percent increase from the prior year's $10.4 billion. Medicaid enrollment as of June 30, 2023 stood at 3.47 million Pennsylvanians, up from 3.21 million in June 2022, reflecting continued post-pandemic enrollment retention.

The Children's Health Insurance Program (CHIP) expenditure totaled $412 million, a modest 2.3 percent increase from $403 million in FY 2021-2022. Mental health and substance abuse services received $1.08 billion, representing a 14.7 percent increase year-over-year, consistent with the Governor's initiative to expand behavioral health access announced in February 2023.

2.2 Education

Education expenditures totaled $14.1 billion, representing 33.0 percent of general fund spending. This figure includes:

- Basic Education Funding: $7.83 billion (increase of $567 million, or 7.8%, from FY 2021-2022)
- Special Education: $1.19 billion (increase of $43 million, or 3.7%)
- Pre-K Counts and Head Start Supplemental Assistance: $388 million combined
- Higher Education (state-related universities including Penn State, Temple, Pitt, and Lincoln): $642 million
- Pennsylvania State System of Higher Education (PASSHE): $477 million

The Basic Education Funding increase reflects the implementation of the adequacy supplement formula adopted following the February 7, 2023 Commonwealth Court ruling in William Penn School District v. Pennsylvania Department of Education, which found the existing school funding system unconstitutional. An emergency supplemental appropriation of $100 million was approved by the General Assembly on May 3, 2023 to begin addressing identified funding gaps.

2.3 Corrections and Public Safety

The Department of Corrections expended $3.12 billion in FY 2022-2023, a 4.1 percent increase from $3.00 billion in the prior year. The average daily inmate population was 37,842, down from a peak of 50,000 in 2010 but up slightly from 37,204 in FY 2021-2022. Per-inmate annual cost reached $82,400, compared to $80,600 in FY 2021-2022.

The Pennsylvania State Police received $1.44 billion, an increase of 6.2 percent, primarily attributable to a negotiated collective bargaining agreement that took effect January 1, 2023, providing a 4.5 percent base salary increase for approximately 4,700 sworn officers.

2.4 Debt Service

General obligation debt service totaled $1.87 billion in FY 2022-2023, representing 4.4 percent of total expenditures. This figure reflects principal payments of $1.12 billion and interest payments of $750 million. The Commonwealth's outstanding general obligation debt as of June 30, 2023 stood at $11.3 billion. Moody's Investors Service affirmed Pennsylvania's Aa3 credit rating on August 22, 2023, citing improved pension funding ratios and structural budget balance.

2.5 General Government Operations

General government operations, including the Governor's Office, Treasury, Auditor General, and various independent agencies, totaled $1.68 billion, or 3.9 percent of total expenditures. This category saw a 2.1 percent decrease from FY 2021-2022, primarily due to the expiration of pandemic-era administrative costs and the conclusion of federal ARPA administrative activities.

SECTION 3: PERSONNEL EXPENDITURE ANALYSIS

Personnel costs, including salaries, wages, and benefits, totaled $9.6 billion across all agencies in FY 2022-2023, representing 22.5 percent of total general fund expenditures. This reflects a 5.8 percent increase from the FY 2021-2022 personnel total of $9.07 billion.

The Commonwealth employed 76,214 full-time equivalent (FTE) positions as of June 30, 2023, compared to 74,988 FTEs in June 2022. The average annual compensation per FTE, including benefits, was $125,970.

Pension contributions to the State Employees' Retirement System (SERS) totaled $1.34 billion, reflecting an employer contribution rate of 34.94 percent of covered payroll. The SERS funded ratio improved to 61.3 percent as of December 31, 2022, up from 57.8 percent in December 2021, reflecting strong investment returns of 8.2 percent in calendar year 2022.

SECTION 4: CAPITAL EXPENDITURES AND INFRASTRUCTURE

Capital expenditures funded through the Capital Budget totaled $2.3 billion in FY 2022-2023, separate from the general fund. Key allocations included:

- PennDOT highway and bridge projects: $1.41 billion
- State facility renovations and construction: $387 million
- Environmental and conservation projects (DCNR): $214 million
- Information technology modernization: $289 million

The IT modernization figure includes $147 million for the SAP enterprise resource planning system upgrade initiated in September 2022, with full implementation projected for March 2025.

SECTION 5: EXPENDITURE VARIANCE FROM APPROPRIATED BUDGET

Total FY 2022-2023 general fund expenditures of $42.7 billion compared to an original appropriated budget of $43.1 billion, resulting in a favorable variance of $400 million, or 0.93 percent below appropriation. The largest favorable variances occurred in:

- Medical Assistance managed care: $218 million under budget due to lower-than-projected utilization rates
- Corrections: $67 million under budget reflecting lower-than-projected population growth
- Debt service: $31 million under budget due to favorable refinancing completed March 14, 2023

The largest unfavorable variance was in the Department of Agriculture's avian influenza response program, which exceeded its appropriation by $44 million due to the outbreak affecting 4.2 million birds in Lancaster and Lebanon counties between October 2022 and February 2023.

SECTION 6: OUTLOOK AND PROJECTIONS

Based on current enrollment trends, legislative commitments, and economic projections from the Independent Fiscal Office (IFO), general fund expenditures for FY 2023-2024 are projected to reach $44.9 billion, representing an estimated 5.2 percent increase. The primary drivers of projected growth include:

1. Medicaid unwinding: As federal continuous enrollment requirements expired April 1, 2023, DHS projects net enrollment reduction of approximately 280,000 individuals by June 2024, partially offsetting cost growth.
2. Education funding adequacy: Continued implementation of court-ordered funding reforms is projected to add $650 million in Basic Education Funding.
3. Pension contributions: SERS employer contribution rates are projected to decline marginally to 33.71 percent in FY 2023-2024.
4. Debt service: Projected to decrease to $1.79 billion as older high-interest bonds mature.

CONCLUSION

FY 2022-2023 expenditure patterns reflect continued structural pressures in health care and education, partially offset by improving pension metrics and disciplined agency management. The $400 million favorable budget variance demonstrates effective expenditure controls. The Bureau of Financial Management recommends that the General Assembly's Appropriations Committees consider the Medicaid enrollment trajectory and education funding court mandate as primary variables in developing FY 2024-2025 appropriations.
