EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents the Treasury Department's quarterly analysis of yield spreads across the federal government's outstanding debt portfolio as of September 30, 2024. The analysis covers spread dynamics between Treasury securities and comparable benchmark instruments, intra-curve spread movements, and implications for federal borrowing costs over the near-term horizon. Total federal debt subject to this analysis stood at $27.4 trillion as of the close of the third quarter, with marketable debt comprising $26.1 trillion of that figure.

The yield curve remained inverted through the majority of Q3 2024, though the degree of inversion narrowed materially following the Federal Open Market Committee's 50-basis-point rate reduction on September 18, 2024. The 2-year/10-year Treasury spread, which opened the quarter at negative 47 basis points on July 1, closed at negative 13 basis points on September 30, representing a 34-basis-point improvement over the three-month period. This narrowing reflects evolving market expectations regarding the Federal Reserve's easing cycle and has meaningful implications for the Department's debt issuance strategy heading into fiscal year 2025.

SECTION 2: INTRA-CURVE SPREAD ANALYSIS

2.1 The 2-Year/10-Year Spread

The 2s/10s spread is the Department's primary indicator of yield curve shape and is monitored on a daily basis by the Office of Federal Finance under Deputy Assistant Secretary Joshua Frost. As noted above, this spread moved from -47 basis points to -13 basis points during Q3 2024. The last time this spread was positive was March 8, 2023, when it briefly touched +2 basis points before resuming its inverted posture. The spread reached its most inverted point of the current cycle at -108 basis points on July 3, 2023.

The narrowing of the inversion during Q3 2024 is consistent with historical patterns observed ahead of Federal Reserve easing cycles. Analysis of the four prior easing cycles beginning in 1989, 1995, 2001, and 2007 shows that the 2s/10s spread typically turned positive within 3.2 months on average following the first rate cut. If this historical pattern holds, the Department projects the spread will turn positive by approximately December 2024.

2.2 The 3-Month/10-Year Spread

The 3-month/10-year spread, which the Federal Reserve Bank of New York uses in its recession probability model, stood at -83 basis points on September 30, 2024, compared to -99 basis points on July 1, 2024. This spread has been continuously negative since October 25, 2022. The New York Fed's model, as of its October 2024 update, assigned a 57.1% probability of recession within the next 12 months based on this spread, down from 61.4% in the July update.

2.3 The 5-Year/30-Year Spread

The 5s/30s spread, a measure of long-end curve steepness, widened from -4 basis points on July 1 to +55 basis points on September 30, 2024. This steepening of the long end reflects growing investor concern about the federal government's long-term debt trajectory. The Congressional Budget Office's June 2024 baseline projects federal debt held by the public will reach $48.3 trillion by 2034, representing 122% of GDP. This fiscal outlook has contributed to elevated term premiums at the long end of the curve.

SECTION 3: AGENCY AND CREDIT SPREAD ANALYSIS

3.1 Agency Spreads

Spreads between Treasury securities and agency debt issued by Fannie Mae (FNMA) and Freddie Mac (FHLMC) remained relatively stable during Q3 2024. The 10-year FNMA benchmark bond traded at a spread of 42 basis points over the comparable Treasury on September 30, 2024, compared to 39 basis points on July 1. The modest widening reflects increased agency issuance volume; Fannie Mae issued $84.6 billion in benchmark notes during Q3 2024, a 12.3% increase over Q3 2023 issuance of $75.3 billion.

3.2 TIPS Breakeven Spreads

The breakeven inflation rate, measured as the spread between nominal Treasury yields and Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS) of equivalent maturity, declined modestly during the quarter:

- 5-Year Breakeven: 2.21% on September 30, down from 2.38% on July 1 (decline of 17 bps)
- 10-Year Breakeven: 2.17% on September 30, down from 2.29% on July 1 (decline of 12 bps)
- 30-Year Breakeven: 2.28% on September 30, down from 2.35% on July 1 (decline of 7 bps)

The decline in breakeven rates suggests that market participants reduced their near-term inflation expectations following a series of favorable CPI prints. The Bureau of Labor Statistics reported that headline CPI rose 2.4% year-over-year in September 2024, down from 3.0% in June 2024.

SECTION 4: IMPLICATIONS FOR FEDERAL BORROWING COSTS

4.1 Weighted Average Interest Rate on Federal Debt

The weighted average interest rate on total federal debt outstanding rose to 3.32% as of September 30, 2024, compared to 2.97% on September 30, 2023. This 35-basis-point increase over twelve months reflects the continued rollover of legacy low-rate debt into higher-rate instruments. The Department estimates that each 25-basis-point increase in the weighted average rate adds approximately $68.5 billion to annual net interest costs based on the current debt stock.

Net interest payments on the federal debt totaled $514 billion for fiscal year 2024 (October 1, 2023 through September 30, 2024), representing 13.1% of total federal outlays of $6.75 trillion and exceeding defense discretionary spending of $886 billion as a share of mandatory obligations for the first time since 1997.

4.2 Debt Maturity Profile and Rollover Risk

As of September 30, 2024, approximately $7.6 trillion in Treasury securities, or 29.1% of total marketable debt, will mature within the next 12 months. This concentration of near-term maturities creates significant rollover exposure. The Department's Debt Management Advisory Committee, chaired by Secretary Janet Yellen, has recommended extending the weighted average maturity of the portfolio from its current 5.9 years toward a target of 6.5 years over the next 24 months through increased issuance of 10-year and 20-year notes.

4.3 Auction Performance Metrics

Treasury auctions during Q3 2024 demonstrated continued strong demand despite elevated supply. Key metrics for the quarter:

- Average bid-to-cover ratio across all coupon auctions: 2.51x (vs. 2.43x in Q3 2023)
- Indirect bidder participation (a proxy for foreign demand): 67.3% (vs. 64.8% in Q3 2023)
- Direct bidder participation: 18.9% (vs. 17.2% in Q3 2023)
- Primary dealer takedown: 13.8% (vs. 18.0% in Q3 2023)

The decline in primary dealer takedown alongside increased indirect and direct bidder participation indicates healthy end-user demand and reduced reliance on dealer intermediation, which the Department views as a positive indicator of market depth.

SECTION 5: OUTLOOK AND POLICY CONSIDERATIONS

The Department anticipates continued yield curve normalization through Q4 2024 and into fiscal year 2025, contingent on the Federal Reserve proceeding with additional rate reductions as indicated in the September 2024 Summary of Economic Projections. The median FOMC participant projected the federal funds rate would reach 4.4% by year-end 2024 and 3.4% by year-end 2025, implying approximately 50 additional basis points of easing before December 31, 2024.

The Department will continue to monitor the following risk factors that could disrupt the anticipated normalization:

- Resurgence of inflationary pressures that could halt or reverse the easing cycle
- Geopolitical developments affecting foreign demand for Treasury securities, particularly given that Japan ($1.13 trillion) and China ($774.6 billion) remain the two largest foreign holders as of July 2024
- Potential credit rating actions; Moody's Investors Service maintained its Aaa rating with a negative outlook as of the date of this report, following Fitch's downgrade to AA+ in August 2023
- Congressional budget negotiations affecting the fiscal year 2025 appropriations framework

The next quarterly yield spread analysis will be released on January 17, 2025, covering the period October 1 through December 31, 2024.
