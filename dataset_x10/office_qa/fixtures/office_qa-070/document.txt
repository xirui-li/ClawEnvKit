EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents a comprehensive analysis of federal government expenditure trends across fiscal years 2019 through 2024. The analysis draws on data compiled by the Office of Management and Budget (OMB), the Congressional Budget Office (CBO), and the Bureau of the Fiscal Service. Total federal outlays grew from $4.45 trillion in FY2019 to $6.75 trillion in FY2024, representing a cumulative increase of 51.7 percent over the five-year period. This growth was driven by pandemic-era emergency spending, sustained increases in mandatory program costs, and elevated defense appropriations. The report identifies key expenditure categories, year-over-year growth rates, and structural shifts in the federal budget composition.

SECTION 2: EXPENDITURE BY MAJOR CATEGORY — FY2024

Federal outlays in FY2024 were distributed across the following primary categories:

MANDATORY SPENDING — $4.11 trillion (60.9% of total outlays)

Social Security: $1.46 trillion
Medicare: $1.05 trillion
Medicaid: $618 billion
Other mandatory programs (including SNAP, Supplemental Security Income, and federal employee retirement): $982 billion

DISCRETIONARY SPENDING — $1.82 trillion (27.0% of total outlays)

Defense discretionary: $886 billion
Non-defense discretionary: $934 billion

NET INTEREST ON THE PUBLIC DEBT — $892 billion (13.2% of total outlays)

The net interest figure of $892 billion in FY2024 marks a 33.7 percent increase from the $667 billion recorded in FY2023, driven primarily by the Federal Reserve's interest rate increases beginning in March 2022. The effective interest rate on federal debt rose from an average of 1.6 percent in FY2021 to 3.2 percent in FY2024.

SECTION 3: MANDATORY SPENDING TRENDS

Mandatory spending has grown as a share of total federal outlays from 61.4 percent in FY2019 to 60.9 percent in FY2024. While the percentage share has remained relatively stable, the absolute dollar value increased by $1.18 trillion over the period.

3.1 Social Security

Social Security outlays increased from $1.04 trillion in FY2019 to $1.46 trillion in FY2024, a 40.4 percent increase. The primary drivers include:

- A cost-of-living adjustment (COLA) of 8.7 percent applied in January 2023, the largest COLA since 1981
- A COLA of 3.2 percent applied in January 2024
- Growth in the number of beneficiaries, which reached 71.6 million in December 2024, up from 64.2 million in December 2019

3.2 Medicare

Medicare outlays grew from $644 billion in FY2019 to $1.05 trillion in FY2024, an increase of 63.0 percent. The Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 (Public Law 117-169) authorized Medicare to negotiate drug prices directly with manufacturers beginning in 2026, but savings from this provision have not yet materialized in the expenditure data covered by this report. Per-beneficiary spending increased at an average annual rate of 4.8 percent between FY2019 and FY2024.

3.3 Medicaid

Medicaid expenditures rose from $409 billion in FY2019 to $618 billion in FY2024, a 51.1 percent increase. The expiration of continuous enrollment provisions under the Families First Coronavirus Response Act in April 2023 resulted in a disenrollment of approximately 22 million individuals from Medicaid by December 2024, moderating expenditure growth in FY2024 relative to FY2023.

SECTION 4: DISCRETIONARY SPENDING TRENDS

4.1 Defense Discretionary

Defense discretionary spending increased from $676 billion in FY2019 to $886 billion in FY2024, a 31.1 percent increase over the period. The FY2024 National Defense Authorization Act (Public Law 118-31, signed December 22, 2023) authorized a topline defense budget of $886 billion, reflecting a 3.2 percent increase over the FY2023 enacted level of $858 billion. Key areas of growth include:

- Procurement accounts: increased by $18.4 billion (14.6%) from FY2023 to FY2024
- Research, Development, Test and Evaluation (RDT&E): $145 billion in FY2024, up from $130 billion in FY2023
- Military personnel costs: $176 billion in FY2024, reflecting a 5.2 percent pay increase effective January 1, 2024

4.2 Non-Defense Discretionary

Non-defense discretionary spending totaled $934 billion in FY2024, compared to $914 billion in FY2023, an increase of 2.2 percent. Major components include:

- Department of Education: $238 billion
- Department of Transportation: $105 billion
- Department of Veterans Affairs (discretionary portion): $135 billion
- Department of Health and Human Services (discretionary portion): $112 billion

Infrastructure-related spending received a significant boost following the enactment of the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (Public Law 117-58, signed November 15, 2021), which provided $550 billion in new spending over five years. Approximately $89 billion of this funding was obligated in FY2024.

SECTION 5: NET INTEREST — A GROWING FISCAL PRESSURE

Net interest payments on the federal debt represent the fastest-growing major expenditure category in the federal budget. The trajectory is as follows:

FY2019: $375 billion (8.4% of outlays)
FY2020: $345 billion (5.3% of outlays) — temporarily reduced by near-zero interest rates
FY2021: $352 billion (5.2% of outlays)
FY2022: $476 billion (7.6% of outlays)
FY2023: $667 billion (10.9% of outlays)
FY2024: $892 billion (13.2% of outlays)

The CBO projects that net interest will exceed $1.0 trillion annually beginning in FY2025, at which point it will surpass defense discretionary spending as the second-largest single expenditure category in the federal budget, trailing only Social Security.

The gross federal debt stood at $35.46 trillion as of September 30, 2024, the close of FY2024. Debt held by the public — the economically relevant measure — totaled $28.18 trillion, equivalent to 98.7 percent of projected FY2024 GDP.

SECTION 7: CONCLUSIONS AND OBSERVATIONS

The expenditure data for FY2019 through FY2024 reveal several structural trends of significance to fiscal policymakers:

1. Mandatory spending dominates the budget and is driven by demographic and economic factors largely outside the annual appropriations process. Social Security and Medicare alone accounted for 37.2 percent of total FY2024 outlays.

2. Net interest has become a material fiscal constraint. At $892 billion in FY2024, interest costs now exceed total non-defense discretionary spending and are projected to surpass defense spending within the next two fiscal years.

3. The post-pandemic normalization of outlays has been incomplete. Despite the expiration of most emergency programs, total outlays in FY2024 remain 51.7 percent above FY2019 levels, indicating that a significant portion of pandemic-era spending has been absorbed into baseline expenditure levels.

4. Defense spending growth has been sustained but moderate in real terms. Adjusted for inflation using the GDP deflator, defense discretionary spending grew by approximately 9.4 percent in real terms between FY2019 and FY2024.

5. The FY2024 deficit of $1.83 trillion occurred during a period of low unemployment (4.1 percent as of September 2024), underscoring the structural, rather than cyclical, nature of current fiscal imbalances.
