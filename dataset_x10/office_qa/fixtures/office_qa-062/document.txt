EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents a comparative analysis of appropriated and expended budgets across major Commonwealth departments for fiscal year 2023–2024 (July 1, 2023 through June 30, 2024) against the prior fiscal year 2022–2023. The analysis was prepared under the direction of Budget Secretary Uri Monson and reflects data compiled from the Commonwealth's SAP financial management system as of August 31, 2024. Total general fund expenditures for FY 2023–2024 reached $42.8 billion, representing an increase of $1.94 billion, or 4.75 percent, over the FY 2022–2023 total of $40.86 billion. This report is submitted in accordance with Section 614 of the Administrative Code of 1929.

SECTION 1: METHODOLOGY AND SCOPE

Data for this report was extracted from the Commonwealth's Integrated Enterprise System (IES) on August 15, 2024. Figures represent final appropriations as enacted by the General Assembly and signed into law by Governor Josh Shapiro on July 11, 2023 (Act 1A of 2023) for FY 2022–2023 supplemental adjustments, and July 5, 2024 (Act 1A of 2024) for FY 2023–2024 appropriations. Expenditure figures reflect actual disbursements and encumbrances as of June 30, 2024.

All figures are reported in U.S. dollars. Percentages are rounded to two decimal places. Departments with total appropriations below $50 million are consolidated under the "Other Agencies" category. Capital budget and federal fund pass-throughs are excluded from this general fund comparison unless otherwise noted.

SECTION 3: NOTABLE FINDINGS AND ANALYSIS

3.1 Department of Education

The Department of Education received the largest single-year dollar increase among all departments, with appropriations rising by $487.8 million, or 6.26 percent, from $7,842.3 million in FY 2022–2023 to $8,314.7 million in FY 2023–2024. This increase reflects the Commonwealth's implementation of the Level Up supplemental funding formula, which directed an additional $225 million to the 100 lowest-wealth school districts as identified in the William Penn School District v. Pennsylvania Department of Education ruling issued by Commonwealth Court Judge Renée Cohn Jubelirer on February 7, 2023. Basic education funding alone increased by $567.3 million over the two-year period ending June 30, 2024.

The department's expenditure rate for FY 2023–2024 was 99.69 percent of appropriated funds, compared to 99.48 percent in the prior year, indicating improved disbursement efficiency.

3.2 Department of Human Services

The Department of Human Services (DHS) remains the largest single budget line in the Commonwealth, accounting for 34.75 percent of total general fund expenditures in FY 2023–2024. Total expenditures of $14,831.2 million represent an increase of $632.8 million over the prior year. The Medical Assistance program, administered by DHS Secretary Dr. Val Arkoosh, accounted for $9,214.6 million of total DHS spending, up from $8,876.3 million in FY 2022–2023, a 3.81 percent increase driven primarily by increased enrollment and pharmacy cost escalation.

The Long-Term Care line item increased by $118.4 million, or 8.2 percent, reflecting Pennsylvania's aging population and expanded home- and community-based services under the OBRA waiver program.

3.3 Department of Corrections

The Department of Corrections expended $3,219.6 million in FY 2023–2024, an increase of $121.4 million over the prior year. The average daily inmate population declined from 38,412 in FY 2022–2023 to 37,891 in FY 2023–2024, a reduction of 521 individuals, or 1.36 percent. Despite this population decrease, per-inmate costs rose from $80,660 to $84,970 annually, an increase of $4,310 per inmate, or 5.34 percent, driven by healthcare contract renegotiations finalized in March 2024 and a 4.1 percent cost-of-living adjustment for correctional officers effective January 1, 2024.

3.4 Department of Health

The Department of Health showed the highest percentage increase among major departments at 7.77 percent, with appropriations rising from $1,127.6 million to $1,204.3 million. Secretary Dr. Debra Bogen oversaw the expansion of the Opioid Use Disorder treatment network, which received $47.2 million in new general fund appropriations, supplemented by $31.6 million in federal Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration (SAMHSA) grants. The department's public health preparedness line item increased by $12.8 million to support regional emergency stockpile maintenance following a 2023 audit that identified gaps in personal protective equipment reserves.

3.5 Pennsylvania State Police

Pennsylvania State Police appropriations increased by $80.3 million, or 5.01 percent, to $1,694.2 million. The increase reflects the hiring of 312 new troopers in the cadet classes of October 2023 and April 2024, bringing total authorized strength to 4,641 sworn personnel as of June 30, 2024. Personnel costs represent 78.4 percent of total PSP expenditures, or approximately $1,327.8 million.

SECTION 5: MULTI-YEAR TREND SUMMARY

Total general fund expenditures have grown from $37.21 billion in FY 2020–2021 to $42.40 billion in FY 2023–2024, representing cumulative growth of 13.94 percent over four fiscal years, or an average annual growth rate of 3.37 percent. Adjusting for the Pennsylvania Consumer Price Index, which averaged 4.1 percent annually over the same period, real expenditure growth was approximately negative 0.73 percent per year on an inflation-adjusted basis, indicating that the Commonwealth's budget has not kept pace with inflation in real terms.

SECTION 6: RECOMMENDATIONS

The Bureau of Financial Management recommends the following actions for consideration in the FY 2024–2025 budget cycle:

1. The Department of Transportation should submit a corrective action plan by November 1, 2024 addressing the $57.3 million unexpended balance and the 214 unfilled positions identified in the Bureau of Driver Licensing.

2. The Department of Corrections should conduct a comprehensive review of healthcare contract costs, which increased by $67.3 million in FY 2023–2024, to identify opportunities for competitive rebidding prior to the contract renewal date of June 30, 2025.

3. The Office of Administration should accelerate implementation of the Commonwealth's shared services consolidation initiative, projected to yield $18.4 million in annual savings beginning in FY 2025–2026.
