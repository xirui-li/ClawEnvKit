EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents a comprehensive Compound Annual Growth Rate (CAGR) analysis of the Federal Highway Trust Fund (HTF) for the ten-year period spanning fiscal year 2014 through fiscal year 2024. The analysis encompasses total fund receipts, expenditures, interest earnings, and net balance positions. The Highway Trust Fund, established under the Federal Aid Highway Act of 1956, receives its primary revenue from federal motor fuel excise taxes and related transportation taxes. This report is prepared in accordance with the requirements set forth under 31 U.S.C. § 3515 and the Chief Financial Officers Act of 1990.

The ten-year CAGR for total HTF receipts from all sources stands at 3.47 percent, while the Highway Account specifically recorded a CAGR of 3.21 percent. The Mass Transit Account demonstrated stronger growth at a CAGR of 4.08 percent over the same period.

---

SECTION 1: BACKGROUND AND FUND STRUCTURE

The Federal Highway Trust Fund is divided into two primary accounts:

1. The Highway Account, which funds federal-aid highway programs administered primarily through the Federal Highway Administration (FHWA).
2. The Mass Transit Account, which funds public transportation programs administered through the Federal Transit Administration (FTA).

The fund receives revenue from several excise tax sources, including the gasoline tax of 18.4 cents per gallon (unchanged since 1993), the diesel fuel tax of 24.4 cents per gallon, and taxes on tires, truck and trailer sales, and heavy vehicle use. General fund transfers have supplemented HTF revenues in multiple fiscal years due to structural imbalances between receipts and expenditures.

---

SECTION 2: METHODOLOGY FOR CAGR COMPUTATION

The Compound Annual Growth Rate was calculated using the standard financial formula:

CAGR = [(Ending Value / Beginning Value) ^ (1 / Number of Years)] - 1

For this analysis:
- Beginning Value (FY 2014): Total HTF receipts of $39.8 billion
- Ending Value (FY 2024): Total HTF receipts of $56.3 billion
- Number of Years: 10

CAGR = [(56.3 / 39.8) ^ (1/10)] - 1
CAGR = [(1.4146) ^ (0.10)] - 1
CAGR = 1.0347 - 1
CAGR = 0.0347, or 3.47 percent

Calculations were performed independently for the Highway Account and Mass Transit Account, as well as for subcategories including motor fuel tax receipts, interest income, and general fund transfers. All figures are presented in nominal dollars unless otherwise noted. Inflation-adjusted figures using the Bureau of Economic Analysis GDP deflator are provided in Appendix C of the full report.

SECTION 4: CAGR RESULTS BY CATEGORY

4.1 Motor Fuel Tax Receipts

Motor fuel taxes represent the largest single source of HTF revenue. In FY 2014, combined gasoline and diesel tax receipts totaled $34.6 billion. By FY 2024, this figure reached $47.9 billion, yielding a ten-year CAGR of 3.31 percent. This growth rate reflects increased vehicle miles traveled and modest growth in commercial freight activity, partially offset by improving fuel economy standards under CAFE regulations.

4.2 Truck and Trailer Excise Taxes

Receipts from the 12 percent federal excise tax on heavy trucks and trailers grew from $2.1 billion in FY 2014 to $3.4 billion in FY 2024, representing a CAGR of 4.93 percent. This category demonstrated the strongest organic growth among all tax receipt categories, driven by sustained expansion in e-commerce logistics and freight demand.

4.3 Heavy Vehicle Use Tax

The Heavy Vehicle Use Tax (HVUT), assessed annually on vehicles with a gross weight of 55,000 pounds or more, generated $1.4 billion in FY 2014 and $1.8 billion in FY 2024, reflecting a CAGR of 2.55 percent.

4.4 Interest Income

Interest credited to the Highway Trust Fund totaled $0.3 billion in FY 2014. Following the Federal Reserve's rate adjustment cycle beginning in March 2022, interest income increased substantially, reaching $1.1 billion in FY 2024. The ten-year CAGR for interest income is 13.84 percent, though this figure is heavily influenced by the low-rate environment of FY 2014 through FY 2021 and the subsequent sharp increase.

4.5 General Fund Transfers

Congress authorized general fund transfers to the HTF on multiple occasions during the analysis period. Transfers totaled $10.8 billion in FY 2014, $6.2 billion in FY 2015, and $70.0 billion under the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (IIJA) of 2021, which provided a one-time supplemental transfer of $118 billion over five years beginning in FY 2022. For CAGR purposes, general fund transfers are tracked separately from organic tax receipts to avoid distortion of underlying revenue trend analysis.

SECTION 5: EXPENDITURE ANALYSIS AND NET BALANCE CAGR

5.1 Total Expenditures

Total HTF expenditures, including both the Highway Account and Mass Transit Account, grew from $50.3 billion in FY 2014 to $72.6 billion in FY 2024, representing a ten-year CAGR of 3.74 percent. Expenditure growth consistently outpaced organic receipt growth, underscoring the structural funding gap that has required periodic congressional intervention.

5.2 Highway Account Expenditures

Highway Account expenditures increased from $42.7 billion in FY 2014 to $61.4 billion in FY 2024, a CAGR of 3.70 percent. Major program categories include the National Highway Performance Program, the Surface Transportation Block Grant Program, and the Highway Safety Improvement Program.

5.3 Mass Transit Account Expenditures

Mass Transit Account expenditures grew from $7.6 billion in FY 2014 to $11.2 billion in FY 2024, a CAGR of 3.95 percent. The FTA's Capital Investment Grant program accounted for approximately 28 percent of Mass Transit Account expenditures in FY 2024.

5.4 Net Fund Balance

The combined HTF net balance stood at $8.4 billion at the close of FY 2014. Following the IIJA supplemental transfer, the net balance reached a ten-year high of $94.3 billion at the close of FY 2022. The FY 2024 closing balance is $71.8 billion, reflecting drawdown of IIJA-authorized funds. The CAGR of the net balance from FY 2014 to FY 2024 is 23.87 percent, though this figure is not analytically meaningful in isolation given the extraordinary nature of the IIJA transfer.

---

SECTION 6: KEY FINDINGS AND OBSERVATIONS

- The ten-year CAGR of total HTF receipts (3.47%) has consistently lagged behind the CAGR of total expenditures (3.74%), confirming a persistent structural imbalance.
- The Mass Transit Account has grown faster than the Highway Account across all measured categories, with a receipt CAGR of 4.08 percent versus 3.21 percent for the Highway Account.
- FY 2020 represents a significant outlier year, with total receipts declining 16.7 percent from FY 2019 levels due to pandemic-related travel reductions.
- The gasoline tax rate of 18.4 cents per gallon has not been adjusted since October 1, 1993, meaning that in inflation-adjusted terms, the effective purchasing power of the tax has declined by approximately 47 percent since that date.
- Truck and trailer excise taxes demonstrated the strongest organic CAGR at 4.93 percent, making commercial freight activity the most dynamic revenue driver within the fund.
- Interest income CAGR of 13.84 percent reflects the Federal Reserve's rate environment rather than structural fund growth.

---

SECTION 7: LIMITATIONS AND FORWARD PROJECTIONS

This analysis is limited to historical data and does not constitute a projection of future fund performance. The Congressional Budget Office's August 2024 baseline projects that absent additional legislative action, the Highway Account will face insolvency by FY 2028, with the Mass Transit Account reaching insolvency by FY 2030. These projections assume continuation of current tax rates and expenditure authorizations under the IIJA.

The Office of Financial Analysis and Reporting recommends that future CAGR analyses incorporate vehicle electrification penetration rates, as the increasing adoption of electric vehicles — which do not pay motor fuel excise taxes — is projected to reduce organic HTF receipts by an estimated $4.2 billion annually by FY 2030 under the Energy Information Administration's reference case scenario.
