EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This technical report presents the results of a comprehensive statistical distribution fitting analysis applied to four major Commonwealth revenue streams for fiscal year 2022–2023. The analysis was conducted to improve the accuracy of short-term revenue forecasting models and to quantify uncertainty bounds around point estimates submitted to the Governor's Budget Office. Using maximum likelihood estimation and goodness-of-fit testing across twelve candidate distributions, the Division of Quantitative Methods identified optimal distributional families for personal income tax receipts, corporate net income tax receipts, sales and use tax receipts, and lottery fund transfers. Key findings indicate that personal income tax monthly receipts are best characterized by a log-normal distribution with parameters μ = 10.847 and σ = 0.312, while corporate net income tax receipts exhibit heavy-tailed behavior consistent with a Pareto distribution. These findings have direct implications for reserve fund sizing and risk-adjusted budget projections.

---

SECTION 1: INTRODUCTION AND POLICY CONTEXT

Pennsylvania's General Fund received $44.1 billion in total revenues during fiscal year 2022–2023, representing a 2.3 percent decline from the $45.2 billion collected in fiscal year 2021–2022. This contraction, the first since fiscal year 2019–2020, was driven primarily by a $1.8 billion reduction in personal income tax receipts attributable to lower capital gains realizations following equity market corrections in calendar year 2022.

Accurate distributional characterization of revenue streams serves three operational purposes within the Commonwealth's fiscal management framework. First, it enables the Budget Office to construct credible intervals around revenue forecasts rather than relying solely on point estimates. Second, it informs the sizing of the Budget Stabilization Reserve Fund, which held a balance of $5.07 billion as of June 30, 2023. Third, it supports stress-testing exercises required under Act 48 of 2017, which mandates that the Secretary of the Budget certify annually that reserve balances are sufficient to cover a revenue shortfall at the 95th percentile of the estimated loss distribution.

The analysis covers 144 monthly observations spanning July 2011 through June 2023, providing a twelve-year window that encompasses two full business cycles and includes the COVID-19 fiscal shock of fiscal year 2019–2020.

Monthly revenue data were extracted from the Commonwealth's Integrated Enterprise System (IES) on August 15, 2023. All figures are reported in millions of dollars and are not seasonally adjusted, as the fitting exercise is intended to capture the full distributional shape including seasonal variation.

SECTION 3: METHODOLOGY

3.1 Candidate Distributions

Twelve candidate distributions were evaluated for each revenue stream: Normal, Log-Normal, Gamma, Weibull, Beta, Exponential, Pareto, Generalized Extreme Value (GEV), Gumbel, Logistic, Log-Logistic, and Burr Type XII. Selection of candidates was guided by prior literature on government revenue distributions, including work by Deschamps and Husted (2018) in the Journal of Public Budgeting and Finance and internal analyses conducted by the California Department of Finance in their 2020 Revenue Uncertainty Report.

3.2 Estimation Procedure

Parameters for each distribution were estimated using maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) implemented in R version 4.3.1 using the fitdistrplus package (version 1.1-11). For distributions where MLE convergence was problematic due to boundary conditions, the method of moments was used as an alternative, specifically for the Pareto distribution applied to corporate net income tax data.

3.3 Goodness-of-Fit Testing

Three complementary goodness-of-fit statistics were computed for each distribution-revenue stream combination:

- Kolmogorov-Smirnov (KS) test statistic and associated p-value
- Anderson-Darling (AD) test statistic, which weights tail fit more heavily
- Akaike Information Criterion (AIC) for model comparison accounting for parameter count

The preferred distribution for each revenue stream was selected based on the lowest AIC value among distributions that also failed to reject the null hypothesis of adequate fit at the 5 percent significance level under both KS and AD tests.

---

SECTION 4: RESULTS

4.1 Personal Income Tax

The log-normal distribution provided the best fit for personal income tax monthly receipts. Estimated parameters are μ = 10.847 and σ = 0.312, corresponding to a median of $51,100 per taxpayer filing unit when scaled appropriately. The KS test statistic was 0.0614 (p-value = 0.623), and the AD statistic was 0.847, both indicating acceptable fit. The AIC value of 2,341.7 was the lowest among all twelve candidates, outperforming the second-best candidate (Gamma distribution, AIC = 2,349.2) by 7.5 AIC units.

Under this fitted distribution, the 5th percentile of monthly personal income tax receipts is estimated at $1,487 million and the 95th percentile at $4,891 million, yielding a 90 percent prediction interval width of $3,404 million.

4.2 Corporate Net Income Tax

The Pareto distribution with shape parameter α = 1.83 and scale parameter xm = 14.7 million provided the best characterization of corporate net income tax receipts. This heavy-tailed distribution reflects the concentration of corporate tax liability among a small number of large filers. In fiscal year 2022–2023, the top 200 corporate filers accounted for 67.4 percent of total corporate net income tax collections of $3,751 million.

The fitted Pareto distribution implies a 95th percentile monthly receipt of $1,214 million, compared to the observed maximum of $1,847.2 million in April 2022. The April 2022 observation falls at approximately the 99.2nd percentile of the fitted distribution, confirming its status as a statistically extreme but not impossible event.

4.3 Sales and Use Tax

Sales and use tax monthly receipts were best described by a Normal distribution with mean μ = 1,089.4 million and standard deviation σ = 198.3 million. This finding is consistent with the central limit theorem effects expected from aggregating millions of individual transactions. The KS p-value of 0.891 and AD statistic of 0.312 indicate excellent fit. The 95 percent prediction interval spans $700.8 million to $1,477.9 million.

4.4 Lottery Fund Transfers

Lottery Fund monthly transfers to the General Fund were best fit by a Normal distribution with mean μ = 83.7 million and standard deviation σ = 14.6 million. The narrow distributional spread reflects the stable, regulated nature of lottery operations administered by the Pennsylvania Lottery, which generated $4.55 billion in total sales during fiscal year 2022–2023.

---

SECTION 5: IMPLICATIONS FOR RESERVE FUND SIZING

Using Monte Carlo simulation with 100,000 iterations drawing from the four fitted distributions simultaneously, with a correlation matrix estimated from historical data, the Division estimates that annual General Fund revenues fall below $40.0 billion with a probability of 8.3 percent and below $37.5 billion with a probability of 2.1 percent.

The current Budget Stabilization Reserve Fund balance of $5.07 billion represents 11.5 percent of fiscal year 2022–2023 revenues. Based on the simulated loss distribution, a reserve of $4.1 billion would be sufficient to cover a revenue shortfall at the 95th percentile of the annual loss distribution, suggesting the current reserve provides a modest buffer above the statutory minimum threshold. However, the analysis recommends maintaining the current balance given the elevated uncertainty in corporate net income tax projections for fiscal year 2023–2024, where preliminary data through September 2023 show collections running 14.7 percent below the same period in fiscal year 2022–2023.

---

SECTION 6: LIMITATIONS AND FUTURE WORK

This analysis treats each fiscal year as exchangeable, which may not fully account for structural breaks associated with the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017 or the COVID-19 pandemic. A regime-switching model is under development and is expected to be completed by March 2024. Additionally, the 144-observation sample, while adequate for MLE estimation, limits the precision of tail quantile estimates. The 95th percentile estimates carry a bootstrapped standard error of approximately $87 million for personal income tax and $214 million for corporate net income tax.
