EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This technical memorandum presents the findings of a comprehensive statistical distribution fitting analysis conducted on Pennsylvania's general fund revenue streams and select expenditure categories for the period spanning fiscal years 2014–2015 through 2022–2023. The analysis was commissioned in response to a directive from Secretary of Revenue Pat Browne issued on November 6, 2023, requiring the Office of Fiscal Analysis to develop probabilistic revenue forecasting models capable of producing 90-day rolling confidence intervals for budget planning purposes.

The primary objective was to identify the best-fitting probability distributions for six major revenue categories and three high-volatility expenditure categories, enabling the Commonwealth to move beyond point-estimate forecasting toward scenario-based fiscal planning. Total general fund revenues analyzed across the nine-year study period amounted to $312.7 billion in aggregate, with annual revenues ranging from a low of $31.4 billion in FY 2014–2015 to a high of $44.6 billion in FY 2022–2023.

─────────────────────────────────────────────

SECTION 1: BACKGROUND AND MOTIVATION

Pennsylvania's general fund has experienced increasing volatility over the past decade, driven by cyclical economic pressures, federal policy changes, and pandemic-related disruptions. The standard deviation of annual general fund revenue growth rates between FY 2015 and FY 2023 was 4.87 percentage points, compared to 2.31 percentage points during the preceding decade (FY 2005–FY 2014). This near-doubling of volatility has rendered traditional linear trend forecasting inadequate for budget risk management.

In FY 2020–2021, actual general fund revenues exceeded the official forecast by $3.2 billion, or 9.4 percent, primarily due to unanticipated federal transfer effects and delayed tax filing deadlines. Conversely, in FY 2018–2019, revenues fell $612 million short of projections, a 1.7 percent shortfall that required a mid-year budget adjustment. These episodes underscore the need for distributional rather than deterministic forecasting.

─────────────────────────────────────────────

SECTION 2: DATA AND METHODOLOGY

2.1 Data Sources

Monthly revenue and expenditure data were drawn from the Commonwealth's Integrated Enterprise System (IES) financial database, covering 108 consecutive months from July 2014 through June 2023. All figures were inflation-adjusted to 2023 dollars using the Bureau of Labor Statistics Consumer Price Index for All Urban Consumers (CPI-U), with a base period of January 2023.

2.2 Revenue Categories Analyzed

Six revenue categories were selected based on their combined share of general fund receipts:

Category | FY 2022–23 Actual ($M) | Share of General Fund
Personal Income Tax | $17,842 | 40.0%
Sales and Use Tax | $12,614 | 28.3%
Corporate Net Income Tax | $5,371 | 12.0%
Inheritance and Estate Tax | $1,247 | 2.8%
Realty Transfer Tax | $689 | 1.5%
Cigarette and Tobacco Tax | $412 | 0.9%

2.3 Expenditure Categories Analyzed

Three expenditure categories exhibiting the highest coefficient of variation over the study period were selected:

Category | Mean Annual Expenditure ($M) | CV (%)
Medical Assistance (Medicaid) | $8,914 | 11.3%
Corrections and Rehabilitation | $2,847 | 6.8%
Debt Service | $1,623 | 14.7%

2.4 Distribution Fitting Procedure

Candidate distributions tested for each data series included the Normal, Log-Normal, Gamma, Weibull, Beta, Generalized Extreme Value (GEV), and Johnson SB distributions. Fitting was performed using maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) implemented in R version 4.3.1, using the fitdistrplus package (version 1.1-11) and the MASS library.

Goodness-of-fit was assessed using three complementary criteria:
- Kolmogorov-Smirnov (KS) test statistic and associated p-value
- Akaike Information Criterion (AIC)
- Anderson-Darling (AD) test statistic, which places greater weight on distributional tails

All tests were conducted at the 5 percent significance level. Where multiple distributions passed all three criteria, the distribution with the lowest AIC was selected as the preferred model.

─────────────────────────────────────────────

SECTION 3: RESULTS BY REVENUE CATEGORY

3.1 Personal Income Tax

Monthly personal income tax receipts, after seasonal adjustment using the X-13ARIMA-SEATS procedure, were best described by a Log-Normal distribution with parameters μ = 7.214 and σ = 0.183. The KS test yielded a p-value of 0.412, and the AIC was –847.3, outperforming the Normal distribution (AIC = –831.6) and the Gamma distribution (AIC = –839.1). The right-skewed nature of the Log-Normal reflects occasional large withholding spikes associated with year-end bonus payments and capital gain realizations.

3.2 Sales and Use Tax

Sales and use tax monthly receipts were best fit by a Gamma distribution with shape parameter α = 28.4 and rate parameter β = 0.00214. The KS p-value was 0.387, and the AD statistic of 0.614 was well below the critical value of 2.502. This distribution captures the moderate positive skewness observed in retail sales data, particularly during the November–December holiday period.

3.3 Corporate Net Income Tax

Corporate net income tax receipts exhibited the highest volatility of any revenue category, with a monthly coefficient of variation of 38.7 percent. The Generalized Extreme Value distribution provided the best fit, with location parameter μ = 421.3, scale parameter σ = 164.7, and shape parameter ξ = 0.218. The positive shape parameter confirms heavy-tailed behavior, consistent with the lumpy nature of corporate estimated payments. The KS p-value was 0.291.

3.4 Inheritance and Estate Tax

The Weibull distribution with shape k = 2.31 and scale λ = 1,412 provided the best fit for monthly inheritance tax receipts (KS p-value = 0.503, AIC = –412.8). The Weibull's flexibility in modeling the timing of estate settlements, which cluster around probate court processing cycles, made it the most appropriate choice.

3.5 Realty Transfer Tax

Realty transfer tax receipts were best described by a Log-Normal distribution with μ = 6.441 and σ = 0.297. This category showed the strongest sensitivity to interest rate movements, with a Pearson correlation of –0.71 between monthly receipts and the 30-year fixed mortgage rate as reported by Freddie Mac. The KS p-value was 0.338.

3.6 Cigarette and Tobacco Tax

Given the secular decline in tobacco consumption, cigarette tax receipts were modeled using a Normal distribution applied to first-differenced (month-over-month change) data, with mean –$1.4 million and standard deviation $8.7 million. The underlying level series declined at an average rate of 3.2 percent annually over the study period.

─────────────────────────────────────────────

SECTION 4: RESULTS FOR EXPENDITURE CATEGORIES

4.1 Medical Assistance

Monthly Medicaid expenditures were best fit by a Gamma distribution with α = 31.6 and β = 0.00423. The AD statistic of 0.782 indicated acceptable fit. The right tail of the distribution reflects occasional large supplemental payments to hospital systems, including a $214 million supplemental payment to UPMC Health System in February 2022 that represented a 3-sigma event relative to the fitted distribution.

4.2 Corrections and Rehabilitation

Corrections expenditures followed a Normal distribution with mean $237.3 million per month and standard deviation $16.1 million (KS p-value = 0.601). This category exhibited the most stable distributional behavior of all nine series analyzed, reflecting the relatively fixed nature of personnel and facility costs.

4.3 Debt Service

Debt service payments were best modeled using a Johnson SB distribution, reflecting the bounded nature of scheduled payments combined with occasional refinancing-driven spikes. Parameters were γ = –0.847, δ = 1.214, ξ = 98.4, and λ = 312.7. The AIC of –389.4 outperformed all competing distributions by a margin of at least 11.2 AIC units.

─────────────────────────────────────────────

SECTION 5: AGGREGATE REVENUE RISK ASSESSMENT

Using Monte Carlo simulation with 100,000 iterations, the fitted distributions for the six revenue categories were combined (assuming a correlation structure estimated from the historical data) to produce an aggregate general fund revenue forecast distribution for FY 2024–2025.

Key simulation outputs:

Percentile | Projected Annual Revenue
10th | $42.1 billion
25th | $43.4 billion
50th (Median) | $44.9 billion
75th | $46.3 billion
90th | $47.8 billion

The official point estimate contained in the Governor's Executive Budget, released February 6, 2024, projects FY 2024–2025 general fund revenues of $45.1 billion, which falls at approximately the 52nd percentile of the simulated distribution. This positioning suggests the official forecast is modestly conservative relative to the central tendency of the probabilistic model, with a 10 percent probability of revenues falling below $42.1 billion and a corresponding 10 percent probability of exceeding $47.8 billion.

─────────────────────────────────────────────

SECTION 6: LIMITATIONS AND FUTURE WORK

Several limitations warrant acknowledgment. First, the nine-year study period includes the COVID-19 fiscal shock of FY 2019–2020 and FY 2020–2021, which may cause the fitted distributions to overweight tail probabilities relative to a more stable economic environment. Second, the correlation structure among revenue categories was estimated from historical data and may not reflect future co-movement during stress scenarios.

Future work will incorporate Copula-based dependence modeling to better capture tail dependence between corporate income tax and personal income tax receipts, which historically exhibit correlation of 0.58 in normal periods but 0.84 during economic contractions. Additionally, the Division plans to extend this framework to the Commonwealth's 14 special revenue funds by September 30, 2024.

─────────────────────────────────────────────

SECTION 7: CONCLUSIONS

This analysis demonstrates that Pennsylvania's major revenue streams are best described by a heterogeneous set of probability distributions rather than a single distributional family. The Log-Normal and Gamma distributions predominate for consumption-based taxes, while the Generalized Extreme Value distribution is required for corporate income tax due to its heavy-tailed behavior. The Monte Carlo simulation framework developed here provides the Commonwealth with a rigorous basis for probabilistic budget planning and reserve fund sizing.

The Office of Fiscal Analysis recommends that the Budget Stabilization Reserve Fund, currently holding $1.83 billion as of December 31, 2023, be evaluated against the 10th percentile revenue shortfall scenario of $2.8 billion relative to the median projection, suggesting the current reserve provides approximately 65 percent coverage of a moderate downside scenario.
