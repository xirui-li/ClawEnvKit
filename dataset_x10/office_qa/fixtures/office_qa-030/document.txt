EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents a comprehensive analysis of Commonwealth expenditure patterns across five fiscal years, from FY2019-20 through FY2023-24. Total general fund expenditures grew from $34.1 billion in FY2019-20 to $45.8 billion in FY2023-24, representing a cumulative increase of 34.3 percent over the five-year period. The analysis identifies key drivers of expenditure growth, examines departmental spending trends, and provides context for budget planning in the upcoming FY2024-25 cycle.

Secretary of the Budget Uri Monson authorized this report pursuant to the Commonwealth's Financial Code, Section 702, which requires annual publication of expenditure trend data for legislative and public review.

SECTION 1: OVERVIEW OF TOTAL EXPENDITURES

1.1 Five-Year Expenditure Summary

Total general fund expenditures by fiscal year are presented below:

Fiscal Year | Total Expenditures | Year-over-Year Change | Percent Change
FY2019-20   | $34.1 billion      | —                     | —
FY2020-21   | $36.7 billion      | +$2.6 billion         | +7.6%
FY2021-22   | $39.8 billion      | +$3.1 billion         | +8.4%
FY2022-23   | $43.2 billion      | +$3.4 billion         | +8.5%
FY2023-24   | $45.8 billion      | +$2.6 billion         | +6.0%

The compound annual growth rate (CAGR) for general fund expenditures over this period was 7.6 percent. Notably, FY2020-21 growth was partially driven by federal American Rescue Plan Act (ARPA) pass-through allocations totaling $7.3 billion, which inflated total government spending figures when all funds are considered. The general fund figures above exclude federal pass-through dollars to maintain comparability across years.

1.2 Inflation-Adjusted Expenditures

When adjusted for inflation using the Bureau of Labor Statistics Consumer Price Index (CPI-U), real expenditure growth was more modest. Using FY2019-20 as the base year:

- FY2020-21 real growth: 4.1%
- FY2021-22 real growth: 2.8% (inflation averaged 7.0% that year)
- FY2022-23 real growth: 2.3% (inflation averaged 4.1% that year)
- FY2023-24 real growth: 3.7%

The divergence between nominal and real growth rates was most pronounced in FY2021-22, when the Commonwealth's nominal spending increased 8.4 percent against a backdrop of 7.0 percent general inflation.

SECTION 2: EXPENDITURES BY MAJOR CATEGORY

2.1 Health and Human Services

The Department of Human Services (DHS) remained the single largest expenditure category throughout the analysis period, accounting for 38.2 percent of total general fund spending in FY2023-24, or approximately $17.5 billion. This represents an increase from $12.8 billion in FY2019-20, a five-year growth rate of 36.7 percent.

Key drivers within DHS expenditures include:

- Medical Assistance (Medicaid): Grew from $8.9 billion in FY2019-20 to $12.1 billion in FY2023-24, a 35.9 percent increase. Enrollment peaked at 3.6 million recipients in March 2023 following the end of continuous enrollment protections under the federal Public Health Emergency, before declining to 3.1 million by June 2024 as redeterminations were completed.

- Long-Term Living Programs: Increased from $1.4 billion to $2.0 billion over the five-year period, driven by rising costs of nursing facility care and expansion of home- and community-based services under the OBRA waiver program.

- Child Welfare Services: Expenditures grew 18.3 percent, from $1.1 billion to $1.3 billion, reflecting increased caseloads and higher per-case costs following workforce wage adjustments implemented in January 2022.

2.2 Education

The Department of Education received $7.9 billion in FY2023-24, compared to $6.4 billion in FY2019-20, representing a 23.4 percent increase. The most significant single-year increase occurred in FY2022-23, when the Basic Education Funding formula was revised following the Commonwealth Court ruling in William Penn School District v. Pennsylvania Department of Education (January 2023), which found the existing school funding system unconstitutional.

Basic Education Funding alone grew from $6.1 billion in FY2022-23 to $6.7 billion in FY2023-24, an increase of $614 million or 10.1 percent in a single year. Governor Josh Shapiro's administration directed an additional $567 million toward historically underfunded districts as part of the Level Up supplemental funding initiative.

2.3 Transportation

The Department of Transportation (PennDOT) expenditures from the general fund totaled $1.2 billion in FY2023-24, relatively stable compared to $1.1 billion in FY2019-20. However, total transportation spending including the Motor License Fund reached $6.8 billion in FY2023-24, up from $5.6 billion in FY2019-20. Federal Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (IIJA) funding, enacted November 2021, contributed $1.4 billion in additional transportation dollars in FY2022-23 and FY2023-24 combined.

2.4 Public Safety and Corrections

The Department of Corrections spent $2.9 billion in FY2023-24, down from a peak of $3.1 billion in FY2021-22. This decline reflects a reduction in the state prison population from 45,800 inmates in June 2020 to 37,400 inmates in June 2024, a 18.3 percent decrease. Per-inmate daily cost rose from $118.42 in FY2019-20 to $141.67 in FY2023-24, an increase of 19.6 percent, driven primarily by healthcare costs and staffing expenses.

SECTION 3: PERSONNEL AND COMPENSATION TRENDS

3.1 Workforce Size and Cost

Commonwealth executive branch employment (excluding independent agencies) stood at 76,842 full-time equivalent positions as of June 30, 2024, compared to 79,114 positions in June 2020. The 2.9 percent reduction in headcount occurred despite overall expenditure growth, reflecting both attrition management and targeted workforce reductions in the Department of Revenue and Department of Labor and Industry following technology modernization initiatives.

Total personnel costs, including salaries, benefits, and pension contributions, reached $11.3 billion in FY2023-24, representing 24.7 percent of total general fund expenditures. This compares to $9.8 billion and 28.7 percent of expenditures in FY2019-20, indicating that while absolute personnel costs grew 15.3 percent, they declined as a share of total spending as other categories grew faster.

3.2 Pension Obligations

State employee pension contributions (SERS and PSERS combined) totaled $4.1 billion in FY2023-24, down from $4.6 billion in FY2021-22. The decline reflects the positive impact of pension reform legislation Act 5 of 2017, which established a hybrid defined benefit/defined contribution plan for employees hired after January 1, 2019. As of June 2024, approximately 28,400 employees were enrolled in the hybrid plan, representing 37 percent of the active workforce.

SECTION 4: DEBT SERVICE AND CAPITAL EXPENDITURES

4.1 Debt Service

General obligation debt service totaled $1.08 billion in FY2023-24, representing 2.4 percent of total general fund expenditures. This is a decrease from 3.1 percent in FY2019-20, reflecting the Commonwealth's policy of limiting new general obligation borrowing and retiring existing debt. Outstanding general obligation debt stood at $10.2 billion as of June 30, 2024, down from $11.7 billion in June 2020.

The Commonwealth's credit rating remained stable throughout the analysis period. Moody's affirmed an Aa3 rating in March 2024, while S&P Global maintained an AA- rating as of the same date.

4.2 Capital Budget

Capital budget expenditures averaged $425 million annually over the five-year period, with a high of $512 million in FY2022-23 reflecting accelerated investment in state facility improvements and technology infrastructure. The Keystone Works technology modernization program, initiated in July 2021 at a projected total cost of $890 million over seven years, accounted for $187 million in capital expenditures through June 2024.

SECTION 5: RESERVE FUNDS AND FISCAL POSITION

The Commonwealth's Rainy Day Fund balance grew substantially during the analysis period, reaching $8.1 billion as of June 30, 2024. This compares to a balance of $340 million in June 2020, representing a 23-fold increase driven by revenue surpluses in FY2020-21 through FY2022-23. At 17.7 percent of annual general fund expenditures, the Rainy Day Fund balance exceeds the Government Finance Officers Association (GFOA) recommended minimum of two months' operating expenditures (approximately 16.7 percent).

SECTION 6: OUTLOOK AND PROJECTIONS

Based on current appropriations and demographic projections, the Bureau of Financial Management projects general fund expenditures will reach $48.3 billion in FY2024-25, representing a 5.5 percent increase over FY2023-24. The primary growth drivers are anticipated to be:

1. Medicaid expenditures increasing approximately $680 million due to rate increases for managed care organizations effective January 1, 2025
2. Education funding growing an estimated $750 million as the administration continues implementing the court-ordered funding equity remediation
3. Personnel costs rising approximately $420 million reflecting collective bargaining agreements ratified in September 2024 providing 3.5 percent annual wage increases for AFSCME Council 13 members

The structural balance between recurring revenues and recurring expenditures remains a concern. The Independent Fiscal Office projects a structural deficit of approximately $1.4 billion by FY2025-26 if current spending trajectories continue without corresponding revenue growth or expenditure reductions.

METHODOLOGY NOTE

All expenditure figures in this report reflect final audited data from the Commonwealth's SAP-based financial management system (COMPASS) for FY2019-20 through FY2022-23. FY2023-24 figures reflect preliminary unaudited data as of September 30, 2024, subject to revision upon completion of the annual audit by the Auditor General's office. Inflation adjustments use the annual average CPI-U as published by the Bureau of Labor Statistics. Departmental allocations reflect direct appropriations and exclude interdepartmental transfers to avoid double-counting.
