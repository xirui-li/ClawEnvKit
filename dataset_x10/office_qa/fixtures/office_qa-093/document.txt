EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents the Office of Debt Management's annual yield spread analysis for fiscal year 2023 (October 1, 2022 through September 30, 2023). The analysis examines spreads across Treasury maturities, compares federal borrowing costs against benchmark rates, and evaluates the implications of current yield curve dynamics for future debt issuance strategy. During the reporting period, the Federal Reserve raised the federal funds rate target seven times, bringing the upper bound from 4.50 percent to 5.50 percent, the highest level since January 2001. These actions produced significant yield curve inversion and elevated borrowing costs across the federal debt portfolio.

Total federal debt subject to the statutory limit reached $31.46 trillion as of September 30, 2023. Net interest costs for fiscal year 2023 totaled $879.3 billion, representing an increase of $214.8 billion, or 32.3 percent, compared to fiscal year 2022's net interest expense of $664.5 billion. Net interest as a share of GDP rose to 3.3 percent, the highest level recorded since fiscal year 1998.

The 2-year/10-year spread, a widely monitored recession indicator, stood at negative 46 basis points as of September 29, 2023. This inversion persisted for 14 consecutive months during the fiscal year, the longest sustained inversion since the period preceding the 2007-2008 financial crisis. The 3-month/10-year spread, which the Federal Reserve Bank of New York uses in its recession probability model, reached a maximum inversion of negative 188 basis points on May 22, 2023, before narrowing to negative 88 basis points at fiscal year end.

1.2 Historical Spread Comparison

The average 10-year Treasury yield for fiscal year 2023 was 3.96 percent, compared to 2.74 percent in fiscal year 2022 and 1.52 percent in fiscal year 2021. The weighted average interest rate on total federal debt outstanding rose to 2.97 percent as of September 30, 2023, up from 2.07 percent as of September 30, 2022. This 90-basis-point increase in the weighted average rate reflects the rollover of maturing low-coupon debt into higher-rate instruments throughout the fiscal year.

SECTION 2: SPREAD ANALYSIS — TREASURY VERSUS BENCHMARK RATES

2.1 Treasury-OIS Spread

The Treasury-OIS (Overnight Index Swap) spread measures the premium investors demand for holding Treasury securities relative to the expected path of overnight rates. During fiscal year 2023, the 10-year Treasury-OIS spread averaged 42 basis points, compared to 28 basis points in fiscal year 2022. The widening reflects increased term premium associated with elevated inflation uncertainty and growing concerns about federal debt sustainability.

On March 13, 2023, following the collapse of Silicon Valley Bank and Signature Bank, the 2-year Treasury-OIS spread compressed sharply by 61 basis points in a single session as investors sought safe-haven assets, briefly pushing the 2-year Treasury yield from 4.60 percent to 3.99 percent before recovering.

2.2 Treasury-TIPS Breakeven Inflation Rates

Breakeven inflation rates, derived from the spread between nominal Treasury yields and Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS) of equivalent maturity, provide a market-based inflation expectation measure:

Maturity | FY2022 Average | FY2023 Average | Change (bps)
5-Year Breakeven | 3.12% | 2.41% | -71
10-Year Breakeven | 2.68% | 2.29% | -39
30-Year Breakeven | 2.41% | 2.24% | -17

The decline in breakeven rates across all maturities indicates that market participants increasingly credited Federal Reserve tightening actions with bringing inflation toward the 2.0 percent long-run target. The 5-year/5-year forward breakeven rate, which measures expected inflation from 2028 to 2033, averaged 2.34 percent during fiscal year 2023, remaining well-anchored relative to the 3.47 percent peak recorded in April 2022.

SECTION 3: DEBT PORTFOLIO COMPOSITION AND MATURITY STRUCTURE

3.1 Issuance Activity

During fiscal year 2023, the Treasury Department issued $22.7 trillion in gross marketable debt, including rollovers of maturing securities. Net new borrowing from the public totaled $1.84 trillion. The weighted average maturity of new issuance was 5.8 years, compared to 6.4 years in fiscal year 2022, reflecting increased reliance on shorter-dated bills and notes in response to investor demand patterns.

Key issuance highlights:
- Treasury bills (maturities of 52 weeks or less): $14.3 trillion gross issuance, representing 63.0 percent of total gross issuance
- Treasury notes (2- to 10-year maturities): $6.9 trillion gross issuance
- Treasury bonds (20- and 30-year maturities): $0.84 trillion gross issuance
- TIPS across all maturities: $0.41 trillion gross issuance
- Floating Rate Notes (2-year): $0.25 trillion gross issuance

The weighted average maturity of total debt outstanding declined to 5 years and 11 months as of September 30, 2023, from 6 years and 2 months as of September 30, 2022.

3.2 Rollover Risk Assessment

Approximately $7.6 trillion in Treasury securities, or 28.4 percent of total publicly held debt, is scheduled to mature within the next 12 months. At current market rates, rolling over this debt at prevailing yields rather than the original coupon rates is projected to add approximately $112 billion in additional annual interest expense, assuming the federal funds rate remains at 5.25-5.50 percent through June 2024 as reflected in the September 2023 Summary of Economic Projections published by the Federal Open Market Committee.

SECTION 5: OUTLOOK AND POLICY IMPLICATIONS

5.1 Projected Interest Cost Trajectory

The Congressional Budget Office's July 2023 baseline projects net interest costs will reach $1.06 trillion in fiscal year 2024, which would represent the first time in U.S. history that annual net interest payments exceed $1 trillion. Under the CBO's extended baseline, net interest is projected to reach 3.7 percent of GDP by fiscal year 2028 and 6.3 percent of GDP by fiscal year 2053.

5.2 Debt Management Recommendations

The Office of Debt Management's Quarterly Refunding Advisory Committee, chaired by Meghan Graper of Barclays Capital, recommended in its August 2, 2023 report that Treasury consider modest increases in coupon auction sizes across the 2-, 3-, 5-, 7-, 10-, 20-, and 30-year maturities to reduce reliance on short-term bill financing and extend the average maturity of the debt portfolio. The committee noted that bill issuance as a share of total debt outstanding had risen to 22.4 percent, approaching the upper bound of the 15-20 percent recommended range.

5.3 Key Risk Factors

The following factors represent primary risks to the federal interest cost outlook:
- A sustained delay in Federal Reserve rate reductions beyond current market expectations could add an estimated $40-60 billion in additional annual interest costs for each quarter the federal funds rate remains above 4.50 percent
- Deterioration in foreign official demand for Treasury securities, particularly from Japan and China (which held $1.10 trillion and $0.82 trillion respectively as of August 2023), could widen term premiums
- Fiscal year 2023 primary deficit of $1.69 trillion (excluding net interest) requires continued large-scale market borrowing regardless of rate environment
