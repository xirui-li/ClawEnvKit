OFFICE OF DEBT MANAGEMENT
U.S. DEPARTMENT OF THE TREASURY
FISCAL YEAR 2023 ANNUAL REPORT

YIELD SPREAD ANALYSIS: FEDERAL DEBT PORTFOLIO AND MARKET CONDITIONS

Prepared by the Office of Debt Management, Bureau of the Fiscal Service
Publication Date: November 14, 2023
Report Reference: ODM-2023-YSA-047

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents a comprehensive yield spread analysis of U.S. Treasury securities relative to benchmark instruments for Fiscal Year 2023 (October 1, 2022 through September 30, 2023). The analysis examines spread dynamics across the yield curve, evaluates the cost implications for federal borrowing, and assesses market conditions that influenced Treasury issuance strategy throughout the fiscal year. Total federal debt subject to the statutory limit reached $33.17 trillion as of September 30, 2023, with marketable debt outstanding totaling $26.76 trillion.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SECTION 1: OVERVIEW OF FEDERAL DEBT PORTFOLIO

As of September 30, 2023, the composition of marketable Treasury securities was as follows:

Treasury Bills (maturity ≤ 52 weeks):    $5.42 trillion    (20.3% of marketable debt)
Treasury Notes (2–10 year maturity):     $14.61 trillion   (54.6% of marketable debt)
Treasury Bonds (maturity > 10 years):    $4.23 trillion    (15.8% of marketable debt)
Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities: $1.97 trillion    (7.4% of marketable debt)
Floating Rate Notes:                     $0.53 trillion    (1.9% of marketable debt)

The weighted average maturity of the marketable debt portfolio stood at 6.1 years as of September 30, 2023, compared to 6.3 years at the close of Fiscal Year 2022. This modest compression reflects increased reliance on short-duration bill issuance during the first half of the fiscal year as the Federal Open Market Committee pursued its rate-tightening cycle.

Net interest costs on the federal debt totaled $879.3 billion in Fiscal Year 2023, representing an increase of $214.8 billion, or 32.3 percent, compared to the $664.5 billion recorded in Fiscal Year 2022. As a share of gross domestic product, net interest costs rose to 3.3 percent in FY2023, the highest level recorded since Fiscal Year 1998.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SECTION 2: YIELD CURVE CONDITIONS AND SPREAD DYNAMICS

2.1 Benchmark Yield Levels

The Federal Reserve raised the federal funds target rate seven times between October 2022 and July 2023, bringing the upper bound of the target range from 3.25 percent to 5.50 percent. This tightening cycle materially affected Treasury yields across all maturities.

Selected benchmark yields as of key dates:

                          Oct 3, 2022    Mar 31, 2023    Sep 29, 2023
3-Month Treasury Bill:      3.32%          4.74%           5.45%
2-Year Treasury Note:       4.10%          4.60%           5.03%
5-Year Treasury Note:       3.96%          3.96%           4.61%
10-Year Treasury Note:      3.83%          3.96%           4.57%
30-Year Treasury Bond:      3.79%          3.94%           4.70%

The 2-year/10-year spread, a widely monitored indicator of yield curve shape, registered negative 46 basis points on September 29, 2023, compared to negative 27 basis points on October 3, 2022. The curve reached its most deeply inverted point on March 8, 2023, when the 2-year/10-year spread touched negative 108 basis points, the widest inversion recorded since September 1981.

2.2 Treasury-OIS Spread Analysis

The Treasury-Overnight Index Swap (OIS) spread measures the premium investors demand for holding Treasury securities relative to risk-free overnight rate expectations. This spread serves as a proxy for term premium and liquidity conditions.

During FY2023, the 10-year Treasury-OIS spread averaged 28 basis points, compared to an average of 19 basis points in FY2022. The spread widened materially in August and September 2023 following Fitch Ratings' downgrade of U.S. long-term sovereign debt from AAA to AA+ on August 1, 2023. In the two weeks following the downgrade, the 10-year Treasury-OIS spread widened by approximately 14 basis points before partially retracing.

2.3 Treasury-TIPS Breakeven Inflation Spreads

The breakeven inflation rate, derived from the spread between nominal Treasury yields and Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities of equivalent maturity, provides a market-based measure of inflation expectations.

5-Year Breakeven Inflation Rate:
  October 3, 2022:     2.47%
  March 31, 2023:      2.26%
  September 29, 2023:  2.31%

10-Year Breakeven Inflation Rate:
  October 3, 2022:     2.38%
  March 31, 2023:      2.27%
  September 29, 2023:  2.32%

The compression in breakeven rates between October 2022 and the end of FY2023 reflects market confidence that Federal Reserve tightening would succeed in returning inflation toward the 2.0 percent target. The 10-year breakeven rate peaked at 2.52 percent on February 2, 2023, coinciding with stronger-than-expected January 2023 employment data.

SECTION 3: AGENCY AND CORPORATE SPREAD COMPARISONS

3.1 Treasury-Agency Spread

Spreads between Treasury securities and agency debt issued by government-sponsored enterprises provide insight into perceived credit differentiation and market liquidity conditions.

The 10-year Fannie Mae benchmark note traded at a spread of 62 basis points over the 10-year Treasury as of September 29, 2023, compared to 54 basis points one year earlier. The 10-year Freddie Mac benchmark note spread stood at 60 basis points over comparable Treasuries on the same date. These spreads remain well below the crisis-era peaks of 2008 but reflect modest widening attributable to uncertainty surrounding the Federal Housing Finance Agency's conservatorship timeline.

3.2 Investment-Grade Corporate Spread

The ICE BofA U.S. Corporate Index option-adjusted spread (OAS) over Treasuries averaged 127 basis points during FY2023, compared to 154 basis points in FY2022. The tightening of corporate spreads despite rising absolute yields indicates that credit quality concerns diminished even as the cost of capital increased. The spread reached a fiscal-year low of 108 basis points on February 2, 2023, and a fiscal-year high of 161 basis points on October 13, 2022.

3.3 High-Yield Spread

The ICE BofA U.S. High Yield Index OAS averaged 432 basis points over Treasuries during FY2023, compared to 498 basis points in FY2022. The compression in high-yield spreads, despite the most aggressive Federal Reserve tightening cycle since the early 1980s, was attributed by market participants to resilient corporate earnings and a lower-than-anticipated default rate of 2.8 percent for calendar year 2023 through September, according to Moody's Investors Service data.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SECTION 4: INTERNATIONAL SOVEREIGN SPREAD COMPARISONS

The spread between U.S. Treasury yields and comparable sovereign instruments of major economies reflects relative monetary policy trajectories, fiscal conditions, and safe-haven demand.

10-Year Sovereign Yield Spreads vs. U.S. Treasury (as of September 29, 2023):

Germany (Bund):          U.S. Treasury +166 basis points
United Kingdom (Gilt):   U.S. Treasury +14 basis points
Japan (JGB):             U.S. Treasury +396 basis points
Canada:                  U.S. Treasury -6 basis points
Australia:               U.S. Treasury -22 basis points

The U.S.-Germany 10-year spread of 166 basis points as of September 29, 2023, compared to 183 basis points one year earlier, reflects the European Central Bank's own tightening cycle, which brought the ECB deposit facility rate to 4.00 percent by September 2023. The U.S.-Japan spread of 396 basis points remained historically elevated as the Bank of Japan maintained its yield curve control policy, though the BOJ adjusted its 10-year JGB yield ceiling from 0.50 percent to 1.00 percent on July 28, 2023.

SECTION 5: COST IMPLICATIONS FOR FEDERAL BORROWING

5.1 Average Interest Rate on Outstanding Debt

The average interest rate on total federal debt outstanding rose to 2.97 percent as of September 30, 2023, compared to 2.07 percent as of September 30, 2022. This represents the largest single-year increase in the average rate since 1981. The average rate on Treasury bills outstanding reached 5.22 percent, while the average rate on Treasury notes stood at 2.61 percent, reflecting the lagged repricing of longer-duration instruments.

5.2 Rollover Risk Assessment

Approximately $7.6 trillion in Treasury securities, representing 28.4 percent of total marketable debt, is scheduled to mature within the next 12 months. Of this amount, $5.42 trillion consists of Treasury bills. The Office of Debt Management estimates that refinancing this maturing debt at prevailing market rates as of September 30, 2023, would increase annual interest costs by approximately $68 billion relative to the rates at which the maturing securities were originally issued.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SECTION 6: ISSUANCE STRATEGY ADJUSTMENTS

In response to yield curve conditions and investor demand signals gathered through the Treasury Borrowing Advisory Committee (TBAC), the Office of Debt Management implemented the following adjustments during FY2023:

- Increased 2-year note auction sizes from $42 billion to $51 billion per monthly cycle
- Increased 5-year note auction sizes from $43 billion to $52 billion per monthly cycle
- Reintroduced 20-year bond buyback operations, with $12.4 billion in securities repurchased across four operations conducted between May and September 2023
- Maintained 30-year bond auction sizes at $18 billion per monthly cycle, unchanged from FY2022
- Issued $284 billion in net new Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities, a 6.2 percent increase from the $267.4 billion issued in FY2022

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SECTION 7: CONCLUSIONS AND OUTLOOK

Yield spread dynamics in FY2023 were dominated by the Federal Reserve's tightening cycle, the Fitch sovereign downgrade, and elevated Treasury supply requirements driven by a federal deficit of $1.695 trillion. The deeply inverted yield curve, while historically associated with economic contraction, has not yet produced the recessionary conditions that such inversions have preceded in prior cycles.

Looking forward to FY2024, the Office of Debt Management projects net marketable borrowing needs of approximately $1.85 trillion, assuming a federal deficit consistent with the Congressional Budget Office's August 2023 baseline projection of $1.54 trillion for FY2024. Continued monitoring of term premium dynamics, foreign official demand for Treasury securities, and the trajectory of Federal Reserve policy normalization will remain central to issuance strategy decisions.
