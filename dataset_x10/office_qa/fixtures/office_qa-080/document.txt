FISCAL YEARS 2019–2024

Prepared by the Bureau of Financial Management

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents a comprehensive analysis of Commonwealth expenditure patterns across six fiscal years, from FY2018–19 through FY2023–24. Total general fund expenditures grew from $32.7 billion in FY2018–19 to $44.1 billion in FY2023–24, representing a cumulative increase of 34.9 percent over the five-year period. The average annual growth rate of 6.2 percent exceeded the state's average inflation rate of 4.1 percent during the same period, indicating real spending growth of approximately 2.1 percent annually. The three largest expenditure categories—Human Services, Education, and Debt Service—collectively accounted for 71.4 percent of total general fund spending in FY2023–24.

---

SECTION 1: OVERVIEW OF TOTAL EXPENDITURES

Pennsylvania's general fund budget has expanded substantially since FY2018–19, driven primarily by increased Medicaid enrollment, rising public school funding obligations under the Basic Education Funding formula, and growing pension contribution requirements under the Public School Employees' Retirement System (PSERS) and the State Employees' Retirement System (SERS).

Total General Fund Expenditures by Fiscal Year:

FY2018–19: $32.7 billion
FY2019–20: $34.1 billion (increase of $1.4 billion, +4.3%)
FY2020–21: $36.8 billion (increase of $2.7 billion, +7.9%)
FY2021–22: $39.2 billion (increase of $2.4 billion, +6.5%)
FY2022–23: $42.3 billion (increase of $3.1 billion, +7.9%)
FY2023–24: $44.1 billion (increase of $1.8 billion, +4.3%)

The spike observed in FY2020–21 reflects emergency expenditures associated with the COVID-19 pandemic response, including $1.1 billion in supplemental appropriations for the Department of Health and $680 million in emergency rental and utility assistance programs administered through the Pennsylvania Housing Finance Agency.

---

SECTION 2: EXPENDITURE BY MAJOR CATEGORY

2.1 Human Services

The Department of Human Services (DHS) remained the single largest spending agency in the Commonwealth for the sixth consecutive year. DHS expenditures totaled $18.2 billion in FY2023–24, up from $13.9 billion in FY2018–19, a 30.9 percent increase over five years.

Medicaid (Medical Assistance) accounted for $14.6 billion of DHS spending in FY2023–24, representing 33.1 percent of total general fund expenditures. Medicaid enrollment peaked at 3.4 million individuals in March 2023 following the expiration of continuous enrollment protections under the federal Families First Coronavirus Response Act. Enrollment declined to approximately 2.9 million by June 2024 following the federally mandated redetermination process.

Long-term care services, including nursing facility payments and home- and community-based waiver programs, totaled $4.1 billion in FY2023–24, an increase of 11.2 percent from the prior year, driven by rate increases averaging 8.7 percent for nursing facilities effective January 1, 2024.

2.2 Education

Total education spending across all funding streams reached $14.8 billion in FY2023–24. Basic Education Funding (BEF) appropriations totaled $7.83 billion, an increase of $567 million (7.8 percent) over FY2022–23. This increase followed the landmark Commonwealth Court ruling in William Penn School District v. Pennsylvania Department of Education, issued February 7, 2023, which found the existing school funding system unconstitutional. Governor Josh Shapiro's administration committed to a multi-year remediation plan beginning with the FY2023–24 budget.

Special Education funding reached $1.27 billion in FY2023–24, up 5.4 percent from $1.21 billion in FY2022–23. Pre-K Counts and Head Start Supplemental Assistance programs combined received $425 million, representing a 12.6 percent increase from the prior year.

Higher education appropriations to the State System of Higher Education (PASSHE), which comprises 10 universities, totaled $477 million in FY2023–24, a modest increase of 2.1 percent from $467 million in FY2022–23.

2.3 Pension Obligations

Combined employer contributions to PSERS and SERS represented one of the fastest-growing expenditure categories over the analysis period. Total pension contributions from the general fund reached $4.9 billion in FY2023–24, compared to $3.8 billion in FY2018–19, an increase of 28.9 percent.

PSERS employer contribution rate stood at 34.94 percent of payroll in FY2023–24, down from a peak of 36.40 percent in FY2021–22, reflecting improved investment returns and actuarial adjustments following the fund's reported net investment return of 12.4 percent for the fiscal year ending June 30, 2023. PSERS managed assets of approximately $73.6 billion as of June 30, 2024.

SERS employer contribution rate was 31.72 percent of payroll in FY2023–24. The combined unfunded actuarial accrued liability for both systems stood at approximately $44.3 billion as of the most recent actuarial valuations.

2.4 Debt Service

General obligation debt service totaled $1.87 billion in FY2023–24, representing 4.2 percent of total general fund expenditures. Outstanding general obligation debt stood at $10.4 billion as of June 30, 2024. The Commonwealth's debt service coverage ratio was 4.8 to 1, and Moody's Investors Service affirmed Pennsylvania's Aa3 bond rating in March 2024, citing improved reserve fund balances and structural budget improvements.

2.5 Public Safety and Corrections

The Department of Corrections operated 23 state correctional institutions with an average daily population of 37,200 inmates in FY2023–24. Total corrections expenditures reached $2.96 billion, an increase of 3.8 percent from $2.85 billion in FY2022–23. The average annual cost to incarcerate one individual was $57,200 in FY2023–24, up from $52,400 in FY2021–22.

The Pennsylvania State Police received an appropriation of $1.43 billion in FY2023–24, including $148 million for capital expenditures related to fleet replacement and communications infrastructure upgrades.

---

SECTION 3: RESERVE FUND AND RAINY DAY FUND STATUS

The Budget Stabilization Reserve Fund (Rainy Day Fund) balance reached $6.1 billion as of June 30, 2024, equivalent to approximately 13.8 percent of annual general fund expenditures. This represents a significant improvement from the fund's balance of $340 million as of June 30, 2019. The substantial accumulation resulted from surplus revenues during FY2020–21 and FY2021–22, when personal income tax and sales tax collections exceeded projections by $3.2 billion and $2.7 billion respectively, partly attributable to federal stimulus payments and elevated consumer spending.

---

SECTION 4: YEAR-OVER-YEAR VARIANCE ANALYSIS

The $1.8 billion increase in general fund expenditures from FY2022–23 to FY2023–24 was distributed as follows:

- Human Services: +$890 million (+5.1%)
- Education: +$680 million (+4.8%)
- Pension contributions: +$210 million (+4.5%)
- Debt service: -$40 million (-2.1%)
- Corrections: +$110 million (+3.8%)
- All other agencies: -$50 million (-0.6%)

The decline in debt service reflects the retirement of $425 million in general obligation bonds that matured on November 1, 2023, partially offset by $385 million in new bond issuances completed in April 2024 at a weighted average interest rate of 4.37 percent.

SECTION 5: FIVE-YEAR COMPOUND ANNUAL GROWTH RATES BY CATEGORY

Human Services: 5.5% CAGR
Education: 6.8% CAGR
Pension Contributions: 5.2% CAGR
Debt Service: 1.9% CAGR
Corrections: 3.4% CAGR
State Police: 4.7% CAGR
General Government Operations: 2.8% CAGR

Education exhibited the highest compound annual growth rate among major categories, reflecting both the court-ordered funding remediation and enrollment-driven formula adjustments. General Government Operations showed the most restrained growth, consistent with administrative efficiency initiatives implemented under Executive Order 2021-07, signed by Governor Tom Wolf on March 15, 2021, which directed a 5 percent reduction in administrative overhead across executive agencies.

---

SECTION 6: OUTLOOK AND PROJECTIONS

The Independent Fiscal Office (IFO) projects general fund expenditures will reach approximately $46.8 billion in FY2024–25, representing a projected increase of 6.1 percent. Key drivers include continued Basic Education Funding increases estimated at $526 million, Medicaid rate adjustments anticipated to add $340 million, and pension contributions projected to remain near current levels at $5.0 billion.

Revenue projections from the IFO's June 2024 Economic and Budget Outlook anticipate general fund revenues of $47.2 billion in FY2024–25, suggesting a projected surplus of approximately $400 million before any supplemental appropriations. However, the IFO noted downside risks including potential softening in personal income tax collections if labor market conditions deteriorate and uncertainty surrounding federal Medicaid matching rate adjustments scheduled for review in October 2024.

---

SECTION 7: METHODOLOGY AND DATA SOURCES

Expenditure data in this report is drawn from the Commonwealth's SAP-based financial management system, the Integrated Enterprise System (IES), as of August 31, 2024. All figures represent actual expenditures unless otherwise noted. Fiscal years run from July 1 through June 30. Pension data was sourced from the PSERS and SERS Comprehensive Annual Financial Reports for FY2023–24. Enrollment and caseload data was provided by the Department of Human Services Office of Medical Assistance Programs. Bond rating information was obtained from published Moody's and Standard & Poor's rating actions. Inflation adjustments use the Bureau of Labor Statistics Consumer Price Index for All Urban Consumers, Philadelphia-Camden-Wilmington metropolitan area.
