EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents the quarterly analysis of U.S. Treasury bond yield changes for the period ending June 30, 2024. During the third quarter of fiscal year 2024, the benchmark 10-year Treasury note yield rose from 4.20 percent on April 1, 2024, to 4.36 percent on June 28, 2024, representing a net increase of 16 basis points over the quarter. Short-term yields demonstrated greater volatility, with the 2-year Treasury note peaking at 4.98 percent on May 14, 2024, before settling at 4.71 percent at quarter close. The Federal Reserve maintained the federal funds rate target range at 5.25 to 5.50 percent throughout the quarter, consistent with its posture since July 2023. Total outstanding marketable Treasury debt as of June 30, 2024, stood at $27.4 trillion.

SECTION 1: METHODOLOGY AND CALCULATION FRAMEWORK

1.1 Basis Point Calculation Standard

All yield change calculations in this report follow the standard basis point convention, where one basis point equals 0.01 percentage points (100 basis points = 1.00 percent). Yield data is sourced from the Daily Treasury Par Yield Curve Rates published by the U.S. Department of the Treasury, which reflects closing market yields on actively traded non-inflation-indexed issues adjusted to constant maturities.

The yield change formula applied throughout this report is:

Yield Change (basis points) = (Ending Yield – Beginning Yield) × 100

For example, the 10-year note calculation for Q3 FY2024:
(4.36% – 4.20%) × 100 = 16 basis points

Duration-adjusted price change estimates use the modified duration approximation:

Approximate Price Change (%) = –Modified Duration × Yield Change (decimal)

For the 10-year benchmark note with a modified duration of 8.7 years:
Approximate Price Change = –8.7 × 0.0016 = –1.39 percent

1.2 Data Sources

Primary yield data is drawn from the H.15 Selected Interest Rates statistical release published by the Federal Reserve Board of Governors. Secondary verification is performed against Bloomberg Generic Composite (BVAL) rates and ICE BofA U.S. Treasury Index data. All figures represent end-of-day closing yields unless otherwise noted.

SECTION 3: YIELD CURVE SHAPE ANALYSIS

3.1 Inversion Metrics

The 2-year/10-year Treasury spread, a widely monitored recession indicator, began the quarter at –42 basis points (4.62% minus 4.20% = –0.42%) and narrowed to –35 basis points by June 28, 2024 (4.71% minus 4.36% = –0.35%). This represents a 7 basis point reduction in inversion depth, suggesting a modest curve steepening trend consistent with market expectations for eventual Federal Reserve rate reductions.

The 3-month/10-year spread, which the Federal Reserve Bank of New York uses in its recession probability model, stood at –98 basis points on June 28, 2024 (5.34% minus 4.36% = –0.98%). The New York Fed's model, as of June 2024, assigned a 56.4 percent probability of recession within the following 12 months based on this spread.

3.2 Term Premium Estimates

The Adrian, Crump, and Moench (ACM) term premium model, maintained by the Federal Reserve Bank of New York, estimated the 10-year term premium at 0.32 percent as of June 28, 2024, compared to –0.14 percent at the start of the quarter on April 1, 2024. This 46 basis point increase in term premium reflects growing investor demand for compensation against duration risk amid persistent inflation uncertainty.

SECTION 4: AUCTION RESULTS AND YIELD IMPLICATIONS

4.1 Key Auction Outcomes

The Treasury conducted 47 separate securities auctions during Q3 FY2024. Selected results of significance include:

10-Year Note Auction, May 8, 2024:
- Amount Offered: $42 billion
- High Yield: 4.483 percent
- Bid-to-Cover Ratio: 2.49
- Indirect Bidders (proxy for foreign demand): 66.8 percent
- Direct Bidders: 19.3 percent
- Primary Dealers: 13.9 percent
- Stop-Through: +1.3 basis points (auction cleared through the when-issued yield)

30-Year Bond Auction, May 9, 2024:
- Amount Offered: $25 billion
- High Yield: 4.635 percent
- Bid-to-Cover Ratio: 2.41
- Indirect Bidders: 63.2 percent
- Tail: +3.4 basis points (auction cleared above the when-issued yield, indicating weak demand)

The May 9 30-year auction result was characterized by Treasury Borrowing Advisory Committee (TBAC) member Janet Rilling, Managing Director at Allspring Global Investments, as "the weakest long-end auction of the calendar year to date," contributing to a 6 basis point selloff in 30-year yields on that date.

4.2 Quarterly Issuance Volume

Total gross Treasury issuance during Q3 FY2024 reached $7.2 trillion, of which $6.1 trillion represented rollover of maturing securities and $1.1 trillion represented net new borrowing. The weighted average maturity of new issuance was 5.8 years, compared to 6.1 years in Q2 FY2024, reflecting Treasury's tactical shift toward shorter-dated instruments in response to elevated long-end yields.

SECTION 5: INFLATION AND MONETARY POLICY CONTEXT

5.1 Inflation Data Impact on Yields

The Consumer Price Index for All Urban Consumers (CPI-U) rose 3.4 percent year-over-year in April 2024 and 3.3 percent in May 2024, both releases occurring within the quarter. The Personal Consumption Expenditures (PCE) price index, the Federal Reserve's preferred inflation gauge, registered 2.7 percent year-over-year in April 2024. These readings, while declining from the 9.1 percent peak recorded in June 2022, remained above the Federal Open Market Committee's 2.0 percent target and contributed to sustained upward pressure on intermediate and long-term yields.

5.2 Federal Reserve Communications

Federal Reserve Chair Jerome Powell, speaking at the May 1, 2024, post-FOMC press conference, stated that the Committee did not expect it would be appropriate to reduce the target range until it had gained greater confidence that inflation was moving sustainably toward 2 percent. The federal funds futures market, as reflected in CME Group's FedWatch Tool, priced in a 67 percent probability of at least one 25 basis point rate cut by the September 18, 2024, FOMC meeting as of June 28, 2024.

SECTION 6: INTEREST COST IMPLICATIONS

6.1 Debt Service Projections

The 16 basis point increase in 10-year yields during Q3 FY2024 carries material implications for federal borrowing costs. Based on the $27.4 trillion outstanding marketable debt portfolio, the Office of Management and Budget estimates that each 100 basis point increase in average borrowing rates adds approximately $274 billion to annual interest expense over a 10-year horizon as debt matures and is refinanced.

For Q3 FY2024 specifically, net interest outlays totaled $234 billion, representing a 34.2 percent increase over the $174 billion recorded in Q3 FY2023. Annualized, net interest payments are projected to reach $892 billion for fiscal year 2024, which would represent 3.1 percent of projected GDP and exceed defense discretionary spending of $886 billion for the first time in modern U.S. fiscal history.

6.2 Break-Even Inflation Rate

The 10-year break-even inflation rate, derived by subtracting the 10-year Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS) yield from the nominal 10-year Treasury yield, stood at 2.33 percent on June 28, 2024, compared to 2.28 percent on April 1, 2024. This 5 basis point widening indicates that market participants modestly increased their long-term inflation expectations during the quarter.

SECTION 7: OUTLOOK AND RISK FACTORS

The Office of Debt Management identifies the following primary risk factors for yield movements in Q4 FY2024 (July 1 – September 30, 2024):

- Federal Reserve rate decision trajectory: A September 2024 rate cut would likely compress short-term yields by 25 basis points while potentially steepening the 2s/10s curve toward –10 basis points.
- Treasury supply pressure: Announced Q4 FY2024 net borrowing need of $740 billion, as disclosed in the May 2024 Quarterly Refunding Statement, may sustain upward pressure on term premiums.
- Geopolitical risk premium: Ongoing conflicts in Ukraine and the Middle East continue to generate episodic flight-to-quality demand, creating downward yield pressure during risk-off episodes.
- Labor market data: Monthly nonfarm payroll releases, which averaged 248,000 jobs per month in Q3 FY2024, will remain a primary driver of rate expectations.
