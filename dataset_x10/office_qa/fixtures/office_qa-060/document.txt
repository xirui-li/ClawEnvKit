EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents a comprehensive analysis of Commonwealth expenditure patterns across all major appropriation categories for fiscal years 2019 through 2024. Total general fund expenditures grew from $32.7 billion in FY2019 to $44.1 billion in FY2024, representing a cumulative increase of 34.9 percent over the five-year period. The analysis identifies healthcare-related spending as the dominant growth driver, examines the impact of federal relief funding on state budget composition, and highlights areas where structural spending pressures require legislative attention in the upcoming FY2025 budget cycle.

This report was prepared by the Bureau of Financial Management under the direction of Budget Secretary Uri Monson and is intended for use by the General Assembly, the Governor's Office, and the public.

SECTION 1: OVERVIEW OF TOTAL EXPENDITURES

General fund spending in Pennsylvania has increased at an average annual rate of 6.2 percent since FY2019. The most significant single-year jump occurred between FY2020 and FY2021, when expenditures rose by $3.4 billion (10.8 percent), driven primarily by expanded Medicaid enrollment during the COVID-19 pandemic and supplemental appropriations authorized under Act 24 of 2020.

The moderation in growth between FY2023 and FY2024 reflects the expiration of several pandemic-era supplemental appropriations and a deliberate effort by the Shapiro administration to constrain discretionary spending growth to below 3 percent in non-entitlement categories.

SECTION 2: EXPENDITURE BY MAJOR CATEGORY

2.1 Health and Human Services

The Department of Human Services (DHS) remains the single largest recipient of general fund appropriations, accounting for $18.6 billion in FY2024, or 42.2 percent of total general fund spending. This compares to $13.1 billion in FY2019, representing a 42.0 percent increase over five years.

Medicaid (Medical Assistance) alone consumed $14.9 billion in FY2024, up from $10.4 billion in FY2019. Enrollment in the Medical Assistance program peaked at 3.71 million recipients in January 2023 before declining to approximately 3.44 million by June 2024 following the federal unwinding of continuous enrollment protections that had been in place since March 2020.

Key DHS expenditure components in FY2024:
- Medical Assistance – Physical HealthChoices: $8.2 billion
- Medical Assistance – Long-Term Care: $3.1 billion
- Mental Health and Substance Abuse Services: $1.4 billion
- Child Welfare Services: $1.2 billion
- Cash Assistance Programs: $0.7 billion

2.2 Education

The Department of Education received $14.8 billion in combined general fund and special fund appropriations in FY2024. Basic education funding, the largest single line item within the education budget, totaled $8.07 billion, an increase of $567 million (7.6 percent) over FY2023. This increase followed the landmark Commonwealth Court ruling in William Penn School District v. Pennsylvania Department of Education, issued February 7, 2023, which found the state's school funding system unconstitutional. The ruling prompted the General Assembly to accelerate equity-based funding reforms.

Special education funding reached $1.27 billion in FY2024, while higher education appropriations to the State System of Higher Education (PASSHE) totaled $477 million, unchanged from FY2023.

2.3 Transportation

The Pennsylvania Department of Transportation (PennDOT) expended $6.3 billion in FY2024 across all fund sources, including $2.1 billion from the Motor License Fund and $1.9 billion in federal highway formula funds received under the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act of 2021. Capital program expenditures for highway and bridge construction totaled $3.8 billion, with 214 bridge replacement projects completed during the fiscal year.

2.4 Public Safety and Corrections

The Department of Corrections operated with a general fund appropriation of $2.84 billion in FY2024, a 2.1 percent increase from $2.78 billion in FY2023. The state prison population stood at 37,412 as of June 30, 2024, down from a peak of 51,638 in FY2008 but representing a slight increase of 1.4 percent from the FY2023 year-end count of 36,895. The average annual cost to incarcerate one individual in a Pennsylvania state correctional institution was $56,200 in FY2024.

2.5 Debt Service

General obligation debt service totaled $1.02 billion in FY2024. The Commonwealth's outstanding general obligation debt stood at $10.3 billion as of June 30, 2024. Pennsylvania's debt service as a percentage of general fund revenues was 2.4 percent, within the 5.0 percent threshold recommended by the Government Finance Officers Association (GFOA).

SECTION 3: FEDERAL FUNDS AND PANDEMIC-ERA SPENDING

Pennsylvania received $7.2 billion in federal American Rescue Plan Act (ARPA) State and Local Fiscal Recovery Funds, allocated to the Commonwealth in two tranches: $3.6 billion in May 2021 and $3.6 billion in May 2022. As of June 30, 2024, $6.8 billion of these funds had been obligated, with the remaining $400 million committed to infrastructure and broadband expansion projects with December 31, 2024 obligation deadlines.

ARPA funds were deployed across the following priority areas:
- Healthcare system stabilization and nursing home support: $1.9 billion
- Water, sewer, and broadband infrastructure: $1.6 billion
- Small business and economic recovery grants: $1.1 billion
- Housing and rental assistance: $0.9 billion
- Education and workforce development: $0.8 billion
- Public health response and vaccine distribution: $0.5 billion

The infusion of federal relief funds temporarily masked structural imbalances in the general fund. The Independent Fiscal Office (IFO) projects that as ARPA spending concludes, the Commonwealth will face a structural deficit of approximately $2.3 billion beginning in FY2026 unless offsetting revenue measures or expenditure reductions are enacted.

SECTION 4: EXPENDITURE EFFICIENCY INDICATORS

The Bureau of Financial Management tracks several efficiency metrics to assess the productivity of state spending relative to population and economic output.

Per Capita Expenditure: General fund spending per Pennsylvania resident increased from $2,556 in FY2019 to $3,441 in FY2024, based on a state population of 12.82 million (2023 U.S. Census Bureau estimate). This represents a 34.6 percent increase in nominal terms and approximately 11.2 percent in inflation-adjusted terms using the Bureau of Labor Statistics CPI-U index.

Expenditure as Percent of Gross State Product: Pennsylvania's general fund expenditures represented 5.8 percent of the state's gross state product of $762 billion in FY2024, compared to 5.4 percent in FY2019.

Administrative Cost Ratio: Statewide administrative and overhead costs across all agencies totaled $1.87 billion in FY2024, representing 4.2 percent of total expenditures. This ratio has remained relatively stable since FY2020, when it stood at 4.4 percent, reflecting workforce reductions through attrition and consolidation of back-office functions under the Governor's Office of Administration.

SECTION 5: FIVE-YEAR OUTLOOK AND RISK FACTORS

The Bureau of Financial Management identifies the following as primary expenditure risk factors for FY2025 through FY2029:

1. Medicaid Cost Growth: Federal enhanced matching funds (FMAP) that provided Pennsylvania with an additional $1.1 billion annually during the pandemic period have fully expired as of December 31, 2023. The state share of Medicaid costs is projected to increase by $680 million in FY2025 as a direct result.

2. Pension Obligations: Combined employer contributions to the Pennsylvania State Employees' Retirement System (SERS) and the Public School Employees' Retirement System (PSERS) totaled $5.1 billion in FY2024. PSERS reported an actuarial funded ratio of 57.8 percent as of December 31, 2023, requiring sustained elevated contributions through at least FY2033 under current actuarial assumptions.

3. Infrastructure Maintenance Backlog: PennDOT estimates a $13.4 billion backlog in deferred maintenance for state highways and bridges as of July 2024. Without additional dedicated funding, this backlog is projected to grow to $18.7 billion by FY2030.

4. Personnel Costs: The Commonwealth employs approximately 78,000 full-time equivalent workers. Collective bargaining agreements covering SEIU Local 668 and AFSCME Council 13, both expiring June 30, 2025, are expected to result in wage increases of 3.5 to 4.5 percent annually, adding an estimated $210 to $270 million per year to personnel expenditures.

SECTION 6: CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS

The five-year expenditure trend reveals a Commonwealth that has expanded service delivery substantially, supported in part by extraordinary federal assistance that is now concluding. The structural fiscal gap projected for FY2026 demands early legislative action. The Bureau of Financial Management recommends:

- Initiating a comprehensive program evaluation review of all DHS line items exceeding $500 million by January 31, 2025
- Establishing a dedicated infrastructure maintenance reserve funded at $300 million annually beginning in FY2026
- Accelerating PSERS reform discussions to reduce the unfunded liability below $30 billion by FY2035
- Implementing zero-based budgeting pilots in at least three cabinet agencies during FY2025
