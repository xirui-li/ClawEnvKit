EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents a comprehensive analysis of United States defense expenditures for Fiscal Year 2024 (October 1, 2023 through September 30, 2024), with comparative data from FY2020 through FY2023. Total national defense spending reached $886.4 billion in FY2024, representing a 4.1 percent increase over the FY2023 enacted level of $858.0 billion. Defense spending constituted approximately 13.1 percent of total federal outlays and 3.2 percent of gross domestic product. This report was prepared by the Office of Management and Budget in coordination with the Department of Defense Comptroller's Office under the direction of Deputy Comptroller Michael McCord.

SECTION 1: OVERVIEW OF DEFENSE APPROPRIATIONS

The FY2024 National Defense Authorization Act, signed into law on December 22, 2023, authorized $886.3 billion for national defense activities. This figure encompasses both discretionary and mandatory spending components across the Department of Defense and defense-related activities of other federal agencies, including the Department of Energy's National Nuclear Security Administration.

The base defense budget for FY2024 stood at $841.4 billion, while Overseas Contingency Operations and emergency supplemental funding accounted for an additional $44.9 billion, primarily directed toward security assistance to Ukraine and Indo-Pacific deterrence initiatives.

Five-Year Defense Spending Trajectory (in billions of dollars):

FY2020: $738.0
FY2021: $753.5
FY2022: $782.0
FY2023: $858.0
FY2024: $886.4

The compound annual growth rate across this five-year period equals 4.7 percent, outpacing the average annual inflation rate of 4.1 percent measured by the Consumer Price Index over the same interval, indicating modest real growth in defense purchasing power.

SECTION 2: BREAKDOWN BY MILITARY DEPARTMENT AND MAJOR CATEGORY

2.1 Allocation by Military Department

The FY2024 base budget was distributed across the military departments as follows:

Department of the Army: $185.7 billion (22.1% of base budget)
Department of the Navy (including Marine Corps): $230.8 billion (27.4%)
Department of the Air Force (including Space Force): $215.1 billion (25.6%)
Defense-Wide Activities: $209.8 billion (24.9%)

The Department of the Navy received the largest single-department increase in absolute terms, growing by $14.2 billion or 6.6 percent compared to FY2023, driven primarily by shipbuilding accounts and submarine procurement under the AUKUS partnership framework.

2.2 Allocation by Major Appropriation Category

Personnel (Military Pay and Benefits): $176.2 billion
Operation and Maintenance: $302.8 billion
Procurement: $170.3 billion
Research, Development, Test and Evaluation (RDT&E): $145.7 billion
Military Construction and Family Housing: $16.9 billion
Revolving and Management Funds: $29.5 billion

Operation and Maintenance represents the single largest category at 34.2 percent of the base budget, a proportion that has remained relatively stable since FY2018. The RDT&E account grew by 7.3 percent year-over-year, the largest percentage increase among major categories, reflecting accelerated investment in hypersonic weapons, artificial intelligence integration, and next-generation communications infrastructure.

SECTION 3: MAJOR PROCUREMENT PROGRAMS

The following programs represented the largest individual procurement investments in FY2024:

F-35 Joint Strike Fighter Program: $15.6 billion
The Department of Defense procured 83 aircraft across all three variants in FY2024. The program, managed by Lockheed Martin Corporation, has delivered a cumulative total of 1,019 aircraft to U.S. and allied forces as of September 30, 2024. Unit cost for the F-35A variant averaged $82.5 million in FY2024, down from $89.2 million in FY2022, reflecting learning curve efficiencies.

Virginia-Class Submarine Program: $9.8 billion
The Navy funded two Virginia-class submarines in FY2024, consistent with the multi-year procurement contract awarded in 2019. General Dynamics Electric Boat and Huntington Ingalls Industries share production responsibilities. The program office, located in Groton, Connecticut, reported an average unit cost of $3.9 billion per submarine for the Block V configuration.

Columbia-Class Ballistic Missile Submarine: $6.1 billion
The lead ship, USS District of Columbia (SSBN-826), remains under construction at General Dynamics Electric Boat. The program represents the Department of the Navy's highest acquisition priority and is scheduled for delivery in FY2027, with initial operational capability projected for 2031.

B-21 Raider Long-Range Strike Bomber: $5.1 billion
Northrop Grumman Corporation continued low-rate initial production of the B-21 Raider at its Palmdale, California facility. The Air Force plans to acquire a minimum of 100 aircraft, with the program achieving first flight on November 10, 2023, at Edwards Air Force Base.

Guided Multiple Launch Rocket System (GMLRS) and HIMARS Procurement: $2.4 billion
Demand for GMLRS munitions surged following combat validation in Ukraine. Lockheed Martin's Camden, Arkansas facility expanded production capacity to 14,000 rockets annually, up from approximately 8,000 in FY2021.

SECTION 4: PERSONNEL COSTS AND MILITARY COMPENSATION

Military end strength as of September 30, 2024, totaled 1,328,400 active duty service members, a decrease of 11,200 from the FY2023 level of 1,339,600. Recruiting shortfalls, particularly in the Army, contributed to this reduction. The Army fell approximately 10,000 soldiers short of its authorized end strength of 452,000.

The FY2024 military pay raise of 5.2 percent, the largest in more than two decades, took effect January 1, 2024. This increase added approximately $8.3 billion to the personnel appropriation compared to FY2023 baseline projections. The Basic Allowance for Housing also increased by an average of 5.4 percent across all military housing areas.

Defense Health Program expenditures totaled $57.8 billion in FY2024, representing 6.5 percent of the total defense budget. The Military Health System served approximately 9.6 million beneficiaries, including active duty personnel, retirees, and their dependents.

Retirement and disability accrual costs, managed through the Military Retirement Fund administered by the Department of Defense, totaled $29.4 billion in FY2024.

SECTION 5: UKRAINE SECURITY ASSISTANCE AND SUPPLEMENTAL FUNDING

Congress appropriated $61.4 billion in supplemental security assistance for Ukraine across multiple legislative packages between February 2022 and April 2024. Of this total, $44.2 billion was designated for direct military assistance, including weapons transfers, ammunition replenishment, and training support. The remaining $17.2 billion supported economic stabilization and humanitarian programs administered by the State Department and USAID.

In FY2024 specifically, the Department of Defense obligated $18.7 billion in Ukraine-related security assistance, including $6.2 billion in Presidential Drawdown Authority transfers of equipment from existing U.S. military stocks and $12.5 billion in Ukraine Security Assistance Initiative funding for new procurement.

SECTION 6: RESEARCH, DEVELOPMENT, TEST AND EVALUATION

The $145.7 billion RDT&E appropriation funded programs across six budget activity categories. The three largest investment areas were:

Advanced Computing and Artificial Intelligence: $3.8 billion, managed through the Chief Digital and Artificial Intelligence Office established in February 2022 under the leadership of Chief Digital and AI Officer Craig Martell.

Hypersonic Weapons Development: $4.7 billion across Army, Navy, and Air Force programs, including the Conventional Prompt Strike program and the Air-Launched Rapid Response Weapon.

Space Systems and Satellite Communications: $6.2 billion, with the Space Force receiving $3.1 billion specifically for next-generation GPS III Follow-On satellites and the Protected Tactical SATCOM program.

SECTION 7: INTERNATIONAL COMPARISONS AND NATO BURDEN SHARING

The United States defense expenditure of $886.4 billion in FY2024 represented approximately 37 percent of total global military spending, based on Stockholm International Peace Research Institute preliminary estimates. The next largest defense budgets were China at an estimated $296 billion, Russia at approximately $109 billion, and India at $83.6 billion.

Among NATO allies, 23 of 32 member nations met or exceeded the alliance's 2 percent of GDP defense spending benchmark in 2024, up from 11 nations in 2023. Poland led all NATO members at 4.12 percent of GDP, followed by Estonia at 3.43 percent and the United States at 3.2 percent.

SECTION 8: PROJECTED SPENDING AND FUTURE YEARS DEFENSE PROGRAM

The FY2025 President's Budget Request, submitted to Congress on March 11, 2024, proposed $849.8 billion for national defense, a figure subsequently revised upward through the congressional appropriations process. The Future Years Defense Program projects cumulative defense spending of approximately $4.6 trillion across FY2025 through FY2029, assuming annual growth rates averaging 1 percent above inflation.

The Congressional Budget Office, in its January 2025 Budget and Economic Outlook, projected that maintaining current defense programs and commitments would require defense spending to reach approximately $1.0 trillion annually by FY2030 under most baseline scenarios.

CONCLUSION

FY2024 defense spending of $886.4 billion reflects sustained investment in modernization, personnel compensation, and allied security commitments. The 4.1 percent year-over-year increase maintained modest real growth while prioritizing readiness, nuclear deterrence recapitalization, and emerging technology domains. Continued oversight by the House and Senate Armed Services Committees, combined with Department of Defense Inspector General reviews, will remain essential to ensuring efficient allocation of these resources in FY2025 and beyond.
