EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents a comprehensive comparison of departmental budget allocations and expenditures for the Commonwealth of Pennsylvania across fiscal years 2022-2023 and 2023-2024. The analysis covers 14 principal departments, examining appropriated amounts, actual expenditures, variance figures, and year-over-year growth rates. Total general fund appropriations for FY2023-2024 reached $45.3 billion, representing an increase of $2.1 billion, or 4.86 percent, over the prior fiscal year's appropriation of $43.2 billion. This report was compiled under the direction of Budget Secretary Uri Monson and reflects data certified by the Office of the Comptroller as of September 30, 2024.

SECTION 1: METHODOLOGY AND SCOPE

Data for this report was drawn from the Commonwealth's Integrated Enterprise System (IES), the official financial management platform administered by the Office of the Budget. Figures represent final appropriations as enacted under Act 1A of 2023 (signed June 30, 2023) and Act 1A of 2024 (signed July 11, 2024). Expenditure data reflects actual disbursements through the close of each fiscal year on June 30.

Departments are categorized under three broad functional areas: Health and Human Services, Public Safety and Justice, and General Government Operations. Supplemental appropriations enacted mid-year are included in the final appropriated totals. Capital expenditures are reported separately from operating budgets and are not included in the general fund comparisons unless otherwise noted.

All figures are reported in millions of U.S. dollars unless otherwise specified. Percentage variances are calculated on appropriated totals, not actual expenditures, to provide a consistent basis for legislative comparison.

SECTION 3: NOTABLE BUDGET CHANGES AND ANALYSIS

3.1 DEPARTMENT OF DRUG AND ALCOHOL PROGRAMS

The most significant percentage increase among all departments was recorded by the Department of Drug and Alcohol Programs, which received an additional $59.6 million in FY2023-2024, representing a 19.85 percent increase over the prior year. Secretary Latika Davis attributed this increase to the continued expansion of the HEAL (Helping Everyone Achieve Long-term Recovery) initiative, which added 47 new treatment facility contracts across 23 counties. Opioid-related overdose deaths in Pennsylvania totaled 5,158 in calendar year 2022, according to the Pennsylvania State Coroners Association, providing the primary justification for the funding surge.

3.2 DEPARTMENT OF EDUCATION

The Department of Education received the largest absolute dollar increase among non-entitlement departments, gaining $717.5 million for a total appropriation of $8,921.6 million. This 8.74 percent increase reflects the implementation of the Commonwealth Court's ruling in William Penn School District v. Pennsylvania Department of Education, decided February 7, 2023, which found the state's school funding system unconstitutional. The Basic Education Funding line item alone increased from $6,124.3 million to $6,789.1 million, a jump of $664.8 million or 10.85 percent. An additional $52.7 million was directed to the Level Up supplemental funding program targeting the 100 lowest-funded school districts.

3.3 DEPARTMENT OF HUMAN SERVICES

As the largest single department in the Commonwealth's budget, the Department of Human Services appropriation of $17,614.3 million represents 38.88 percent of total general fund spending in FY2023-2024. The $771.6 million increase was driven primarily by Medical Assistance (Medicaid) enrollment growth, with the department serving an average of 3.4 million enrollees per month during FY2023-2024, up from 3.1 million in the prior year. Secretary Dr. Val Arkoosh noted that the expiration of federal continuous enrollment protections under the Families First Coronavirus Response Act on March 31, 2023, required the department to conduct eligibility redeterminations for all enrollees, resulting in a net reduction of approximately 287,000 individuals from the rolls by June 30, 2024.

3.4 DEPARTMENT OF LABOR AND INDUSTRY

The only department to experience a budget reduction was the Department of Labor and Industry, which saw its appropriation decrease by $14.5 million, or 1.31 percent, to $1,089.7 million. This reduction reflects the normalization of Unemployment Compensation program costs following the post-pandemic period. UC benefit payments peaked at $4.2 billion in FY2020-2021 and have declined steadily, with FY2023-2024 benefit disbursements projected at $1.1 billion. The department's administrative operations budget remained essentially flat at $214.3 million.

SECTION 5: FIVE-YEAR TREND SUMMARY

Total general fund appropriations have grown from $36.8 billion in FY2019-2020 to $45.3 billion in FY2023-2024, representing cumulative growth of 23.1 percent over five fiscal years. Adjusted for the Pennsylvania Consumer Price Index, which increased 19.4 percent over the same period, real budget growth amounts to approximately 3.7 percent in inflation-adjusted terms.

The Health and Human Services functional category has grown from 56.2 percent of total appropriations in FY2019-2020 to 59.4 percent in FY2023-2024, reflecting the increasing share of the budget consumed by Medicaid and related entitlement programs. Public Safety and Justice has remained relatively stable at approximately 11.3 percent of total appropriations across the five-year period.

SECTION 6: RECOMMENDATIONS

The Bureau of Financial Management and Analysis offers the following recommendations for consideration in the FY2024-2025 budget development process:

1. The Department of Drug and Alcohol Programs should be required to submit quarterly performance metrics tied to the HEAL initiative, including treatment completion rates and 12-month sobriety outcomes, to justify continued funding at the elevated FY2023-2024 level.

2. The Department of Health should work with the federal Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services to accelerate grant matching timelines to reduce year-end lapse balances, which totaled $63.7 million in FY2023-2024.

3. The Basic Education Funding formula should be reviewed by the Joint State Government Commission to assess whether the $664.8 million increase in FY2023-2024 is being distributed in accordance with the court's equity requirements.

4. Departments with utilization rates below 95 percent for two consecutive fiscal years should be subject to enhanced budget justification requirements during the annual budget hearing process before the House and Senate Appropriations Committees.
