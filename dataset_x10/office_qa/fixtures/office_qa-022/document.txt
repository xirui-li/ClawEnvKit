EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents a comprehensive comparison of departmental appropriations and expenditures for the Commonwealth of Pennsylvania across fiscal year 2023–2024 (July 1, 2023 through June 30, 2024) and fiscal year 2022–2023 (July 1, 2022 through June 30, 2023). The analysis was compiled under the direction of Budget Secretary Uri Monson and reflects final certified expenditure data submitted by all executive branch agencies as of September 30, 2024.

Total general fund expenditures for FY 2023–2024 reached $42.8 billion, representing an increase of $2.1 billion, or 5.2 percent, over the FY 2022–2023 total of $40.7 billion. This growth was driven primarily by increased allocations in the Department of Human Services, the Department of Education, and the Department of Corrections. Three departments recorded year-over-year spending decreases, while two departments held expenditures flat within a margin of 0.5 percent.

SECTION 1: METHODOLOGY AND DATA SOURCES

All figures presented in this report are drawn from the Commonwealth's Integrated Enterprise System (IES), the official accounting platform maintained by the Office of Comptroller Operations. Departmental submissions were reviewed for accuracy by the Bureau of Financial Management and Analysis between August 1 and September 30, 2024. Figures represent actual expenditures against enacted appropriations and do not include supplemental appropriations approved after June 30, 2024.

Percentage change calculations are based on final certified expenditures, not original appropriation amounts. Departments with federal pass-through funding exceeding 40 percent of their total budget are noted separately in Section 4. All dollar figures are expressed in nominal terms and have not been adjusted for inflation.

SECTION 3: NOTABLE BUDGET CHANGES AND ANALYSIS

3.1 Department of Education — Largest Proportional Increase Among Major Agencies

The Department of Education recorded the largest percentage increase among agencies with budgets exceeding $1 billion, growing 7.0 percent from $7.841 billion to $8.391 billion. This increase reflects the full-year implementation of Act 54 of 2023, the Pennsylvania School Funding Reform Act, which directed an additional $567 million in basic education subsidies to the 100 lowest-wealth school districts in the Commonwealth. Secretary of Education Khalid Mumin identified the Allentown, Reading, and Philadelphia school districts as the three largest recipients of the new formula-driven allocations.

The Department's special education line item grew from $1.24 billion in FY 2022–2023 to $1.38 billion in FY 2023–2024, an increase of $140 million or 11.3 percent, the largest single-line growth within the education budget.

3.2 Department of Human Services — Largest Absolute Dollar Increase

The Department of Human Services (DHS) accounted for $913.6 million of the Commonwealth's total $2.101 billion spending increase, making it the single largest driver of year-over-year budget growth. DHS Secretary Dr. Valerie Arkoosh attributed the increase to three primary factors:

- Medicaid enrollment growth: Pennsylvania's Medical Assistance program served an average of 3.47 million enrollees per month in FY 2023–2024, up from 3.31 million in FY 2022–2023, a 4.8 percent increase in caseload.
- Behavioral health services expansion: The Commonwealth allocated an additional $118.4 million to county mental health and substance use disorder programs, partially in response to a 14 percent increase in overdose-related emergency department visits recorded by the Department of Health between January and December 2023.
- Long-term care rate adjustments: Nursing facility reimbursement rates were increased by 6.2 percent effective January 1, 2024, adding approximately $204 million in annualized costs.

3.3 Department of Corrections — Continued Pressure from Personnel Costs

The Department of Corrections budget grew from $3.112 billion to $3.288 billion, an increase of $175.2 million or 5.6 percent. As of June 30, 2024, the Department operated 25 state correctional institutions housing 38,214 inmates, compared to 37,891 inmates on June 30, 2023. Personnel costs, which represent 71 percent of the Department's total budget, increased by $134.7 million due to a collective bargaining agreement ratified in March 2023 that provided correctional officers with a 4.5 percent wage increase effective July 1, 2023, and an additional 3.0 percent increase effective January 1, 2024.

3.4 Departments Recording Expenditure Decreases

Three departments recorded year-over-year spending reductions:

Department of Environmental Protection: Expenditures fell from $398.2 million to $381.9 million, a decrease of $16.3 million or 4.1 percent. The reduction reflects the completion of the Chesapeake Bay Phase III Watershed Implementation Plan grant cycle, which had provided $22.7 million in one-time federal matching funds in FY 2022–2023 that were not renewed in the subsequent year.

Department of Agriculture: Expenditures declined from $441.6 million to $427.3 million, a decrease of $14.3 million or 3.2 percent. The reduction is attributable to the conclusion of the Pennsylvania Agricultural Surplus System (PASS) emergency food purchasing program, which had operated with $18.5 million in supplemental COVID-19 recovery funding through June 30, 2023.

Department of Labor & Industry: Expenditures declined modestly from $1.205 billion to $1.189 billion, a decrease of $16.1 million or 1.3 percent. Unemployment compensation administrative costs fell as the volume of active claims processed by the Office of Unemployment Compensation dropped from an average of 74,200 weekly claims in FY 2022–2023 to 61,800 weekly claims in FY 2023–2024.

SECTION 4: FEDERAL FUNDS AND SPECIAL FUNDS OVERVIEW

In addition to general fund expenditures, Commonwealth departments expended $28.4 billion in federal funds and $9.7 billion in special funds during FY 2023–2024, bringing total all-funds expenditures to approximately $80.9 billion. The Department of Human Services accounted for $19.2 billion of total federal fund expenditures, primarily through the federal Medicaid matching program, which provided a Federal Medical Assistance Percentage (FMAP) of 55.63 percent for Pennsylvania in FY 2023–2024.

The Department of Transportation received $2.1 billion in federal highway and transit funds through the Federal Highway Administration and the Federal Transit Administration, representing 40.8 percent of the Department's total all-funds budget of $5.14 billion.

SECTION 5: APPROPRIATION LAPSE SUMMARY

Departments collectively lapsed $387.4 million in unspent general fund appropriations at the close of FY 2023–2024, compared to $412.1 million lapsed at the close of FY 2022–2023. The Department of Transportation recorded the largest lapse at $94.2 million, primarily due to project delays in the Interstate 95 reconstruction corridor in Philadelphia County. The Office of Administration recorded the smallest lapse among cabinet-level agencies at $1.3 million, representing a lapse rate of 0.47 percent of its total appropriation.

SECTION 6: OUTLOOK FOR FISCAL YEAR 2024–2025

Governor Josh Shapiro's FY 2024–2025 Executive Budget, submitted to the General Assembly on February 6, 2024, proposed total general fund expenditures of $48.3 billion, representing a proposed increase of 12.9 percent over FY 2023–2024 actual expenditures. The General Assembly enacted a final appropriations bill on July 11, 2024, at a total general fund level of $47.6 billion. The Office of the Budget projects that actual FY 2024–2025 expenditures will fall between $46.8 billion and $47.2 billion based on current caseload and revenue trends as of the date of this report.
