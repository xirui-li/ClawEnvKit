EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Department of Defense received a total appropriation of $886.3 billion for Fiscal Year 2024, representing a 3.2 percent increase over the FY2023 enacted level of $858.5 billion. This report provides a comprehensive breakdown of defense expenditures across major categories, compares current spending to historical benchmarks, and evaluates alignment with strategic priorities outlined in the 2022 National Defense Strategy authored under Secretary of Defense Lloyd Austin. Defense spending as a share of Gross Domestic Product stands at approximately 3.1 percent for FY2024, down from a post-9/11 peak of 4.7 percent recorded in FY2010.

SECTION 1: TOTAL DEFENSE APPROPRIATIONS — HISTORICAL CONTEXT

The United States has maintained the world's largest defense budget for more than six consecutive decades. The following table illustrates the trajectory of defense spending over the past ten fiscal years, adjusted to FY2024 constant dollars:

FY2015      | $795.4                    | 3.3%     | 1,301,350
FY2016      | $782.1                    | 3.2%     | 1,286,800
FY2017      | $804.2                    | 3.1%     | 1,281,900
FY2018      | $824.6                    | 3.1%     | 1,287,000
FY2019      | $838.9                    | 3.2%     | 1,281,900
FY2020      | $857.0                    | 3.4%     | 1,346,400
FY2021      | $852.6                    | 3.3%     | 1,388,100

FY2023      | $858.5                    | 3.1%     | 1,328,500
FY2024      | $886.3                    | 3.1%     | 1,336,000 (projected)

The $27.8 billion year-over-year increase from FY2023 to FY2024 represents the largest single-year nominal dollar increase since FY2020, driven primarily by accelerated procurement timelines and expanded readiness funding in response to evolving threats in the Indo-Pacific region.

SECTION 2: BREAKDOWN BY MAJOR APPROPRIATIONS CATEGORY

The FY2024 defense budget is distributed across the following primary categories:

OPERATIONS AND MAINTENANCE (O&M)
Total Allocation: $310.7 billion (35.1% of total budget)
O&M funding covers day-to-day operational costs including training exercises, base maintenance, fuel, and equipment sustainment. The Army received the largest O&M allocation at $98.4 billion, followed by the Navy and Marine Corps combined at $87.2 billion, the Air Force at $72.6 billion, and Defense-Wide accounts at $52.5 billion. Fuel costs alone account for $14.3 billion of O&M spending, reflecting an 8.7 percent increase over FY2023 due to sustained elevated energy prices.

MILITARY PERSONNEL
Total Allocation: $176.2 billion (19.9% of total budget)
This category funds salaries, housing allowances, healthcare, and retirement benefits for approximately 1.336 million active-duty service members and 800,000 civilian Department of Defense employees. The average annual compensation package for an active-duty service member, including all benefits, reached $119,400 in FY2024, up from $112,800 in FY2022. The Basic Allowance for Housing saw a 5.4 percent increase effective January 1, 2024, the largest single adjustment since 2006.

PROCUREMENT
Total Allocation: $167.4 billion (18.9% of total budget)
Procurement funding covers the purchase of new weapons systems, aircraft, ships, vehicles, and equipment. Key procurement items in FY2024 include:

- 48 F-35 Joint Strike Fighter aircraft across all variants: $7.9 billion
- 2 Virginia-class submarines: $8.4 billion
- 1 Gerald R. Ford-class aircraft carrier (CVN-82): $3.1 billion (advance procurement)
- 13 CH-47F Chinook Block II helicopters: $0.4 billion
- 96 Patriot Advanced Capability-3 (PAC-3) missiles: $1.2 billion
- 24 MQ-9 Reaper unmanned aerial vehicles: $0.6 billion

RESEARCH, DEVELOPMENT, TEST AND EVALUATION (RDT&E)
Total Allocation: $145.9 billion (16.5% of total budget)
RDT&E funding reached its highest level in inflation-adjusted terms since the Strategic Defense Initiative era of the 1980s. The Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency (DARPA) received $4.1 billion, a 12.3 percent increase over FY2023. Hypersonic weapons development programs received $6.9 billion across the services, reflecting the Pentagon's stated priority of closing the hypersonic capability gap with China and Russia. Artificial intelligence and autonomous systems research received $3.7 billion, up from $2.4 billion in FY2022.

MILITARY CONSTRUCTION AND FAMILY HOUSING
Total Allocation: $18.6 billion (2.1% of total budget)
This category funds construction of new facilities, renovation of existing infrastructure, and family housing programs. The Indo-Pacific Command received the largest regional allocation at $4.2 billion, including $1.8 billion for expanded basing infrastructure in Guam under the Guam Defense Posture Initiative.

OTHER DEFENSE PROGRAMS AND ACTIVITIES
Total Allocation: $67.5 billion (7.6% of total budget)
Includes the Defense Health Program ($41.2 billion), the Chemical Agents and Munitions Destruction program ($1.1 billion), and Inspector General operations ($0.4 billion).

SECTION 3: SERVICE-BY-SERVICE ALLOCATION

DEPARTMENT OF THE ARMY: $185.7 billion
DEPARTMENT OF THE NAVY (including Marine Corps): $230.8 billion
DEPARTMENT OF THE AIR FORCE (including Space Force): $231.6 billion
DEFENSE-WIDE ACCOUNTS: $238.2 billion

The Air Force and Space Force combined allocation surpassed the Navy for the first time in FY2024, driven by a $14.3 billion increase in space-based capabilities funding following the establishment of Space Force priorities under Chief of Space Operations General B. Chance Saltzman. The United States Space Force, established December 20, 2019, received $30.3 billion in FY2024, representing a 19.4 percent increase over its FY2023 allocation of $25.4 billion.

SECTION 4: OVERSEAS CONTINGENCY OPERATIONS AND SUPPLEMENTAL FUNDING

Congress approved $61.4 billion in supplemental security assistance funding in April 2024 under the National Security Supplemental Act, of which $26.4 billion was designated for direct military assistance to Ukraine, $14.1 billion for Israel security assistance, and $8.1 billion for Indo-Pacific deterrence initiatives. This supplemental is not included in the base $886.3 billion figure cited throughout this report.

Ukraine security assistance cumulative total from February 24, 2022 through January 31, 2024 reached $44.2 billion in direct military aid, including 31 M1A2 Abrams main battle tanks delivered in September 2023, more than 1,600 Stinger anti-aircraft missiles, and 8,500 Javelin anti-tank systems.

China's defense budget has grown at an average annual rate of 7.2 percent over the past decade, compared to the U.S. average of 2.1 percent over the same period. The Department of Defense's FY2024 China Military Power Report, released November 19, 2023, assessed that China's actual defense-related spending may be 1.5 to 2 times higher than officially reported figures.

SECTION 6: AUDIT STATUS AND FINANCIAL ACCOUNTABILITY

The Department of Defense has failed to pass a comprehensive financial audit for the sixth consecutive year since the first-ever departmental audit was conducted in FY2018. The FY2023 audit, completed November 15, 2023, identified $3.8 billion in accounting discrepancies and resulted in 28 material weaknesses, down from 35 material weaknesses identified in the FY2022 audit. Deputy Secretary of Defense Kathleen Hicks stated in testimony before the Senate Armed Services Committee on December 5, 2023, that the Department expects to achieve a clean audit opinion no earlier than FY2028.

SECTION 7: PROJECTED FIVE-YEAR DEFENSE SPENDING OUTLOOK

Under the President's FY2025 Budget Request submitted March 11, 2024, the Future Years Defense Program projects the following top-line defense budgets:

FY2025: $895.2 billion (proposed)
FY2026: $912.0 billion (projected)
FY2027: $928.4 billion (projected)
FY2028: $944.1 billion (projected)
FY2029: $961.7 billion (projected)

The five-year cumulative total of $4.641 trillion represents an average annual growth rate of 2.1 percent in nominal terms, which falls below the Congressional Budget Office's projected inflation rate of 2.6 percent annually, implying a modest real-terms decline in purchasing power over the period absent additional supplemental appropriations.

CONCLUSION

The FY2024 defense budget of $886.3 billion reflects a department navigating simultaneous demands: sustaining near-term readiness, recapitalizing aging equipment inventories, investing in next-generation technologies, and supporting allied partners. The 3.2 percent nominal increase over FY2023 maintains defense spending at 3.1 percent of GDP, consistent with the past three fiscal years. Ongoing audit failures, rising personnel costs, and the accelerating pace of Chinese military modernization represent the three most significant fiscal and strategic challenges identified in this analysis. Continued congressional oversight and rigorous program evaluation will be essential to ensuring that the defense dollar delivers maximum strategic value in an increasingly competitive global security environment.
