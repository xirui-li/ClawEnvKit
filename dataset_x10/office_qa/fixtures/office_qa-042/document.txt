EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents a comparative analysis of appropriated and expended budgets across major Commonwealth departments for fiscal years 2022–2023 and 2023–2024. The Commonwealth's total General Fund appropriation for FY 2023–2024 was $45.3 billion, representing an increase of $2.1 billion, or 4.9 percent, over the FY 2022–2023 appropriation of $43.2 billion. Actual expenditures for FY 2023–2024 totaled $44.8 billion, leaving an unspent balance of $502 million, or approximately 1.1 percent of total appropriations.

This document was prepared under the direction of Budget Secretary Uri Monson and reflects data compiled from the Commonwealth's Integrated Enterprise System (IES) as of September 30, 2024.

SECTION 1: METHODOLOGY AND DATA SOURCES

All figures in this report are drawn from final certified expenditure data submitted by department comptrollers no later than August 31, 2024, in accordance with Commonwealth accounting procedures outlined in the Fiscal Code, Act 84 of 2016. Appropriation figures reflect enacted budgets as signed by Governor Josh Shapiro on July 11, 2023, for FY 2023–2024, and as signed on July 8, 2022, for FY 2022–2023.

Figures are reported in thousands of dollars unless otherwise noted. Percentage changes are calculated on the basis of enacted appropriations. Departments with total appropriations below $50 million have been consolidated into the "Other Agencies" category for the purposes of this summary report. Supplemental appropriations approved after the initial budget enactment are included in the final appropriation totals.

SECTION 3: NOTABLE BUDGET CHANGES AND ANALYSIS

3.1 Department of Education — Largest Dollar Increase

The Department of Education received the largest single-department increase in FY 2023–2024, with appropriations rising by $886.3 million, or 6.2 percent. This increase was driven primarily by the Commonwealth's implementation of the adequacy funding formula following the landmark William Penn School District v. Pennsylvania Department of Education ruling issued by Commonwealth Court Judge Renée Cohn Jubelirer on February 7, 2023. Of the $886.3 million increase, approximately $617 million was directed to the Basic Education Funding line item, which reached a total of $8.04 billion in FY 2023–2024. The department's expenditure rate of 99.9 percent reflects strong absorption capacity and indicates that allocated funds were deployed effectively.

3.2 Department of Health — Highest Percentage Increase

The Department of Health recorded the highest percentage increase among major departments at 12.6 percent, adding $143.7 million over the prior fiscal year. This growth reflects expanded investment in behavioral health infrastructure, including $48.2 million in new funding for county mental health services and $31.6 million for the expansion of the Naloxone Distribution Program, which served 214 counties and municipalities during FY 2023–2024. The department's unexpended balance of $25.5 million, representing 1.98 percent of its appropriation, is attributable primarily to delayed hiring in the Bureau of Laboratories, where 47 positions remained vacant as of June 30, 2024.

3.3 Department of Human Services — Largest Overall Appropriation

The Department of Human Services retained the largest single-department appropriation at $16.0 billion, accounting for 35.3 percent of the total General Fund budget. The Medical Assistance program alone accounted for $10.4 billion of this total, reflecting continued enrollment growth in the Commonwealth's Medicaid program. As of June 30, 2024, 3.1 million Pennsylvanians were enrolled in Medical Assistance, a decrease of approximately 187,000 from the prior year's peak enrollment, as the federal continuous enrollment requirement under the COVID-19 public health emergency concluded. The department's expenditure rate of 99.8 percent indicates near-complete utilization of appropriated funds.

3.4 Department of Corrections — Personnel Cost Pressures

The Department of Corrections appropriation increased by $133.3 million, or 4.3 percent, to $3.24 billion. Personnel costs, including salaries and benefits for approximately 16,800 full-time employees, constitute 71.4 percent of the department's total expenditures. A new collective bargaining agreement ratified on March 14, 2024, with the Pennsylvania State Corrections Officers Association (PSCOA) resulted in a 3.5 percent salary increase effective April 1, 2024, contributing to higher-than-projected personnel expenditures in the fourth quarter of the fiscal year. The department's unexpended balance of $43.5 million was largely the result of 312 unfilled correctional officer positions as of June 30, 2024.

3.5 Pennsylvania State Police — Cadet Class Expansion

The Pennsylvania State Police appropriation grew by $76.5 million, or 5.1 percent, to $1.59 billion. This increase funded the graduation of Cadet Class 183 on March 22, 2024, which added 171 new troopers to the force, and supported the activation of a second cadet class within the same fiscal year. The agency's total authorized complement as of June 30, 2024, was 4,712 sworn officers.

SECTION 4: OVERALL FISCAL POSITION

The Commonwealth ended FY 2023–2024 with a General Fund unspent balance of $502 million, representing 1.1 percent of total appropriations. This compares favorably to the FY 2022–2023 unspent balance of $389 million, or 0.9 percent of appropriations. The increase in the unspent balance is attributable in part to conservative revenue projections that were exceeded by $318 million, as certified by the Independent Fiscal Office in its August 2024 final revenue report.

The Commonwealth's General Fund ending balance, inclusive of the unspent appropriations and prior-year carryforward, stood at $1.87 billion as of June 30, 2024. The Rainy Day Fund balance as of the same date was $5.1 billion, equivalent to approximately 41 days of General Fund expenditures.

SECTION 5: RECOMMENDATIONS

The Bureau of Financial Management and Analysis offers the following recommendations for consideration in the FY 2024–2025 budget cycle:

  • The Department of Health should submit a revised workforce plan by December 1, 2024, addressing the 47 Bureau of Laboratories vacancies that contributed to the $25.5 million unexpended balance.

  • The Department of Corrections should provide a quarterly report to the Office of the Budget on correctional officer recruitment outcomes, given the ongoing impact of staffing shortfalls on expenditure projections.

  • Departments with unexpended balances exceeding 1.5 percent of their appropriation should be required to submit explanatory memoranda to the Secretary of the Budget no later than 45 days after fiscal year close.
