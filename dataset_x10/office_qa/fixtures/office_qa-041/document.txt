EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Department of Defense (DoD) obligated $858.5 billion in total budgetary resources during Fiscal Year 2023, representing a 3.2 percent increase over the FY2022 enacted level of $831.7 billion. This figure encompasses base budget appropriations, Overseas Contingency Operations (OCO) funding, and emergency supplemental appropriations. Defense spending as a share of Gross Domestic Product stood at 3.1 percent in FY2023, consistent with the prior fiscal year and below the 40-year average of 4.7 percent. This report provides a comprehensive breakdown of expenditures by service branch, capability category, and program, with comparative analysis against prior fiscal years and NATO allied spending benchmarks.

SECTION 1: TOTAL APPROPRIATIONS AND BUDGET AUTHORITY

Congress enacted the Consolidated Appropriations Act, 2023 (Public Law 117-328) on December 29, 2022, providing the Department of Defense with a base budget of $797.7 billion. An additional $44.0 billion was subsequently authorized through the Ukraine Security Supplemental Appropriations Act, signed into law on December 29, 2022, as part of the same omnibus package. Emergency supplemental funding of $16.8 billion was approved in May 2023 to address replenishment of munitions stocks drawn down in support of Ukraine.

Operations and Maintenance (O&M) remains the single largest spending category, growing 4.7 percent from $285.2 billion in FY2022. The O&M increase reflects elevated operational tempo related to European deterrence initiatives, increased fuel costs averaging $3.84 per gallon for military aviation fuel compared to $3.21 in FY2022, and expanded cybersecurity operations.

The Air Force recorded the largest percentage increase among the military departments, driven primarily by accelerated procurement of F-35A aircraft and continued investment in the B-21 Raider long-range strike bomber program. The Army's increase reflects expanded European Deterrence Initiative (EDI) funding, which totaled $4.2 billion in FY2023, up from $3.7 billion in FY2022.

Defense-Wide accounts, managed by the Office of the Secretary of Defense and including agencies such as the Defense Intelligence Agency, Defense Logistics Agency, and Special Operations Command, declined by 1.8 percent due to the completion of several COVID-19 related medical support contracts and reduced spending by the Defense Health Agency following normalization of healthcare utilization rates.

The F-35 program, managed by the F-35 Joint Program Office under Program Executive Officer Lt. Gen. Michael Schmidt, delivered 98 aircraft across all variants in FY2023, falling short of the planned 141 deliveries due to ongoing disputes between the DoD and Lockheed Martin over acceptance of aircraft without the Technology Refresh 3 (TR-3) avionics upgrade. This shortfall resulted in approximately $1.4 billion in delayed obligation authority being carried forward into FY2024.

The Virginia-class submarine program, jointly managed by General Dynamics Electric Boat and Huntington Ingalls Industries, delivered two boats in FY2023: USS Oregon (SSN-793) and USS Montana (SSN-794). The program is under pressure to increase production rates to three boats per year by 2028 in support of AUKUS commitments to Australia.

SECTION 4: PERSONNEL COSTS AND END STRENGTH

Military personnel costs of $179.3 billion in FY2023 reflect a 4.6 percent pay raise effective January 1, 2023, the largest military pay increase in 20 years, authorized under the National Defense Authorization Act for Fiscal Year 2023 (Public Law 117-263). Active duty end strength across all services totaled 1,328,400 personnel, compared to an authorized level of 1,339,500, representing a shortfall of 11,100 personnel or approximately 0.8 percent below authorization.

Recruiting shortfalls were most acute in the Army, which ended FY2023 with 452,000 active duty soldiers against an authorized strength of 473,000, a deficit of 21,000 personnel. Army Secretary Christine Wormuth testified before the Senate Armed Services Committee on February 28, 2023, attributing the shortfall to declining propensity to serve among eligible youth, with only 9 percent of Americans aged 17 to 24 meeting all qualification standards for military service without a waiver.

Reserve component end strength totaled 798,500 across all components, compared to an authorized level of 822,900, a shortfall of 24,400 or approximately 3.0 percent.

Average annual compensation per active duty service member, including pay, allowances, and benefits, reached $135,400 in FY2023, compared to $127,200 in FY2022.

SECTION 5: RESEARCH AND DEVELOPMENT PRIORITIES

The $130.1 billion RDT&E budget represented a 7.8 percent increase over the FY2022 level of $120.7 billion, the largest single-year percentage increase in RDT&E spending since FY2002. Priority investment areas included:

- Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning: $1.8 billion across 685 distinct program elements, coordinated through the Chief Digital and Artificial Intelligence Office (CDAO) established in February 2022 under Director Craig Martell.

- Hypersonic Weapons Development: $4.7 billion across Army, Navy, and Air Force programs, including the Long-Range Hypersonic Weapon (LRHW), Conventional Prompt Strike (CPS), and Air-Launched Rapid Response Weapon (ARRW). The ARRW program was terminated in FY2023 following four failed flight tests.

- Space Systems: $13.4 billion managed primarily through the Space Force and the National Reconnaissance Office, including $2.9 billion for the Next Generation Overhead Persistent Infrared (Next Gen OPIR) satellite constellation.

- Directed Energy Weapons: $1.1 billion across 23 programs, including the High Energy Laser with Integrated Optical-dazzler and Surveillance (HELIOS) system deployed aboard USS Preble (DDG-88).

SECTION 6: INTERNATIONAL COMPARISONS AND NATO BURDEN SHARING

U.S. defense spending of $858.5 billion in FY2023 represented approximately 68.4 percent of total NATO alliance defense expenditures, estimated at $1.255 trillion for calendar year 2023 by NATO's annual defense expenditure report published in July 2023. The United States spent 3.49 percent of GDP on defense when measured using NATO's standardized methodology, which differs from the 3.1 percent figure using U.S. budgetary accounting.

Among NATO allies meeting the 2 percent of GDP spending target in 2023, eleven countries qualified: the United States, Greece (2.9%), Poland (3.9%), Estonia (2.7%), Latvia (2.3%), Lithuania (2.5%), Finland (2.5%), Hungary (2.1%), Romania (2.4%), Slovakia (2.1%), and the United Kingdom (2.1%). Germany spent 1.57 percent of GDP, below the 2 percent threshold despite commitments made following Russia's invasion of Ukraine in February 2022.

China's official defense budget for 2023 was announced at approximately $224.8 billion (1.55 trillion yuan), representing a 7.2 percent nominal increase over 2022. U.S. intelligence assessments, as cited in the DoD's annual China Military Power Report released in October 2023, estimate actual Chinese defense spending may be 1.4 to 1.9 times the officially reported figure.

SECTION 7: AUDIT STATUS AND FINANCIAL MANAGEMENT

The Department of Defense received its sixth consecutive disclaimer of opinion on its department-wide financial statements for FY2023, as issued by the DoD Inspector General on November 15, 2023. The disclaimer reflects the DoD's continued inability to fully account for its assets, liabilities, and transactions in accordance with generally accepted government auditing standards. However, the Army achieved a clean audit opinion on its General Fund financial statements for the first time in FY2023, representing a significant milestone.

The DoD identified $3.1 billion in improper payments in FY2023, a reduction from $3.8 billion in FY2022, representing 0.36 percent of total outlays. The Defense Finance and Accounting Service (DFAS), headquartered in Indianapolis, Indiana, processed approximately 7.4 million payment transactions per month during FY2023.

CONCLUSION

FY2023 defense spending of $858.5 billion reflects sustained investment in modernization, elevated operational requirements related to European security, and the largest military pay increase in two decades. Key challenges include persistent recruiting shortfalls, major acquisition program delays particularly in the F-35 program, and the ongoing inability to achieve a clean department-wide audit opinion. The President's FY2024 budget request, submitted to Congress on March 9, 2023, proposed $886.4 billion in total national defense spending, representing a 3.2 percent nominal increase and a slight real decrease when adjusted for the 3.4 percent inflation rate recorded for FY2023.
