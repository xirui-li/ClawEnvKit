FISCAL YEAR 2023 ANNUAL REPORT

YIELD SPREAD ANALYSIS: FEDERAL DEBT PORTFOLIO AND MARKET CONDITIONS

Prepared by the Office of Debt Management, Bureau of the Fiscal Service
Publication Date: November 14, 2023
Report Reference: ODM-2023-YSA-047

─────────────────────────────────────────────

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents a comprehensive yield spread analysis of U.S. Treasury securities across the maturity spectrum for fiscal year 2023 (October 1, 2022 through September 30, 2023). The analysis examines spreads between Treasury instruments of varying durations, compares domestic yields against international sovereign benchmarks, and evaluates the implications of current spread dynamics for federal borrowing costs and debt management strategy. Total federal debt subject to limit reached $31.4 trillion as of September 30, 2023, with net interest costs to the federal government totaling $659 billion for the fiscal year, representing a 39 percent increase over the $474 billion recorded in fiscal year 2022.

─────────────────────────────────────────────

SECTION 1: OVERVIEW OF TREASURY YIELD CURVE CONDITIONS

1.1 Yield Curve Inversion and Duration

The U.S. Treasury yield curve remained inverted throughout the majority of fiscal year 2023, a condition that began in October 2022 following the Federal Open Market Committee's aggressive rate tightening cycle. As of September 29, 2023 (the final business day of the fiscal year), the 2-year Treasury note yielded 5.03 percent while the 10-year Treasury note yielded 4.57 percent, producing a 2-year/10-year spread of negative 46 basis points. This inversion represents one of the most sustained inversions in the post-World War II era, having persisted for 14 consecutive months.

The 3-month Treasury bill yield stood at 5.45 percent on September 29, 2023, while the 30-year Treasury bond yielded 4.70 percent, creating a 3-month/30-year spread of negative 75 basis points.

SECTION 2: SPREAD ANALYSIS BY INSTRUMENT CATEGORY

2.1 On-the-Run vs. Off-the-Run Treasury Spreads

On-the-run Treasury securities, which represent the most recently issued instruments at each benchmark maturity, typically trade at a premium to off-the-run securities due to superior liquidity. During fiscal year 2023, the on-the-run/off-the-run spread for 10-year Treasuries averaged 4.2 basis points, compared to 3.1 basis points in fiscal year 2022. This widening reflects increased market volatility and reduced dealer balance sheet capacity following the March 2023 regional banking stress events involving Silicon Valley Bank and Signature Bank.

The widest on-the-run/off-the-run spread observed during the fiscal year occurred on March 15, 2023, when the spread reached 8.7 basis points for the 10-year benchmark, coinciding with peak uncertainty surrounding the banking sector.

2.2 TIPS vs. Nominal Treasury Spreads (Breakeven Inflation)

Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS) breakeven inflation rates, calculated as the difference between nominal Treasury yields and TIPS yields of equivalent maturity, provide a market-based measure of inflation expectations.

The 10-year breakeven rate of 2.37 percent as of September 29, 2023 compares to 2.61 percent at the start of the fiscal year on October 3, 2022, indicating that long-term inflation expectations moderated by 24 basis points over the course of the year, consistent with the Federal Reserve's progress in reducing headline CPI from 8.2 percent in September 2022 to 3.7 percent in September 2023.

2.3 Agency Spread Analysis

The spread between Fannie Mae (FNMA) 10-year benchmark debt and comparable Treasury securities averaged 62 basis points during fiscal year 2023, compared to 54 basis points in fiscal year 2022. Freddie Mac (FHLMC) 10-year spreads averaged 64 basis points over the same period. These wider agency spreads reflect increased investor demand for yield premium amid elevated rate uncertainty.

The JPMorgan Emerging Market Bond Index (EMBI) Global Diversified spread over U.S. Treasuries averaged 398 basis points during fiscal year 2023, compared to 441 basis points in fiscal year 2022. The compression reflects improved risk appetite in the second half of the fiscal year as U.S. inflation data improved.

SECTION 4: IMPLICATIONS FOR FEDERAL BORROWING COSTS

4.1 Weighted Average Interest Rate on Federal Debt

The weighted average interest rate on total federal debt outstanding rose to 2.97 percent as of September 30, 2023, up from 2.07 percent on September 30, 2022. This 90-basis-point increase reflects the rollover of maturing low-rate debt issued during 2020 and 2021 into higher-rate instruments.

The Treasury Department issued $22.7 trillion in gross marketable debt during fiscal year 2023, including rollovers of maturing securities. Net new borrowing totaled $2.02 trillion. Bills accounted for 73 percent of gross issuance, reflecting the Treasury's reliance on short-term financing to meet cash management needs during the debt ceiling impasse that was resolved by the Fiscal Responsibility Act of 2023, signed by President Biden on June 3, 2023.

4.2 Interest Cost Projections

Based on current yield curve conditions and the Congressional Budget Office's September 2023 baseline, net interest costs are projected to reach $745 billion in fiscal year 2024, representing 2.7 percent of projected GDP. This would mark the first time since 1996 that net interest costs exceeded 2.5 percent of GDP.

The Office of Debt Management estimates that each 100-basis-point increase in Treasury yields across the curve adds approximately $130 billion to annual interest costs within a 12-month period, rising to approximately $280 billion on a fully rolled-over basis over a 10-year horizon.

─────────────────────────────────────────────

SECTION 5: DEBT MANAGEMENT STRATEGY CONSIDERATIONS

5.1 Maturity Profile and Rollover Risk

As of September 30, 2023, approximately 31 percent of marketable Treasury debt matures within one year, totaling $9.7 trillion. This concentration in short-term maturities creates significant rollover risk should market conditions deteriorate. The average maturity of the total marketable debt portfolio stood at 6 years and 2 months, down from 6 years and 4 months at the end of fiscal year 2022.

The Borrowing Advisory Committee, which met most recently on October 31, 2023, recommended that the Treasury consider gradually extending the average maturity of new issuance to reduce rollover exposure, a recommendation consistent with the department's stated preference for regular and predictable issuance.

5.2 TIPS Issuance Strategy

TIPS outstanding totaled $1.97 trillion as of September 30, 2023, representing 8.4 percent of total marketable debt. The Treasury issued $220 billion in TIPS during fiscal year 2023 across the 5-year, 10-year, and 30-year maturities. Given that breakeven inflation rates remain above the Federal Reserve's 2.0 percent long-run target, TIPS continue to represent a cost-effective hedging instrument for the federal government against unexpected inflation.

─────────────────────────────────────────────

SECTION 6: CONCLUSIONS AND OUTLOOK

The yield spread environment in fiscal year 2023 was characterized by persistent curve inversion, elevated short-term rates driven by Federal Reserve policy, and moderating long-term inflation expectations. Net interest costs of $659 billion represent the highest nominal level in U.S. history and underscore the fiscal sensitivity of the federal government to sustained high interest rates.

The Office of Debt Management will continue to monitor spread dynamics across the maturity spectrum and will adjust issuance strategy as warranted to minimize borrowing costs consistent with prudent risk management. The next quarterly refunding announcement is scheduled for November 1, 2023.
