EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents a comprehensive Compound Annual Growth Rate (CAGR) analysis of the Federal Highway Trust Fund (HTF), covering the ten-year period from fiscal year 2014 through fiscal year 2024. The analysis examines revenue streams, expenditure patterns, and fund balance trajectories to inform congressional budget deliberations and support the reauthorization discussions anticipated for fiscal year 2026. The Highway Trust Fund, established under the Federal Aid Highway Act of 1956, remains the primary financing mechanism for surface transportation infrastructure in the United States, drawing revenues principally from federal motor fuel excise taxes and heavy vehicle use taxes.

Over the ten-year analytical window, the Highway Trust Fund demonstrated a CAGR of 2.31 percent in total receipts, rising from $39.4 billion in fiscal year 2014 to $49.7 billion in fiscal year 2024. However, this growth rate has consistently lagged behind both inflation and the rate of infrastructure cost escalation, creating a structural imbalance that has required repeated congressional intervention through general fund transfers.

---

SECTION 1: BACKGROUND AND STATUTORY FRAMEWORK

The Federal Highway Trust Fund operates under authority granted by 26 U.S.C. § 9503 and is administered jointly by the Department of Transportation and the Department of the Treasury. The fund comprises two accounts: the Highway Account and the Mass Transit Account. As of September 30, 2024, the Highway Account held a balance of $31.2 billion, while the Mass Transit Account held $11.8 billion, for a combined trust fund balance of $43.0 billion.

Federal motor fuel taxes, which have remained unchanged at 18.3 cents per gallon for gasoline and 24.3 cents per gallon for diesel since their last adjustment in October 1993, constitute approximately 87.2 percent of total HTF receipts. The heavy vehicle use tax, assessed annually on trucks with gross vehicle weights exceeding 55,000 pounds, contributed $1.6 billion in fiscal year 2024, representing 3.2 percent of total receipts. The remaining 9.6 percent derives from tire taxes, truck and trailer sales taxes, and interest earnings on invested balances.

---

SECTION 2: CAGR METHODOLOGY

The Compound Annual Growth Rate was calculated using the standard formula:

CAGR = (Ending Value / Beginning Value) ^ (1 / Number of Years) - 1

For the primary ten-year analysis, the beginning value is the fiscal year 2014 total receipt figure of $39.4 billion and the ending value is the fiscal year 2024 total receipt figure of $49.7 billion, yielding a ten-year CAGR of 2.31 percent. Sub-period analyses were conducted for the five-year windows of 2014-2019 and 2019-2024 to identify inflection points attributable to the COVID-19 pandemic and the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act of 2021.

All figures are presented in nominal dollars unless otherwise specified. Inflation-adjusted comparisons utilize the Bureau of Labor Statistics Consumer Price Index for All Urban Consumers (CPI-U), with fiscal year 2014 as the base period. The GDP deflator series published by the Bureau of Economic Analysis was employed as a secondary inflation benchmark for capital expenditure comparisons.

The fiscal year 2020 decline of 15.02 percent represents the most significant single-year contraction in the fund's history, attributable to the dramatic reduction in vehicle miles traveled during the COVID-19 pandemic. The Federal Highway Administration reported that vehicle miles traveled fell by 13.2 percent nationally in calendar year 2020, directly suppressing fuel tax receipts. Recovery was robust in fiscal years 2021 and 2022, with the latter recording an 11.44 percent increase driven by post-pandemic travel normalization and elevated commercial freight activity.

The five-year CAGR differential between the 2014-2019 period (2.51 percent) and the 2019-2024 period (2.18 percent) reflects the lasting structural impact of the pandemic on travel patterns, including the sustained increase in remote work arrangements that has reduced commuter vehicle miles traveled by an estimated 6.4 percent compared to pre-pandemic baselines, according to Federal Highway Administration traffic monitoring data published in August 2024.

SECTION 4: EXPENDITURE ANALYSIS

Total obligations from the Highway Trust Fund reached $68.3 billion in fiscal year 2024, substantially exceeding receipts of $49.7 billion. This gap of $18.6 billion was bridged through a combination of beginning-of-year fund balances and a $6.4 billion general fund transfer authorized under the Surface Transportation Reauthorization provisions of the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (Public Law 117-58, signed November 15, 2021).

The expenditure CAGR of 2.97 percent exceeded the receipt CAGR of 2.31 percent by 66 basis points over the ten-year period, confirming the structural imbalance that has characterized the fund since the last federal fuel tax increase in 1993. In inflation-adjusted terms, the purchasing power of the 18.3 cents-per-gallon gasoline tax has declined to an equivalent of approximately 10.1 cents in 1993 dollars, representing a real-terms erosion of 44.8 percent over thirty-one years.

SECTION 5: GENERAL FUND TRANSFERS AND SUPPLEMENTAL FINANCING

Congress has authorized cumulative general fund transfers to the Highway Trust Fund totaling $275.1 billion since fiscal year 2008, when the first emergency transfer of $8.0 billion was enacted. The most recent transfer, $6.4 billion in fiscal year 2024, was the fourteenth such intervention in sixteen years. These transfers are not reflected in the CAGR calculations for dedicated receipts, as they represent discretionary appropriations rather than trust fund revenue.

Key transfer milestones include:
- August 2008: $8.0 billion emergency transfer (first general fund infusion in HTF history)
- September 2014: $10.8 billion transfer under the Highway and Transportation Funding Act of 2014
- December 2015: $70.0 billion authorized over five years under the Fixing America's Surface Transportation (FAST) Act
- November 2021: $118.0 billion in supplemental HTF funding over five years under the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act

SECTION 6: PROJECTED CAGR THROUGH FISCAL YEAR 2030

Based on the Congressional Budget Office's August 2024 baseline projections and Federal Highway Administration vehicle miles traveled forecasts, the Office of Financial Analysis projects the following receipt scenarios through fiscal year 2030:

Baseline Scenario (2.1% annual growth): Total receipts reach $56.2 billion by FY 2030, yielding a six-year CAGR of 2.07 percent from the FY 2024 base.

Optimistic Scenario (3.4% annual growth, reflecting electric vehicle transition mitigation through new user fees): Total receipts reach $60.8 billion by FY 2030, yielding a six-year CAGR of 3.41 percent.

Pessimistic Scenario (0.8% annual growth, reflecting accelerated electric vehicle adoption without compensating revenue mechanisms): Total receipts reach $52.1 billion by FY 2030, yielding a six-year CAGR of 0.79 percent.

The electric vehicle penetration rate presents the most significant long-term risk to HTF revenue adequacy. The Energy Information Administration projects that battery electric vehicles will constitute 18.7 percent of new vehicle sales by 2030, compared to 7.6 percent in calendar year 2023. Since electric vehicles pay no federal fuel excise tax, each percentage point increase in EV market share is estimated to reduce annual HTF gasoline tax receipts by approximately $380 million at current vehicle miles traveled levels.

---

SECTION 7: CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS

The ten-year CAGR of 2.31 percent for Highway Trust Fund receipts is insufficient to sustain current surface transportation investment levels without continued reliance on general fund transfers. The Division of Trust Fund Accountability offers the following findings for congressional consideration:

1. The structural revenue gap between the 2.31 percent receipt CAGR and the 2.97 percent expenditure CAGR will widen absent legislative action, with the fund projected to reach a zero balance in the Highway Account by fiscal year 2028 under the baseline scenario if general fund transfers are not continued.

2. Indexing the federal motor fuel tax to inflation at the 1993 rate would have produced a gasoline tax of approximately 33.1 cents per gallon in fiscal year 2024, generating an estimated $22.4 billion in additional annual revenue.

3. A vehicle miles traveled fee pilot program, authorized at $125 million under the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act, is currently being administered by the Department of Transportation and is scheduled to report preliminary findings to Congress by March 31, 2025.
