EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Department of Defense received a total appropriation of $886.3 billion for Fiscal Year 2024, representing a 3.2 percent increase over the FY2023 enacted level of $858.5 billion. This report provides a comprehensive breakdown of defense expenditures across all major spending categories, compares current outlays against prior fiscal years, and identifies key programmatic trends affecting long-term budget projections. The analysis draws on data submitted by the Office of the Secretary of Defense, the Joint Chiefs of Staff, and the four military departments through December 31, 2024.

SECTION 1: TOTAL APPROPRIATIONS AND OUTLAYS

The FY2024 National Defense Authorization Act, signed into law on December 22, 2023, authorized $886.3 billion in total defense spending. Of this amount, $849.8 billion was designated for the Department of Defense base budget, while $36.5 billion was allocated to defense-related activities within the Department of Energy, primarily nuclear weapons programs managed by the National Nuclear Security Administration (NNSA).

Actual outlays through the end of FY2024 (September 30, 2024) reached $874.2 billion, reflecting an execution rate of 98.6 percent against total appropriations. The remaining $12.1 billion in unobligated balances has been carried forward into FY2025 under standard multi-year appropriations authority.

Year-over-Year Comparison (Base DOD Budget):

  FY2022 Enacted:    $782.0 billion
  FY2023 Enacted:    $858.5 billion   (+9.8% over FY2022)
  FY2024 Enacted:    $886.3 billion   (+3.2% over FY2023)
  FY2025 Request:    $919.4 billion   (+3.7% projected increase)

Defense spending as a share of Gross Domestic Product stood at 3.1 percent in FY2024, unchanged from FY2023, and down from 3.5 percent recorded in FY2021.

SECTION 3: SPENDING BY MAJOR CATEGORY

3.1 Military Personnel (MILPERS)

Total military personnel costs in FY2024 reached $182.7 billion, accounting for 20.6 percent of total defense outlays. This category covers pay, allowances, retirement accrual, and healthcare for approximately 1.33 million active-duty service members. The FY2024 military pay raise of 5.2 percent, the largest in more than two decades, added an estimated $4.8 billion to personnel costs compared to FY2023 projections. The Defense Health Program, which falls under MILPERS-related accounts, consumed $57.4 billion, a 6.1 percent increase over FY2023.

3.2 Operations and Maintenance (O&M)

Operations and Maintenance represented the single largest spending category at $314.6 billion, or 35.5 percent of total outlays. Key O&M expenditures included:

  - Depot maintenance and equipment sustainment: $68.3 billion
  - Training and recruiting activities: $29.7 billion
  - Base operations support: $41.2 billion
  - Intelligence and communications: $52.8 billion
  - Overseas contingency-related activities: $18.4 billion

The O&M category grew by 4.1 percent over FY2023, outpacing overall defense budget growth, largely due to increased fuel costs and expanded cyber operations requirements.

3.3 Procurement

Total procurement spending reached $168.4 billion in FY2024, representing 19.0 percent of total outlays. Major procurement programs included:

  - F-35 Lightning II Joint Strike Fighter: $15.6 billion (83 aircraft delivered)
  - Columbia-class ballistic missile submarine: $6.2 billion
  - Virginia-class attack submarine: $7.8 billion (2 boats funded)
  - CH-47F Chinook helicopter upgrades: $1.4 billion
  - Guided Multiple Launch Rocket System (GMLRS) munitions: $2.1 billion
  - Shipbuilding and conversion, Navy (total): $32.7 billion

Procurement spending declined by 1.8 percent from FY2023, reflecting delays in several major programs including the Next Generation Air Dominance (NGAD) fighter and the Army's XM30 Mechanized Infantry Combat Vehicle.

3.4 Research, Development, Test and Evaluation (RDT&E)

RDT&E funding totaled $145.7 billion, or 16.4 percent of total outlays, making it the third-largest spending category. This represents a 7.3 percent increase over FY2023 and reflects the administration's emphasis on emerging technologies. Priority investment areas included:

  - Hypersonic weapons development: $11.2 billion
  - Artificial intelligence and autonomous systems: $8.9 billion
  - Space-based capabilities: $14.3 billion
  - Directed energy weapons: $3.7 billion
  - Microelectronics and semiconductor research: $2.4 billion

The Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency (DARPA) received $4.1 billion in FY2024, a 9.6 percent increase from its FY2023 allocation of $3.74 billion.

3.5 Military Construction and Family Housing

Military construction appropriations totaled $18.3 billion, with $14.9 billion for new construction and $3.4 billion for family housing programs. Notable projects included $2.2 billion for infrastructure improvements at Indo-Pacific Command installations, including expanded facilities at Andersen Air Force Base in Guam and Marine Corps Base Camp Blaz.

SECTION 4: UKRAINE SECURITY ASSISTANCE

Through September 30, 2024, the United States had committed $61.4 billion in total security assistance to Ukraine since February 2022, of which $44.8 billion represented direct military equipment and training support. In FY2024 alone, Ukraine-related security assistance drawdowns and Presidential Drawdown Authority (PDA) packages totaled $13.7 billion. The Ukraine Security Supplemental Appropriations Act, enacted April 24, 2024, provided an additional $61.0 billion in emergency funding, of which $13.8 billion was designated for replenishment of DOD stockpiles.

SECTION 5: DEFENSE AGENCY AND FIELD ACTIVITY EXPENDITURES

The Defense Logistics Agency (DLA) managed $47.3 billion in supply chain transactions during FY2024, supporting all military departments. The Defense Contract Audit Agency (DCAA) conducted audits covering $243.8 billion in contractor costs, identifying $5.6 billion in questioned costs, a recovery rate of approximately 2.3 percent. The Missile Defense Agency received $10.2 billion in FY2024, a 5.2 percent increase over FY2023, supporting continued development of the Next Generation Interceptor program.

SECTION 6: LONG-TERM PROJECTIONS AND FISCAL RISK

The Congressional Budget Office projects that under current policy, defense spending will reach $1.02 trillion annually by FY2030, driven by personnel cost growth averaging 3.8 percent per year, nuclear modernization costs peaking between FY2027 and FY2032, and continued investment in space and cyber capabilities. The DOD's own Future Years Defense Program (FYDP) submitted with the FY2025 budget request projects cumulative defense spending of $4.87 trillion over the FY2025-FY2029 period.

The Government Accountability Office identified 28 major defense acquisition programs as experiencing cost growth or schedule delays as of June 2024, with aggregate program of record costs increasing by $43.2 billion compared to original baseline estimates. The F-35 program alone carries a total program cost estimate of $412.0 billion through 2044, making it the most expensive weapons system in United States history.

SECTION 7: KEY FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS

1. The 5.2 percent military pay raise, while necessary for retention, creates structural cost pressure that will require offsetting efficiencies in O&M and procurement accounts in FY2025 and beyond.

2. Procurement execution delays in NGAD and XM30 programs resulted in $3.1 billion in appropriated funds not being obligated within the intended fiscal year, requiring reprogramming actions.

3. RDT&E growth of 7.3 percent reflects appropriate prioritization of emerging technology competition with peer adversaries, but transition of laboratory programs to fielded capabilities remains a persistent challenge.

4. The Defense Health Program's 6.1 percent cost growth continues to exceed overall defense budget growth and warrants a comprehensive review of TRICARE contract structures ahead of the FY2026 budget cycle.
