EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This technical supplement presents the results of statistical distribution fitting analyses conducted on federal revenue collections and discretionary expenditure data for Fiscal Year 2023 (October 1, 2022 through September 30, 2023). The Office of Fiscal Analysis and Risk Management (OFARM), under the direction of Deputy Assistant Secretary for Quantitative Analysis Dr. Margaret Holloway, undertook this analysis to improve probabilistic forecasting models used in the President's Budget submissions and mid-session reviews.

Total federal revenues for FY2023 reached $4.439 trillion, representing a 9.3 percent decline from the FY2022 record of $4.896 trillion, primarily attributable to reduced capital gains realizations and the expiration of pandemic-era tax provisions. Total outlays stood at $6.134 trillion, producing a unified budget deficit of $1.695 trillion. This report characterizes the distributional properties of monthly revenue and expenditure streams to support Value-at-Risk (VaR) calculations and scenario planning exercises.

SECTION 1: DATA DESCRIPTION AND PREPROCESSING

1.1 Data Sources

The primary dataset consists of 144 monthly observations of federal revenue and expenditure flows drawn from the Monthly Treasury Statement (MTS) series, spanning January 2012 through December 2023. Data were obtained from the Bureau of the Fiscal Service's USASpending.gov database and cross-validated against the Financial Management Service records maintained by the Federal Reserve Bank of New York's Government Accounts Division.

Revenue categories analyzed include:
- Individual income taxes (mean monthly collection: $178.4 billion)
- Corporate income taxes (mean monthly collection: $31.2 billion)
- Payroll taxes (mean monthly collection: $107.6 billion)
- Excise taxes (mean monthly collection: $8.9 billion)
- Estate and gift taxes (mean monthly collection: $2.1 billion)
- Customs duties (mean monthly collection: $6.7 billion)

1.2 Preprocessing Steps

Analyst Jennifer Castillo of the Quantitative Methods Division applied the following preprocessing steps prior to distribution fitting:

First, seasonal adjustment was performed using the X-13ARIMA-SEATS procedure developed by the U.S. Census Bureau, with additive decomposition selected based on a coefficient of variation below 0.15 for all major revenue categories. Second, outlier detection using the Grubbs test at a significance level of 0.01 identified three anomalous observations: April 2020 (individual income tax collections of $241.7 billion, distorted by the tax filing deadline extension to July 15, 2020), April 2021 ($563.8 billion, reflecting pandemic relief interactions), and September 2022 ($497.3 billion, reflecting Inflation Reduction Act implementation effects). These observations were Winsorized at the 99th percentile rather than excluded, preserving sample size while limiting leverage effects.

Third, all series were deflated to constant FY2023 dollars using the GDP Implicit Price Deflator published by the Bureau of Economic Analysis, with a base quarter of Q3 2023.

SECTION 2: CANDIDATE DISTRIBUTIONS AND FITTING METHODOLOGY

2.1 Candidate Distributions

Based on prior literature and the known characteristics of government revenue streams (positive support, right skewness, and potential heavy tails during fiscal stress periods), the following seven candidate distributions were evaluated for each revenue and expenditure category:

1. Normal (Gaussian)
2. Lognormal
3. Gamma
4. Weibull
5. Generalized Extreme Value (GEV)
6. Beta (applied to revenue-to-GDP ratios)
7. Student's t (with estimated degrees of freedom)

2.2 Estimation Method

Parameters were estimated using Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE) implemented in R version 4.3.1 using the fitdistrplus package (version 1.1-11), authored by Delignette-Muller and Dutang. For the GEV distribution, L-moment estimation was used as a supplementary check, following the methodology described in Hosking and Wallis (1997). All computations were performed on OFARM's secure analytical server cluster, designated FISCAL-COMPUTE-04, running Red Hat Enterprise Linux 9.2.

2.3 Goodness-of-Fit Testing

Three complementary goodness-of-fit statistics were computed for each candidate distribution:

- Kolmogorov-Smirnov (KS) test statistic and associated p-value
- Anderson-Darling (AD) test statistic (weighted toward tail fit)
- Akaike Information Criterion (AIC) for model comparison

The Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC) was computed as a secondary selection criterion. Final distribution selection required a KS test p-value exceeding 0.05 and the lowest AIC among non-rejected candidates.

SECTION 3: RESULTS BY REVENUE CATEGORY

3.1 Individual Income Tax Collections

Individual income tax monthly collections (seasonally adjusted, constant FY2023 dollars) exhibited strong right skewness with a sample skewness coefficient of 1.847 and excess kurtosis of 4.312, indicating heavier tails than the normal distribution.

Selected Distribution: Generalized Extreme Value (GEV)
Rationale: Lowest AIC (1,824.3), KS p-value of 0.3892, and superior Anderson-Darling statistic of 0.847 versus 1.203 for the Gamma. The positive shape parameter ξ = 0.214 confirms heavy right-tail behavior consistent with April filing season spikes.

3.2 Corporate Income Tax Collections

Corporate income tax collections displayed the highest volatility of any revenue category, with a coefficient of variation of 0.683 and a sample standard deviation of $21.3 billion against a mean of $31.2 billion.

Selected Distribution: Lognormal
Parameters: μ_log = 3.287, σ_log = 0.612
KS p-value: 0.1876
AIC: 1,203.4

The lognormal fit reflects the multiplicative nature of corporate profit generation and the asymmetric impact of tax loss carryforward provisions, which suppress collections in recession years while allowing rapid recovery in expansion periods.

3.3 Payroll Tax Collections

Payroll taxes, the most stable revenue source, were best described by a Normal distribution after seasonal adjustment, consistent with their direct linkage to employment levels.

Selected Distribution: Normal
Parameters: μ = $107.6 billion, σ = $9.4 billion
KS p-value: 0.4712
AIC: 1,644.7

The 95th percentile of this fitted distribution corresponds to monthly collections of $123.1 billion, while the 5th percentile corresponds to $92.1 billion, providing a $31.0 billion confidence band for budget planning purposes.

3.4 Discretionary Expenditure Distributions

Defense discretionary outlays were best fit by a Gamma distribution with shape parameter α = 18.3 and rate parameter β = 0.317, yielding a mean of $57.7 billion per month and a standard deviation of $13.5 billion. Non-defense discretionary outlays were best fit by a Lognormal distribution with parameters μ_log = 4.021 and σ_log = 0.389.

SECTION 4: AGGREGATE DEFICIT DISTRIBUTION AND VALUE-AT-RISK

4.1 Monte Carlo Simulation

Using the fitted marginal distributions and a Gaussian copula with correlation structure estimated from the 144-month historical record, OFARM conducted 500,000 Monte Carlo simulations of annual deficit outcomes. The correlation between individual income tax and corporate income tax monthly collections was estimated at 0.412, reflecting common sensitivity to economic growth. The correlation between total revenues and total outlays was estimated at negative 0.187, consistent with automatic stabilizer dynamics.

4.2 Simulated Deficit Distribution Results

The simulation produced the following percentile estimates for the FY2024 annual unified budget deficit:

- 5th percentile (favorable scenario): $1.312 trillion deficit
- 25th percentile: $1.587 trillion deficit
- 50th percentile (median): $1.748 trillion deficit
- 75th percentile: $1.934 trillion deficit
- 95th percentile (adverse scenario): $2.287 trillion deficit

The mean simulated deficit of $1.763 trillion compares to the Office of Management and Budget's official FY2024 baseline projection of $1.801 trillion published in the February 2024 Mid-Session Review, a difference of $38 billion or 2.1 percent, within acceptable model tolerance.

4.3 Value-at-Risk Estimates

At a 95 percent confidence level, the annual deficit Value-at-Risk (the amount by which the deficit could exceed the median in an adverse year) is estimated at $539 billion. At a 99 percent confidence level, the VaR rises to $741 billion. These figures inform the Treasury's contingency borrowing authority calculations submitted to the Congressional Budget Office on February 28, 2024.

SECTION 5: LIMITATIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS

5.1 Limitations

The 144-month sample period encompasses two recessions (2008-2009 and 2020) and one period of historically elevated inflation (2021-2023), which may cause the fitted distributions to overweight tail events relative to a longer historical baseline. Additionally, the Gaussian copula assumption may underestimate joint tail dependence during fiscal crises, a known limitation documented by Embrechts, McNeil, and Straumann (2002).

The analysis does not incorporate legislative risk — the probability that tax law changes will alter the structural parameters of revenue distributions. The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017 produced a measurable structural break in corporate income tax collections beginning in January 2018, reducing mean monthly corporate receipts by an estimated $8.3 billion relative to the pre-reform trend.

5.2 Recommendations

OFARM recommends the following actions for FY2024 modeling cycles:

1. Expand the candidate distribution set to include the Generalized Pareto Distribution for tail-specific modeling of extreme revenue shortfalls.
2. Implement a regime-switching framework with two states (expansion and contraction) to better capture the bimodal characteristics observed in corporate tax collections.
3. Conduct quarterly re-estimation of distribution parameters rather than the current annual cycle, with the next scheduled re-estimation on June 30, 2024.
4. Engage the Congressional Budget Office's Budget Analysis Division to align distributional assumptions with those used in the Long-Term Budget Outlook.
