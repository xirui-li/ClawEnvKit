EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents the methodology, calculations, and findings related to changes in U.S. Treasury bond yields during the third quarter of fiscal year 2024. The analysis covers yield movements across the 2-year, 5-year, 10-year, and 30-year benchmark maturities, examines the drivers of those movements, and quantifies the budgetary implications for federal debt service costs. During the quarter ending June 30, 2024, the 10-year Treasury yield rose from 4.20 percent to 4.36 percent, representing an increase of 16 basis points. This movement, combined with shifts across the yield curve, contributed to an estimated $3.8 billion increase in projected annual interest expense on newly issued and refinanced debt.

---

SECTION 1: BACKGROUND AND PURPOSE

The U.S. Treasury regularly monitors changes in bond yields as part of its debt management responsibilities. Yield changes directly affect the cost of financing the federal debt, which stood at $34.6 trillion as of June 30, 2024. The federal government issued approximately $2.1 trillion in new marketable securities during the third quarter of fiscal year 2024, making yield fluctuations a matter of significant fiscal consequence.

This report fulfills the Treasury's obligation under the Government Performance and Results Modernization Act to provide transparent accounting of debt management activities and their cost implications. The calculations herein follow the methodology established in Treasury Circular 1500-002, last revised on March 15, 2022.

---

SECTION 2: YIELD CHANGE CALCULATION METHODOLOGY

2.1 Basis Point Definition and Conversion

All yield changes in this report are expressed in basis points (bps), where one basis point equals 0.01 percentage point. Yield changes are calculated as the arithmetic difference between the yield at the end of the reporting period and the yield at the beginning of the reporting period:

Yield Change (bps) = [Yield(end) – Yield(start)] × 100

For example, a movement from 4.20 percent to 4.36 percent is calculated as:
(4.36 – 4.20) × 100 = 16 basis points

2.2 Benchmark Yield Sources

All yields referenced in this report are drawn from the H.15 Selected Interest Rates statistical release published daily by the Federal Reserve Board of Governors. The closing yields on March 28, 2024 (the last business day of Q2 FY2024) and June 28, 2024 (the last business day of Q3 FY2024) serve as the start and end points for quarterly calculations.

2.3 Dollar Cost Calculation

The incremental annual interest cost attributable to a yield change is calculated as follows:

Incremental Annual Cost = Principal Issued × (Yield Change in decimal form)

For a $2.1 trillion issuance volume and a weighted average yield increase of 0.18 percent (18 basis points) across all maturities:

Incremental Annual Cost = $2,100,000,000,000 × 0.0018 = $3,780,000,000

This figure is rounded to $3.8 billion in the executive summary.

SECTION 4: DRIVERS OF YIELD CHANGES

4.1 Federal Reserve Policy Signals

The Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC) maintained the federal funds target rate at 5.25 to 5.50 percent throughout the entire third quarter of fiscal year 2024, a level that had been in place since July 26, 2023. At its May 1, 2024 meeting, the FOMC voted unanimously to hold rates steady. Chair Jerome Powell's post-meeting press conference indicated that the Committee did not expect it would be appropriate to reduce the target range until it had gained greater confidence that inflation was moving sustainably toward 2 percent. This statement was interpreted by markets as pushing back the expected timing of rate cuts, contributing to upward pressure on yields across the curve.

4.2 Inflation Data

The Consumer Price Index (CPI) for All Urban Consumers rose 3.5 percent year-over-year in March 2024, 3.4 percent in April 2024, and 3.3 percent in May 2024. Core CPI, which excludes food and energy, registered 3.8 percent, 3.6 percent, and 3.4 percent for the same months, respectively. The persistence of inflation above target reinforced market expectations that the Federal Reserve would maintain restrictive monetary policy for longer than previously anticipated.

4.3 Treasury Issuance Volume

The Treasury's Quarterly Refunding Announcement issued on April 29, 2024 confirmed auction sizes that were broadly in line with the February 2024 announcement. The Treasury auctioned $58 billion in 3-year notes, $42 billion in 10-year notes, and $25 billion in 30-year bonds during the May 2024 refunding cycle. The 10-year note auction on May 8, 2024 produced a high yield of 4.483 percent with a bid-to-cover ratio of 2.49, which was below the prior six-auction average of 2.54, suggesting modestly weaker demand.

4.4 International Demand Factors

Foreign official holdings of U.S. Treasury securities, as reported in the Treasury International Capital (TIC) data for April 2024, totaled $3.31 trillion, a decrease of $18.4 billion from March 2024. Japan remained the largest foreign holder with $1.128 trillion, followed by China with $767.4 billion. Reduced foreign official demand contributed marginally to upward yield pressure during the quarter.

---

SECTION 5: YIELD CURVE ANALYSIS

The yield curve steepened modestly during the quarter. The 2-year to 10-year spread, a commonly monitored indicator of curve shape, moved from negative 39 basis points on March 28, 2024 to negative 35 basis points on June 28, 2024, representing a 4 basis point steepening. Despite this movement, the curve remained inverted, continuing a period of inversion that began on July 5, 2022.

The 10-year to 30-year spread widened from 14 basis points to 15 basis points over the quarter, indicating a slight steepening at the long end of the curve.

SECTION 7: COMPARISON TO PRIOR QUARTERS

Yield changes in Q3 FY2024 were larger than those observed in Q2 FY2024, when the 10-year yield declined 8 basis points from 3.97 percent to 4.20 percent between December 29, 2023 and March 28, 2024. The reversal in Q3 reflects the recalibration of market expectations regarding Federal Reserve rate cuts. At the start of calendar year 2024, federal funds futures markets were pricing in approximately six 25-basis-point rate cuts during 2024. By June 28, 2024, markets were pricing in fewer than two cuts for the remainder of the year.

---

SECTION 8: CONCLUSION AND FORWARD OUTLOOK

The 16 basis point increase in the 10-year Treasury yield during Q3 FY2024, alongside comparable increases across other maturities, reflects a sustained repricing of interest rate expectations driven by persistent inflation and Federal Reserve policy signals. The estimated incremental annual debt service cost of approximately $3.8 billion underscores the sensitivity of federal finances to yield movements given the scale of Treasury issuance.

The Office of Debt Management will continue to monitor yield developments and will publish updated analysis in the Q4 FY2024 report, scheduled for release on October 17, 2024. Parties with questions regarding this report should contact the Office of Debt Management at (202) 622-2960.

APPENDIX A: GLOSSARY OF TERMS

Basis Point (bps): One one-hundredth of one percentage point (0.01%).
Bid-to-Cover Ratio: Total bids received at auction divided by the amount offered.
H.15 Release: Federal Reserve statistical release of selected interest rates, published each business day.
Yield Curve: A graphical representation of yields across different maturities at a given point in time.
Yield Curve Inversion: A condition in which short-term yields exceed long-term yields.
