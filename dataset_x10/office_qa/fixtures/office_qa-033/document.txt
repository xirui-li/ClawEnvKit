EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents the Office of Debt Management's analysis of yield spreads across U.S. Treasury securities for the third quarter of fiscal year 2024, covering the period from July 1 through September 30, 2024. The analysis examines the relationship between short-term and long-term Treasury yields, the implications for federal borrowing costs, and comparisons with corporate and municipal bond markets. During this reporting period, the yield curve underwent a significant structural shift following the Federal Open Market Committee's decision on September 18, 2024, to reduce the federal funds rate target by 50 basis points to a range of 4.75 to 5.00 percent—the first rate reduction since March 2020.

Total federal debt subject to the statutory limit stood at $35.3 trillion as of September 30, 2024. The weighted average interest rate on all outstanding Treasury securities was 3.32 percent, representing an increase of 47 basis points from the same period in fiscal year 2023, when the weighted average rate was 2.85 percent.

The 2-year/10-year spread, one of the most closely monitored indicators of yield curve shape, narrowed from negative 52 basis points on June 30, 2024, to positive 14 basis points on September 30, 2024. This represents the first positive reading for this spread since July 2022, signaling a meaningful normalization of the yield curve following an extended inversion period that began in October 2022.

1.2 Yield Curve Inversion Duration and Magnitude

The 2-year/10-year inversion that began on October 18, 2022, persisted for approximately 24 months, making it the longest sustained inversion since the period from August 1978 through June 1980. At its most extreme point, recorded on July 3, 2023, the spread reached negative 108 basis points, with the 2-year yield at 5.12 percent and the 10-year yield at 4.04 percent.

SECTION 2: FEDERAL BORROWING COST IMPLICATIONS

2.1 Interest Expense Projections

The Congressional Budget Office's August 2024 baseline projects net interest outlays of $892 billion for fiscal year 2024, representing 3.1 percent of gross domestic product. This compares to net interest outlays of $659 billion in fiscal year 2023 and $475 billion in fiscal year 2022. The year-over-year increase of $233 billion, or 35.4 percent, reflects both the higher interest rate environment and the continued growth of outstanding debt.

The Office of Management and Budget, in its Mid-Session Review released July 24, 2024, projected that net interest costs would reach $1.01 trillion in fiscal year 2025 if the yield curve stabilizes near current levels, which would mark the first time in U.S. history that interest payments exceed $1 trillion in a single fiscal year.

2.2 Rollover Risk and Maturity Profile

As of September 30, 2024, approximately $8.9 trillion in Treasury securities, representing 25.2 percent of total outstanding debt, is scheduled to mature within the next 12 months. This concentration of near-term maturities reflects the Treasury's increased reliance on short-duration financing during the 2020-2021 period, when bill issuance was expanded substantially to fund pandemic-related expenditures.

The average maturity of the total outstanding debt portfolio was 5 years and 10 months as of September 30, 2024, compared to 6 years and 2 months on September 30, 2023. The shortening of average maturity increases the federal government's exposure to interest rate fluctuations in the near term.

2.3 Auction Results: Third Quarter 2024

Key auction results during the reporting period are summarized below:

- 10-Year Treasury Note Auction (August 7, 2024): $42 billion issued at a high yield of 3.96 percent; bid-to-cover ratio of 2.32; indirect bidders (a proxy for foreign demand) took 67.3 percent of the offering.

- 30-Year Treasury Bond Auction (August 8, 2024): $25 billion issued at a high yield of 4.31 percent; bid-to-cover ratio of 2.41; indirect bidders took 70.1 percent.

- 2-Year Treasury Note Auction (September 24, 2024): $69 billion issued at a high yield of 3.52 percent; bid-to-cover ratio of 2.60; indirect bidders took 61.8 percent.

The average bid-to-cover ratio across all coupon security auctions during the third quarter was 2.47, compared to 2.38 in the second quarter of fiscal year 2024, suggesting stable investor demand despite the rate volatility surrounding the September FOMC decision.

SECTION 3: COMPARATIVE SPREAD ANALYSIS

3.1 Treasury-to-Corporate Spread Dynamics

Option-adjusted spreads on investment-grade corporate bonds, as measured by the ICE BofA U.S. Corporate Index, tightened to 93 basis points over comparable Treasury securities as of September 30, 2024, down from 104 basis points on June 30, 2024. This tightening reflects improved risk sentiment following the Federal Reserve's pivot toward accommodation.

High-yield corporate spreads, measured by the ICE BofA U.S. High Yield Index, stood at 312 basis points over Treasuries on September 30, 2024, compared to 341 basis points at the close of the second quarter. The compression in high-yield spreads suggests that credit markets are not pricing in a significant near-term recession probability, despite the extended yield curve inversion that preceded the current quarter.

3.2 Municipal Bond Spread Analysis

The ratio of 10-year AAA-rated municipal bond yields to 10-year Treasury yields, commonly used to assess relative value in the tax-exempt market, stood at 68.4 percent as of September 30, 2024. This ratio implies a 10-year AAA municipal yield of approximately 2.59 percent, compared to the 10-year Treasury yield of 3.78 percent. For investors in the 37 percent federal income tax bracket, the tax-equivalent yield on the 10-year AAA municipal bond was approximately 4.11 percent, representing a modest premium of 33 basis points over the comparable Treasury.

SECTION 4: TIPS BREAKEVEN INFLATION ANALYSIS

Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities breakeven rates, which represent the market's implied inflation expectations, showed notable movement during the third quarter. The 5-year breakeven rate declined from 2.31 percent on July 1, 2024, to 2.14 percent on September 30, 2024. The 10-year breakeven rate moved from 2.27 percent to 2.19 percent over the same period.

The decline in breakeven rates is consistent with the Federal Reserve's assessment that inflation is on a sustainable path toward its 2 percent target. The 5-year/5-year forward breakeven rate, which measures expected inflation from 2029 to 2034, stood at 2.24 percent on September 30, 2024, suggesting that long-run inflation expectations remain well-anchored near the Fed's target.

Total outstanding TIPS as of September 30, 2024, were $2.07 trillion, representing 5.9 percent of total marketable debt outstanding.

SECTION 5: OUTLOOK AND RISK FACTORS

The Office of Debt Management identifies the following key risks to the federal government's borrowing cost outlook for the fourth quarter of fiscal year 2024 and beyond:

- Monetary Policy Trajectory: Market participants, as reflected in federal funds futures pricing as of September 30, 2024, anticipate an additional 50 basis points of rate reductions before year-end 2024. If realized, this would bring the federal funds rate target range to 4.25 to 4.50 percent by December 2024.

- Foreign Demand Concentration: Foreign and international investors held $8.33 trillion in Treasury securities as of July 2024, representing 23.6 percent of total outstanding debt. Japan ($1.11 trillion) and China ($775 billion) remain the two largest foreign holders. Any material reduction in demand from these holders could exert upward pressure on yields.

- Fiscal Trajectory: The federal deficit for fiscal year 2024 is projected at $1.9 trillion, or approximately 6.7 percent of GDP, according to the CBO's August 2024 update. Continued large deficits will require sustained high levels of Treasury issuance, which may test market absorption capacity.

- Debt Ceiling: The current debt ceiling suspension expires January 1, 2025. Treasury Secretary Janet Yellen communicated to congressional leadership on September 20, 2024, that extraordinary measures would be available to fund government operations for a period following that date, though the precise duration of those measures remains uncertain.
