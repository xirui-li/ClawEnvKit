EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Department of Defense submitted a total budget request of $886.3 billion for Fiscal Year 2024, representing a 3.2 percent increase over the FY2023 enacted level of $858.5 billion. This analysis examines the allocation of defense appropriations across major spending categories, compares current expenditures against five-year historical trends, and evaluates projected outlays through FY2028. The report was prepared under the direction of CBO Director Phillip Swagel and reflects data compiled through January 31, 2024.

SECTION 1: OVERVIEW OF FY2024 DEFENSE APPROPRIATIONS

The FY2024 National Defense Authorization Act, signed into law by President Biden on December 22, 2023, authorized $886.3 billion in total defense spending. Of this total, $849.8 billion is designated for the Department of Defense base budget, while $32.4 billion is allocated to Department of Energy national security programs, and $4.1 billion covers other defense-related activities.

The enacted FY2024 defense budget represents 12.9 percent of total projected federal outlays of $6.87 trillion and accounts for approximately 3.1 percent of projected Gross Domestic Product. Defense spending as a share of GDP has declined from a post-9/11 peak of 4.7 percent in FY2010 but has stabilized in the 3.0 to 3.2 percent range since FY2019.

FY2020      | $738.0                  | +3.1%                 | 3.4%

Note: The significant 9.8 percent increase in FY2023 reflected supplemental appropriations of $44.2 billion related to Ukraine Security Assistance and accelerated procurement of munitions stocks.

SECTION 2: SPENDING BY MAJOR CATEGORY

2.1 Military Personnel

Military personnel costs represent the single largest discrete category within the DoD base budget, totaling $176.2 billion in FY2024. This reflects a 5.6 percent increase over FY2023 personnel costs of $166.9 billion, driven primarily by the 5.2 percent across-the-board pay raise for active duty service members that took effect January 1, 2024. This pay raise, the largest since FY2002, applies to all 1.33 million active duty personnel across the Army, Navy, Marine Corps, Air Force, Space Force, and Coast Guard.

Reserve component personnel costs account for an additional $22.1 billion, bringing total military compensation to $198.3 billion, or 23.3 percent of the DoD base budget.

2.2 Operations and Maintenance

Operations and Maintenance (O&M) funding totals $295.6 billion in FY2024, representing 34.8 percent of the DoD base budget and the largest single appropriations title. O&M funds day-to-day readiness activities, training, base operations, and depot maintenance. The FY2024 O&M request increased by $8.4 billion, or 2.9 percent, compared to FY2023.

Key O&M sub-categories include:
- Base Operations Support: $47.3 billion
- Depot Maintenance: $28.9 billion
- Training and Recruiting: $19.4 billion
- Fuel and Energy Costs: $16.2 billion (reflecting average jet fuel price of $3.47 per gallon)
- Overseas Contingency Operations: $12.8 billion

2.3 Procurement

Procurement appropriations total $170.4 billion in FY2024, a 4.1 percent increase from $163.7 billion in FY2023. Major procurement programs include:

- F-35 Joint Strike Fighter: $15.6 billion (83 aircraft across all variants)
- Virginia-class Submarine Program: $9.8 billion (2 boats)
- DDG-51 Arleigh Burke Destroyer: $6.1 billion (2 ships)
- CH-47F Chinook Helicopter Modernization: $1.4 billion
- Stryker Combat Vehicle Upgrades: $1.1 billion
- Army Integrated Air and Missile Defense: $2.3 billion

The Lockheed Martin F-35 program remains the largest single procurement line item in the defense budget for the eleventh consecutive year. The unit cost for the F-35A variant stands at $82.5 million in FY2024, down from $89.2 million in FY2021, reflecting economies of scale as production rates have increased.

2.4 Research, Development, Test and Evaluation

RDT&E funding totals $145.9 billion in FY2024, an increase of $6.2 billion, or 4.4 percent, over FY2023. This represents the highest RDT&E appropriation in DoD history in nominal terms. Priority investment areas include:

- Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning: $1.8 billion
- Hypersonic Weapons Development: $4.7 billion
- Next Generation Air Dominance (NGAD): $1.5 billion
- Space-based Capabilities: $11.4 billion (across all services)
- Cyber Operations and Defense: $3.1 billion
- Nuclear Modernization (non-NNSA): $8.9 billion

2.5 Military Construction and Family Housing

Military Construction appropriations total $16.8 billion in FY2024, including $2.1 billion specifically designated for INDOPACOM infrastructure improvements in Guam, Hawaii, and Japan under the Pacific Deterrence Initiative. Family Housing operations and construction account for $1.9 billion.

SECTION 4: NUCLEAR MODERNIZATION COSTS

Total nuclear modernization spending across DoD and the Department of Energy's National Nuclear Security Administration (NNSA) reaches $61.1 billion in FY2024. The NNSA portion accounts for $23.9 billion, while DoD nuclear programs account for the remaining $37.2 billion.

Major nuclear modernization programs and their FY2024 funding levels:
- B-21 Raider Bomber (Northrop Grumman): $5.1 billion
- Columbia-class Ballistic Missile Submarine: $6.2 billion
- Sentinel ICBM (formerly GBSD, Northrop Grumman): $3.7 billion
- B61-12 Nuclear Gravity Bomb Life Extension: $1.5 billion (NNSA)
- W80-4 Warhead Life Extension (ALCM replacement): $1.1 billion (NNSA)

The Congressional Budget Office projects that nuclear modernization costs will peak between FY2028 and FY2032, with annual expenditures potentially reaching $75 billion to $80 billion during that window.

SECTION 5: UKRAINE SECURITY ASSISTANCE

Since February 24, 2022, the United States has committed $75.4 billion in total assistance to Ukraine, of which $46.6 billion represents security assistance administered through the DoD. In FY2024, Congress appropriated an additional $61.4 billion supplemental package on April 24, 2024, of which $37.6 billion is designated for DoD security assistance and weapons transfers to Ukraine.

Presidential Drawdown Authority has been used 47 times since August 2021 to transfer equipment from existing U.S. military stocks, with a cumulative value of $19.7 billion.

SECTION 6: FIVE-YEAR PROJECTIONS (FY2025-FY2028)

Based on current planning assumptions, including 2.4 percent average annual inflation and continued modernization requirements, the CBO projects total defense spending will reach $1.02 trillion by FY2028. This projection assumes no major supplemental appropriations beyond currently planned Ukraine assistance.

Projected Total Defense Budgets:
- FY2025: $919.2 billion (+3.7%)
- FY2026: $951.4 billion (+3.5%)
- FY2027: $984.7 billion (+3.5%)
- FY2028: $1,021.3 billion (+3.7%)

SECTION 7: KEY FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS

1. The 5.2 percent military pay raise, while necessary for retention, adds approximately $8.7 billion in recurring annual baseline costs that will compound in future budget years.

2. The F-35 program, despite per-unit cost reductions, continues to face sustainment cost challenges, with projected lifetime sustainment costs now estimated at $1.58 trillion across the program's lifespan.

3. Space Force growth at 11.8 percent annually is unsustainable without corresponding reductions elsewhere; the CBO recommends a comprehensive space portfolio review by September 30, 2024.

4. Nuclear modernization costs will require difficult tradeoffs beginning in FY2027 when multiple major programs reach peak concurrent production.

5. The DoD's total obligation authority of $886.3 billion remains below the level recommended by the National Defense Strategy Commission, which in its October 2023 report called for annual real growth of 3 to 5 percent above inflation.
