EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents a comprehensive analysis of yield changes across U.S. Treasury securities during the third quarter of fiscal year 2024, covering the period from April 1 through June 30, 2024. The analysis encompasses nominal yield movements, real yield adjustments, and the calculation methodology used by the Office of Debt Management to assess borrowing cost implications for the federal government. During this quarter, the 10-year Treasury note yield rose by 48 basis points, from 4.20 percent on April 1 to 4.68 percent on June 28, 2024, reflecting persistent inflationary pressures and revised Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC) rate cut expectations.

─────────────────────────────────────────────

SECTION 1: METHODOLOGY FOR BOND YIELD CHANGE CALCULATION

1.1 Yield Change Definition

The Office of Debt Management calculates bond yield changes using the standard basis point convention, where one basis point equals 0.01 percent (or 0.0001 in decimal form). Yield change is computed as:

Yield Change (bps) = [Yield (End Date) – Yield (Start Date)] × 10,000

For percentage-based reporting, the formula applied is:

Yield Change (%) = Yield (End Date) – Yield (Start Date)

All yields referenced in this report are expressed as bond-equivalent yields (BEY), consistent with Treasury auction conventions and secondary market quotation standards established under 31 CFR Part 356.

1.2 Data Sources

Yield data is sourced from the Daily Treasury Par Yield Curve Rates published by the U.S. Department of the Treasury at treasury.gov. Secondary validation is performed using Federal Reserve H.15 Selected Interest Rates release data. All calculations use closing market yields as of 3:00 PM Eastern Time on each reference date.

1.3 Duration-Adjusted Cost Implications

To translate yield changes into borrowing cost implications, the Office applies modified duration analysis. For a given security with modified duration D and outstanding principal P, the approximate dollar price change per $100 face value is:

ΔPrice ≈ –D × ΔYield × P

This calculation is applied across the Treasury's outstanding debt portfolio to estimate the mark-to-market impact of yield movements on refinancing costs.

The yield curve exhibited a bear steepening pattern during the quarter, with intermediate and long-term maturities rising more sharply than short-term instruments. The 2s/10s spread widened from –42 basis points on April 1 to –19 basis points on June 28, indicating a partial normalization of the inverted yield curve that has persisted since July 2022.

2.2 Intra-Quarter Volatility

The 10-year yield reached its quarterly high of 4.74 percent on May 29, 2024, following the release of the Bureau of Labor Statistics Personal Consumption Expenditures (PCE) price index showing core PCE at 2.8 percent year-over-year for April 2024. The quarterly low of 4.18 percent occurred on April 5, 2024, following weaker-than-expected nonfarm payroll revisions.

SECTION 3: BORROWING COST IMPACT ANALYSIS

3.1 Outstanding Debt Portfolio Context

As of June 30, 2024, total marketable Treasury debt outstanding stood at $27.4 trillion. The weighted average maturity of the portfolio was 5.8 years, and the weighted average interest rate on outstanding debt was 3.32 percent, up from 3.11 percent as of June 30, 2023.

3.2 New Issuance Yield Comparison

During Q3 FY2024, the Treasury conducted 47 coupon security auctions. The following highlights key benchmark auction results:

- May 8, 2024: 10-year T-Note auction (CUSIP 91282CKH3) cleared at a high yield of 4.483 percent, with a bid-to-cover ratio of 2.49 and $42 billion in securities offered.

- June 12, 2024: 30-year T-Bond auction (CUSIP 912810TZ6) cleared at a high yield of 4.403 percent, with a bid-to-cover ratio of 2.36 and $22 billion in securities offered.

- April 17, 2024: 2-year T-Note auction cleared at 4.898 percent, with a bid-to-cover ratio of 2.66 and $69 billion offered, the largest 2-year offering since November 2023.

3.3 Estimated Incremental Annual Interest Cost

The 48-basis-point increase in the 10-year yield over the quarter has direct implications for new borrowing costs. Based on projected net issuance of $1.85 trillion in notes and bonds during fiscal year 2024 (as published in the May 2024 Quarterly Refunding Statement), the Office estimates that each 10-basis-point increase in average auction yields adds approximately $1.9 billion in annual interest expense to the federal budget.

The 48-basis-point rise in 10-year yields observed this quarter therefore implies an estimated incremental annual interest cost of approximately $9.1 billion on new long-term issuance, assuming the yield environment persists through the remainder of fiscal year 2024.

─────────────────────────────────────────────

SECTION 4: REAL YIELD AND TIPS ANALYSIS

4.1 TIPS Yield Movements

Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS) real yields also rose during the quarter. The 10-year TIPS real yield increased from 2.07 percent on April 1 to 2.31 percent on June 28, 2024, a rise of 24 basis points. This compares to the 48-basis-point rise in nominal 10-year yields over the same period.

4.2 Breakeven Inflation Rate

The 10-year breakeven inflation rate, calculated as the difference between nominal 10-year yields and 10-year TIPS real yields, widened from 2.13 percent to 2.37 percent during the quarter, an increase of 24 basis points. This suggests that approximately half of the nominal yield increase was attributable to rising inflation compensation, while the remaining half reflected higher real interest rate expectations.

─────────────────────────────────────────────

SECTION 5: COMPARATIVE HISTORICAL CONTEXT

5.1 Year-Over-Year Yield Comparison

The 10-year Treasury yield on June 28, 2024, of 4.68 percent compares to 3.84 percent on June 30, 2023, representing a year-over-year increase of 84 basis points. This is the highest June 30 closing yield for the 10-year note since June 30, 2007, when it closed at 5.03 percent.

5.2 Federal Reserve Policy Context

The Federal Reserve maintained the federal funds target rate at 5.25–5.50 percent throughout Q3 FY2024, unchanged since July 26, 2023. As of June 28, 2024, federal funds futures markets were pricing in only one 25-basis-point rate cut before year-end 2024, compared to market expectations of six cuts priced in at the start of calendar year 2024. This repricing of rate cut expectations was the primary driver of intermediate and long-term yield increases during the quarter.

─────────────────────────────────────────────

SECTION 6: KEY FINDINGS AND FORWARD OUTLOOK

Key Findings:

- The 10-year Treasury yield rose 48 basis points during Q3 FY2024, from 4.20% to 4.68%.
- Short-term yields (3-month T-bill) rose only 9 basis points, producing a bear steepening pattern.
- The 2s/10s spread narrowed from –42 bps to –19 bps, partially reducing yield curve inversion.
- The 10-year TIPS real yield rose 24 basis points to 2.31%, while breakeven inflation rose 24 basis points to 2.37%.
- Total marketable Treasury debt outstanding reached $27.4 trillion as of June 30, 2024.
- The weighted average interest rate on outstanding debt rose to 3.32%, up 21 basis points year-over-year.
- The May 8 10-year auction cleared at 4.483% with a bid-to-cover ratio of 2.49.
- Each 10-basis-point yield increase adds approximately $1.9 billion in annual interest expense on new issuance.
- The June 28 10-year yield of 4.68% is the highest June 30 reading since 2007.
- Market expectations for 2024 rate cuts fell from six cuts to one cut during the quarter.

Forward Outlook:

The Office of Debt Management will continue to monitor yield curve dynamics and their implications for federal borrowing costs. The August 2024 Quarterly Refunding Statement, scheduled for release on July 31, 2024, will present updated financing estimates and any adjustments to auction sizes based on revised deficit projections from the Congressional Budget Office's July 2024 Budget and Economic Outlook.
