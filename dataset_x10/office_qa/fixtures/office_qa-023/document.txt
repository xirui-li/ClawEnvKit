EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents the Office of Debt Management's quarterly analysis of yield spreads across U.S. Treasury securities for the period April 1, 2024 through September 30, 2024. The analysis examines the relationship between short-term and long-term Treasury yields, compares domestic spreads against international sovereign benchmarks, and evaluates implications for federal borrowing costs. During the third quarter of fiscal year 2024, the 2-year/10-year Treasury spread narrowed by 34 basis points relative to the prior quarter, moving from negative 43 basis points to negative 9 basis points as of September 30, 2024. This narrowing reflects evolving market expectations regarding Federal Reserve monetary policy and signals a partial normalization of the yield curve following the most prolonged inversion since 1980.

Total federal debt subject to limit stood at $35.46 trillion as of September 30, 2024. The weighted average interest rate on all outstanding Treasury securities reached 3.32 percent, compared to 2.97 percent recorded at the close of fiscal year 2023. Net interest costs for the federal government are projected to reach $892 billion for fiscal year 2024, representing 3.1 percent of gross domestic product and a 33.8 percent increase from the $667 billion recorded in fiscal year 2023.

SECTION 1: YIELD CURVE OVERVIEW AND SPREAD METRICS

1.1 Current Yield Levels

As of September 30, 2024, benchmark Treasury yields across the maturity spectrum were as follows:

Maturity         Yield (%)    Change from June 30, 2024 (bps)
3-Month          5.02         -11
6-Month          4.87         -18
1-Year           4.61         -27
2-Year           3.64         -41
5-Year           3.57         -38
10-Year          3.73         -22
20-Year          4.09         -17
30-Year          4.03         -14

The Federal Reserve's target federal funds rate remained at 5.25 to 5.50 percent through July 31, 2024, before the Federal Open Market Committee voted on September 18, 2024 to reduce the target range by 50 basis points to 4.75 to 5.00 percent. This represented the first rate reduction since March 2020 and contributed significantly to the steepening observed across short-duration maturities during the quarter.

1.2 Key Spread Metrics

The following spread relationships are monitored as primary indicators of yield curve shape and market sentiment:

2-Year / 10-Year Spread: Negative 9 basis points as of September 30, 2024, compared to negative 43 basis points on June 30, 2024, and negative 107 basis points on September 30, 2023. The spread reached its most inverted point of the current cycle at negative 189 basis points on July 5, 2023.

3-Month / 10-Year Spread: Negative 129 basis points as of September 30, 2024. This spread, which the Federal Reserve Bank of New York uses in its recession probability model, has remained negative for 26 consecutive months as of the report date.

5-Year / 30-Year Spread: Positive 46 basis points as of September 30, 2024, widening from positive 31 basis points at the close of the prior quarter. This segment of the curve has maintained a positive slope throughout the inversion period, suggesting long-duration investors continue to demand a term premium for 30-year obligations.

10-Year / 2-Year Real Yield Spread (TIPS-adjusted): Negative 4 basis points as of September 30, 2024, based on Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities yields of 1.89 percent for the 10-year maturity and 1.93 percent for the 2-year maturity.

SECTION 2: HISTORICAL CONTEXT AND INVERSION ANALYSIS

The current yield curve inversion began on October 25, 2022, when the 2-year Treasury yield exceeded the 10-year Treasury yield for a sustained period. As of September 30, 2024, the inversion has persisted for approximately 24 months, making it the longest sustained 2s/10s inversion since the period spanning August 1978 through September 1980, which lasted 26 months.

Historical analysis conducted by the Office of Debt Management, drawing on Federal Reserve H.15 data from 1953 through 2024, identifies six prior instances of 2s/10s inversions lasting more than six months:

Period                    Duration (months)    Max Inversion (bps)    Recession Follow?
Aug 1978 – Sep 1980       26                   -328                   Yes (1980, 1981-82)
Sep 1980 – Jan 1981       5                    -241                   Yes (1981-82)
Dec 1988 – Mar 1990       16                   -62                    Yes (1990-91)
Feb 2000 – Jan 2001       12                   -52                    Yes (2001)
Aug 2006 – Jun 2007       11                   -19                    Yes (2007-09)
Oct 2022 – Present        24+                  -189                   Pending

The average lag between the end of a 2s/10s inversion and the onset of recession across the five completed historical episodes is 11.4 months. The National Bureau of Economic Research has not declared a recession as of the publication date of this report.

SECTION 3: INTERNATIONAL SOVEREIGN SPREAD COMPARISONS

To contextualize U.S. Treasury yield spreads within the global sovereign debt market, the Office of Debt Management tracks spread differentials between 10-year U.S. Treasury securities and comparable maturities issued by G7 and select emerging market sovereigns.

3.1 G7 Sovereign Spread Differentials (10-Year, as of September 30, 2024)

Sovereign          10-Year Yield (%)    Spread vs. UST 10Y (bps)
United States      3.73                 —
Germany            2.12                 -161
United Kingdom     4.00                 +27
Japan              0.85                 -288
Canada             3.21                 -52
France             2.97                 -76
Italy              3.52                 -21

The U.S.-Germany 10-year spread of negative 161 basis points represents a narrowing of 22 basis points from the June 30, 2024 level of negative 183 basis points, driven primarily by the European Central Bank's rate reduction cycle, which began in June 2024. The ECB reduced its deposit facility rate from 4.00 percent to 3.50 percent across two 25-basis-point reductions in June and September 2024.

Japan's 10-year yield of 0.85 percent reflects the Bank of Japan's July 31, 2024 decision to raise its policy rate from 0.10 percent to 0.25 percent, the second rate increase of 2024 following the March 2024 exit from negative interest rate policy. The U.S.-Japan spread of negative 288 basis points remains the widest among G7 pairs and continues to support yen carry trade dynamics.

3.2 Emerging Market Sovereign Spreads

The JPMorgan EMBI Global Diversified Index spread over U.S. Treasuries stood at 378 basis points as of September 30, 2024, tightening from 412 basis points at the close of the prior quarter. Brazil's 10-year sovereign spread over U.S. Treasuries was 198 basis points, while Mexico's comparable spread was 142 basis points.

SECTION 4: FEDERAL BORROWING COST IMPLICATIONS

4.1 Debt Issuance and Rollover Risk

The Treasury Department issued $23.1 trillion in gross marketable debt during fiscal year 2024, of which $19.8 trillion represented rollovers of maturing obligations and $3.3 trillion represented net new issuance to finance the fiscal year 2024 deficit. The Congressional Budget Office estimated the fiscal year 2024 deficit at $1.897 trillion as of its August 2024 update.

The weighted average maturity of the public debt portfolio stood at 70.2 months (5.85 years) as of September 30, 2024, compared to 73.4 months at the same point in fiscal year 2023. This shortening reflects Treasury's increased reliance on bill issuance during the inversion period, as the Office of Debt Management sought to minimize borrowing costs by issuing at shorter maturities. Bills as a share of total marketable debt outstanding reached 22.4 percent as of September 30, 2024, above the 15 to 20 percent target range articulated in Treasury's November 2023 Quarterly Refunding Statement.

4.2 Interest Rate Sensitivity

A parallel shift of 100 basis points upward across the yield curve would increase projected annual net interest costs by approximately $354 billion based on the current debt maturity profile, assuming all maturing securities are refinanced at prevailing market rates. Conversely, a 100-basis-point downward shift would reduce projected annual net interest costs by approximately $298 billion. The asymmetry reflects the concentration of fixed-rate long-duration securities in the portfolio that would not benefit proportionally from rate declines.

SECTION 5: OUTLOOK AND POLICY CONSIDERATIONS

Market participants surveyed through the Primary Dealer Survey conducted by the Federal Reserve Bank of New York in September 2024 assigned a median probability of 72 percent to the federal funds rate falling below 4.00 percent by December 2025. If realized, this trajectory would likely produce a fully positive 2s/10s yield curve by mid-2025, ending the current inversion.

The Office of Debt Management will continue to monitor the following indicators in the fourth quarter of fiscal year 2024:

- The pace and magnitude of Federal Reserve rate reductions and their differential impact across the maturity spectrum
- Foreign official demand for Treasury securities, which totaled $8.1 trillion in holdings as of August 2024, representing 22.8 percent of total public debt outstanding
- Term premium estimates from the Adrian-Crump-Moench model maintained by the Federal Reserve Bank of New York, which stood at negative 0.12 percent for the 10-year maturity as of September 30, 2024, compared to a long-run average of positive 1.5 percent
- Auction demand metrics, including bid-to-cover ratios, which averaged 2.47 across all coupon auctions during the third quarter of fiscal year 2024

This report will be updated following the close of the fourth quarter of fiscal year 2024, with the next publication scheduled for release on or about December 15, 2024.
