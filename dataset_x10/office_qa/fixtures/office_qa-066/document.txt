FEDERAL HIGHWAY TRUST FUND
Compound Annual Growth Rate Analysis and Long-Term Financial Projection
Fiscal Years 2014 through 2024

─────────────────────────────────────────────

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents a comprehensive Compound Annual Growth Rate (CAGR) analysis of the Federal Highway Trust Fund (HTF) for the ten-year period spanning fiscal year 2014 through fiscal year 2024. The analysis encompasses both the Highway Account and the Mass Transit Account, examining revenue inflows, expenditure patterns, and net fund balances. The findings are intended to inform congressional budget deliberations, support the Federal Highway Administration's long-term infrastructure planning, and satisfy reporting requirements under the Highway and Transportation Funding Act of 2014 and the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act of 2021.

The Highway Trust Fund recorded a total balance of $68.4 billion as of September 30, 2024, compared to $10.2 billion on September 30, 2014. The blended CAGR across both accounts for the ten-year measurement period stands at 20.8 percent, though this figure is substantially influenced by extraordinary general fund transfers authorized by Congress during fiscal years 2014 through 2022.

─────────────────────────────────────────────

SECTION 1: BACKGROUND AND FUND STRUCTURE

The Federal Highway Trust Fund was established under the Federal Aid Highway Act of 1956 and is funded primarily through federal excise taxes on motor fuels and related transportation products. The fund is divided into two distinct accounts:

1. The Highway Account, which finances the construction and maintenance of the Interstate Highway System and other federal-aid highway programs.
2. The Mass Transit Account, which supports capital and operating assistance to public transportation systems nationwide.

As of fiscal year 2024, the primary revenue sources for the Highway Trust Fund include:

- Federal gasoline tax: 18.4 cents per gallon (unchanged since 1993)
- Federal diesel fuel tax: 24.4 cents per gallon
- Heavy vehicle use tax: $550 per vehicle annually for vehicles weighing 55,000 pounds or more
- Truck and trailer sales taxes: 12 percent on the first retail sale of heavy trucks and trailers
- Tire excise taxes: ranging from $0.15 to $0.50 per pound on tires exceeding 40 pounds

─────────────────────────────────────────────

SECTION 2: CAGR METHODOLOGY

The Compound Annual Growth Rate is calculated using the standard financial formula:

CAGR = (Ending Value / Beginning Value) ^ (1 / Number of Years) - 1

For this analysis, the beginning value is the fund balance recorded on September 30, 2014, and the ending value is the fund balance recorded on September 30, 2024, representing a ten-year measurement horizon. Separate CAGR calculations are performed for the Highway Account, the Mass Transit Account, total revenues, total expenditures, and the combined fund balance.

All figures are presented in nominal dollars unless otherwise noted. Inflation-adjusted figures using the Bureau of Economic Analysis GDP deflator are provided in Appendix C of the full report.

SECTION 4: CAGR COMPUTATION RESULTS

4.1 Combined Fund Balance CAGR

Beginning Value (FY2014): $10.2 billion
Ending Value (FY2024): $68.4 billion
Number of Years: 10

CAGR = ($68.4B / $10.2B) ^ (1/10) - 1
CAGR = (6.706) ^ (0.10) - 1
CAGR = 1.2083 - 1
CAGR = 20.83 percent

4.2 Highway Account CAGR

Beginning Value (FY2014): $8.1 billion
Ending Value (FY2024): $59.6 billion
Number of Years: 10

CAGR = ($59.6B / $8.1B) ^ (1/10) - 1
CAGR = (7.358) ^ (0.10) - 1
CAGR = 1.2199 - 1
CAGR = 21.99 percent

4.3 Mass Transit Account CAGR

Beginning Value (FY2014): $2.1 billion
Ending Value (FY2024): $8.8 billion
Number of Years: 10

CAGR = ($8.8B / $2.1B) ^ (1/10) - 1
CAGR = (4.190) ^ (0.10) - 1
CAGR = 1.1543 - 1
CAGR = 15.43 percent

4.4 Revenue CAGR

Total excise tax receipts deposited into the Highway Trust Fund grew from $38.6 billion in fiscal year 2014 to $47.3 billion in fiscal year 2024, yielding a revenue CAGR of 2.05 percent over the ten-year period. This modest growth rate reflects the static nature of the per-gallon fuel tax rates, partially offset by increased vehicle miles traveled and growth in the heavy truck fleet.

4.5 Expenditure CAGR

Total obligations and expenditures from the Highway Trust Fund increased from $52.8 billion in fiscal year 2014 to $61.7 billion in fiscal year 2024, representing an expenditure CAGR of 1.57 percent. The slower growth in expenditures relative to revenues in recent years reflects improved project delivery timelines and obligation limitation controls established under the Fixing America's Surface Transportation (FAST) Act of 2015.

─────────────────────────────────────────────

SECTION 5: IMPACT OF GENERAL FUND TRANSFERS

The elevated CAGR figures for fund balances are substantially attributable to a series of congressional general fund transfers that supplemented declining excise tax revenues. The following transfers were authorized and executed during the measurement period:

- August 2014: $10.8 billion transfer authorized under the Highway and Transportation Funding Act of 2014
- December 2015: $70.0 billion transfer authorized under the FAST Act of 2015, disbursed over five years
- March 2020: $13.6 billion emergency transfer authorized under the CARES Act
- November 2021: $118.0 billion in new contract authority and $90.0 billion in supplemental general fund transfers authorized under the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (IIJA), also known as the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law, disbursed through fiscal year 2026

Without these general fund transfers, the organic CAGR of the Highway Trust Fund based solely on excise tax revenues minus expenditures would have been negative, estimated at approximately -3.2 percent annually, reflecting the structural imbalance between static fuel tax rates and growing infrastructure investment demands.

─────────────────────────────────────────────

SECTION 6: COMPARATIVE ANALYSIS

6.1 Comparison with Consumer Price Index

The 10-year CAGR of 20.83 percent for the combined fund balance substantially exceeds the 10-year average annual CPI inflation rate of 3.4 percent recorded between fiscal years 2014 and 2024. However, analysts should note that this comparison is not indicative of real purchasing power growth, as the fund balance increases are primarily the result of appropriated transfers rather than organic revenue growth.

6.2 Comparison with Highway Construction Cost Index

The Federal Highway Administration's National Highway Construction Cost Index (NHCCI) recorded a CAGR of 4.7 percent between 2014 and 2024, meaning that construction costs grew at more than twice the rate of organic fuel tax revenue growth (2.05 percent), further underscoring the structural funding gap that necessitated congressional intervention.

6.3 State Apportionment Growth

Federal-aid highway apportionments to states grew from $40.3 billion in fiscal year 2014 to $68.1 billion in fiscal year 2024, a CAGR of 5.38 percent, driven primarily by the increased authorization levels under the IIJA. California received the largest single-state apportionment in fiscal year 2024 at $6.2 billion, followed by Texas at $5.1 billion and Florida at $3.4 billion.

─────────────────────────────────────────────

SECTION 7: FORWARD PROJECTIONS

Based on current authorization levels under the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act, which extends through fiscal year 2026, and assuming no additional congressional action, the Office of Financial Analysis projects the following:

- The combined Highway Trust Fund balance is projected to decline to approximately $41.2 billion by September 30, 2028, as IIJA supplemental transfers are exhausted and expenditure obligations continue.
- The projected CAGR from fiscal year 2024 through fiscal year 2028 is estimated at -11.8 percent under a baseline scenario.
- Under a scenario in which Congress enacts a new surface transportation reauthorization bill with a 10-cent-per-gallon fuel tax increase effective January 1, 2026, the fund balance would stabilize at approximately $52.7 billion by fiscal year 2028, with a four-year CAGR of -6.3 percent.

─────────────────────────────────────────────

SECTION 8: CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS

The ten-year CAGR of 20.83 percent for the Federal Highway Trust Fund combined balance reflects extraordinary legislative intervention rather than sustainable organic growth. The structural mismatch between the 18.4-cent-per-gallon gasoline tax — last adjusted in 1993 — and the growing cost of maintaining and expanding the nation's surface transportation infrastructure represents the central long-term fiscal challenge facing the fund.

The Office of Financial Analysis recommends that the Office of Management and Budget, in coordination with the Department of Transportation and the Congressional Budget Office, initiate a comprehensive revenue adequacy study no later than March 31, 2025, to evaluate alternative funding mechanisms including vehicle miles traveled fees, electric vehicle road usage charges, and indexed fuel tax adjustments.
