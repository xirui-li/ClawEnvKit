OFFICE OF MANAGEMENT AND BUDGET
FISCAL YEAR 2024 DEFENSE SPENDING ANALYSIS
Prepared by the Budget Analysis Division | Released March 15, 2025

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Department of Defense received a total appropriation of $886.3 billion in Fiscal Year 2024, representing a 3.2 percent increase over the FY2023 enacted level of $858.5 billion. This report provides a comprehensive breakdown of defense expenditures across major categories, compares current spending against historical benchmarks, and evaluates cost efficiency across major acquisition programs. The analysis draws on data submitted by the Office of the Secretary of Defense, the military departments, and the Defense Finance and Accounting Service through December 31, 2024.

SECTION 1: TOTAL DEFENSE BUDGET OVERVIEW

The FY2024 National Defense Authorization Act, signed into law on December 22, 2023, authorized $886.3 billion in total defense spending. This figure encompasses both discretionary and mandatory spending components administered through the Department of Defense and related defense activities.

As a percentage of Gross Domestic Product, defense spending in FY2024 stood at approximately 3.1 percent, consistent with the FY2023 figure and slightly below the ten-year average of 3.4 percent recorded between 2010 and 2020. The Congressional Budget Office projects this ratio will decline modestly to 2.9 percent by FY2028 absent supplemental appropriations.

Total defense outlays by fiscal year (in billions):

FY2020: $738.0
FY2021: $753.5
FY2022: $782.0
FY2023: $858.5
FY2024: $886.3

The compound annual growth rate over this five-year period equals 4.7 percent, outpacing the general inflation rate of 3.8 percent over the same window, indicating modest real growth in defense purchasing power.

SECTION 2: SPENDING BY MILITARY DEPARTMENT

The following table presents FY2024 appropriations distributed across the three military departments and defense-wide accounts:

Department of the Army: $185.7 billion (21.0% of total)
Department of the Navy (including Marine Corps): $230.8 billion (26.1% of total)
Department of the Air Force (including Space Force): $239.4 billion (27.0% of total)
Defense-Wide Accounts: $230.4 billion (26.0% of total)

The Department of the Air Force received the largest single-department allocation for the third consecutive year, driven primarily by continued investment in the F-35A procurement program and the B-21 Raider long-range strike bomber. The Defense-Wide category includes funding for the Defense Intelligence Agency, the Missile Defense Agency, the Defense Logistics Agency, and special operations forces under U.S. Special Operations Command.

SECTION 3: SPENDING BY APPROPRIATION CATEGORY

Operation and Maintenance (O&M) accounts represent the single largest category of defense spending in FY2024 at $368.2 billion, or 41.5 percent of the total budget. This reflects the sustained operational tempo maintained across combatant commands and the ongoing costs of readiness, training, and base operations.

Military Personnel accounts totaled $183.4 billion, representing 20.7 percent of total defense spending. This category funds the salaries, benefits, and retirement accruals for approximately 1.34 million active-duty service members and 800,000 civilian employees of the Department of Defense. The FY2024 military pay raise of 5.2 percent, the largest in more than two decades, contributed significantly to growth in this category compared to FY2023.

Procurement accounts received $170.6 billion, or 19.3 percent of the total. Major procurement programs driving this figure include:

- F-35 Joint Strike Fighter: $15.6 billion (covering 83 aircraft across all variants)
- Virginia-class submarine: $9.4 billion (covering 2 boats)
- B-21 Raider bomber: $5.1 billion
- CH-47F Chinook helicopter upgrades: $1.2 billion
- Guided Multiple Launch Rocket System (GMLRS) munitions: $2.8 billion

Research, Development, Test and Evaluation (RDT&E) funding reached $145.9 billion, representing 16.5 percent of total spending and a 7.1 percent increase over FY2023 levels. The growth in RDT&E reflects accelerated investment in hypersonic weapons development, directed energy systems, artificial intelligence integration, and next-generation space capabilities.

Military Construction and Family Housing accounts totaled $18.2 billion, while the remaining $0.0 billion in revolving and management funds and trust funds accounted for the balance.

SECTION 4: MAJOR ACQUISITION PROGRAM COST ANALYSIS

The Government Accountability Office's annual assessment of major defense acquisition programs, published in May 2024 under report number GAO-24-106047, identified 76 programs with a combined estimated acquisition cost of $1.79 trillion. The following programs represent the highest-value and highest-scrutiny acquisitions in the current portfolio:

F-35 Joint Strike Fighter Program
Program Executive Officer: Lieutenant General Michael Schmidt
Total program acquisition cost (current estimate): $412.0 billion
Cost growth since program inception: 17.4 percent above original baseline
Cumulative deliveries through September 30, 2024: 963 aircraft
Unit recurring flyaway cost (FY2024 lot 17): $82.5 million (F-35A variant)

Columbia-class Ballistic Missile Submarine
Lead contractor: General Dynamics Electric Boat
Total program acquisition cost: $132.0 billion
Lead boat (USS Columbia) delivery scheduled: FY2027
Current cost growth: 6.8 percent above original baseline
Number of boats planned: 12

Sentinel Intercontinental Ballistic Missile (formerly GBSD)
Prime contractor: Northrop Grumman
Total program acquisition cost: $131.0 billion
Cost growth since Milestone B approval in 2020: 81 percent
This program triggered a Nunn-McCurdy critical breach notification in November 2023, requiring formal recertification by the Secretary of Defense. Deputy Secretary of Defense Kathleen Hicks certified the program as essential to national security on February 14, 2024.

SECTION 5: OVERSEAS CONTINGENCY OPERATIONS AND SUPPLEMENTAL FUNDING

Beginning in FY2022, the Overseas Contingency Operations (OCO) account was formally eliminated as a separate budget mechanism following recommendations from the Commission on Defense Planning. Funding previously carried in OCO was absorbed into the base budget. However, Congress provided supplemental appropriations in FY2024 totaling $61.4 billion, of which $14.7 billion was designated for Ukraine Security Assistance, $4.4 billion for Indo-Pacific security initiatives, and the remainder for replenishment of U.S. munitions stocks drawn down through foreign military assistance programs.

Cumulative U.S. security assistance to Ukraine from February 2022 through September 30, 2024, reached $64.1 billion in total value, including equipment drawdowns, foreign military financing, and direct budget support administered through the Ukraine Security Assistance Initiative.

SECTION 6: DEFENSE SPENDING IN INTERNATIONAL CONTEXT

The United States accounted for approximately 37 percent of total global military expenditure in 2024, according to preliminary estimates from the Stockholm International Peace Research Institute. The next largest defense budgets were:

China: estimated $296 billion (official figure; independent estimates range to $450 billion)
Russia: approximately $109 billion
India: $83.6 billion
Saudi Arabia: $75.8 billion
United Kingdom: $74.9 billion

Among NATO allies, 23 of 32 member nations met or exceeded the alliance's two-percent-of-GDP defense spending benchmark in 2024, compared to only seven nations meeting the threshold in 2014. Germany's defense budget reached 2.12 percent of GDP in 2024, the first time the country exceeded the NATO target since reunification.

SECTION 7: AUDIT FINDINGS AND FINANCIAL MANAGEMENT

The Department of Defense has now completed seven consecutive annual financial statement audits since the first full-scope audit in FY2018. The FY2024 audit, conducted by independent public accounting firms including KPMG LLP, Ernst & Young LLP, and Deloitte & Touche LLP across various components, resulted in a disclaimer of opinion for the consolidated DoD financial statements, consistent with prior years. Auditors identified 28 material weaknesses, down from 35 in FY2022, indicating measurable progress in financial management reform.

The Army achieved a qualified opinion on its General Fund financial statements for the first time in FY2024, representing a significant milestone. The Defense Logistics Agency maintained its clean opinion for the third consecutive year.

SECTION 8: PROJECTED SPENDING AND BUDGET OUTLOOK

The FY2025 President's Budget Request, submitted to Congress on March 11, 2024, proposed $849.8 billion in base defense spending plus $32.0 billion in supplemental funding, for a total of $881.8 billion. The request represented a 0.5 percent nominal decrease from FY2024 enacted levels, reflecting administration priorities to constrain discretionary spending growth while protecting modernization investments.

The Congressional Budget Office's ten-year baseline projects cumulative defense spending of approximately $9.7 trillion between FY2025 and FY2034, assuming annual growth averaging 1.0 percent above inflation. Independent analysts at the Center for Strategic and Budgetary Assessments have argued that sustaining current modernization programs while maintaining readiness will require annual real growth of at least 3 to 5 percent, implying a potential funding gap of $400 billion to $700 billion over the decade.
