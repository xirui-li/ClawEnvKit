EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The United States federal government collected $4.44 trillion in total tax revenue during Fiscal Year 2023 (October 1, 2022 through September 30, 2023), representing a decline of 9.3 percent compared to the $4.90 trillion collected in Fiscal Year 2022. This contraction marks the first year-over-year decrease in federal tax receipts since Fiscal Year 2020, when collections fell sharply due to pandemic-related disruptions. The decline is attributable primarily to reduced capital gains realizations, lower corporate profit margins, and the expiration of several pandemic-era tax provisions. Despite the overall decrease, individual income tax collections remained the dominant revenue source, accounting for 49.3 percent of total receipts.

This report was prepared by the Office of Tax Analysis under the direction of Assistant Secretary for Tax Policy Lily Batchelder and presents a comprehensive breakdown of revenue by category, year-over-year comparisons, and projections for Fiscal Year 2024.

SECTION 1: TOTAL REVENUE OVERVIEW

Federal tax receipts for FY2023 totaled $4.44 trillion, down from $4.90 trillion in FY2022 and $4.05 trillion in FY2021. The FY2023 figure represents 16.5 percent of Gross Domestic Product, compared to 19.6 percent in FY2022, which had been the highest share of GDP since 2000. The Congressional Budget Office had projected FY2023 receipts of $4.51 trillion in its May 2022 baseline, meaning actual collections came in approximately $70 billion below that estimate.

SECTION 2: INDIVIDUAL INCOME TAX ANALYSIS

Individual income taxes declined by $456 billion, or 17.3 percent, from FY2022 to FY2023. This represents the largest single-year dollar decline in individual income tax receipts in recorded history, surpassing the $319 billion drop observed in FY2009 during the financial crisis.

The primary driver of this decline was a dramatic reduction in capital gains realizations. In calendar year 2021, taxpayers reported approximately $1.9 trillion in net capital gains, fueled by strong equity markets and elevated real estate transactions. By calendar year 2022, that figure fell to an estimated $863 billion, a decline of roughly 55 percent, as the S&P 500 index lost 19.4 percent of its value during the year. Because individual income tax payments are made on a lag — with estimated payments and final returns reflecting prior-year income — the collapse in 2022 capital gains flowed through to reduced FY2023 receipts.

Withholding taxes, which reflect wages and salaries, showed considerably more resilience. Withheld income and payroll taxes totaled $3.14 trillion in FY2023, up 4.2 percent from $3.01 trillion in FY2022, consistent with continued labor market strength. The unemployment rate averaged 3.6 percent in calendar year 2023, and total wages and salaries grew by approximately 5.8 percent year-over-year according to Bureau of Economic Analysis data.

Non-withheld payments — including estimated quarterly payments and final balance-due payments — fell by 38.7 percent, from $1.07 trillion in FY2022 to $656 billion in FY2023. This category is most sensitive to investment income and self-employment earnings, both of which contracted significantly.

Refunds issued to individual taxpayers totaled $621.5 billion in FY2023, compared to $659.2 billion in FY2022, a decrease of 5.7 percent. The average refund amount for the 2022 tax year (processed in early calendar 2023) was $2,753, down from $3,012 for the 2021 tax year, reflecting the expiration of the enhanced Child Tax Credit and other pandemic-era provisions.

SECTION 3: PAYROLL TAX ANALYSIS

Payroll taxes collected under the Federal Insurance Contributions Act (FICA) totaled $1.614 trillion in FY2023, an increase of $130.4 billion or 8.8 percent over FY2022. This category encompasses Social Security taxes (12.4 percent combined employer-employee rate on wages up to $160,200 in 2023, up from $147,000 in 2022) and Medicare taxes (2.9 percent combined rate with no wage ceiling, plus the 0.9 percent Additional Medicare Tax on high earners).

The Social Security wage base increase from $147,000 to $160,200 — an increase of $13,200 or approximately 9 percent — contributed meaningfully to higher collections. The Social Security Administration estimates this adjustment alone generated an additional $22 billion in payroll tax revenue.

Total employment covered by FICA reached 168.4 million workers in FY2023, compared to 165.9 million in FY2022. Average covered wages grew by 4.9 percent, reflecting continued nominal wage growth even as real wage growth remained modest due to inflation averaging 4.1 percent for calendar year 2023.

SECTION 4: CORPORATE INCOME TAX ANALYSIS

Corporate income tax receipts totaled $419.7 billion in FY2023, a modest decline of $5.2 billion or 1.2 percent from FY2022's $424.9 billion. This relative stability is notable given that corporate profits before tax declined by approximately 8.3 percent in calendar year 2022 according to Bureau of Economic Analysis national accounts data.

The 21 percent statutory corporate tax rate, established by the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of December 2017, remained in effect throughout FY2023. The Inflation Reduction Act of 2022, signed by President Biden on August 16, 2022, introduced a new 15 percent Corporate Alternative Minimum Tax (CAMT) applicable to corporations with average annual adjusted financial statement income exceeding $1 billion. The CAMT generated approximately $4.8 billion in additional revenue in FY2023, its first year of implementation, somewhat below the Joint Committee on Taxation's initial estimate of $9 billion for the year.

The Inflation Reduction Act also imposed a 1 percent excise tax on corporate stock buybacks, which took effect January 1, 2023. This provision generated approximately $8.6 billion in FY2023 receipts, classified under excise taxes.

Corporate refunds totaled $97.3 billion in FY2023, compared to $74.1 billion in FY2022, an increase of 31.3 percent, reflecting larger overpayments of estimated taxes as corporations adjusted to lower-than-anticipated profit levels.

SECTION 5: OTHER REVENUE SOURCES

EXCISE TAXES
Federal excise taxes totaled $87.4 billion in FY2023, a decline of 2.0 percent from $89.2 billion in FY2022. Highway-related excise taxes, including the 18.4 cents-per-gallon federal gasoline tax and 24.4 cents-per-gallon diesel tax, contributed $39.1 billion. Tobacco excise taxes generated $12.2 billion, continuing a multi-decade decline as smoking rates fall. The new stock buyback excise tax contributed $8.6 billion as noted above.

CUSTOMS DUTIES
Customs duties fell sharply by 19.8 percent, from $99.8 billion in FY2022 to $80.0 billion in FY2023. This decline reflects reduced import volumes as consumer spending shifted back toward services from goods, as well as the partial expiration of Section 301 tariffs on certain Chinese goods.

ESTATE AND GIFT TAXES
Estate and gift tax collections rose 5.3 percent to $34.0 billion. The basic exclusion amount for estate tax purposes was $12.92 million per individual in 2023, up from $12.06 million in 2022, indexed for inflation under current law. This exclusion is scheduled to revert to approximately $6 million (inflation-adjusted) after December 31, 2025, absent Congressional action.

MISCELLANEOUS RECEIPTS
Miscellaneous receipts fell dramatically from $138.3 billion in FY2022 to $29.0 billion in FY2023, a decline of 79.0 percent. The FY2022 figure was elevated by Federal Reserve remittances to the Treasury of approximately $107 billion, reflecting profits earned on the Fed's expanded balance sheet. As the Federal Reserve raised interest rates aggressively throughout 2022 and 2023, its interest expenses on reserve balances exceeded its portfolio income, effectively eliminating remittances to Treasury.

SECTION 6: FISCAL YEAR 2024 PROJECTIONS

The Office of Management and Budget projects FY2024 total tax receipts of $4.73 trillion, representing a recovery of 6.5 percent from FY2023 levels. This projection assumes:

- Real GDP growth of 1.5 percent in calendar year 2024
- Continued labor market strength with unemployment remaining below 4.5 percent
- Partial recovery in capital gains realizations as equity markets stabilize
- Full-year impact of the Corporate Alternative Minimum Tax and stock buyback excise tax

Individual income taxes are projected to recover to $2.37 trillion, an increase of 8.9 percent, driven by higher capital gains realizations and continued wage growth. Payroll taxes are projected to reach $1.69 trillion, reflecting the Social Security wage base increase to $168,600 effective January 1, 2024.

CONCLUSION

Fiscal Year 2023 presented a significant correction in federal tax revenues following two consecutive years of historically elevated collections. The $460 billion decline from FY2022 to FY2023 reflects the normalization of investment income, the cessation of Federal Reserve remittances, and the expiration of temporary pandemic-era tax provisions rather than any fundamental deterioration in the tax base. Underlying structural revenue drivers — particularly payroll taxes tied to employment — demonstrated continued strength. The Office of Tax Analysis will publish quarterly updates to revenue projections throughout FY2024, with the next report scheduled for release on May 15, 2024.
