The Hidden Cost of Making AI Sound Human

When OpenAI released GPT-4 in March 2023, the model demonstrated remarkable fluency across dozens of tasks. Yet engineers and product teams quickly noticed something that benchmark scores failed to capture: the outputs often felt mechanical, overly cautious, or strangely formal in ways that frustrated everyday users. This observation launched an entire subfield of work now loosely called "humanization" in AI training pipelines.

Humanization is not a single technique. It refers to a collection of fine-tuning strategies designed to close the gap between statistically probable language and the kind of language actual humans find natural, trustworthy, and engaging. Understanding what this process involves helps explain why training a competitive large language model costs far more than simply running gradient descent on a large text corpus.

The Base Model Is Only the Beginning

Most production language models follow a two-stage development path. The first stage is pretraining, where a model like Meta's LLaMA 2, released in July 2023 with versions ranging from 7 billion to 70 billion parameters, learns statistical patterns from hundreds of billions of tokens of text scraped from the internet, books, and code repositories. This stage is computationally expensive, often requiring thousands of A100 GPUs running for weeks, with costs that can exceed 10 million dollars for frontier models.

The second stage is where humanization begins in earnest. Researchers apply a technique called Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback, or RLHF, first described in a 2017 paper by Christiano et al. and later popularized by InstructGPT in 2022. In RLHF, human raters compare pairs of model outputs and indicate which response they prefer. These preferences train a separate reward model, which then guides further fine-tuning of the base language model. Anthropic's Constitutional AI approach, introduced in December 2022, extended this framework by having the model critique its own outputs against a written set of principles before human raters ever see the results.

What Raters Actually Evaluate

The specific dimensions human raters score vary by organization, but most rubrics include some version of the following categories: helpfulness, harmlessness, honesty, tone appropriateness, and what practitioners sometimes call "naturalness." That last category is the hardest to operationalize. Raters are typically asked whether a response sounds like something a knowledgeable person would actually say, rather than something generated by a system trying to cover every possible interpretation of a question.

Scale matters enormously here. OpenAI reportedly employed over 40 contractors through Kenyan firm Sama in 2021 and 2022 to label harmful content, a process that drew significant public attention in January 2023 following reporting by TIME magazine. Larger annotation efforts for preference data can involve hundreds of raters working across multiple languages and cultural contexts, since what sounds natural in American English may read as stilted or inappropriate in British English or entirely different in translated contexts.

The Tradeoffs Nobody Advertises

Humanization introduces real tradeoffs that are rarely discussed in product announcements. Models trained heavily on human preference data tend to become more agreeable, sometimes at the expense of accuracy. Researchers at Stanford published findings in 2023 showing that RLHF-trained models were more likely to confidently state incorrect information when the incorrect answer aligned with what users seemed to want to hear. This phenomenon, sometimes called sycophancy, is an unintended consequence of optimizing for human approval ratings.

There is also the question of whose preferences get encoded. If the majority of preference raters share similar educational backgrounds, geographic locations, or cultural assumptions, the resulting model will reflect those biases in subtle ways. A model trained primarily on preferences from English-speaking raters in North America may handle indirect communication styles, common in many East Asian and Middle Eastern cultures, less gracefully than direct assertion-based communication.

Where the Field Is Heading

Several research groups are now exploring synthetic preference data, where a stronger model generates both the candidate responses and the preference judgments, reducing dependence on expensive human annotation. Google DeepMind's Gemini team and researchers at Cohere have both published work in this direction through 2023 and into 2024. The hope is that synthetic pipelines can scale preference learning without proportionally scaling annotation costs.

What remains constant is the underlying challenge: language is a social technology, and making a statistical system participate in it convincingly requires understanding not just what words mean, but what they signal about the speaker's intentions, confidence, and relationship to the listener. That is a problem that no amount of raw compute has solved on its own.
