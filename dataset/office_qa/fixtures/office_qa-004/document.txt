EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This technical report presents the results of a comprehensive statistical distribution fitting analysis applied to five major Commonwealth revenue streams over the fiscal years 2019 through 2023. The analysis was commissioned following the Governor's Budget Office directive of September 8, 2023, which required all revenue forecasting units to adopt probabilistic modeling frameworks by the close of Fiscal Year 2024. The primary objective is to identify the best-fit probability distributions for each revenue category, enabling the Office of the Budget to construct confidence intervals around revenue projections and improve fiscal planning under uncertainty.

Total revenues analyzed across all five categories amounted to $214.7 billion over the five-year study period. The analysis employed the Anderson-Darling goodness-of-fit test as the primary selection criterion, supplemented by the Kolmogorov-Smirnov test and Akaike Information Criterion (AIC) for model comparison.

---

SECTION 1: BACKGROUND AND MOTIVATION

Pennsylvania's General Fund revenues have exhibited increasing volatility since Fiscal Year 2019, driven by pandemic-related disruptions, federal stimulus flows, and structural shifts in the labor market. The standard practice of point-estimate forecasting, while adequate during periods of stable growth, proved inadequate during FY2020 when actual Personal Income Tax receipts fell $3.2 billion below the initial projection of $14.8 billion, representing a 21.6 percent shortfall. Similarly, Corporate Net Income Tax collections in FY2021 exceeded projections by $1.9 billion, or 38.4 percent above the forecast of $4.95 billion.

These forecast errors prompted the Secretary of Revenue, Katherine Drummond, to authorize a formal review of forecasting methodology in January 2022. The current analysis represents the culmination of that review process.

---

SECTION 2: DATA DESCRIPTION

Monthly revenue data were obtained from the Commonwealth's Integrated Enterprise System (IES) for the period July 1, 2018 through June 30, 2023, yielding 60 monthly observations per revenue category. All figures are reported in nominal dollars. The five revenue streams analyzed are:

1. Personal Income Tax (PIT)
2. Sales and Use Tax (SUT)
3. Corporate Net Income Tax (CNIT)
4. Realty Transfer Tax (RTT)
5. Inheritance and Estate Tax (IET)

SECTION 3: METHODOLOGY

3.1 Candidate Distributions

For each revenue stream, the following eight probability distributions were evaluated as candidate models:

- Normal
- Lognormal
- Gamma
- Weibull
- Beta (applied to normalized data)
- Generalized Extreme Value (GEV)
- Log-logistic
- Pearson Type III

Parameters were estimated using Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE) for all distributions. The fitting procedure was implemented in R version 4.3.1 using the fitdistrplus package (version 1.1-11) and the MASS library.

3.2 Goodness-of-Fit Testing

The Anderson-Darling (AD) test statistic was computed for each candidate distribution. The AD test was selected over the chi-square test due to its superior sensitivity to deviations in the tails of the distribution, which are of particular concern for revenue forecasting. A significance threshold of alpha = 0.05 was applied. Distributions failing to reject the null hypothesis of adequate fit were retained for further comparison using AIC.

3.3 Seasonal Adjustment

Prior to fitting, monthly data were seasonally adjusted using the X-13ARIMA-SEATS procedure maintained by the U.S. Census Bureau. Seasonal indices were computed separately for each revenue category. The largest seasonal factor observed was for PIT in April, which carried a multiplicative index of 2.47, reflecting the concentration of final tax payments in that month.

---

SECTION 4: RESULTS

4.1 Personal Income Tax

The lognormal distribution provided the best fit for monthly PIT receipts, with an AD statistic of 0.312 (critical value at alpha = 0.05: 0.752) and an AIC of 847.3. The fitted parameters were: mu = 7.214 (log-scale mean) and sigma = 0.418 (log-scale standard deviation). The normal distribution was rejected with an AD statistic of 1.847, exceeding the critical value. The lognormal fit implies a 90 percent confidence interval for annual PIT revenue of $15.1 billion to $20.4 billion, compared to the point estimate of $17.5 billion used in the FY2024 Executive Budget.

4.2 Sales and Use Tax

SUT receipts were best described by the normal distribution, consistent with the category's low volatility. The AD statistic was 0.284 with an AIC of 612.7. Fitted parameters: mean = $1,053.5 million per month, standard deviation = $61.2 million. The 95 percent prediction interval for monthly SUT receipts ranges from $933.5 million to $1,173.5 million. This finding supports the continued use of simple linear trend models for SUT forecasting.

4.3 Corporate Net Income Tax

CNIT exhibited the most complex distributional behavior. The Generalized Extreme Value (GEV) distribution provided the best fit, with an AD statistic of 0.441 and AIC of 923.6. The GEV shape parameter (xi) was estimated at 0.187, indicating a heavy-tailed Frechet-type distribution. This finding has significant implications: the probability of a monthly CNIT receipt exceeding $1.2 billion is estimated at 4.3 percent under the GEV model, compared to only 1.1 percent under a normal assumption. The lognormal distribution was the second-best fit with an AIC of 931.2.

4.4 Realty Transfer Tax

RTT receipts were best fit by the Gamma distribution, with shape parameter alpha = 6.84 and rate parameter beta = 0.0172. The AD statistic was 0.389 and AIC was 701.4. The Gamma fit captures the right-skewed nature of RTT receipts, which are sensitive to large commercial real estate transactions. A single transaction in October 2021 involving the sale of the One Liberty Place office complex in Philadelphia generated $18.7 million in RTT receipts in a single month, representing an outlier that the Gamma distribution accommodated more effectively than symmetric alternatives.

4.5 Inheritance and Estate Tax

IET receipts were best described by the Weibull distribution, with shape parameter k = 3.42 and scale parameter lambda = 3,512 (in thousands of dollars). The AD statistic was 0.298 and AIC was 534.1. The Weibull fit reflects the bounded lower tail of estate tax receipts, which cannot fall below zero and are constrained by the minimum taxable estate threshold of $50,000 under Pennsylvania law (72 P.S. Section 9116).

SECTION 5: FISCAL IMPLICATIONS

5.1 Aggregate Revenue Risk Assessment

Using Monte Carlo simulation with 100,000 iterations, the combined five-category revenue distribution for FY2025 was estimated. The simulation incorporated the fitted distributions and a correlation matrix derived from the historical data. PIT and SUT exhibited the highest positive correlation (r = 0.74), while CNIT and RTT showed a moderate correlation of 0.41.

The simulation produced the following aggregate revenue estimates for FY2025:

- 10th percentile: $42.8 billion
- 25th percentile: $44.6 billion
- 50th percentile (median): $46.3 billion
- 75th percentile: $48.1 billion
- 90th percentile: $50.4 billion

The FY2025 Executive Budget revenue estimate of $46.9 billion falls at approximately the 56th percentile of the simulated distribution, suggesting a modest upward bias in the official projection.

5.2 Rainy Day Fund Adequacy

Pennsylvania's Budget Stabilization Reserve Fund held a balance of $1.83 billion as of June 30, 2023. Under the 10th percentile revenue scenario, the projected shortfall relative to budgeted expenditures of $47.6 billion would be $4.8 billion, exceeding the reserve fund balance by $2.97 billion. This finding supports the recommendation of the Independent Fiscal Office, issued in its November 2023 report, that the reserve fund target be increased to at least 8 percent of General Fund revenues.

---

SECTION 6: LIMITATIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS

The 60-month observation window, while sufficient for initial distribution fitting, limits the precision of tail estimates. The GEV fit for CNIT, in particular, would benefit from a longer historical series. Analysts are advised against extrapolating the GEV model beyond the 99th percentile without additional validation.

The Division of Quantitative Methods recommends the following actions:

- Adopt the fitted distributions identified in this report as the basis for probabilistic revenue forecasting beginning with the FY2026 budget cycle.
- Update distribution parameters annually using a rolling 60-month window.
- Commission an independent validation of the CNIT GEV fit using data from comparable states, specifically Ohio and New Jersey, which maintain similar corporate tax structures.
- Expand the analysis to include federal funds receipts and lottery revenues in the next reporting cycle, scheduled for release in September 2024.
