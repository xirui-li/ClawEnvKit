EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents a detailed analysis of yield changes across the United States Treasury securities portfolio for the third fiscal quarter of 2024, covering the period from July 1, 2024 through September 30, 2024. During this period, the Federal Open Market Committee reduced the federal funds target rate by 50 basis points on September 18, 2024, marking the first rate reduction since March 2020. This action produced measurable shifts across the Treasury yield curve, with significant implications for outstanding debt obligations, new issuance pricing, and the government's projected interest expense for fiscal year 2025.

Total marketable Treasury debt outstanding as of September 30, 2024 stood at $27.7 trillion. The weighted average interest rate on this debt declined from 3.32 percent as of June 30, 2024 to 3.29 percent as of September 30, 2024, representing a decrease of 3 basis points over the quarter. This modest decline reflects the lagged effect of rate changes on a large, diversified portfolio with varying maturities.

SECTION 1: METHODOLOGY FOR YIELD CHANGE CALCULATION

1.1 Data Sources and Benchmark Selection

Yield calculations in this report are derived from the Daily Treasury Par Yield Curve Rates published by the Department of the Treasury, available at fiscaldata.treasury.gov. Benchmark dates used for comparison are June 28, 2024 (last business day of Q2 FY2024) and September 30, 2024 (last business day of Q3 FY2024).

Yield change is calculated using the following standard formula:

Yield Change (basis points) = [Yield(t1) - Yield(t0)] x 10,000

Where t0 represents the beginning benchmark date and t1 represents the ending benchmark date. One basis point equals 0.01 percent, or 0.0001 in decimal form.

1.2 Duration and Price Sensitivity

To assess the price impact of yield changes on outstanding securities, this office employs modified duration as the primary sensitivity measure. Modified duration estimates the percentage change in a bond's price for a 1 percent (100 basis point) change in yield, expressed as:

Modified Duration = Macaulay Duration / (1 + y/n)

Where y equals the annual yield and n equals the number of compounding periods per year. For standard Treasury notes and bonds paying semiannual coupons, n equals 2.

Price change approximation uses the following formula:

Approximate Price Change (%) = -Modified Duration x Yield Change (decimal)

Key observations from Table 1:

- The 2-year Treasury note experienced the largest yield decline of the quarter, falling 107 basis points from 4.71 percent to 3.64 percent. This reflects the 2-year note's high sensitivity to near-term monetary policy expectations.

- The 3-month Treasury bill yield declined 87 basis points, from 5.47 percent to 4.60 percent, consistent with the FOMC's 50 basis point rate cut and market anticipation of additional cuts in late 2024.

- The 30-year Treasury bond yield declined by only 39 basis points, from 4.51 percent to 4.12 percent, demonstrating the typical pattern of long-end yields being less responsive to short-term policy changes.

- The yield curve steepened materially during the quarter. The spread between the 2-year and 10-year Treasury yields moved from -35 basis points on June 28, 2024 (inverted) to +14 basis points on September 30, 2024, marking the end of the yield curve inversion that had persisted since July 2022.

SECTION 3: PRICE IMPACT CALCULATIONS ON REPRESENTATIVE SECURITIES

3.1 10-Year Treasury Note — CUSIP 91282CKH5

The on-the-run 10-year Treasury note issued August 15, 2024 carries a coupon rate of 3.875 percent and matures August 15, 2034. As of June 28, 2024, the prior on-the-run 10-year note (CUSIP 91282CJQ6, coupon 4.250 percent, maturity May 15, 2034) had a yield of 4.36 percent and a modified duration of 8.21 years.

Yield change for the 10-year maturity: -58 basis points (-0.0058 in decimal)

Approximate price change = -8.21 x (-0.0058) = +4.76 percent

This calculation indicates that holders of 10-year Treasury notes experienced an approximate mark-to-market gain of 4.76 percent on their positions during the quarter, driven by the 58 basis point yield decline.

3.2 2-Year Treasury Note — CUSIP 91282CKG7

The on-the-run 2-year Treasury note issued September 30, 2024 carries a coupon rate of 3.625 percent and matures September 30, 2026. The modified duration of a 2-year note at a yield of approximately 3.64 percent is estimated at 1.91 years.

Yield change for the 2-year maturity: -107 basis points (-0.0107 in decimal)

Approximate price change = -1.91 x (-0.0107) = +2.04 percent

3.3 30-Year Treasury Bond — CUSIP 91282CJX1

The 30-year Treasury bond issued February 15, 2024 carries a coupon rate of 4.500 percent and matures February 15, 2054. At a yield of 4.51 percent as of June 28, 2024, this bond carried a modified duration of approximately 15.87 years.

Yield change for the 30-year maturity: -39 basis points (-0.0039 in decimal)

Approximate price change = -15.87 x (-0.0039) = +6.19 percent

Despite the smallest yield change among the three securities analyzed, the 30-year bond produced the largest price appreciation due to its substantially higher duration.

SECTION 4: IMPACT ON GOVERNMENT INTEREST EXPENSE

4.1 New Issuance Savings

The Treasury Department conducted 247 separate public debt auctions during fiscal year 2024. During Q3 FY2024 alone (July through September), the government issued approximately $2.4 trillion in new marketable securities across all maturities. The decline in yields during the quarter produced measurable savings on new issuance compared to rates prevailing at the start of the quarter.

Estimated annualized interest savings on Q3 FY2024 new issuance, calculated by applying the average yield decline of 68 basis points across all maturities to the $2.4 trillion in new securities issued, amounts to approximately $16.3 billion annually. This figure represents a gross estimate and does not account for the maturity distribution of new issuance or the rollover of maturing securities at prevailing rates.

4.2 Projected Fiscal Year 2025 Interest Expense

The Congressional Budget Office, in its August 2024 Budget and Economic Outlook update, projected net interest outlays for fiscal year 2025 at $892 billion, representing 3.1 percent of projected GDP of $28.8 trillion. This projection was prepared prior to the September 18, 2024 rate cut. The Office of Debt Management estimates that the 50 basis point rate reduction, if sustained and reflected in new issuance over fiscal year 2025, could reduce net interest expense by approximately $12 billion to $18 billion relative to the CBO baseline, depending on the pace of additional rate reductions and the maturity composition of new debt issuance.

4.3 Weighted Average Maturity

The weighted average maturity of the Treasury's marketable debt portfolio stood at 5 years and 10 months as of September 30, 2024, compared to 5 years and 11 months as of September 30, 2023. The slight shortening reflects increased issuance of shorter-duration bills and notes to meet financing needs while managing rollover risk.

SECTION 5: CONVEXITY ADJUSTMENT

For large yield movements, the linear duration approximation understates actual price changes due to the convex relationship between bond prices and yields. A convexity adjustment is applied as follows:

Convexity-Adjusted Price Change (%) = (-Modified Duration x Δy) + (0.5 x Convexity x Δy²)

For the 30-year bond (CUSIP 91282CJX1), estimated convexity is 3.42 (expressed in years squared, divided by 100 for calculation purposes). Applying the convexity adjustment to the 39 basis point yield change:

Convexity adjustment = 0.5 x 342 x (0.0039)² = 0.5 x 342 x 0.00001521 = +0.26 percent

Total convexity-adjusted price change = 6.19% + 0.26% = 6.45 percent

For the 2-year note, convexity is negligible at approximately 0.04, producing an adjustment of less than 0.01 percent, which is immaterial for reporting purposes.

- Largest yield decline by maturity: 2-year note, down 107 basis points
- Smallest yield decline by maturity: 30-year bond, down 39 basis points
