EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents a comparative analysis of appropriated versus actual expenditures across major Commonwealth departments for Fiscal Year 2023-2024 (July 1, 2023 through June 30, 2024). Total general fund appropriations for FY 2023-2024 were set at $47.6 billion, representing a 4.2 percent increase over the FY 2022-2023 enacted budget of $45.7 billion. Actual expenditures came in at $46.9 billion, leaving an unspent balance of approximately $700 million, or 1.47 percent of total appropriations.

This document is intended for use by the General Assembly, the Governor's Office, department secretaries, and the public as part of Pennsylvania's commitment to transparent fiscal governance.

SECTION 1: METHODOLOGY AND SCOPE

Data for this report was compiled from the Commonwealth's SAP-based financial management system, known as the Commonwealth Financial Reporting System (CFRS), with final entries closed on August 31, 2024. All figures reflect general fund expenditures only and exclude federal funds, special funds, and augmentations unless otherwise noted. Year-over-year comparisons reference the FY 2022-2023 final expenditure report published February 12, 2024.

Departments included in this analysis represent 94 percent of total general fund spending. Smaller agencies with appropriations under $50 million are aggregated under the "Other Agencies" category.

SECTION 3: NOTABLE FINDINGS BY DEPARTMENT

3.1 DEPARTMENT OF EDUCATION

The Department of Education received the second-largest general fund appropriation at $14.82 billion and expended $14.79 billion, achieving a 99.8 percent utilization rate. The Basic Education Funding subsidy, which accounts for $8.1 billion of the department's total budget, was distributed in full to all 500 school districts by June 28, 2024. Special Education funding totaled $1.35 billion, up from $1.27 billion in FY 2022-2023, reflecting a 6.3 percent year-over-year increase driven by expanded Individualized Education Program (IEP) enrollment.

Secretary of Education Dr. Khalid Muñoz noted in his July 22, 2024 budget review memo that the $28.6 million unspent balance was primarily attributable to delayed hiring in the Bureau of Special Education, where 47 budgeted positions remained vacant as of June 30, 2024.

3.2 DEPARTMENT OF HUMAN SERVICES

Human Services remains the Commonwealth's single largest spending department, with actual expenditures of $16.50 billion exceeding its original appropriation of $16.34 billion by $162.7 million. The overage was driven primarily by Medicaid enrollment growth. As of June 30, 2024, Pennsylvania's Medical Assistance program served 3.1 million enrollees, an increase of 87,000 individuals compared to the same date in 2023.

The General Assembly approved a $175 million supplemental appropriation on March 4, 2024, of which $162.7 million was ultimately utilized. The Long-Term Care line item alone totaled $4.2 billion, representing 25.4 percent of the department's entire budget.

3.3 DEPARTMENT OF CORRECTIONS

The Department of Corrections expended $3.09 billion against an appropriation of $3.18 billion, finishing $85.4 million under budget. Secretary of Corrections Dr. Laurel Harry attributed the savings primarily to a declining inmate population. The average daily inmate count for FY 2023-2024 was 37,842, down from 39,106 in FY 2022-2023, a reduction of 3.2 percent. Per-inmate daily cost averaged $117.43, compared to $114.88 in the prior fiscal year, a 2.2 percent increase largely driven by pharmaceutical and healthcare contract renewals.

3.4 PENNSYLVANIA STATE POLICE

The State Police exceeded its $1.56 billion appropriation by $11.2 million, finishing at $1.571 billion. The overage was covered under the supplemental appropriation approved in March 2024. The primary driver was overtime expenditures totaling $94.6 million, which exceeded the budgeted overtime line of $81.0 million by $13.6 million. Overtime was concentrated in the first and fourth quarters of the fiscal year, coinciding with major weather events in January 2024 and June 2024.

3.5 DEPARTMENT OF AGRICULTURE

Agriculture finished the year at $287.4 million, spending only 92.7 percent of its $310 million appropriation. The $22.6 million unspent balance was largely the result of the Farmland Preservation Program completing fewer easement transactions than projected. The program had targeted 85 new easements in FY 2023-2024 but completed 61, leaving $19.3 million in committed but unspent funds that will carry forward into FY 2024-2025 under a continuing appropriation authorized by Act 14 of 2024.

SECTION 5: PERSONNEL EXPENDITURES

Commonwealth-wide personnel costs, including salaries, benefits, and pension contributions, totaled $9.87 billion in FY 2023-2024, representing 21.0 percent of total general fund expenditures. This compares to $9.41 billion in FY 2022-2023, an increase of $460 million or 4.9 percent.

The State Employees' Retirement System (SERS) employer contribution rate for FY 2023-2024 was 34.94 percent of payroll, down slightly from 35.12 percent in FY 2022-2023. Total SERS contributions from the general fund were $1.43 billion.

As of June 30, 2024, the Commonwealth employed 77,614 full-time equivalent positions funded through the general fund, compared to 76,988 in the prior year, an increase of 626 positions or 0.8 percent.

SECTION 6: REVENUE CONTEXT

General fund revenues for FY 2023-2024 totaled $47.4 billion, falling $200 million short of the $47.6 billion estimate certified by the Independent Fiscal Office on June 15, 2023. Personal income tax collections of $16.8 billion were $340 million below estimate, while sales and use tax collections of $12.1 billion exceeded estimate by $180 million. The revenue shortfall, combined with the $700.5 million in unspent appropriations, resulted in a year-end general fund balance of $500.5 million, which was transferred to the Budget Stabilization Reserve Fund on July 15, 2024.

SECTION 7: RECOMMENDATIONS

The Bureau of Financial Management offers the following recommendations for FY 2024-2025 budget management:

1. The Department of Human Services should submit a revised Medicaid enrollment projection by December 1, 2024, to allow for proactive supplemental appropriation planning rather than reactive emergency requests.

2. The State Police overtime budget should be increased by at least 12 percent in FY 2024-2025 to reflect demonstrated spending patterns over the past three fiscal years.

3. The Department of Agriculture's Farmland Preservation Program should revise its easement completion targets to align with actual transaction capacity, which has averaged 63 easements per year over the past four fiscal years.

4. Departments with vacancy rates exceeding 8 percent of budgeted positions should submit workforce plans to the Office of Administration by November 15, 2024.
