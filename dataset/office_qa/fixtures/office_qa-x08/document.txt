EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents a comprehensive analysis of yield changes across the United States Treasury securities portfolio during the second quarter of fiscal year 2024. The Federal Reserve's decision to maintain the federal funds target rate at 5.25–5.50 percent throughout the quarter, combined with stronger-than-anticipated employment data released on February 2, 2024, produced notable upward pressure on intermediate and long-term Treasury yields. The 10-year Treasury note yield rose from 3.88 percent on January 2, 2024, to 4.20 percent on March 28, 2024, representing a change of 32 basis points over the 87-day measurement period. This report documents the methodology used to calculate yield changes, quantifies the mark-to-market impact on the $26.9 trillion outstanding public debt, and provides duration-adjusted sensitivity estimates for portfolio planning purposes.

─────────────────────────────────────────────

SECTION 1: METHODOLOGY FOR YIELD CHANGE CALCULATION

1.1 Data Sources and Benchmark Selection

All yield data referenced in this report are drawn from the Daily Treasury Par Yield Curve Rates published by the U.S. Department of the Treasury at www.treasury.gov. Benchmark maturities analyzed include the 3-month, 6-month, 2-year, 5-year, 10-year, and 30-year constant maturity Treasury (CMT) rates. Closing yields are recorded at 3:00 p.m. Eastern Time on each business day.

Yield change (ΔY) is calculated using the following standard formula:

ΔY = Y(t₁) – Y(t₀)

Where Y(t₀) is the yield at the beginning of the measurement period and Y(t₁) is the yield at the end of the measurement period. All yields are expressed in annualized percentage terms to two decimal places. Basis point changes are derived by multiplying the decimal yield change by 10,000.

1.2 Duration and Price Sensitivity

To translate yield changes into estimated price impacts, the Office of Debt Management applies modified duration (MD) as the primary sensitivity measure. The price change approximation formula used is:

ΔP/P ≈ –MD × ΔY

For convexity adjustment on securities with modified duration exceeding 7.0 years, a second-order convexity term is added:

ΔP/P ≈ –MD × ΔY + (1/2) × Convexity × (ΔY)²

This methodology is consistent with standards established by the Government Finance Officers Association (GFOA) and aligns with practices described in the Federal Reserve Bank of New York's Treasury Market Practices Group guidelines updated in September 2022.

SECTION 3: PORTFOLIO IMPACT ANALYSIS

3.1 Outstanding Debt Composition

As of March 31, 2024, total marketable Treasury debt outstanding stood at $26.9 trillion. The composition by security type is as follows:

- Treasury Bills (maturities ≤ 1 year): $5.8 trillion (21.6% of total)
- Treasury Notes (maturities 2–10 years): $14.1 trillion (52.4% of total)
- Treasury Bonds (maturities > 10 years): $4.3 trillion (16.0% of total)
- Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS): $2.0 trillion (7.4% of total)
- Floating Rate Notes (FRNs): $0.7 trillion (2.6% of total)

3.2 Weighted Average Maturity and Duration

The weighted average maturity (WAM) of the marketable debt portfolio as of March 31, 2024, was 5.9 years, compared to 6.1 years as of September 30, 2023. The weighted average modified duration of the portfolio was 5.4 years, reflecting the relatively high proportion of shorter-dated notes issued during the 2022–2023 period when the Treasury increased bill and 2-year note issuance to meet near-term financing needs.

3.3 Mark-to-Market Sensitivity Calculations

Using the modified duration approximation, the estimated mark-to-market change in portfolio value attributable to yield movements during Q2 FY2024 is calculated as follows:

For Treasury Notes (primary example using 5-year segment):
- Outstanding: $14.1 trillion
- Weighted average modified duration: 4.2 years
- Weighted average yield change: +25 basis points (0.0025)
- Estimated price change: –4.2 × 0.0025 = –1.05%
- Estimated mark-to-market loss: $14.1 trillion × 1.05% = approximately $148.1 billion

For Treasury Bonds (30-year segment):
- Outstanding: $4.3 trillion
- Weighted average modified duration: 14.8 years
- Yield change: +31 basis points (0.0031)
- Convexity (estimated): 2.85
- Price change (with convexity): –(14.8 × 0.0031) + (0.5 × 2.85 × 0.0031²)
  = –0.04588 + 0.0000137 = –4.587%
- Estimated mark-to-market loss: $4.3 trillion × 4.587% = approximately $197.2 billion

Note: These mark-to-market figures represent unrealized valuation changes and do not affect the government's cash obligations or debt service costs, which are fixed at issuance. The federal government does not carry its debt at fair value for budgetary purposes; these calculations are provided for analytical and risk management purposes only.

SECTION 4: INTEREST COST IMPLICATIONS

4.1 New Issuance Costs

The yield increases observed during Q2 FY2024 have direct implications for the cost of new debt issuance. The Treasury auctioned $1.03 trillion in new marketable securities during the quarter. The weighted average interest rate on new 10-year notes auctioned during the quarter was 4.18 percent, compared to 3.96 percent for equivalent securities auctioned in Q2 FY2023, representing an increase of 22 basis points year-over-year.

The February 7, 2024, auction of $42 billion in 10-year Treasury notes cleared at a high yield of 4.093 percent, with a bid-to-cover ratio of 2.53. The March 6, 2024, reopening of the same note cleared at 4.166 percent, with a bid-to-cover ratio of 2.34, indicating moderately softer demand at the higher yield level.

4.2 Projected Annual Interest Expense

Based on the March 28, 2024, yield levels and the current debt composition, the Congressional Budget Office (CBO) projects net interest outlays for fiscal year 2024 will reach $870 billion, representing 3.1 percent of projected GDP of $28.1 trillion. This compares to actual net interest outlays of $659 billion in fiscal year 2023, an increase of $211 billion or 32.0 percent year-over-year. The primary driver of this increase is the rollover of maturing low-coupon debt issued between 2020 and 2021 into current higher-rate instruments.

SECTION 5: COMPARATIVE HISTORICAL CONTEXT

The 32-basis-point increase in the 10-year yield during Q2 FY2024 compares to the following historical quarterly movements:

- Q1 FY2024 (Oct–Dec 2023): –69 basis points (10-year yield fell from 4.57% to 3.88%)
- Q2 FY2023 (Jan–Mar 2023): +40 basis points
- Q2 FY2022 (Jan–Mar 2022): +83 basis points (largest single-quarter increase in 40 years)
- Q2 FY2020 (Jan–Mar 2020): –125 basis points (COVID-19 flight-to-safety)

The current quarter's movement is therefore moderate by recent historical standards, falling within one standard deviation of the average quarterly yield change of +18 basis points measured over the 20-year period from 2004 through 2023.

─────────────────────────────────────────────

SECTION 6: CONCLUSIONS AND FORWARD GUIDANCE

The 32-basis-point rise in 10-year Treasury yields during Q2 FY2024 reflects market repricing of Federal Reserve rate cut expectations following stronger-than-forecast labor market data. The January 2024 nonfarm payrolls report, released February 2, 2024, showed 353,000 jobs added, far exceeding the consensus estimate of 185,000, and was the primary catalyst for the yield adjustment observed in early February.

The Office of Debt Management will continue to monitor yield curve dynamics and adjust issuance strategy as needed. The Treasury's current strategy of maintaining approximately 20 percent of the portfolio in bills while extending weighted average maturity through increased 10-year and 20-year note issuance remains appropriate given current market conditions.

This report will be updated following the close of Q3 FY2024 on June 30, 2024.
