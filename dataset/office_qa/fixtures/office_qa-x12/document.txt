EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Department of Defense concluded Fiscal Year 2024 (October 1, 2023 through September 30, 2024) with total obligated spending of $874.2 billion, representing a 4.1 percent increase over the FY2023 enacted level of $839.8 billion. This report provides a comprehensive breakdown of defense expenditures across major appropriations categories, compares current spending to historical benchmarks, and evaluates the department's performance against the priorities established in the National Defense Authorization Act for Fiscal Year 2024 (Public Law 118-31), signed by President Biden on December 22, 2023.

Defense spending in FY2024 represented approximately 13.1 percent of total federal outlays and 3.1 percent of gross domestic product, consistent with the department's sustained commitment to maintaining readiness while managing fiscal constraints. The following analysis draws on data compiled by the Defense Finance and Accounting Service (DFAS), the Congressional Budget Office (CBO), and the Government Accountability Office (GAO).

SECTION 1: TOTAL APPROPRIATIONS OVERVIEW

The FY2024 National Defense Authorization Act authorized a topline of $886.3 billion for national defense, encompassing both the Department of Defense base budget and nuclear weapons activities managed by the Department of Energy's National Nuclear Security Administration (NNSA). The DoD base budget alone was authorized at $841.4 billion, with an additional $32.8 billion allocated for Overseas Contingency Operations (OCO) and emergency supplemental funding, primarily directed toward security assistance to Ukraine and Indo-Pacific deterrence initiatives.

SECTION 2: MILITARY PERSONNEL COSTS

Military personnel accounts totaled $183.7 billion in FY2024, the largest single-year increase in this category since FY2019. The increase reflects a 5.2 percent basic pay raise for all uniformed service members effective January 1, 2024, the largest pay increase in more than two decades, authorized under Section 602 of the NDAA FY2024. This raise was intended to address recruitment and retention challenges that saw active-duty end strength fall to 1.28 million personnel in FY2023, below the congressionally mandated floor of 1.32 million.

Key personnel cost drivers in FY2024:

- Basic Pay: $98.3 billion (up from $93.4 billion in FY2023)
- Basic Allowance for Housing (BAH): $27.6 billion, reflecting a 5.4 percent average rate increase across 300 military housing areas
- Basic Allowance for Subsistence (BAS): $4.1 billion
- Retired Pay Accrual: $34.2 billion
- Health Care (TRICARE): $19.5 billion, representing a 6.8 percent increase driven by pharmaceutical costs

The Army experienced the most significant personnel funding increase at 5.9 percent, while the Space Force, now in its fifth year of operation, saw its personnel budget grow by 18.3 percent to $1.4 billion as the service continued to expand toward its authorized end strength of 9,400 guardians.

SECTION 3: OPERATIONS AND MAINTENANCE

Operations and Maintenance (O&M) funding of $311.2 billion constituted 35.6 percent of total DoD spending in FY2024, the largest single appropriations category. O&M funds the day-to-day operational activities of the armed forces, including training, base operations, equipment maintenance, and contracted services.

Notable O&M expenditures included:

- Ukraine Security Assistance Initiative: $7.8 billion obligated in FY2024, bringing the cumulative total since February 2022 to $43.6 billion
- Pacific Deterrence Initiative: $9.1 billion, a 22.3 percent increase over FY2023 levels, funding infrastructure improvements at Guam, the Commonwealth of the Northern Mariana Islands, and allied facilities in Japan and the Philippines
- Depot Maintenance: $26.4 billion, addressing a backlog that the GAO estimated at $31.2 billion as of March 2024
- Fuel Costs: $14.7 billion, reflecting an average Defense Logistics Agency fuel price of $3.84 per gallon, down from $4.21 per gallon in FY2023

The Air Force accounted for the largest share of O&M spending at $112.3 billion, followed by the Navy at $98.7 billion, the Army at $78.4 billion, and the Marine Corps at $21.8 billion.

SECTION 5: RESEARCH, DEVELOPMENT, TEST AND EVALUATION

RDT&E funding of $145.6 billion in FY2024 sustained the department's investment in next-generation capabilities. The department's Science and Technology (S&T) portfolio received $17.3 billion, with artificial intelligence and machine learning programs receiving $3.8 billion across 685 separate program elements, a 41 percent increase over FY2022 levels.

Hypersonic weapons development received $4.7 billion in FY2024 RDT&E funding, distributed across the Army's Long-Range Hypersonic Weapon (LRHW), the Navy's Conventional Prompt Strike (CPS) program, and the Air Force's AGM-183A Air-launched Rapid Response Weapon (ARRW) follow-on development. The Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency (DARPA) received $4.2 billion, a 7.7 percent increase over FY2023.

SECTION 6: HISTORICAL CONTEXT AND COMPARATIVE ANALYSIS

In inflation-adjusted (constant FY2024) dollars, the FY2024 defense budget of $874.2 billion compares as follows to selected historical benchmarks:

- FY2010 (post-Iraq surge peak): $858.3 billion (constant FY2024 dollars)
- FY2000 (pre-9/11 baseline): $512.7 billion (constant FY2024 dollars)
- FY1985 (Reagan buildup peak): $703.4 billion (constant FY2024 dollars)
- FY2024 actual: $874.2 billion

Defense spending as a percentage of GDP has declined significantly from its Cold War peak of 9.4 percent in 1968 and its post-9/11 peak of 4.7 percent in FY2010. The current level of 3.1 percent of GDP remains above the NATO guideline of 2.0 percent, which 23 of 32 NATO member nations met in 2024, up from 11 nations in 2023.

SECTION 7: AUDIT STATUS AND FINANCIAL MANAGEMENT

The Department of Defense received its seventh consecutive disclaimer of opinion on its department-wide financial statements from the independent auditor, KPMG Government Services, in November 2024. While the department has not yet achieved a clean audit opinion, the FY2024 audit identified 28 material weaknesses, down from 37 in FY2022, representing measurable progress. The Army achieved a clean opinion on its General Fund financial statements for the first time in FY2024, a significant milestone.

The department obligated 98.7 percent of its FY2024 appropriations prior to the September 30, 2024 fiscal year-end, with $11.4 billion in unobligated balances returned to the Treasury.

CONCLUSION

FY2024 defense spending of $874.2 billion reflects a department managing competing demands: sustaining current readiness, recapitalizing aging equipment, supporting allied partners, and investing in future capabilities. The 4.1 percent nominal increase over FY2023, when adjusted for the 3.4 percent defense inflation rate calculated by the Office of the Secretary of Defense Cost Assessment and Program Evaluation (CAPE), represents real growth of approximately 0.7 percent. Future budget projections from the CBO's January 2025 baseline estimate that defense spending will reach $1.02 trillion in nominal terms by FY2030, assuming annual growth of approximately 2.5 percent above inflation.
