EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents a comprehensive Compound Annual Growth Rate (CAGR) analysis of the Federal Highway Trust Fund (HTF) covering the ten-year period from fiscal year 2014 through fiscal year 2024. The analysis was conducted in accordance with the requirements set forth under 31 U.S.C. § 9601 and the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act of 2021 (Public Law 117-58), which mandated enhanced financial transparency reporting for all major federal trust funds receiving dedicated tax revenues.

The Highway Trust Fund, established under the Highway Revenue Act of 1956, receives its primary revenues from federal motor fuel taxes and heavy vehicle use taxes. Over the ten-year period under review, the Fund experienced significant volatility driven by pandemic-related disruptions, legislative transfers, and shifting fuel consumption patterns. The overall CAGR for total HTF receipts from FY2014 to FY2024 was calculated at 2.7 percent, while expenditures grew at a CAGR of 4.1 percent, underscoring the structural imbalance that has required repeated congressional intervention.


SECTION 1: METHODOLOGY

1.1 CAGR Calculation Formula

The Compound Annual Growth Rate was calculated using the standard financial formula:

CAGR = (Ending Value / Beginning Value) ^ (1 / Number of Years) - 1

For this analysis, the beginning value corresponds to the audited balance or receipt figure recorded as of September 30, 2014 (the close of FY2014), and the ending value corresponds to the figure recorded as of September 30, 2024 (the close of FY2024). All figures are expressed in nominal dollars unless otherwise noted. Inflation-adjusted figures using the Bureau of Economic Analysis GDP deflator are provided in Appendix C of the full report.

1.2 Data Sources

All financial data was drawn from the following verified sources:

- Monthly Treasury Statement (MTS), published by the Bureau of the Fiscal Service
- Annual Report of the Highway Trust Fund, Federal Highway Administration (FHWA)
- Combined Statement of Receipts, Outlays, and Balances of the United States Government
- Congressional Budget Office (CBO) Budget and Economic Outlook, January 2024 edition
- Internal Revenue Service Statistics of Income data for excise tax collections

1.3 Scope and Limitations

This analysis covers both the Highway Account and the Mass Transit Account of the HTF. General fund transfers authorized by Congress are treated as receipts for purposes of total fund inflow calculations but are separately identified in Table 2 to allow for organic revenue analysis. The analysis does not include the Airport and Airway Trust Fund or the Inland Waterways Trust Fund, which are subject to separate reporting requirements.

2.2 CAGR Computation — Total Receipts

Beginning Value (FY2014): $40.9 billion
Ending Value (FY2024): $53.6 billion
Number of Years: 10

CAGR = ($53.6B / $40.9B) ^ (1/10) - 1
CAGR = (1.3105) ^ (0.10) - 1
CAGR = 1.02745 - 1
CAGR = 2.74%, rounded to 2.7%

2.3 CAGR Computation — Dedicated Tax Revenue Only

Beginning Value (FY2014): $38.4 billion
Ending Value (FY2024): $44.9 billion
Number of Years: 10

CAGR = ($44.9B / $38.4B) ^ (1/10) - 1
CAGR = (1.1693) ^ (0.10) - 1
CAGR = 1.01578 - 1
CAGR = 1.58%, rounded to 1.6%

The dedicated tax revenue CAGR of 1.6 percent is notably below the average annual inflation rate of 3.1 percent recorded over the same period (Bureau of Labor Statistics, CPI-U, FY2014–FY2024), indicating a real-terms decline in the purchasing power of HTF dedicated revenues.

The expenditure CAGR of 4.1 percent significantly outpaces the receipt CAGR of 2.7 percent, producing a widening structural gap. The Mass Transit Account expenditures grew at a CAGR of 4.4 percent (from $8.8 billion in FY2014 to $13.5 billion in FY2024), while Highway Account expenditures grew at 4.0 percent (from $41.3 billion to $61.4 billion).


SECTION 4: FUND BALANCE ANALYSIS

4.1 Combined HTF Balance — End of Fiscal Year

The combined balance of the Highway Trust Fund at the close of FY2014 stood at $8.4 billion. By the close of FY2024, the combined balance had grown to $11.2 billion, reflecting the impact of substantial general fund transfers authorized under the Fixing America's Surface Transportation (FAST) Act of 2015 and the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act of 2021.

The CAGR of the fund balance over the ten-year period was:

CAGR = ($11.2B / $8.4B) ^ (1/10) - 1 = 2.92%, rounded to 2.9%

However, analysts should note that without the $55.1 billion in cumulative general fund transfers received between FY2014 and FY2024, the fund would have faced insolvency as early as the fourth quarter of FY2016, based on projected outlay trajectories modeled by the CBO in its January 2016 Budget Outlook.

4.2 Federal Motor Fuel Tax Revenue Context

The federal gasoline tax has remained fixed at 18.4 cents per gallon since its last adjustment on October 1, 1993, under the Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act of 1993. The federal diesel tax stands at 24.4 cents per gallon. The failure to index these rates to inflation represents the primary structural driver of the gap between dedicated revenues and expenditures. Had the gasoline tax been indexed to inflation from 1993 forward, the rate would have been approximately 39.2 cents per gallon by FY2024, generating an estimated additional $26.3 billion in annual dedicated revenue.

5.2 Notable Observations

- The FY2020 decline in total receipts to $36.1 billion represents the lowest annual receipt figure in the ten-year period, attributable to a 15.3 percent reduction in vehicle miles traveled during the COVID-19 pandemic.

- The largest single-year general fund transfer occurred in FY2022, totaling $15.2 billion, authorized under the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act of 2021.

- The Mass Transit Account maintained a positive balance throughout the review period, closing FY2024 with a balance of $3.1 billion.

- The Highway Account balance at the close of FY2024 was $8.1 billion, compared to $6.2 billion at the close of FY2014.

- Electric vehicle adoption, estimated at 8.4 percent of new vehicle sales in calendar year 2024 (per the International Energy Agency), poses an emerging long-term risk to fuel tax revenue that is not yet materially reflected in the ten-year CAGR figures but is projected to reduce dedicated HTF revenues by $4.8 billion annually by FY2035 under CBO baseline assumptions.


SECTION 6: CONCLUSION AND FORWARD PROJECTIONS

Based on the ten-year CAGR analysis, the Federal Highway Trust Fund faces a persistent and widening structural imbalance between dedicated revenues growing at 1.6 percent annually and expenditures growing at 4.1 percent annually. Without legislative action to either increase dedicated revenue sources, reduce authorized spending levels, or establish a permanent supplemental funding mechanism, the CBO projects the combined HTF balance will fall below the minimum operating threshold of $4.0 billion by the end of FY2028.
