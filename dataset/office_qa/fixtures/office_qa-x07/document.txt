COMMONWEALTH OF PENNSYLVANIA
OFFICE OF THE BUDGET
BUREAU OF FINANCIAL MANAGEMENT

ANNUAL EXPENDITURE TREND ANALYSIS
FISCAL YEARS 2019-2020 THROUGH 2023-2024

Published: October 15, 2024
Report Reference: BFM-2024-ETR-007

---

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents a comprehensive analysis of Commonwealth expenditure patterns across five fiscal years, from FY 2019-2020 through FY 2023-2024. Total general fund expenditures grew from $34.1 billion in FY 2019-2020 to $44.7 billion in FY 2023-2024, representing a cumulative increase of 31.1 percent over the five-year period. The average annual growth rate of 5.6 percent exceeded the state's average inflation rate of 4.1 percent during the same period, indicating real spending growth of approximately 1.5 percent annually. This analysis identifies key drivers of expenditure growth, highlights areas of fiscal concern, and provides projections for FY 2024-2025.

---

SECTION 1: OVERVIEW OF TOTAL EXPENDITURES

Pennsylvania's general fund expenditures have followed an upward trajectory since FY 2019-2020, interrupted only briefly by pandemic-related spending reallocations in FY 2020-2021. The following table summarizes total expenditures by fiscal year:

FISCAL YEAR | TOTAL EXPENDITURES | YEAR-OVER-YEAR CHANGE | PERCENT CHANGE
FY 2019-2020 | $34.1 billion | — | —
FY 2020-2021 | $36.8 billion | +$2.7 billion | +7.9%
FY 2021-2022 | $39.4 billion | +$2.6 billion | +7.1%
FY 2022-2023 | $42.3 billion | +$2.9 billion | +7.4%
FY 2023-2024 | $44.7 billion | +$2.4 billion | +5.7%

The deceleration in growth between FY 2022-2023 and FY 2023-2024 reflects the expiration of federal American Rescue Plan Act (ARPA) supplemental funding, which had contributed approximately $1.8 billion in additional spending capacity during FY 2021-2022 and FY 2022-2023.

---

SECTION 2: EXPENDITURE BY MAJOR CATEGORY

2.1 Health and Human Services

The Department of Human Services (DHS) remained the single largest expenditure category in every year analyzed, accounting for 39.2 percent of total general fund spending in FY 2023-2024, or approximately $17.5 billion. This represents an increase from $13.2 billion in FY 2019-2020, a growth of 32.6 percent over five years.

Key drivers within DHS include:

- Medical Assistance (Medicaid): Expenditures grew from $8.9 billion in FY 2019-2020 to $12.1 billion in FY 2023-2024, a 35.9 percent increase. Enrollment peaked at 3.6 million recipients in March 2023 before declining to 3.1 million by June 2024 following the end of continuous enrollment protections under the federal Public Health Emergency.

- Long-Term Care Services: Costs rose from $2.4 billion to $3.2 billion between FY 2019-2020 and FY 2023-2024, driven by increased utilization of home and community-based services and a 14 percent increase in the average daily rate for nursing facility care, which reached $312 per resident day in FY 2023-2024.

- Child Welfare Services: Expenditures increased from $1.1 billion to $1.4 billion, reflecting a 27.3 percent rise in the number of children receiving out-of-home placements, which reached 14,800 children as of June 30, 2024.

2.2 Education

The Department of Education received $14.2 billion in FY 2023-2024, representing 31.8 percent of total general fund expenditures. This compares to $11.6 billion in FY 2019-2020, an increase of 22.4 percent.

- Basic Education Funding: The Basic Education Funding formula distributed $7.9 billion to school districts in FY 2023-2024, up from $6.1 billion in FY 2019-2020. Governor Josh Shapiro's administration added $567 million in new basic education funding in FY 2023-2024 alone, the largest single-year increase in the program's history.

- Special Education: Funding grew from $1.15 billion to $1.42 billion over the five-year period, a 23.5 percent increase, reflecting both enrollment growth in Individualized Education Program (IEP) services and increased per-pupil costs averaging $28,400 annually for students requiring intensive support.

- Higher Education: Appropriations to the State System of Higher Education (PASSHE) declined from $477 million in FY 2019-2020 to $452 million in FY 2023-2024, a reduction of 5.2 percent, as the system consolidated from 14 universities to 10 through the integration completed in July 2022.

2.3 Transportation

The Pennsylvania Department of Transportation (PennDOT) managed $5.1 billion in combined state and federal expenditures in FY 2023-2024. General fund contributions to transportation totaled $1.9 billion, supplemented by $3.2 billion in federal Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (IIJA) funding. The state's bridge replacement program, which targeted 3,300 structurally deficient bridges identified in the 2021 infrastructure assessment, expended $890 million in FY 2023-2024, completing repairs on 418 bridge structures during the fiscal year.

2.4 Public Safety and Corrections

The Department of Corrections operated with a budget of $2.8 billion in FY 2023-2024, down from $3.1 billion in FY 2020-2021. This 9.7 percent reduction reflects a decline in the state prison population from 45,200 inmates in January 2020 to 37,600 inmates as of June 30, 2024. The average annual cost per inmate rose from $42,700 to $51,300 during the same period, partially offsetting population-driven savings.

---

SECTION 3: PERSONNEL COSTS AND WORKFORCE TRENDS

Personnel costs, including salaries, benefits, and pension contributions, totaled $9.8 billion in FY 2023-2024, representing 21.9 percent of total general fund expenditures. This compares to $8.4 billion in FY 2019-2020, an increase of 16.7 percent.

The Commonwealth employed 78,400 full-time equivalent (FTE) positions as of June 30, 2024, compared to 81,200 FTEs in June 2020. The 3.4 percent reduction in workforce was achieved primarily through attrition rather than layoffs, consistent with the Governor's Office directive issued in February 2022.

Pension obligations through the State Employees' Retirement System (SERS) required a Commonwealth contribution of $1.47 billion in FY 2023-2024, reflecting an employer contribution rate of 34.06 percent of covered payroll. The SERS funded ratio improved from 57.3 percent in FY 2019-2020 to 63.8 percent in FY 2023-2024, reflecting strong investment returns averaging 8.2 percent annually over the period.

---

SECTION 4: DEBT SERVICE AND CAPITAL EXPENDITURES

Debt service payments on general obligation bonds totaled $1.2 billion in FY 2023-2024, representing 2.7 percent of total expenditures. The Commonwealth's outstanding general obligation debt stood at $11.4 billion as of June 30, 2024. Moody's Investors Service affirmed Pennsylvania's Aa3 bond rating in September 2024, citing improved reserve fund balances and structural budget improvements.

The Budget Stabilization Reserve Fund (Rainy Day Fund) held $1.83 billion as of June 30, 2024, equivalent to approximately 4.1 percent of annual general fund expenditures. This represents a significant improvement from the $340 million balance recorded in June 2020.

---

SECTION 5: PROJECTIONS FOR FY 2024-2025

Based on enacted appropriations and current expenditure trends, total general fund expenditures for FY 2024-2025 are projected at $47.6 billion, representing a 6.5 percent increase over FY 2023-2024. Key assumptions underlying this projection include:

- Medicaid expenditure growth of 4.8 percent, reflecting enrollment stabilization at approximately 3.0 million recipients
- Basic Education Funding increase of $300 million above FY 2023-2024 levels
- Debt service payments declining to $1.1 billion due to scheduled bond retirements
- Personnel cost growth of 3.2 percent, consistent with the collective bargaining agreement ratified by AFSCME Council 13 in April 2024, which provides for 3.0 percent base wage increases in each of the next three fiscal years

---

SECTION 6: AREAS OF FISCAL CONCERN

The Bureau of Financial Management identifies three primary areas requiring continued monitoring:

1. Medicaid Cost Trajectory: Despite enrollment declines following the end of continuous enrollment protections, per-member per-month costs increased 8.3 percent in FY 2023-2024, driven by behavioral health service utilization and pharmacy costs. If this trend continues, Medicaid expenditures could reach $14.0 billion by FY 2026-2027.

2. Federal Funding Uncertainty: Approximately $4.2 billion of FY 2023-2024 expenditures were supported by time-limited federal grants, including IIJA transportation funds and remaining ARPA allocations. The expiration of these funding streams without replacement could create structural budget gaps beginning in FY 2025-2026.

3. Pension Funding: While the SERS funded ratio has improved, the Public School Employees' Retirement System (PSERS) remains at 73.4 percent funded as of June 30, 2024, with unfunded liabilities of $18.7 billion. Required employer contributions are projected to increase by $240 million over the next five years.

---

CONCLUSION

Pennsylvania's expenditure growth over the FY 2019-2024 period reflects both deliberate policy investments in education and human services and structural cost pressures in healthcare and personnel. The improvement in reserve fund balances and bond ratings indicates strengthened fiscal management. However, the expiration of federal supplemental funding and persistent cost pressures in Medicaid and pension obligations present significant challenges for long-term fiscal sustainability. The Bureau recommends that the Governor's Budget Office conduct a comprehensive program efficiency review by March 31, 2025, targeting $500 million in recurring savings to be incorporated into the FY 2025-2026 Executive Budget proposal.
